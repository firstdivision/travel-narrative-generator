## Introduction

This is meant to be a travel log for all of Asia, or at least my attempt at making sense of it as I go.

I want to keep a record of the movement, the logistics, the places themselves, and whatever happens in between. "I'm doing it my way", as the song goes. Bullet points that become paragraphs.

That is the plan, anyway.

Asia is too large, too varied, and too contradictory to pretend I am going to understand it in any complete way in so short an amount of time. So this is not that kind of project. It is just a running account, one day at a time, of where I went, what I noticed, what went wrong, what surprised me, and what stayed with me by the end of the day.

I expect there will be long transit days, minor mistakes, overheated afternoons, stations and airports that blur together, and moments that feel sharper than they should for no obvious reason. Those are usually the parts worth writing down.

So this is the beginning.

## Thursday, April 2 - Coffee Before Contrails

At 2:30 AM, Joe picked me up and took me to the airport.

It was painfully early, but that hardly seemed like the moment to get casual about timing. There had been plenty of news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the latest "Unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how these unprecedented things keep happening so regularly.

It was too early for Taco Deli to be open, which meant I was denied one last taste of Tex-Mex before leaving. A small tragedy, if not exactly a surprising one. I did manage to find a much-needed, much-desired coffee, and since there was nothing decent to eat yet, I sat down and watched people instead.

That kind of hour makes everything feel slightly unreal. I found myself thinking about the situation I had arranged for myself, the scale of it finally landing with a little force. It was April 4, and I would not be back home until the end of May. A shot of adrenaline ran through me when I thought about that, and about my house, asleep without me. The water was turned off. Blankets were spread over the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I had thrown out anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants had been handed off to my cousin down the road. They were on a travel adventure of their own, in a manner of speaking.

Then the airport speaker crackled to life and yanked me out of all that. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes. Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful and short, just an hour and twenty minutes. The layover was four hours, but somehow it passed quickly. I finally found some "real food," an airport egg sandwich that cost $14 and did not exactly justify its own existence. I was in the international terminal, so I kept watching people and wondering which of them might be on my next flight, and which were headed somewhere else entirely. Were they going home, or leaving it? Were they thinking ahead the way I was, trying to picture the shape of the days waiting for them? My thoughts kept circling back to food. I wondered what I would end up eating, what I would find in these foreign lands, what the future might taste like.

One guy near the gate looked especially lost when the agents began announcing that passports had to be signed. He had the expression of a baby deer trying to understand bureaucracy. He kept looking down at his passport, at a very empty signature line, then looking around.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we started talking. It turned out this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him. It felt like the sort of thing airports are full of, these brief crossings of other people's lives, entire plots compressed into a few sentences at the gate.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I told him.

Then the flight to Narita boarded.

It was a 777, but an old one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone that slightly yellowed, ivory color that says an aircraft has been doing this for a long time. Not filthy, exactly, just seasoned. This old lady had clearly been flying for years. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to help the passengers wandering around with their bags, trying to find overhead space. It was mild chaos, the kind that feels more annoying when you know there are almost 14 hours still ahead of you.

As soon as I finally found somewhere to stash my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I could switch places with her because the people to my right were her group. Both seats were aisles, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, almost immediately after I sat down in the new seat, that woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was seated on the left side of the plane.

I looked over and saw him looking back at me with a hopeful expression. I suspect he also caught the look in my eyes, which was saying, as clearly as possible, *please don't make me do this*. He said something to his wife in a language I didn't recognize, and she turned back to me.

"He says it is fine."

Maybe he truly was fine sitting apart from his wife and children for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes. Maybe he had instantly understood that I was at the end of my seat-swapping generosity.

After takeoff, I managed to sleep through a good chunk of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep helped. The plane was freezing, aggressively so, and I eventually figured out a system. I pulled the hoodie strings tight so only my mouth and nose were exposed. The black fabric acted as a sort of improvised sleep mask over my eyes. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It made a little heat igloo, trapping some warmth and even a bit of humidity in that dry airplane air.

There was a meal service somewhere in there, but it was so unremarkable that I remember almost nothing about it.

Then I slept again.

At some point, somewhere over the Pacific, we crossed the international date line and it became Friday.

## Friday, April 3 - Godzilla, Jetlag, Ramen

```poem
My suitcase died with a thump and a drag
While Tokyo watched me tow half of a bag
I chased a blue arrow that lied with zeal
Then fixed it with ramen and sleep as a meal
```

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there. From 30,000 feet it looked like any other stretch of ocean, but I knew it wasn't. This was JAPAN ocean, which felt different mostly because I had been trying to get here for a very long time.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose because I wanted the train ride into Tokyo. I'd heard it was a great little approach to the city, and after that many hours in transit, a train sounded more civilized than another sealed tube.

I made my way toward the station dragging my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on had decided, back at the Austin airport, that the dawn of a two-month trip was the ideal moment for its two front wheels to self-destruct. Pulling it the normal way now produced an irritating THUMP THUMP as the ruined wheel treads slapped against the floor. The timing had a kind of personal disrespect to it.

The signs to the trains were easy enough to find, and they were in English too, which felt like one of those quiet privileges Americans get used to without examining too closely. Even so, I managed to get lost and had to backtrack to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed to go to the office with the big `JR` sign over it.

At the `JR` office, another very nice and very polite woman sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, nearly direct to Shinjuku Station. I was starting to notice a pattern. The train arrived exactly on time, of course. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time. Whoever it was, the line had clearly survived the trip to Japan. The train was clean and quiet, and I spent the ride staring out the window through a thick haze of jet lag and general travel fatigue, the kind that comes not just from being tired but from having moved yourself absurd distances across the planet.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, broken roller bag still complaining, Google Maps loaded, hotel only 1.5 kilometers away. This will be a snap, I thought, which is the sort of thing a person says shortly before being corrected by reality.

The GPS was being weird. I was walking one way, but the magic blue arrow insisted I was headed ninety degrees to the right. I felt like one of the pilots in those *Air Crash Investigations* shows I watch. Trust your instruments. I was trying to, but something was obviously wrong. In the end I decided I would solve that problem later and just watch how my position moved on the map. Crude, but workable. It was an unfortunate complication for someone like me trying to navigate a new city while half-conscious.

Tokyo unfolded in front of me anyway. Crowds, lights, motion. Company men in near-identical black-and-white suits moved through the streets like extras from *The Matrix*. It was Friday, around 4 p.m. for them, which seemed like an appropriate hour for the city to start loosening its tie.

Even after I figured out how to balance the roller bag on its two surviving wheels, I kept lifting it by the handle now and then because I found the noise annoying and assumed everyone around me probably did too. The city itself was loud, and I saw other people doing much the same thing, but there was still that familiar voice in the back of my head saying, Be a good ambassador. Then again, I do this at home too. I tend to assume I am the one causing the problem when in reality I am probably not on anyone's radar at all.

After staircases, street crossings, a tunnel I think, and perhaps a vertical ascent up an ice wall, memory gets hazy there, I finally found Hotel Gracery, the one with the giant Godzilla on top. That was me. Home for the next two nights.

Check-in was smooth. A charming front desk woman smiled, handed me my key card with both hands, and escorted me to the correct elevator. I mangled my first of what would become many attempts at saying *Arigato Gozaimasu*, but she seemed to appreciate the effort. I am sure she had heard worse, or no attempt at all, from clueless Americans like me showing up with no local language skills. It reminded me of a midwestern lady ordering "kay suh dill uhs" in a Mexican restaurant, except this time I was the problem.

The room was tiny, but not in any surprising way. Hotel rooms in Japan are small. Land is expensive, Tokyo is dense, and these places are built for efficient short stays, bed, shower, out the door, which described me perfectly. I was here for two nights and then on a plane again. The compactness felt intentional rather than stingy.

My first goal was a shower, and the shower setup was a mystery at first glance. There was a tub and a shower wand on a hose, but the hose sat over by the sink. On the floor was a bowl and a small stool with a very conspicuous ass-shaped curve in it. I was too tired to decode the whole system, so I stood in the tub holding the shower wand and let the hot water wash away airport, airplane, train, and the general residue of being in motion for too long.

My brain wanted sleep with real desperation. The poor thing thought it was 4 a.m. when it was actually something like 5 p.m. local time. My notes from this stretch are about as clear as my head was. I denied the brain the sleep it wanted, partly out of practical necessity and partly out of spite. It has, after all, denied me sleep plenty of times when *I* wanted it. Revenge is mine.

I stepped back out into a dark sky and the neon brightness of Shinjuku at night. I wandered around for a while like a drunken sailor whose only intoxicant was sleep deprivation.

There was a place on my list, and by this point I had also figured out how to fix the blue arrow on Google Maps, which made me feel briefly like an expert. The place was Ichiran Ramen, just down the road.

The line was long. Not Disney long, but long enough to disappear around a corner in a stairwell that looked more like the entrance to a mine shaft than to dinner. About thirty of us shuffled slowly downward toward the promise of hot ramen.

Inside, the process was efficient in that quietly intimidating way efficient systems can be. At an electronic kiosk I ordered the basics, ramen and a beer. Then I got a paper slip to refine the details: noodles soft, broth medium, spice level white, that sort of thing. The form even had suggested settings marked with a light oval dotted line. Circle me, it seemed to say. Let's not make this harder than it needs to be.

I was seated in one of those individual booths, a whole row of ramen cubicles, like a library for broth or a driving range with better outcomes. Soon the bowl arrived. I had never had what I thought of as real ramen before. My experience was mostly instant packets, or the version I make at home with plain noodles and whatever happens to be around. So I did not have much to compare it to, but it was very good. The noodles interested me most. They had more flavor than I expected, and more bite. Maybe it was the flour, maybe they were fresh-made, maybe both.

Whatever the reason, I took the advice of the many YouTube videos I had watched in preparation for this trip and ordered a noodle refill. Another slip of paper. More circles around noodle settings. Two 200-yen coins. The little door opened, the paper and coins disappeared, two smaller coins came back as change, and before long I was adding fresh noodles to the broth I had left. I could get used to this. I only wished I had ordered the refill noodles soft instead of medium, since that is how I make them at home. I had been a little flustered and failed to think ahead.

Unfortunately, all good things end, even bowls of ramen in private booths. I left. The staff called goodbye and thank you, I think. I butchered the thank you again. He did not flinch. Like the woman at the hotel, he had clearly seen it all before.

Back in the room, I finally put the ass-shaped stool to use and washed my feet off. I could see the logic of it now. Tomorrow I would try using the whole bathing setup the way it was meant to be used, sitting there properly and washing first. But for the moment sleep had become the single dominant fact of my existence.

With clean feet and a full stomach, I crawled into the small bed and closed my eyes. Sleep arrived instantly.

## Saturday, April 4 - Blossoms, Bidets, and Rain

This was my first full day.

I woke before sunrise, then forced myself back to sleep. A couple of hours later the room had gone lighter, enough that I finally lifted my head and opened the curtains. Outside was a pink sunrise, soft and almost theatrical in the way it made the city look gentler than it probably was. I took a photo, then lay back down.

As promised to myself, I took a bath in the traditional style, seated on the little special stool. It was actually quite enjoyable. Relaxing, practical, and much easier to reach my feet and toes than standing in a shower like a normal, less enlightened person.

I made it to hotel breakfast right after they opened, 06:30, I think. It was buffet style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple that was so fresh and ripe it almost felt rude to call it hotel fruit. I went back for seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down. I had not been entirely sure what it was, but I signed up anyway, and I was glad I did.

After breakfast I started trying to plan the day. Rain was supposed to come later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the `Go` app for taxis, one of those modern love-hate parts of travel where every country seems to require its own digital ecosystem, its own payment cards, its own tiny bureaucracies hidden inside your phone. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and Touch 'n Go. The world is globalized right up until it absolutely is not.

Before heading out, coffee made another decision for me. Back in the room, I had my first encounter with the fancy Japanese bidet. It had a control panel for type, strength, and options I did not fully need explained. It was unusual at first. Then, very quickly, it made perfect sense. The internet comment section argument floated into my head, the one that always appears when bidets come up and people, mostly Americans if we are being honest, act scandalized by them: if you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel, would you? They have a point.

Fresh and clean, I stepped outside and started ordering my `Go` taxi. Right there by the hotel stone facade was a man with what could only be described as a level 100 hangover. He was leaning against the wall with his head in his hands, visibly regretting whatever alliance he had formed with alcohol the night before. It was early, and Tokyo was already offering little side scenes.

My taxi arrived, and through a mix of translation apps and gestures we confirmed where I wanted to go. I had the sense my driver was a little concerned because my destination was not some standard tourist stop, especially not for someone leaving from the Godzilla-themed hotel in the middle of tourist central. I asked him about the cherry blossoms. He replied that they were all in bloom everywhere.

When we arrived, he opened my door, then held up one finger and typed into his app. After a moment he turned his phone around. It said, "Enjoy the cherry blossoms!" I smiled, he noticed, and we had a brief little *arigato gozaimasu* competition, each of us saying it back and forth several times until I finally walked away with a last wave and a grin.

I climbed up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were out with their dogs, photographing the dogs with the blossoms, lifting them up for selfies with the trees behind them. Was this a tradition? I had no idea. But it was fun, and oddly wholesome in a way that made me linger.

I took my own photos, notably minus a dog, and walked down one of the promenades along the river. I was still taking more pictures and was about to head toward a nearby museum when my intestines made their feelings known.

*Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.*

No. Oh no.

I kept walking to the intersection and took a few more photos, hoping it had been a one-off. It was not. The grumbling returned, and with it came a hot feeling in my belly, like I had swallowed a baked potato whole. It felt like I needed to fart, but I was very certain this was not a gas-only negotiation.

So, reluctantly, I ordered a ride back to the hotel. It was all I could think to do. Most places were not open yet, and I did not even know the word for bathroom, much less whether it was socially acceptable to ask for one if I was not a customer. All of that ran through my head while I waited.

The fifteen-minute cab ride felt long. I made it, barely, and it was not a bluff. The bidet was used again.

After that I lay on the bed for about an hour to make sure there would not be a round two. When it seemed safe enough, I decided the next stop would be Meiji Jingu, temple, shrine, park, the whole spread. It was about a 30-minute, 2-kilometer walk. Under normal circumstances that was nothing. Under uncertain intestinal circumstances, it had the psychological weight of an expedition. It had also started raining, so I took the hotel-issued umbrella and ventured out.

The sidewalks were full of umbrellas, and I started learning the little choreography of how to move among them, lifting mine up, angling it to the side, copying what everyone else was doing. As usual, the fastest way to learn how to blend in was to imitate the locals until it felt natural, so that was what I did.

At Meiji Jingu I walked the paths, toured the shrine, the garden, and the well. The shrine area was busy. The garden, which required an extra payment, was not. I liked the garden more. I am, after all, very much an introvert, and I am most at peace in quiet places like that, or out on the water. The rain made everything darker and softer around the edges. It suited the garden better than it suited the crowds.

Afterward I doubled back toward the hotel. The rain kept changing its mind, sometimes coming down hard, sometimes just misting, what we would call spitting back home. At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned as a place to go anyway. A giant mall with many floors. In the rain, it seemed like the obvious place to be, so I went in.

I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed, but I was not sure whether the hotel would let me ask them to throw away my old suitcase. So I did not buy the one I liked, even though I suspected it was much better quality than the old one I was dragging around. Maybe, I told myself, I would find it again in Malaysia, where I might have more time for practical errands like buying luggage.

The mall had a basement food section, which I had read about beforehand. Somehow I completely forgot this while I was inside, and instead headed back out into the rain toward the hotel. A real triumph of planning.

I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but by then I had stepped in one puddle too many. My socks and shoes were wet, and that tipped the scales. I decided to regroup with myself at the hotel instead.

Back in the room I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, where everyone was still asleep and would only read them in their morning, and checked the weather radar. A clear patch was coming. It looked like enough time to go back out and finally check the Chicken Katsu box. So I put on fresh socks, listened to my stomach grumble in a more manageable way, and headed out again.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the destination, another of the many blue pins on my Google map. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately. Ordering was easy, just another touchscreen. The only hiccup was the waitress having to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. Fine. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side was an ocean of brown beef gravy because I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, like some kind of seasoned professional. It was exactly the sort of meal that made the rain and the damp socks feel less personal.

Back outside, I was not quite sure what to do next. I had a mild case of analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., but was actually only 3 p.m. Jet lag had turned time into a prank.

So I just wandered. On one street I heard music and followed it to an outdoor JPOP concert already underway. I tried to take a video, and a security guard quickly shut that idea down. Fair enough. Some things are apparently for memory only.

Sometime later, maybe 5:30, though time still had very little meaning to me, I headed back to the hotel again. At the front desk I asked about getting a taxi tomorrow. They simply made a reservation for me. Well. That was easy.

Back in the room I tried not to fall asleep. I failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up at 9. Since I was leaving tomorrow, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure.

There were more texts. Then my phone rang, and the name on the screen was one of my friends from back home. The sun had come up on that side of the planet. It was their morning, so I took my first phone call from this side of the world back to home. Someone was awake and wondering what I was up to over here.

From 10:30 to 11:30 I filled out this journal.

As I faded toward sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in my room swaying back and forth. Wind, I realized. I could hear it now. It had been predicted, and it had arrived. The sound was strangely soothing. My eyes gave up on staying open and closed on their own.

## Sunday, April 5 - From Haneda to Home

It turned out I did not need a cab that early.

Haneda was nearly empty, security especially, and I should have breezed through it. Instead I managed a small performance of incompetence by forgetting that, yes, I did in fact need to remove my watch, wallet, and belt. So I got to do the apologetic shuffle, the universal *sorry, I'm an idiot* routine, while they rescanned my things and I walked back through trying not to look like someone who had never seen an airport before.

With time to spare, I drifted into the duty free fragrance and skin-care shop and texted Kate like she was a remote consultant. I sent photos, waited for rulings, considered my options. In the end, the verdict was simple enough, still too expensive, not worth it. The fluorescent glamour of airport luxury failed to make its case.

Boarding started on time. They announced there would be no wifi, which felt mildly primitive and also, as it turned out, kind of useful. Compared to the American Airlines flight, this one was smooth as butter. No drama, no chaos, just a plane doing the very basic and increasingly rare thing of behaving properly.

The food was good, memorably good. Noodles with some sort of sauce, and chicken curry with rice. I actually remember the meal, which is more than I can say for most airline food. See, AA? That is the point. If a meal is decent, it becomes a thing a person can recall later without resentment. Maybe *you* should take some *notes*, American Airlines.

Since there was no wifi, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and went through the movie selection. I found two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, which felt like a decent way to keep moving mentally in the same direction my body was already going, deeper into Asia, or at least deeper into the kinds of stories and settings that make a place feel less abstract.

Landing did not lead to any graceful transfer. I had to run for the next flight, which meant a shuttle to a different terminal and then another security check right at the gate. The gate area itself was sealed off behind glass walls, self-contained and slightly strange. I had not been expecting any of that, and by the time I got there I was sweaty from the run and generally in that airport state where you are not exactly panicking but your body seems convinced that maybe you should be. Still, all was well. Soon enough I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.

Kuala Lumpur airport made a good first impression. Clean, clear, plenty of signs. Not that this prevented me from following one labeled "e-rides" to a place that was apparently not the right one. Airports do enjoy that kind of thing. Fortunately, the Grab app had built-in navigation for the airport and guided me with surprising competence, down two escalators and over to the correct pickup spot.

Again, like walking through Tokyo in the rain, I fell back on one of my more reliable travel strategies, do what others do. Find the people who look confident and copy them. Assume they know what *they* are doing, and maybe some of that knowledge will rub off. Fake it till you make it, just with more luggage.

At the pickup area there was a board of monitors divided into A and B, green and red, and for a while I stared at it as if I were trying to decode some bizarre random-letter bingo game. Eventually I realized the numbers were license plates for the e-ride cars entering the pickup zone. New ones appeared on the right and drifted left until they vanished, presumably with passengers inside and confusion transferred elsewhere.

So I watched the board and the little car icon on the Grab app until the moment finally came. My car was here. I stepped out into a sea of people and immediately lost all sense of where I was supposed to be, scanning for the correct area and for a red car. Then I saw the plate number. It matched. I waved. He saw me and waved back, though not in any friendly social sense. His wave clearly meant, *Get in here now*. Then he said it out loud.

"Hurry! Get in!"

This was my first experience with Grab no-wait zones. They are not kidding. Be ready to get in.

Then it was new country time. I had that wide-eyed feeling, looking out the window at everything, taking in the roads, the traffic, the signs, the buildings, all of it. The driver wove expertly in and out of lanes, either to get me to my destination faster or for his own entertainment. Hard to say. In any case, before long I was deposited at what would be my home for the next five weeks, Tower #2 of *Eclipse @ Pan'gaea*.

And immediately I was in the next thing.

There were guards at the building. Not only at the main gate, but also in the car drop-off area that served as the main lobby. I would learn very quickly that this was completely normal in Malaysia. The same uniforms, the same berets, guards in grocery stores, malls, seemingly everywhere. But when you are not expecting it, it lands with a little jolt.

My Airbnb instructions said to show a QR code to the guard, so I did, and he seemed to have no idea why I was showing him a QR code. Instead he presented a log book and asked me to fill in my details. Name, phone number, passport number, which gave me pause. I hesitated there for a second, then wrote it anyway. Date, signature, done. When I handed the pen back, he smiled and said something that sounded to me like, "now you are new man." Whether that was exactly what he said or not, that is what I heard.

Over the next few days, some kind of bond would form between us, at least in my head. On one day he would come over and chat with me about my work.

"Computers," I would say.

"Ahh, hardware?" he would ask.

"No, software actually."

He would nod, satisfied.

For now, though, he let me into the elevator lobby behind the key-carded doors and pointed me toward the mailroom. Right, the keys. I had nearly forgotten. They were in the mailbox, which I opened with the provided code.

Then it was elevator to 19, two right-hand turns, and there I was in front of the double doors to my new place. The outer door was more like a gate, with a small landing, and then the main door two feet behind it. I sorted out which key opened which lock and let myself in.

You never quite know what you are getting with an Airbnb. Usually it is some version of under-decorated, just the basics, the bare necessities, or the Bare Neccecities if you want to drag a certain animated movie into it. This one was a little more spartan than I had expected. A washer-dryer combo next to a medium-sized fridge. White, basic countertops. A two-burner glass-top electric stove with the usual scorch marks and scratches that seem to appear on every glass cooktop after enough years of service. A microwave. A small utilitarian table with four mismatched chairs. A similarly plain living room. Two bedrooms, two bathrooms.

To my American preconceptions, it looked barren at first. So much so that I sent a few texts to my future travel companions warning them about the state of it. But even then I could sense that first impressions might not be the final word. Over the coming days and weeks I would come to like the simplicity. I would buy a few things it seemed to need, most importantly a comfortable couch blanket so I could burrito myself after work, plus some hand towels for the bathrooms. I would even put a little elbow grease into the shower grout. But even on day one, tired and a little out of sync, I could feel the place beginning to make its case.

I texted the host to say I was in and asked where I might get some basic supplies for the night, water and orange juice, and also let him know there was a bag of garbage left behind, presumably by the cleaning crew. He replied almost immediately, said he would come pick it up and bring me supplies.

And he did. Soon he arrived with a case of bottled water and two things of orange juice. I thanked him. He responded with a quick Malaysian-style bow with hands together. Then he was gone, and I was on my own again.

Not long after that, I fell asleep on the couch under one of the bedroom blankets.

## Monday, April 6 - Doors, Coffee, and Concrete

```poem
Jetlag kept watch for a tiger all night
While doors mocked me politely by refusing my might
I paid more for coffee than taxis would claim
```

## 2026-05-09

## Saturday, May 9 - Ferries, False Alarms, and Salt Water

```poem
We chased a gate that wasn’t boarding at all
And sacrificed hot tea to airport protocol
By sunset I was wave-drunk, sun-soft, and awake
Then came home to share the patio with a snake
```

We woke up and the Grab was already waiting for us, which is always a slightly accusatory way for a day to begin. It was time to leave Malaysia and start the next leg: Thailand.

Somewhere tucked at the end of a peninsula sits an island called Koh Lanta, in Krabi province, along Thailand's southwestern coast on the Andaman Sea. Geographically, the Andaman Sea is bordered by Thailand to the east, Myanmar to the north, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands of India to the west, and the Strait of Malacca to the south. The Strait of Malacca is one of the most important shipping routes on Earth, the narrow sea lane connecting the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea and the Pacific. To get from the Andaman Sea into the broader Indian Ocean, you simply sail west or southwest past the Andaman and Nicobar island chain. There isn't some dramatic boundary where one suddenly becomes the other. The Andaman Sea is basically a northeastern arm of the Indian Ocean.

But at that moment all of that was just "where we are going."

Our Grab driver, on the way to the airport, told us it would probably be raining in Thailand. Thanks, dude.

Airport security was easy, except I had forgotten there was a second, actual security check before the gate. So instead of following the usual rule number one of flying, which is go directly to your gate and assess the situation from there, I confidently led everyone into another part of the airport while declaring that there was not much available down by our gate. Overconfidence is such a useful travel skill until it isn't.

We did find a nice place to get food, and they ordered Teh Halia. It is the kind of drink that feels medicinal in the best possible way: sweet, spicy, creamy, and hot enough to fog your glasses the second it lands on the table. The name is simple. *Teh* means tea. *Halia* means ginger in Malay. So it's literally ginger tea, but in practice it usually means strong black tea pulled together with fresh ginger, condensed milk or evaporated milk, and enough sugar to make it comforting instead of sharp.

While we were ordering, we noticed the signboard for our flight said **NOW BOARDING**, even though our flight did not leave for another three hours.

That put us into a slight emergency mode.

We were fairly sure, very sure in fact, that the flight was not really boarding, but who knows? Airports occasionally decide to remind you that confidence is not the same thing as knowledge. We hurried to the entrance to Concourse L, where our gate was, and found the second security line. Then came the entirely fair complaint: "Andrew... you said there was nothing down here. Now we have these teas that we will need to pour out!"

I chugged my latte. They tried to do the same with their teas and mostly just scorched their mouths. A ridiculous little scene, but an accurate one.

Eventually we got through security without their drinks, and I was mostly right: there really was not much there. A Burger King and an OldTown White Coffee. OldTown White Coffee is popular in Malaysia for some of the same reasons diners, Waffle House, or Tim Hortons become fixtures elsewhere. It's familiar, reliable, affordable, everywhere, and tied to a specific kind of comfort. But there is also a more Malaysia-specific thing going on. OldTown built itself around Ipoh white coffee, one of Malaysia's signature coffee styles. Unlike darker roasted Western coffee, Ipoh white coffee beans are traditionally roasted with margarine or palm oil and brewed strong, then mixed with condensed milk. The result is smoother, sweeter, less bitter, and very easy to drink in tropical heat. It became nostalgic because kopitiams, the traditional coffee shops, were social hubs for generations. OldTown basically industrialized and modernized that experience.

And of course the flight was not boarding at all. So we waited the customary amount of time before we actually boarded, got on the plane, and continued on to Thailand.

Customs and immigration in Thailand were easy-peasy. There was no line. I suppose there *are* benefits sometimes to being near the front of the plane.

The driver from Pimalai Resort was there to meet us, and we got into the van. Then came one of my favorite feelings in the world, one that anyone who has spent time on the water probably knows immediately. You are in a car and it is headed toward the water. Salt water, most likely. You come over a hill and there it is. The road slopes downward toward the shore. It narrows. There are random cars parked alongside it, small boats on trailers, maybe half in the bushes, waiting to be launched one day. Sometimes there is a ferry terminal at the bottom of it all, waiting to take you somewhere else. If you have salt water in your blood, it does something immediate to your nervous system. It relaxes your soul. It is an affliction, really, but a welcome one.

As this all unfolded in front of us through the van windows, each of us said basically the same thing in different words.

The van turned off the main ferry road and dropped us at a comfortable gazebo by the water, with a long pier leading out to a floating concrete dock. We were drawn toward it like thirsty desert travelers toward an oasis, except this oasis was mostly logistics and diesel and a nice view.

After a short wait, the boat arrived: a Gilligan's Island-esque motor cruiser converted into a passenger ferry. The ride to the other side took about five minutes total. We all wished it had taken longer.

Then another van ride, another forty minutes, and we were at the resort. It was very nice. Hushed tones. Warm woods. Flowered necklaces placed around our necks. A refreshing citrus-based drink with a straw made from some sort of plant. Ice clinking in the glass while the resort host led us to comfortable seating. It was the kind of arrival designed to lower your shoulders by force.

We had an elephant trip booked for the next morning, but the host told us there was a boat trip we could join instead, if we wanted. We said that would be nice. She went off with that information and returned a few minutes later to tell us she had managed to reschedule our elephant trip to Monday and, weather permitting, we would be able to join the boat trip as well.

Then we were taken to our room, which was very, very nice. Two buildings connected by a private infinity pool, with the bedrooms on one side and a common room or living room on the other. It felt faintly absurd in the way luxury sometimes does when you arrive slightly sweaty and underfed and carrying airport brain.

We went to check out the grounds and get situated, but mostly we were hungry, so we found one of the restaurants. It was a much-needed lunch, especially for me since I had not eaten much. I devoured the bread and butter. Then I crushed a plate of rice with stir-fried beef and peppers, spicy and savory and exactly what I needed. The beef came in thin slices, flash-seared in a screaming-hot wok with onions, chilies, and sweet bell peppers that stayed bright and just a little crisp. One of the chilies was extremely hot, hot enough that after eating one I decided one was enough, which felt like useful research. The beef was tender, and the salty, peppery juices ran down into the rice underneath. There was also warm bread with butter, sea salt, and a black pepper grinder. It all disappeared quickly, and I felt much better afterward. The Pink Gin and Tonic may have helped too. I can't remember what was in it. It must have been really good.

Later we went down to the beach. The water had that high salinity that makes floating feel almost suspiciously easy. There were waves, and we discovered they were actually body-surfable. A quick stop at the resort beach cabana produced two boogie boards. I slowly remembered how those things work. At one point I managed to carve into a wave, but they were breaking quickly and right on the beach, so I turned parallel to shore and rolled the board over the top of me in a kind of 360 barrel-roll move. I was in heaven. Back in the water. Not my usual ocean, but I did not care. I was exactly where I wanted to be.

There were drinks on the beach too, buy one get one free, gin drinks and piña coladas, the sort of offering that feels impossible to argue with when you are damp and full of salt.

Once it got dark, we headed back to the room. We ordered a buggy, which is what the resort called the extra-small lorry-style trucks with rows of seats that they used to shuttle people around.

Back at our rooms, there was a snake on the patio by our pool.

I suppose that is the tradeoff in a jungle climate. The wildlife has not been informed that this is a very nice resort built for humans by humans. We are in *their* house, really. The hotel was quick to send someone with a snake hook. I offered to help illuminate the snake with both of our phones, and he gladly accepted. It felt like a very practical collaboration.

With the patio now free of wildlife, I took the opportunity to get into our private infinity pool, which looked out over the water below. After that we ordered room service: chicken satay and mango sticky rice.

Then came the shower, with genuinely excellent hot water, which was unfortunately not a universal experience in our villa. One of my fellow travelers got the opposite version of events. Her shower not only ran out of hot water, but the water pressure dropped to nearly zero at the exact wrong moment, when she had shampoo in her hair. Travel has a way of distributing luxury unevenly, sometimes within the same bathroom block.

For some reason I seemed to get both the responsibility and the credit for everything in the resort. We thought maybe it was because I was a man, because even though the credit card used for the booking belonged to Kate and she had made the reservation, I was always the one being asked to sign. Strange how quickly systems make assumptions and then never revisit them.

At some point my mind wandered, as it does when travel days stretch across too many modes of transport. Aren't continents really just large islands? Where exactly is the line between island and mainland and continent? And then another old classroom thought floated back to me, the one about the sheer number of oxygen molecules in a breath, and how in every breath you take you almost certainly inhale at least one molecule once breathed by someone historical. Julius Caesar, Shakespeare, Einstein, whoever your school wanted to use to make science feel intimate.

By then I was showered, fed, and finally still. Outside, somewhere beyond the room and the patio and the recently relocated snake, was the dark water. And we had made it.
