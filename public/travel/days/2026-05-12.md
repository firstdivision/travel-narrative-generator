What is a long tail boat?  You probably know already but don't realize it.  These narrow wooden boats are powered by a car or truck engine mounted on a long exposed driveshaft that extends behind the boat like a tail—hence the name. They're often brightly painted with colorful ribbons, flowers, and fabric tied to the bow for good luck.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess — all glass, river reflections, designer perfume, boat traffic, durian snacks, and air conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths — it’s probably ICONSIAM.

Sitting directly on the banks of the Chao Phraya River, ICONSIAM feels less like a mall and more like somebody tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a gigantic mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment venues.

But the weird magic of ICONSIAM is that beneath the Louis Vuitton storefronts and marble floors, there’s also a fake indoor floating market called SOOKSIAM. And I mean that affectionately. Downstairs you’ll find smoky grilled seafood, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to sustain civilization after the collapse. It’s touristy, yes — but also legitimately fun and one of the easiest places in Bangkok to sample regional Thai food without crossing the city.

Pistachio milk in Bangkok feels like one of those drinks invented by somebody who got tired of ordinary coffee shop menus and decided the world needed something softer, greener, and vaguely luxurious.

The first thing you notice is the color. Not bright cartoon green like mint ice cream, but a muted pistachio tone — pale jade, almost dusty. Sometimes it arrives layered over ice in a tall plastic cup sweating instantly in the Bangkok heat, with the top crowned by crushed pistachios or cold foam. Other times it’s blended thick like a milkshake, especially in trendier cafés around places like Thonglor or Ari.

Flavor-wise, it lands somewhere between nutty gelato and melted ice cream, but less sugary than you expect. Pistachio has this naturally buttery, almost roasted richness to it. In Thailand, cafés often lean into that creaminess with condensed milk, oat milk, or fresh dairy, so the drink can end up tasting incredibly smooth — cold, silky, slightly sweet. There’s usually a toasted note underneath everything, like warm nuts fresh from a pan.
