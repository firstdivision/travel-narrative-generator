- Somewhere tucked up at the end of a peninsula is an island called "Koh Lanta" in Krabi province, along Thailand’s southwestern coast on the Andaman Sea.  

Geographically, the Andaman Sea is bordered by:

Thailand to the east, Myanmar to the north, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands (India) to the west, and the Strait of Malacca to the south. The Strait of Malacca is one of the most important shipping routes on Earth — the narrow sea lane connecting the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea and the Pacific.

To get from the Andaman Sea into the broader Indian Ocean, you simply sail west or southwest past the Andaman and Nicobar island chain. There isn’t a dramatic “boundary” where one suddenly becomes the other — the Andaman Sea is basically a northeastern arm of the Indian Ocean.

But right now that is just "where we are going". Or Grab driver on the way to the airport tells us it will probably be raining in Thailand. thanks dude. 

Airport security is easy. Except I forgot there was a second "real" security before the gate. So, instead of going directly to our gate which is usually "rule number one" of flying, and overconfidently led my companions into another area of the airport, declaring that "there is not much available down by our gate".

We do find a nice place to get food, and they order some Teh Halia:

Teh Halia is the kind of drink that feels medicinal in the best possible way. Sweet, spicy, creamy, and hot enough to fog your glasses the second it lands on the table.

The name is simple:

Teh = tea
Halia = ginger in Malay

So it’s literally “ginger tea,” but in practice it usually means strong black tea pulled together with fresh ginger, condensed milk or evaporated milk, and enough sugar to make it comforting rather than sharp.

Eventually we get through security, without their drinks, and I am mostly right there there is not much here.  A Burger King and a "Old Town Coffee" restaurant.

OldTown White Coffee is popular in Malaysia for kind of the same reason diners, Waffle House, or Tim Hortons become cultural fixtures elsewhere: it’s familiar, reliable, affordable, everywhere, and tied to a very specific local comfort-food identity.

But there’s also a deeper Malaysia-specific thing going on.

OldTown built itself around Ipoh white coffee, which is one of Malaysia’s signature coffee styles. Unlike darker roasted Western coffee, Ipoh white coffee beans are traditionally roasted with margarine or palm oil and brewed strong, then mixed with condensed milk. The result is:

smoother
sweeter
less bitter
very easy to drink in tropical heat

Ipoh white coffee became hugely nostalgic for Malaysians because kopitiams — traditional coffee shops — were social hubs for generations. OldTown basically industrialized and modernized that experience.

The flight was not boarding, so we wait the customary amount of time before we actually board. Get on the plane and are on our way to Thailand!

Customs / Immigration in Thailand is easy-peasy, there is no line.  I guess there *are* benefits sometimes to being in the front of the plane.

The driver from Pimali resort is there to meet us, we get in the van and are on our way.

Anyone who has spent time on the water knows this particular feeling: You are in a car and it is headed towards the water.  Salt water most probably.  You come up over a hill and there it is.  The road in front of you heads downhill into the water that you now see in front of you. It narrows, probably, and there are random cars parked alongside the road, small boats on trailers, possibly in the bushes, waiting to be launched again one day. In many cases, as it is here, there may even be a ferry terminal waiting to take you to another place. It relaxes your soul, if you are fortunate enough to have "salt water in your blood".  It is an affliction, but a welcome one that draw us inexorably towards the water.

In the van, as we see this play out in front of us we each say this same thing in different ways.

The van takes a turn off of the ferry road and deposits us into a comfortable gazeebo by the water, with a long pier leading into the water, at the end of which is a floating concrete floating dock. We are drawn towards this like thirsty desert travellers to an oasis of water.

After a short wait, the boat arrives.  Gilligan's Island-esque moter-cruiser converted into a passenger ferry.  The ride to the other side takes a grand total of about five minutes.  We each wish that it would have taken longer.

Another van ride, and another 40 minutes and we are at the resort.  It is very nice.  Hushed tones, warm woods.  Flowered neckales are given to us, followed by a refreshing citrus-based drink, with a straw made from some sort of plant.  The ice clinks in the glass, and the resort host takes us to comfortable seating.  

We have an elephant trip booked for the next morning, but the host tells us that there is a boat trip we can join the next morning instead, if we want.  We say that would be nice, and she takes that information back to us, a few minutes later she returns and tells us she was able to reschedule our elephant trip to Monday, and that weather permitting, we will be able to get on the boat trip as well.

- get taken to the room.  It is very, vert nice.  Two buildings connected by a private infinity pool.  Bedrooms on one side and a "common room" / "living room" on the other.

- Go to check out the grounds - "Get situated".  We are hungry so we find one of the restaurants. Much-needed lunch, especially for me since I hadn't eaten much.  Devour the bread and butter. Crush the rice and stir fry beef and peppers. Spicy and savory. 

Thin slices of beef flash-seared in a screaming-hot wok with onions, chilies (very hot ones that after eating one I decided one was enough) and sweet bell peppers that stay bright and a little crisp, the beef tender, and everything spills over fragrant rice that soaks up the salty, peppery juices collecting underneath.  There is also bread.  Warm delicious bread with butter, sea salt, and a black pepper grinder. It all disappears and I am feeling much better.  The Pink Gin and Tonic may have helped too.  I'm can't remember what was in it.  It must have been really good.

We go to the beach.  Swim in the high-salinity water that makes floating so easy.  There are waves that we discover are body-surfable, and a quick visit to the resort beach cabana results in us aquiring two boogie boards. I slowly remember how these things work.  I am able to actually carve into a wave at one point, but the waves are breaking quickly and right on the beach, so I turn parallel to the beach and roll the board over the top of me, doing a 360 "barell roll" type move.  I am in heaven, back in the water, it is not my normal ocean, but I do not care.  I am exactly where I want to be.

It gets dark and we head back to the room.  We order a "buggy" -- what they call the extra-small lorry-style trucks that have rows of seats in them, and that the resort uses to shuttle people around.

We get back to our rooms and there is a snake on the patio of our pool!  I guess this is the tradeoff you get for being in the jungle climate.  The wildlife is still unaware that this is a very nice resort, built for humans, by humans.  We are in *their* house really.  But the hotel is quick to send someone with a snake hook.  I offer to illuminate the snake with both our phones, and he gladly accepts.  

- With the patio now free of wildlife, I take the opportunity to take a dip in our private infinity pool that looks out over the
