## Monday, May 11

- wake up to 7am alarm.  We have booked an Elephant Tour today.  At an elthical elephant sanctuary.

- breakfast at the resort brakfast buffet.  Today I elect to go low on the spice, not becasue I don't want it, but because my insides protested it a little bit this morning.  Sort of a naval "wrning shot over the bow"

- just after the quick breakfast, we take a "resort buggy" to the welcome center building, where another truck with an open back is waiting for us to take us to the elephant sancuary.

- Arrive at "Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary", which is:
    - Ethical “no riding, no tricks, no bathing” elephant sanctuary near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand  
    - Focused on observing elephants naturally instead of interacting with them like tourist attractions  
    - Visitors walk through jungle trails while following elephants at a respectful distance  
    - Elephants roam freely through forest, mud, ponds, and bamboo areas on their own schedule  
    - Supported by animal welfare organizations promoting humane elephant tourism  
    - Many elephants came from former riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work  
    - Experience feels more like hiking with wildlife than visiting a zoo  
    - Dense tropical jungle atmosphere: cicadas, humidity, mud, giant leaves, and heavy rainforest smells  
    - Elephants move with a strange mix of gentleness and overwhelming size and power  
    - Guides explain elephant behavior, history, personalities, and rescue stories  
    - Quiet, reflective experience rather than a flashy tourist show  
    - Surreal moments watching a truck-sized elephant disappear silently into thick jungle vegetation  

    My "intestinal situation" re-asserts itself a little ways into the trip.  For the first thirty minutes, while walking along the dirt trails I am listening more to my body than I am to the guide list off elephant facts.  Instead I am wondering how long it might take me to get back to the main entrance where there are toilets, if maybe there are toilets along the tour, and how long this tour might take.  Thankfully, and eventually, my intestines go back to sleep and I am able to enjoy the tour and take some photographs of elephants.  There is even a baby elephant that has just been born.  Still a little unsure of how to use his legs, he comes up to the edge of the maternity area where he and his mother are staying to say hello to me.  He reached out with his trunk to investigate me.  He found my camera which I assume was a new experience for him since he spent some time there.  They told us not to touch the elephants back, so as much as I wanted to say hello back, I instead let him do his thing until he got bored and moved on.

    At the end of the your we are served a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink.  I did pause for a second, wondering if this was one of those things that "maybe you should not drink".  This isn't the Pimalai resort any more, and I still have a lot of travelling left to do.

    Back at the resort, after a couple more truck and buggy rides, we take one last swim in our pool, and order room service of club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey, and a fried egg which was unusual but very welcome change to the standard.  We also order some coconut drinks too, they are too good to pass up.

    we have to leave this resort. but the trip is really just beginning. all points of the travel compass must be investigated. and tonight we will, hopefully, be in Bangkok. So we will explore east -- we are off to the airport in, you guessed it, a buggy ride, a van ride, a boat ride, another van ride, which finally delivers us to the airport.

    Security is easy, we were told by the host back at the resort thatit would be since it is a domestic flight.  We settle into the rythmn of the airport traveller, waiting for out flight's boarding annoucement to be mumbled over speakers that are both loud and unintelligble at the same time.
