- First stop is Cat Cat Village.  And the first realization that this entire day might be "max tourism".  Cat Cat Village is one of those places that feels caught between a real mountain village and a carefully constructed outdoor theme park for tourists. Just outside the misty hills of Sapa, the village pulls in crowds with terraced rice fields, waterfalls, wooden bridges, traditional Black H’mong clothing, and endless photo spots. Everywhere you walk, there are souvenir stalls, rented ethnic costumes, staged “Instagram” viewpoints, and streams of visitors slowly shuffling downhill with cameras out. It’s beautiful in flashes — especially when the clouds roll through the valley — but it rarely feels quiet or untouched.

- Afterwards we go to the next "stop", yes this is clearly an orchestrated tourism experience. Moana Sapa feels less like a traditional mountain village and more like somebody dropped a Bali-themed Instagram park onto the side of a Vietnamese mountain. You pay an entrance fee, walk through curated photo sets — giant stone hands, fake “Heaven Gates,” infinity swings, glass viewpoints, oversized statues, flower gardens, and carefully framed mountain backdrops — while streams of tourists rotate through each spot waiting for their turn to pose. It’s incredibly photogenic, but almost aggressively designed for social media.

And honestly? It kind of knows exactly what it is. Moana Sapa doesn’t really pretend to be an untouched cultural experience the way places like Cat Cat sometimes do. It’s a manufactured “check-in” attraction from the start — a mountain photo park with coffee, misty Fansipan views, and enough staged scenery to fill an influencer’s entire camera roll in an hour. On a clear day, though, the surrounding landscape genuinely is stunning, and the contrast between artificial fantasy props and real northern Vietnam mountains creates this surreal atmosphere that’s weirdly entertaining, even if you leave feeling like you just visited a high-altitude content factory.

I am able to play, what I assume is a gravely out of tune "Dan Tranh". The Vietnamese đàn tranh is a long, elegant string instrument that looks a little like a polished wooden canoe crossed with a harp laid flat on a table. Traditionally it has 16 strings stretched across a curved wooden body, with movable bridges underneath each string that let musicians fine-tune the notes. The player sits beside it and plucks the strings with picks attached to their fingers while the other hand bends and presses the strings to create wavering vibrato, sliding notes, and those haunting pitch bends that make the instrument sound almost like it’s sighing or singing.  However, in my case I am only able to get it to make vaugely musical noises.

We have lunch back in the main Sapa town at a place called Little Sapa. Little Sapa restaurant feels like the kind of place travelers stumble into after a freezing, fog-soaked trek through Sapa and then end up talking about for the rest of the trip. Review after review mentions the same things: giant portions, shockingly low prices, steaming hot local food, and the relief of finding somewhere warm while the mountain air outside turns your fingers numb. People rave about dishes like wild pig, venison, sizzling beef noodles, spring rolls, salmon hot pot, and even “burnt banana with rum” desserts, often describing the food as fresh, deeply comforting, and far better than they expected from such an unassuming little restaurant

Afterwards we shop along the street that Little Sapa restaurant is on.  We find an art shop, and each find something to buy there, including a "Smoking Lady" painting.

Back in the car we next go to "Love Falls":
    Love Waterfall (Thác Tình Yêu) sits hidden in the forests outside Sapa near the base of the Fansipan mountain range.
    Reaching the waterfall requires a scenic walk through bamboo groves, pine forest, and misty mountain trails alongside small streams and rushing water.
    The falls themselves drop roughly 100 meters (330 feet) down dark rock into a cold turquoise pool below.
    The air is noticeably cooler there, with constant mist hanging in the trees and the sound of water echoing through the valley.
    According to local legend, the waterfall was named after a fairy who fell in love with a flute-playing woodcutter and would secretly meet him there.
    Despite being a tourist attraction, it usually feels calmer and less commercialized than places like Cat Cat Village or Moana Sapa.
    During rainy or foggy weather, the entire area can feel almost prehistoric — wet stone, jungle smells, clouds drifting through the trees, and water roaring somewhere ahead before you even see it.

- Back at the car we have to wake up Thran, but soon we are on our way to the next spot: 

    Rong May Glass Bridge — also called the Dragon Cloud Glass Bridge — feels like somebody took an extreme amusement park ride and bolted it directly onto the side of a mountain above the clouds. Sitting high on the legendary Sky Gate of O Quy Ho pass, the attraction launches you out over a massive valley on transparent glass panels more than 2,000 meters above sea level. The first few steps are pure brain-confusion: your eyes see empty air beneath your feet while your body refuses to believe the glass is actually solid.

    The whole place is unapologetically built for adrenaline and social media. There are glass elevators climbing the cliffside, rainbow staircases, suspension bridges, “death swings,” rope bikes, drone videos, and endless photo platforms hanging over impossible mountain views. On clear days, the valleys and jagged peaks around Sapa look absolutely unreal — layers of blue mountains fading into cloud banks like watercolor paintings. But there’s also something slightly surreal about the experience itself: terrified tourists inching across transparent floors while influencers pose dramatically five feet away pretending they aren’t scared at all.
