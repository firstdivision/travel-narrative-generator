## Wednesday, May 20

- Breakfast is in the hotel.  Something tht when booking the hotel rooms seemed like something we would never want.  We would surely want to be out on the street at some noodle place, right?  But in the thick of it, sometimes it is nice to have a quiet sit-down breakfast with your fellow travellers (and other hotel guests), before really starting the day.  Plus it gives you time to use the bathroom one last time before leaving the hotel.

- And use the bathroom is what one of us did.  Many times, in fact.  She even went to a pharmacy after she heard of a special tea used for "situations" such as the one she now found herself in.  In a very nice gesture, and a reflection of the kind of good hotel service you get at places like this, they called ahead to the pharmacy for her to explain exactly what she needed, so they could have it ready.

- Eventually the special tea is drank, and things seem to be, "on the mend".  Sometimes on these trips certain bathroom stops become mandatory and a somewhat right of passage.

- After we take a Grab fairly far out of the downtown / old quarter area to what appears to be an actual "pottery district".  Pottery stores and possibly warehouses full of it line the street on the way to a pottery museam:

    Walking into Bat Trang Pottery Museum feels less like entering a normal museum and more like stepping inside a giant sculpture made from clay and motion. Rising out of the old pottery village outside Hanoi, the building twists upward in layered spirals inspired by spinning pottery wheels and traditional kiln shapes. From above, it almost looks like giant clay vessels colliding together. The reddish-brown curves, textured walls, and canyon-like interior spaces make the whole place feel organic — as if it was shaped by hand rather than constructed.

    Inside, the museum mixes contemporary architecture with centuries of ceramic history from the nearby Bat Trang Pottery Village, one of Vietnam’s oldest pottery communities. You move through displays of ancient porcelain, glazed jars, ceremonial pieces, delicate tea sets, and modern ceramic art while sunlight pours down from skylights above. Some floors focus on the history of Bat Trang craftsmanship and the families who kept the tradition alive for generations, while other areas let visitors try pottery making themselves — hands covered in wet clay while spinning wheels wobble beneath nervous tourists.

- We take a pottery making class.  Mine, unsurprisingly, looks like a child made it.  The two girls do much better.  But it was fun and I can perhaps see myself taking an actual pottery class.  It has the slow methodical relaxedness of kneading bread.  And I like relaxing things.

- Afterwards we go upstairs for a light painting exhibition. We are directed into a small temple-looking like room, and the employees, apart from poiting towards small sandals that we should wear after taking our shoes off, seem to ignore us while walking another couple around and explaining what everything is (in Vietnamese we assume).

Ater we are seated at a low wooden fish pond aquarium with that same South Korean couple, and like has happened on other parts of the trip, translation apps on our phones allow us to actually have a conversation.  They tell us what they can about the pieces in the room (that were explained to them by the employee), we all exchange names, where we are from, where we have been and are going.

Afterwards we also sit eith them at a table to all take a group picture, and write down our "wishes" onto small bookmark-shaped pieces of wood that we then tie to a tree.

Finally we visit another floor that has light sculpture art on it, and this one did come with an English-speaking tour guide. The exhibit you saw is part of a relatively new Vietnamese art form called “light sculpture art,” pioneered by Vietnamese artist Bùi Văn Tự. The basic idea sounds simple at first: carved blocks of wood are lit from a specific angle so their shadows combine into detailed images on a wall. But when you actually stand there and watch it happen, it feels almost supernatural — rough, chaotic chunks of wood suddenly projecting perfectly recognizable portraits, historical scenes, or landscapes out of pure shadow and light.

At the Bát Tràng Pottery Museum, the exhibit is tied heavily to Vietnamese history and cultural storytelling. Some installations depict national heroes, historical struggles, or symbolic moments in Vietnam’s past. Others are more philosophical — exploring memory, time, knowledge, or the hidden meaning inside ordinary objects. The artist’s whole concept is that reality contains “invisible images” waiting to be revealed depending on perspective and illumination.

Our tour guide takes us to each exhibit.  Describing what it is, and turning the light on, then off again.  Each time the light is turned off she says "When I turn off the light, you will notice that the painting disappears.".  After the fifth or so time is oddly endearing, perhaps some sort of "lost in translation" thing where that phrase means more in the original language than after being translated.  

We go back to the hotel to repack since we are carrying more "stuff" now -- notable our still-somewhat-soft pottry that we made at class.  There is no way, we all think, that any of these are making it home in once piece. We do this repack in the hotel lobby sice they have been holding our bags for us since we checked out this morning. 

After we go to the Water theater show:
    Thang Long Water Puppet Theatre is the most famous place in Hanoi to watch traditional Vietnamese water puppetry.
    The stage is an actual pool of water, with puppets appearing to glide across the surface.
    Puppeteers stand hidden behind a bamboo screen, waist-deep in water, controlling puppets with long underwater rods.
    The art form dates back nearly 1,000 years to flooded rice paddies in northern Vietnam.
    Stories usually focus on village life, fishing, farming, legends, dragons, spirits, and Vietnamese folklore.
    Live musicians perform beside the stage using traditional instruments like drums, flutes, and the Đàn bầu.
    Many shows include fireworks, splashing water effects, smoke, and fire-breathing dragon puppets.
    The lacquered wooden puppets are handmade and often brightly painted in red, gold, green, and black.
    The theater is heavily tourist-oriented now, with packed seating and multiple daily performances near Hoàn Kiếm Lake.
    Despite the tourist crowds, the performance still feels strangely magical — part folklore, part comedy, part ancient illusion show.

It also smells musty inside, likely from years of water both in the air and making it onto the carpets.  One of the instruments I notice is the same one I played back in Sapa at the Moana stop, the "Dan Tranh". The stories they tell are of prosperity, fertility with charachters like swans, phoenix, and unicorns.

Now it is really time to "leave" and after going back to the hotel to get our bags, we summon a final Grab to the airport.  There we discover tht you cannot check in, or go through securty, more than three hours before your flight.  We had intended on getting to our airline lounge early, having showers, some food, some drink, etc.

We find that there is an upstairs part we can still get to and are able to get some food.  There is a spa there, but we all feel grody and aren't really in the mood for that is we can't shower first (and the girl at the door when we ask says that no, they do not have showers there).  Seems like a missed busniess opportunity to me!

Eventually we are inside the three hour window, we get past security and finally into the airport lounge where there are some additedly nice showers.  There is a sign that says "showers require a reservation".  But there is nobody there to make one with, so we all pretend we did not see that sign and instead shower when one becomes free.

Finally we board, and our place leaves the gate to begin its taxi, only to return to the gate.  Slowly the information dribbles out of announcements made by the head purser with each successive announcement more information is reveled - luggage, a passenger's luggage, a powerbank in a passenger's luggage.  We are all sceptical, why would the plane taxi half way to the runway and then someone be able to discover a power bank in the luggage hold?

In any case there is nothing we can do as passengers except sit there and hope we don't start seeing smoke.  We do not, and eventually the place taxis again out to the runway, and pushes itself into the air.  We are on an A380, it must take a lot of gas and thrust to get a fully loaded one of these into the air!
