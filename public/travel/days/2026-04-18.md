## Saturday April 18 to Sunday April 19

I've decided to be lazy this weekend.  I won't bore you with the pedestrian things I did like laundry, cleaning bathrooms, grocery shopping for basic supplies.  But there were still some standout moments.

Breakfast at The Botanist Cafe is Shakshuka, and it’s doing exactly what it’s supposed to do — be simple, bold, and a little messy in the best way.

A wide, shallow bowl filled with a thick tomato base—slow-cooked down so it’s rich, slightly sweet, and a little tangy. It’s not a light sauce; it’s got weight to it. You can see bits of pepper and seasoning worked in, giving it texture instead of being smooth.

The eggs are the centerpiece. They’re cracked right into the sauce and cooked just until the whites set and the yolks stay soft. Here, they’re glossy and just barely holding together, ready to spill into the tomatoes the second you break them.

On top, there’s a mix of extras—crumbled meat, herbs. It adds a savory layer that makes it feel more like a full meal than just eggs in sauce.

Off to the side, thick slices of toasted bread—golden, a little rough-cut. You use it to scoop everything up: egg, sauce, toppings, all in one bite. No finesse required.

And then there’s the small salad—crisp lettuce, light dressing. It’s there to cut through the richness, give you a break between bites.

Overall, it’s hearty without being heavy, rustic without trying too hard. The kind of breakfast that leans more toward lunch—but you’re glad it showed up early.

I wander around Tamerind Square a bit to see what else I can find. I find a place selling teas and smoothies that I haven't seen open yet, so having no schedule and nothing to do I take a seat and order a hot "cleansing tea" because I see that it has Turmeric in it, and I like turmeric.

It shows up in a French press and a simple glass, just hot, slightly cloudy, amber-orange liquid that tells you right away this isn’t your standard tea. It’s turmeric-based, so the flavor leans earthy and a little bitter, with some warmth behind it—probably ginger in there doing some of the work. There’s a faint graininess to it, too. Turmeric doesn’t fully dissolve, so you get that slightly textured, almost rustic feel. It floats around the bottom of the french press when I go to pour more, daring me to see if I want to drink all of it.

It’s not delicate or refined. It drinks more like something functional—something people have been making for a long time because it works, not because it looks good.

You take a sip, and it’s warming, a bit drying on the tongue, and it lingers. The kind of drink you have slowly, especially sitting outside like this, where it fits the setting more than it tries to impress you. Surrounded by green leaves inside what I know is the concrete box that makes up Tamrind Square, I can trick myself into thinking I'm somewhere else for a while.  This, and the turmeric perhaps, puts me at ease as I take small sips and read the book I brought with me.

Next I head to the book store and find a fun section called "Blind Date With A Book". It’s a shelf of books that don’t want to tell you what they are.

Each one is wrapped in plain brown paper, tied up with string like a small mystery. No cover art, no author name staring back at you—just a few typed bullet points on the front. “Dream girl.” “National tragedy.” “Survives a crash.” That’s all you get.

It’s called a “blind date,” which is about right. You’re not picking based on hype or reputation. You’re going off instinct, a handful of clues, maybe a mood you can’t quite explain.

There’s something a little old-school about it. Stripped down. No algorithm trying to guess what you’ll like, no reviews to lean on. Just you, a short description, and the risk that you might hate it or unexpectedly love it.

You stand there longer than you think you will, reading the same four lines over and over, trying to decide if that’s enough.

And eventually, you just pick one.

Later, back in the apartment the sound of an approaching thunderstorm catches my attention.  I love thunderstorms, I always have.  My mother and I would always watch from the window as a thunderstorm rolled through.  The ones at night were always the best, but it was also always so hard for me to stay awake. I would sit with her by the window, the pitch black frequently relaced by the bright flashes of lightning.  We would then count seconds until we heard the accompanying thunder.  Five seconds means the storm is a mile away, ten is two. We would count, and then count the next one to see if the storm was getting closer or farther away.  A game, but a useful one, and for us, tucked away in blankets by the window, we would both be hoping that it would get closer.  Sleep would always sneak up on me though, and my eyelids would be weighed down by some unseen weight.  As hard as I would try to stay awake I always failed.

It’s late. Not “late dinner,” but nothing else is open late. Lights half on, kitchen half alive, and you’re not entirely sure what’s happening behind the counter when you ask if they still have the Black Pepper Beef Noodles. There’s a pause. A look. Maybe a nod. Good enough.

What shows up is a plate of black pepper beef noodles—no presentation tricks, just a dark, glossy pile of food that smells like it means business.

The noodles are thick, slightly chewy, coated in a sauce that’s heavy on soy and cracked black pepper. Not subtle. You can see the pepper clinging to everything—little black flecks that promise heat, not from chili, but that slow, building kind that sits in your throat.

The beef is sliced thin, cooked hard enough to get a bit of char on the edges. That’s where the flavor is—salty, smoky, slightly caramelized. Mixed in are bits of cabbage and onion, softened down, picking up the sauce, adding just enough texture so it’s not all noodles and meat.

It’s oily in the way good stir-fry is supposed to be. Not greasy, just enough to carry the flavor and coat everything so each bite hits the same way.

You take a bite, and it’s immediate—pepper, salt, umami. No buildup, no subtle introduction. It’s hot, savory, and exactly what you want at that hour, whether you planned to want it or not.

And whatever was going on behind that counter? Doesn’t matter. They made this. That’s enough.

Back at home I have the munchies still and remember I bought a big bag of Wasabi peas, but not the usual loose, open-to-the-air kind that turns into a shared buffet for you and whatever else is living in the room (ants) or the humidity. These are individually wrapped—small, little packets, each one sealed like it knows exactly what it’s protecting.

You tear one open and it’s the same thing every time: hard, glossy green peas with that thin, crisp shell. First bite is crunchy—clean snap—then the wasabi (or really green-dyed horseradish) hits. Not immediately brutal, but fast enough. Sharp, nasal, clears-your-sinuses kind of heat that disappears almost as quickly as it shows up, leaving behind a little salt and a faint sweetness from the pea itself.

The wrapping matters more than you’d think. In a place where ants seem to operate like a coordinated search party, always probing, always finding something, this is one of the few snacks that holds the line. No open bag left on the counter. No quiet invasion overnight. Just you, opening one at a time, on your terms.

It’s simple, addictive, a little aggressive in flavor—but controlled. And for once, you’re not sharing.
