- Visit the Temple of Literature
    Ancient Confucian temple and Vietnam’s first national university, founded in 1070.
    Dedicated to Confucius, scholars, and the importance of learning and civil service.
    Located in the heart of Hanoi but feels surprisingly quiet once inside the walls.
    Built with layered courtyards, tiled roofs, red wooden pillars, ponds, and carefully shaped gardens.
    The complex unfolds gradually through gates and stone pathways, almost like moving deeper into another century.
    Famous for its stone turtles holding carved stelae listing successful doctoral scholars from imperial exams.
    Symbolizes education, discipline, scholarship, and intellectual prestige in Vietnamese history.
    Architecture mixes symmetry and calm with weathered age — mossy stone, dark wood, incense smoke, and soft lantern light.
    Students still visit before important exams to pray for luck and academic success.
    Feels less flashy than many Southeast Asian temples — more restrained, thoughtful, and contemplative.
    Early morning light and rainy weather give the place an especially cinematic atmosphere.
    The contrast between chaotic Hanoi traffic outside and the quiet inner courtyards inside is part of what makes it memorable

- I have an "O Mai" drink.  I don't recall exactly what was in it, but a quick google search reveals that it was probably:

Ô mai is made from preserved fruits like:
plum
apricot
kumquat
peach
tamarind
The fruits are salted, sugared, dried, sometimes spiced with ginger, chili, or licorice.
The flavor profile is intensely Vietnamese:
sweet
sour
salty
slightly medicinal/herbal
sometimes smoky or spicy
In Hanoi, ô mai is considered a classic old-city snack, especially around the Old Quarter.
Cocktail bars in Hanoi have started using ô mai flavors in drinks because they create this weirdly addictive salty-sour edge that cuts through sweetness.
So an “O Mai” cocktail at the rooftop bar of GM Premium Hotel Hanoi was likely:
fruit-forward
tangy and salted
possibly plum- or apricot-based
with a salty rim or preserved-fruit syrup
mixed with spirits like gin, vodka, rum, or soju-style ingredients
Some Hanoi cocktail versions even use an “ô mai salt” rim specifically for that sour-salty kick.
The taste usually lands somewhere between:
a cocktail
a dried fruit candy
and the kind of sweet-sour snack your brain can’t decide whether it loves or hates for the first five seconds
