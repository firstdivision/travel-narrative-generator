## Wednesday, April 22 

One thing this trip has reminded me of is the absolute misery that can be living in an apartment building. In my case, I at least have the knowledge that this is a temporary situation. That I will not be subject to this torment for too long because at random points throughout the day sometimes in the morning sometimes at night like right now as I write this at 10 PM, a strange noise begins emanating from the ceiling above me. It sounds like a Dremel tool grinding away at something sometimes it changes pitch. Sometimes it gets higher or lower. Sometimes it lasts a long time. Sometimes it only lasts a little while, but it is always there every day every morning every night.

Today I decided I should go for a run outside. I've run in Texas Summers running at 10 in the morning in Malaysia should be a snap. That's what I told myself. surely, at least, it can't possibly be worse than listening to that weird noise come out of the ceiling above me. 

So I put on my long sleeve sun shirt, my black, running shorts and head out to go explore. It started getting hot. It was very humid as mornings are here. I started to get hot. I started to sweat. I started to regret this decision. 

Turn after turn just more road sidewalks that sometimes ended and became grass if I was lucky or otherwise I would be forced back onto the road to try to continue my journey. Having run too far already there was no turning back. I had to complete this loop. Parts of me started wondering, "what are the initial symptoms of heat stroke?", I think it's if you stop feeling hot well I still feel hot so I must be OK a few kilometers later. I arrived back at the apartment overheated, but at least with that runners satisfaction of having gone for the run even if you didn't necessarily really want to .

It takes a while sometimes to remember that, much like yoga, most people are doing their own thing and as long as you aren't directly in their way or doing something truly offensive, then they probably don't mind your presence or even haven't noticed you at all. 

Next we go to get Durian. It shows up split open like something you’re not entirely sure you should be eating.

Spiky shell, pale interior, and inside—these thick, glossy, yellow lobes that honestly look like oversized grubs. Soft, slightly wrinkled, almost custard-like, just sitting there. You’ve heard about it already—Black Thorn durian—the reputation, the smell, the strong opinions. People either love it or act like it’s a biohazard.

At this point, you’re committed. No overthinking. Just a straight YOLO approach.

You grab a piece, and it gives a little under your fingers—soft, dense, not quite liquid but close. You take a bite.

First thing that happens? Your teeth hit something solid. Not gently. A quick, unmistakable thunk. There’s a pit in there, and you found it immediately.

Once you adjust and actually taste it, it’s not what you expect. It’s rich—almost absurdly rich. Sweet, but not clean sweetness. There’s a savory edge to it, something a little fermented that decided to become dessert. The texture is thick and creamy, like a custard that forgot to set properly but doesn’t care.

It lingers, too. The flavor sticks around, evolving a bit after each bite. Not refreshing, not light—this is a commitment.

And somewhere between the weird texture, the hit of the pit, and the fact that it looks like something you’d normally avoid—you realize you’re still going back for another bite. I have to because there are only two of us sitting at the table and these things are also not exactly cheap.
