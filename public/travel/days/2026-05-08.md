## April 8th, 2026

The first day of "The Trio Trip".  My two cousins and I are about to start a long-await Southeast Asia trip!  We will start here, in Kuala Lumpur, and from there journey on to Krabi and Bangkok Thailand, Siam Reap Cambodia, Hanoi Vietnam, and Sapa Vietnam, and then back to Hanoi.

But today is to tour Kuala Lumpur, as much as we can in a single day anyway.

First I have to say goodbye to my friend.  She is working today and has to leave somewhat early.  As it is we linger a while, delaying the inevitable, not wanting to have to say goodbye.  But eventually we can wait no longer.  What was supposed to be a 6:30am departure becomes an 8:30am departure.  One final time we ride together through the parking garage in her car, round and round the six levels of ramps, until we are parked in the main entrance of the apartment complex.  There is a final goodbye, and I have to get out of the car, I don't want to, I must.  A final wave goodbye and I turn away to walk back into the buiding as she drives away.  I turn once to look, like something out of a movie.

I get back to my apartment and one of my cousins, who slept on the couch, is awake.  We sit and chat for a while.  I think we made tea.  We are waiting for the second cousin to wake up, assuming she is sleeping from travel fatigue and jet lag.  As it turns out she thought it was much earlier and was just relaxing, but awake. She eventually wakes up, showers are taken, and we begin the day's adventure.

The first stop is Tamarind Square.  In addition to showing them Kuala Lumpur, I am supposed to be showing them what I have been up to this past month, and Tamarind Square has certainly been a large part of it.  So where else would I take them but The Botanist that has become one of my favorite places in Tamarind Square.  

We sit down at a table with four places.  I order, what else but Shakshuka! One of my cousins does the same, following my lead, and the second orders something musroom-based tha comes with a sunny side up egg on top.  They both agree with me that this is an excellent way to start the day.

After we go to Village Grocer, both to continue to show them "how I lived here", but also because we need some things like drinks for the day.  We peruse the aisles, buy waters and some other small things.  While there we meet Alan, the owner of the new restaurant in Tamarind Square that I recently had breakfast at (also ordering Shakshuka if you recall correctly).  He is in a hurry, the restaurant had some sort of delivery problem so he is buying milk or something.

Afterwards, we three get a Grab to the train station, and I mentor them in how to buy a Touch 'n Go card, and how to use it to get through the train gates.  Soon we are on the train that I have learned how to use on my couple trips into downtown Kuala Lumpur.  We have tickets to go to the Petronas Towers, and we soon realize that we need to adjust our train plan.  After consulting the train map, we realize we can go past TRX station that I had planned on getting off at (only because that was what I had always done), and instead continue on to Persiaran KLCC.

When we get off the train it is raining, but our GPSs say it is a fairly short walk to Petronas Towers, so we dutifly follow their instructions.  Thankfully much of the walk is on a covered walkway.  We duck through the mall entrance, follow some signs, go down a level, and are in line for the Towers right on time - 5 minutes before our scheduled ticket!

Much like the Sand Hotel in Singapore, we are directed through a waiting area where a "history of the towers" video plays, and we then ascend at high speed to the Skybridge that connects the two towers more than 170 meters above the city. From this suspended walkway, Kuala Lumpur stretches in every direction—a dense patchwork of skyscrapers, mosques, parks, highways, and distant mountains that reveals just how vast the city has become.

On this level we record some video shots for a "skit" that we are planning.  This has become somewhat of a tradition on these vacations, to try to come up with some fun comedy bits to record.

The experience reaches its peak on the observation deck near the top of Tower 2. Looking out through floor-to-ceiling windows, the city seems to transform from a bustling metropolis into a miniature model, with landmarks, neighborhoods, and traffic patterns unfolding far below. Beyond the views, the tour offers a closer appreciation of the towers themselves: the Islamic geometric patterns woven into their design, the engineering required to build the world's tallest towers of their time, and the ambition they came to symbolize for modern Malaysia. It's part architectural showcase, part engineering achievement, and part reminder of how dramatically Kuala Lumpur has risen onto the world stage.

On our way out we have what may be called the "Apple Pay Event".  One of my cousins tried to pay with Apple Pay, I think for the first time. It didn't work, she tried again, it didn't work, she starts becoming exasperated at the fact that she can't get it to work.  Another attempt, another error.  Now everybody is laughing, including the employees of the Petronas Towers gift shop.  Tody we gave *them* a gift, the gift of laughyter and a story to tell other employees, "Oh, you should have been here Friday..."

Afterwards we are back out on the street, and it is still raining.  Raining hard in fact!  There is some confusion about where to go -- which train station will take us to our next stop, Central Market.  I only know for sure that we need to get to Pasar Seni.  We decide on not walking back to the Persiaran stop and walk towards one that should be closer.  It is pouring rain now, and we are looking a little like drowned rats, and I am failing in my duties as "tour guide" since I am out of my element, in a part of the city I've never been in before.

Lukilly the alternative station is where our maps said it shuould be, and a short ride later we are walking into Central Market.  Finally I am back on "familiar ground"!

We do some shopping, I buya shirt, and I think the girls bought some dresses and stuff.

Afterwards I take them to Petaling Street, aka Chinatown.  It seems to have stopped raining for the moment, fingers crossed!

I am happy that I am able to find the entrance to the china town market easy enough.  We enter and are consumed by the hustle and bustle immediately. 

What we are really interested in is food, and I am pretty sure I know which way to go to get it.  Thankfuly I am right.  We emerge onto the street with all the food vendors.  After a few back and forths I say howthere was this dumpling place I really liked, and my two cousins say "Well, let's go there then!"

We sit down and the older wife and husband prepare us some pork and chive dumplings, they are as good as I remember.  

We leave there and continue walking down the street. We come upon a large outdoor seating area, and it is very busy.  The usual rule applies.  We are still hungry, this place looks busy. Let's go in.

We are seated immediately in the upstairs dining room.  And after a slight snafu trying to order from the QR code-based menu system (the waiter helped me connect to their wi-fi), we are able to order.  More dumplings, beef raman, some other noodle soups.  It is all very good.  We are "in it" now! This is what we have all been wanting, and I am glad I am able to get our trip off on a good foot by sharing my little knowledge of this city with my two cousins.

Afterwards we go back out onto the street, walking in the same direction around a curve that I know goes back to Pasar Seni station.  We now find ourselves waiting in front of a street vendor selling some sort of fried dessert.  There is loud rhythmic music playing, people are laughing and joking, so we decide to try some too.  That is the spirit of this trip.

Two of us each order a bannana and chocolate dessert, not realizing that the other had done so. Sometimes small details get lost in the medium chaos of street food ordering.  When they are done one of my cousins goes to pay for hers, and the food stall operator hands her three, sayin gthe others are free. "Free? Really?" she asks.  He nods and says yes.  We are not sure why but are grateful nonetheless.  And we are also grateful for how goos these things taste.  Still hot from the griddle the chocolate and bannana mix together with the crispy outer shell.  A figurative cherry on top of the sundae that is this Friday night.

We go to the station, get on the train home, discussing the night, anticipating the next 13 days ahead of us.

What will those days contain? What new adventures await us?
