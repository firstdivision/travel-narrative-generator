## Wednesday, April 22 - Ceiling Noise and Black Thorn

```poem
A ceiling whined like tools with nowhere good to go
I ran through heat and sidewalks that kept ending just so
Then horses, boats, a mosque, and durian's custard thorn
By night I’d eaten half a life I hadn’t planned that morn
```

One thing this trip kept reminding me of was the absolute misery of apartment living. It has the same basic problem as airplanes and every other shared public space, which is that there is always at least one person doing something that annoys everyone else, and nowhere, in my experience, is that more reliable than an apartment complex.

In my case, at least, I had the comfort of knowing it was temporary. I would not be subjected to this forever. Still, at random points throughout the day, sometimes in the morning, sometimes at night, like right then as I wrote this around 10 PM, a strange noise would begin emanating from the ceiling above me. It sounded like a Dremel tool grinding away at something. Sometimes the pitch changed. Sometimes it got higher, sometimes lower. Sometimes it lasted a long time, sometimes only a little while, but it was always there, every day, every morning, every night.

Today I decided I should go for a run outside. I had run in Texas summers, so running at 10 in the morning in Malaysia should be a snap. That was what I told myself. Surely, at least, it could not be worse than listening to that weird mechanical suffering coming out of the ceiling above me.

So I put on my long-sleeve sun shirt and black running shorts and headed out to explore. What I had been looking for was what appeared to be a running loop I had seen while gazing over the wall of the rooftop pool. As soon as I got to the road and took my first couple of turns, I realized whatever I had seen was not a running loop. So instead I decided to follow what I knew was some sort of pond or body of water, figuring that as long as I kept it on my right and followed the coastline, I should be OK.

This was optimistic.

Turn after turn, it was just more roads, sidewalks that sometimes ended and turned into grass if I was lucky, or forced me back onto the road if I was not. It got hot. It was already humid, the way mornings are here. Then I got hot. Then I started sweating hard. Then I started regretting the decision. Having run too far already, there was no turning back. I had to complete the loop. Part of my brain started wondering, *what are the initial symptoms of heat stroke?* I thought maybe one sign was if you stopped feeling hot. Well, I still felt hot, so I must have been fine.

A few kilometers later I arrived back at the apartment overheated, but with that familiar runner's satisfaction of having done the run even if I had not exactly wanted to.

Later at work, lunch was again at what I called the food truck place, even though they were not really food trucks. This time I got some sort of beef noodle soup and was given two hot sauces. I poured them half-and-half into a plastic cup, trying to make it photogenic. Only afterward did I realize I could have done a better job and made it look like a yin-yang symbol, with the orange and dark brown sauces standing in for black and white. Too late. The soup was still good.

After work that night, I met someone at a place called Ekues Cabin Cafe. It was mostly open air, so you heard everything, wind, people talking, and every now and then a horse moving past, or noises from farther off. That was what made it feel so different. Cyberjaya had struck me as very concrete and industrial, whereas one of my first real experiences in Putrajaya was this cafe where you sat with a cup of coffee or a plate of food and horses were just off to the side, polo horses grazing or being walked around. The horses, to be clear, not the polo players.

In another now-familiar occurrence, my credit card did not work with the cafe's QR code ordering system. When my friend arrived, I explained the situation and she immediately offered to pay, even though she had really just met me. It was a welcome change from what I suspected might have been the reaction in the United States, something closer to, well, I hope you do not expect me to pay for you. She asked what I wanted to eat, and I told her I had a late lunch, which was true, but also I did not want to have her buying me dinner too. So I ordered only an iced latte.

She ordered food, and when it arrived it was a very good-looking roast chicken dish with mashed potatoes and salad. When she had eaten about half, she asked if I wanted the rest. I asked, "You're full?" She said yes, and I was genuinely glad, because by then I had already started regretting my noble decision not to order food. The mashed potatoes and gravy on that plate were seriously calling my name. I had not had mashed potatoes in a very, very long time. As previously established, most of my carbs, and probably most of my calories, had lately been rice and noodles. I made quick work of the rest of the plate.

Then we realized the clock was ticking. The next thing we planned to do, a lake cruise around Putrajaya, had a cutoff time of 7 PM, and it was getting dangerously close to that. We drove over, parked, did the customary searching around for the correct staircase to get down to the ticket office, and managed to buy tickets and get on the boat.

Just being on a boat is always a pleasure for me. It does not have to be saltwater, though I would prefer that, of course. Still, just being on a boat, listening to the engine rumble somewhere underneath and behind me as it pushed us forward, hearing the sound of the wake coming off the bow, that put me at ease.

As promised, the sun set during the sunset cruise, and the boat slipped underneath a few of the bridges connecting Putrajaya and Cyberjaya. And like all boat trips, the part where it turned around and started heading back to port came too soon.

Afterward she took me to a park with lights, the kind with little illuminated paths and colored lights and white lights everywhere. Some of it was clearly meant for children, including a dinosaur outlined in LED bulbs like Christmas lights. That did not stop me from pretending the dinosaur was about to eat me. A brief public appearance by the inner child that is always trying to escape the professional-engineer facade I present to people I do not know well.

Next to the cruise terminal was what looked to me like a large mosque, although this being the only mosque I had ever seen in person, I was in no position to say whether it was large, small, or gigantic. It was ornate and lit up beautifully. We walked up to the entrance and took off our shoes before entering the main courtyard. She told me she was not allowed into the area ahead, which was only for men, and I could now see some of them praying. But she told me to go inside and check it out, so I did, first asking just to make sure that it was OK that I was wearing socks. She said that was fine. I thought I detected the hint of a smile when she said it.

Inside the mosque, like in many buildings in Malaysia, the first thing I appreciated was the air-conditioning. The second was the openness of it, the high ceilings, the artwork, and of course the people kneeling and praying, with distant voices coming from somewhere at the far end of the room. Later research would tell me I think the voice was from the person delivering the sermon from something called a minbar. I did not go very deep inside because I already felt like I was intruding, even though I am sure no one would have minded and most of that was in my head. That is one of those things I carry with me when I go places, the feeling that I am somehow in the way. It takes me a while, sometimes, to remember that, much like yoga, most people are busy doing their own thing, and as long as you are not directly in their way or doing something truly offensive, they probably do not mind your presence, or have not even noticed you at all.

Next we went to get durian.

It arrived split open like something you were not entirely sure you should be eating. Spiky shell, pale interior, and inside, thick, glossy yellow lobes that honestly looked like oversized grubs. Soft, slightly wrinkled, almost custard-like, just sitting there. I had already heard about it, Black Thorn durian, the reputation, the smell, the strong opinions. People either loved it or talked about it like it was some sort of biological incident.

At that point, I was committed. No overthinking, just a straight YOLO approach.

I picked up a piece and it gave a little under my fingers, soft and dense, not quite liquid but close. I took a bite.

The first thing that happened was that my teeth hit something solid. Not gently. A quick, unmistakable thunk. There was a pit in there, and I found it immediately.

Once I adjusted and actually tasted it, it was not what I expected. It was rich, almost absurdly rich. Sweet, but not a clean sweetness. There was a savory edge to it, something a little fermented that had somehow decided to become dessert. The texture was thick and creamy, like a custard that had forgotten to set properly and did not care.

It lingered too. The flavor stuck around and kept evolving after each bite. It was not refreshing. It was not light. This was a commitment.

And somewhere between the weird texture, the surprise impact of the pit, and the fact that it looked like something I would normally avoid, I realized I kept going back for another bite. I kind of had to. There were only two of us at the table, and these things were not exactly cheap.
