## Wednesday, April 22 - Heat, Horses, and Prayer

```poem
A ceiling whined like bad home repair all day and night
So I fled to the heat and called that choice bright
There were horses, a boat, and mashed potatoes by grace
Then I padded through a mosque in borrowed calm and socked feet space
```

One thing this trip had reminded me of was the absolute misery of apartment living. It was like airplanes and every other public place, where there was always at least one person doing something that annoyed everybody else, except in an apartment complex it felt concentrated, distilled, perfected.

In my case, at least, I knew it was temporary. I would not be subject to this forever. Still, at random points throughout the day, sometimes in the morning, sometimes at night, like right now as I wrote this at 10 PM, a strange noise started up from the ceiling above me. It sounded like a Dremel grinding away at something. Sometimes the pitch changed. Sometimes it got higher, sometimes lower. Sometimes it lasted a long time, sometimes only a little while. But it was always there, every day, every morning, every night.

Today I decided I should go for a run outside. I had run in Texas summers. Running at 10 in the morning in Malaysia should be a snap, or so I told myself. Surely it could not be worse than lying there listening to that mystery tool in the ceiling.

So I put on my long-sleeve sun shirt and black running shorts and headed out to explore. I was looking for what had seemed, from the rooftop pool, to be a running loop. As soon as I got down to the road and made the first couple of turns, I realized that whatever I had seen from above was not a running loop at all. Instead I decided to follow what I knew was some sort of pond or body of water, figuring that as long as I kept taking it right and followed the coastline, I should be fine.

This was optimistic.

Turn after turn, it was more roads, more sidewalks that sometimes simply ended and became grass if I was lucky, or forced me back onto the road if I was not. It was already hot, and very humid in the way mornings here tend to be. Then I started getting really hot. Then I started sweating hard. Then I started regretting the decision.

By the time the route had become an argument with myself, I had already gone too far to turn back. I had to complete the loop. Parts of me started wondering, *what are the initial symptoms of heat stroke?* I thought maybe one sign was if you stopped feeling hot. Well, I still felt very hot, so I figured I was probably OK. A few kilometers later I arrived back at the apartment overheated, but at least with that familiar runner's satisfaction of having done the run even if I had not exactly wanted to.

Later at work, lunch was again at what I called the food truck place, even though they were not really food trucks. This time I had some sort of beef noodle soup, plus two hot sauces that came from the stall. I poured them half-and-half into a plastic cup, partly because I wanted it to look photogenic. Only afterward did I realize I could have done better, arranged it like a yin-yang symbol, the orange and dark brown sauces standing in for black and white. A missed opportunity, but the soup itself did not seem to care.

After work I met someone at a place called Ekues Cabin Cafe. It was mostly open air, which meant you heard everything, wind, people talking, the general movement of a place that was not trying too hard to seal itself off from the world. And every now and then, a horse moved past, or you saw one in the distance. That was the thing that made it feel completely different to me. Cyberjaya had struck me as concrete and industrial. My first real impression of Putrajaya, meanwhile, was this cafe where you sat with a coffee or a plate of food and there were horses and polo players nearby, grazing and walking. The horses, I mean, not the polo players.

In another familiar inconvenience, my credit card would not work with the cafe's QR ordering system. When my friend arrived I explained the situation, and she immediately offered to pay, even though she had really just met me. It was a welcome change from the kind of guarded reaction I could easily imagine back in the United States, something along the lines of, *well, I hope you don't expect me to pay for you*. She asked what I wanted to eat, and I told her I had had a late lunch, which was true, but also I did not want to make her buy me dinner too. So I ordered only an iced latte.

Then her food arrived.

It was a roasted chicken dish with mashed potatoes and salad, and it looked excellent. When she had eaten roughly half, she asked if I wanted the rest. I asked, "You're full?" She said yes, and I was extremely glad, because by that point I had already started regretting my noble little decision not to order food. The mashed potatoes and gravy were seriously calling my name. I had not had mashed potatoes in a very, very long time. As previously established, most of my carbs, and probably most of my calories, had lately come in the form of rice and noodles. I made quick work of the rest of the plate.

We then realized the clock was ticking. The next thing we had planned, a lake cruise around Putrajaya, had a 7 PM cutoff, and it was getting dangerously close. We drove over, parked, did the customary bit of looking around to determine which staircase we were supposed to go down to reach the ticket office, and managed to buy our tickets and get on the boat.

Just being on a boat is always a pleasure for me. It does not have to be saltwater, though of course I would prefer that. Still, even on a lake, just being there, hearing the engine rumble somewhere underneath and behind me, feeling the boat pushed forward, listening to the water peel away off the bow, all of it put me at ease.

As promised, the sun set during the sunset cruise. The boat slipped under a few of the bridges connecting Putrajaya and Cyberjaya, and everything took on that end-of-day softness that makes even modern infrastructure seem briefly forgiving. And like all boat trips, the point where it turned around and started heading back to port came too soon.

Afterward she took me to a park with lights, the kind with little lit pads running everywhere, colored lights, white lights, all of it slightly theatrical in the dark. Some of it was clearly meant for children, including a dinosaur lit up with LED bulbs like Christmas tree lights. This did not stop me from pretending the dinosaur was about to eat me. It was a brief escape by the inner child who is always trying to break out past the professional engineer facade I tend to present to people I do not know well.

Next to the cruise terminal was what looked to me like a large mosque, though as this was the only mosque I had ever seen in person, I had no real basis for comparison. Maybe it was large, maybe small, maybe average by mosque standards. What I did know was that it was ornate and beautifully lit. We walked up to the entrance and took off our shoes before entering the main courtyard. She told me she was not allowed into the area ahead of me, which was only for men. I could see some of them praying. She told me to go inside and look around, so I did, first asking just to make sure that it was OK that I was wearing socks. She said that was fine. I thought I detected the faintest smile when she said it.

Inside, like in many buildings in Malaysia, the first thing I appreciated was the air-conditioning. The second was the openness of the space, the high ceilings, the artwork, and the people kneeling in prayer while distant voices carried from somewhere at the far end of the room. Later research told me that the voice was probably coming from the person delivering the sermon from something called a minbar. I did not go too far in. I already felt, irrationally or not, that I was intruding, even though I am sure no one would have minded and most of that feeling was in my own head. That is one of the things I carry around with me when I go places, the persistent sense that I am in the way, or trespassing somehow. It takes me a while to remember that, much like yoga, most people are busy doing their own thing, and as long as I am not directly in their way or doing something genuinely offensive, they probably do not mind my presence at all, or have not noticed me in the first place.
