## Wednesday, April 22 - Heat, Horses, Durian

```poem
A ceiling sang Dremel hymns at ten at night
So I ran through swampy roads to prove I might
Then coffee, boats, a mosque, and mashed potatoes won
And durian finished the job, weird, rich, and fun
```

One thing this trip had reminded me of was the absolute misery that can be apartment living. It has the same basic problem as airplanes and every other shared public space, which is that there is always at least one person doing something that manages to annoy everybody else. In my experience, nowhere was that more true than in an apartment complex.

At least for me it was temporary. I had the comfort of knowing I would not be subjected to this forever, even if it felt strangely relentless in the moment. At random points throughout the day, sometimes in the morning, sometimes at night, like right then as I was writing this at 10 PM, a strange noise would start up from the ceiling above me. It sounded like a Dremel grinding away at something. Sometimes the pitch changed. Sometimes it went higher or lower. Sometimes it lasted a long time, sometimes only a little while, but it was there every day, every morning, every night.

Today I decided I should go for a run outside. I had run in Texas summers, so running at 10 in the morning in Malaysia should be a snap. That was what I told myself. Surely, at least, it could not possibly be worse than listening to that weird mechanical ghost in the ceiling.

So I put on my long-sleeve sun shirt and black running shorts and headed out to explore. What I had in mind was a running loop I thought I had spotted while looking over the wall of the rooftop pool. As soon as I got out to the road and took the first couple of turns, I realized that whatever I had seen from above was not, in fact, a running loop. So I improvised. I knew there was some sort of pond or body of water nearby, and figured that as long as I kept it on my right and followed the shoreline, I should be fine.

That theory held up less well in practice.

It was already hot, with that heavy morning humidity that seems to settle on you before you have even earned it. I started to sweat, then to really sweat, then to regret the entire decision. Turn after turn, it was just more roads and sidewalks that occasionally ended without warning and gave way to grass, if I was lucky. Otherwise I got pushed back onto the road and had to keep going there. By that point I had already run too far to make turning back feel sensible. I had committed myself to completing the loop.

Part of my brain began asking, with very little urgency but a fair amount of concern, what the initial symptoms of heat stroke were. I thought maybe one of them was that you stop feeling hot. Well, I still felt hot, so I must have been okay.

A few kilometers later I made it back to the apartment overheated, but at least with that runner's satisfaction of having done the run even if I had not exactly wanted to.

Later at work I had lunch again at what I called the food truck place, even though they were not really food trucks. This time I got some kind of beef noodle soup and two hot sauces supplied by the stall. I poured them half and half into a plastic cup because I wanted them to look photogenic. Only afterward did I realize I could have made it better by arranging them like a yin-yang symbol, the orange and the dark brown standing in for black and white. Too late. The soup was still good.

After work that night I was meeting someone at a place called Ekues Cabin Cafe. It was, as I thought of it, a get-to-know-each-other coffee date. Usually I was very nervous for these things, but for some reason this time I wasn't. Partly I had spent the whole day telling myself, over and over, that it was just coffee, just two people having coffee. Somehow that worked. Maybe it was the repetition, maybe it was being so far from home that the usual scripts had loosened their grip a bit.

The cafe was mostly open-air, so you heard everything around you, wind, people talking, the movement of horses every now and then, sometimes close by and sometimes farther off. That was the thing that made it feel so different and so new. Cyberjaya often struck me as concrete and industrial, but one of my first experiences in Putrajaya was this cafe where you could sit with a coffee or a plate of food and look over to the side and see horses, polo players, and animals grazing and being walked. The horses, I mean, not the polo players.

In another familiar bit of travel friction, my credit card did not work with the cafe's QR code ordering system. So when she arrived, I explained the situation, and she immediately offered to pay even though she had just met me. It was a welcome change from what I suspected might have happened back in the United States, which would have had more of a *well, I hope you don't expect me to pay for you* energy. None of that here. Quite the opposite.

She asked what I wanted to order for food, and I told her I had had a late lunch. That was true, but it was also true that I did not want to make her buy me dinner. So I ordered only an iced latte. She ordered food, and when it arrived it was a very good-looking roasted chicken with mashed potatoes and salad. Once she had eaten about half of it, she asked if I wanted the rest.

I asked, "You're full?"

She said yes, and I was genuinely relieved because by then I had very much started regretting my decision not to order food. The mashed potatoes and gravy on that plate had been calling to me from the moment they appeared. I had not had mashed potatoes in a very, very long time. As previously established, most of my carbs and most of my calories lately had been rice and noodles. I made quick work of the rest of the plate. By the end I was dragging my fork across it to get the last remnants of mashed potatoes and the dark brown gravy they were sitting in. If I had been alone, I think there was a real chance I would have licked the plate.

We realized the clock was ticking on the next plan, a lake cruise around Putrajaya, because the cutoff was 7 PM and it was getting dangerously close to that. We drove over, parked, did the customary looking around trying to figure out which staircase we were supposed to go down to reach the ticket office, and managed to buy tickets and get on the boat.

Just being on a boat is always a pleasure for me. It does not have to be saltwater, although of course I would prefer that. Still, just being on a boat at all, hearing the engine rumble somewhere under and behind me and feeling it push us forward, hearing the sound of the wake coming off the bow, all of that put me at ease.

As promised, the sun set during the sunset cruise. The boat slipped under a few of the bridges connecting Putrajaya and Cyberjaya, and like all boat rides, the moment when it turned around and headed back to port came too soon.

Afterward she took me to a park with lights, the kind with little lit-up pads and colored lights running all over the place, white lights too. Some things were clearly meant for children, like the LED dinosaur figures lit up like Christmas decorations. That did not stop me from pretending one of the dinosaurs was about to eat me. A brief escape by the inner child, who is always trying to get out from under the professional engineer routine I present to people I do not know well.

Next to the cruise terminal was what looked to me like a large mosque. Since it was the only mosque I had ever seen in person, I was not really in a position to judge whether it counted as large, small, or giant, but it was ornate and lit in a way that made it striking at night. We walked up to the entrance and took off our shoes before entering the main courtyard. She told me she was not allowed into the area in front of me because it was only for men, and I could see some of them praying there. She told me to go inside and have a look, so I did, first asking just to make sure that wearing socks was okay. She said it was. I thought I caught a little smile when she said that.

Inside, like in many buildings in Malaysia, the first thing I appreciated was the air-conditioning. The second was the openness, the high ceilings, the artwork, and the people kneeling in prayer while distant voices carried from somewhere at the other end of the room. Later research would tell me I think that voice was coming from the person delivering the sermon from something called a minbar. I did not go very deep inside. I already felt like I was intruding a bit, even though I was sure no one there would have minded and that most of this was in my own head. That is one of those things I carry around with me when I go places, that sense that I am in the way or trespassing somehow. It takes me a while sometimes to remember that, much like yoga, most people are doing their own thing and if you are not directly in their way or doing something truly offensive, they probably do not mind your presence at all, or have not even noticed you.

Then we went to get durian.

It arrived split open like something you were not entirely sure should be considered food. The shell was spiky, the inside pale, and sitting within it were these thick, glossy yellow lobes that honestly looked like oversized grubs. Soft, slightly wrinkled, almost custard-like. I had already heard enough about it by then, Black Thorn durian, the smell, the reputation, the strong opinions. People either loved it or treated it like a biological threat.

At that point there was nothing to do but commit. No overthinking, just a straight YOLO approach.

I picked up a piece and it gave a little under my fingers, soft and dense, not liquid but not all that far from it. I took a bite.

The first thing that happened was that my teeth hit something solid. Not subtly, either. A quick, unmistakable thunk. There was a pit in there, and I found it immediately.

Once I adjusted and actually tasted it, it was not what I expected. It was rich, almost absurdly rich. Sweet, but not a clean sweetness. There was a savory edge to it, something faintly fermented that had somehow decided to become dessert. The texture was thick and creamy, like a custard that had forgotten to set properly and did not care.

It lingered too. The flavor hung around and shifted a little after each bite. It was not refreshing, not light. This was a commitment.

And somewhere between the strange texture, the surprise impact of the pit, and the fact that it looked like something I would normally avoid, I realized I kept going back for another bite. I had to, really. There were only two of us at the table, and these things were not exactly cheap.
