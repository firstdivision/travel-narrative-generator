## Wednesday, April 22 - Ceiling Noise and Durian

```poem
A Dremel sang overhead with villainous cheer
I ran through the humidity and questioned heat stroke near
A boat found sunset, a mosque found me in socks
Then durian closed the night with custard, pits, and shocks
```

One thing this trip kept reminding me of was the absolute misery of apartment living. It has the same basic problem as airplanes and every other public space, which is that there is always at least one person doing something that annoys everybody else. In my experience nowhere is that more reliably true than an apartment complex.

At least for me this was temporary. I kept telling myself that. I would not be subject to this forever, even if at random points throughout the day, sometimes in the morning and sometimes at night, like right now as I wrote this at 10 PM, a strange noise started coming from the ceiling above me. It sounded like a Dremel grinding away at something. Sometimes the pitch changed. Sometimes it went higher, sometimes lower. Sometimes it dragged on. Sometimes it only lasted a little while. But it was there every day, every morning, every night, like a personal curse installed somewhere in the floor above.

So today I decided I should go for a run outside. I had run in Texas summers. Running at 10 in the morning in Malaysia should be a snap, or that was what I told myself. Surely it could not be worse than sitting there listening to that weird mechanical whine leak out of the ceiling.

I put on my long-sleeve sun shirt and black running shorts and headed out to explore. What I was looking for was what had appeared, from the rooftop pool, to be some kind of running loop beyond the wall. The illusion lasted about two turns. Once I got out to the road and started following it, I realized whatever I had seen from above was not a running loop at all. So I improvised and decided to trace what I knew was some kind of pond or body of water. As long as I kept taking it right and following the coastline, I figured I should eventually be fine.

That logic became less convincing as the heat built.

It was already humid in the way mornings are here, the kind that makes your skin feel occupied. Turn after turn, it was more roads, sidewalks that occasionally just ended for no particular reason, then patches of grass if I was lucky, and otherwise a return to the road to keep the loop alive. By then I had gone too far to sensibly turn back, so I had no choice but to complete it. At some point part of my brain began wondering what the initial symptoms of heat stroke were. I thought maybe it was when you stopped feeling hot. Well, I still felt hot, so I must have been OK. A few kilometers later I got back to the apartment overheated, but with that runner's satisfaction of having done the run even if I had not really wanted to.

Later at work I went back to what I called the food truck place, even though they were not really food trucks. This time I had some sort of beef noodle soup. There were two hot sauces supplied at the stall, and I poured them half and half into a plastic cup because I thought it might look photogenic. Only afterward did I realize I could have done it better, made it into something like a yin-yang symbol, the orange and the dark brown standing in for black and white. Too late. Still, lunch was lunch.

After work I was meeting someone at a place called Ekues Cabin Cafe. It was a get-to-know-each-other coffee date. Usually I am very nervous for these, but for some reason this time I wasn't. Partly I had spent the day telling myself, over and over, *It's just coffee. Just two people having coffee.* For whatever reason that worked. Maybe it was the repetition. Maybe being in a faraway country had somehow made the whole thing feel less heavy.

The cafe itself was mostly open-air, and that changed everything. You could hear the wind, people talking, and every now and then a horse moving past nearby or off in the distance. That was the strange part, and the part that made it feel new. Cyberjaya had mostly struck me as concrete and industrial, but my first real experience of Putrajaya was this cafe, where you could sit with a cup of coffee or a plate of food while horses and polo players moved around the edges of the scene. Or grazing horses, walking horses. Not the polo players.

In another very familiar development, my credit card did not work with the cafe's QR code ordering system. So when she arrived, I explained the situation, and she immediately offered to pay even though she had just met me. It was surprising and refreshing. She asked if I wanted food, and I told her I had a late lunch. That was true, but I also did not want to make her buy me dinner. So I ordered only an iced latte.

Then her food arrived, roasted chicken with mashed potatoes and salad, and it looked excellent. Once she had eaten about half, she asked if I wanted the rest. I asked, "You're full?" She said yes, and I was very glad because by then I had very much started regretting my noble decision not to order food. The mashed potatoes and gravy were seriously calling my name. I had not had mashed potatoes in a very, very long time. Lately most of my carbs, and frankly most of my calories, had come in the form of rice and noodles. I made quick work of the plate. By the end I was dragging my fork across it to collect the last remnants of mashed potato and the dark brown gravy underneath. If I had been alone, I think I might have licked the plate.

Then we realized the clock was ticking. The next thing we had planned, a lake cruise around Putrajaya, had a 7 PM cutoff, and it was getting dangerously close. We drove over, parked, did the customary wandering around while trying to figure out which staircase actually led to the ticket office, and managed to buy tickets and get on the boat.

Just being on a boat is always a pleasure for me. It does not have to be saltwater, though I would prefer that, obviously. Still, just being on a boat, listening to the engine rumble somewhere underneath and behind me, feeling it push us forward, hearing the sound of the wake off the bow, it put me at ease almost immediately.

As promised, the sun set during the sunset cruise. The boat slipped beneath a few of the bridges connecting Putrajaya and Cyberjaya, and everything softened into evening light. And like all boat trips, the part where it turned around and started heading back to port came too soon.

Next to the cruise terminal was what looked to me like a large mosque, although this being the only mosque I had ever seen in person, I had no real basis for comparison. Maybe it was large. Maybe it was average. Maybe this was me being inexperienced. In any case it was ornate and lit in a way that made it look especially good at night. We walked up to the entrance and took off our shoes before entering the main courtyard. She told me she was not allowed into the area ahead, which was only for men. I could see some of them praying. She told me I should go inside and look around, so I did, after first asking just to make sure that it was OK I was wearing socks. She said that was fine. I thought I detected a small smile when she said it.

Inside, like in many buildings in Malaysia, the first thing I appreciated was the air-conditioning. The second was the openness of the space, the high ceilings, the artwork, and the people kneeling in prayer while distant voices carried from somewhere at the far end of the room. Later research would tell me I think the voice was coming from the person delivering the sermon from something called a minbar. I did not go too deep inside. I already felt a bit like I was intruding, even though I am sure no one minded and most of that feeling was entirely in my own head. That is one of those things I carry around with me, the suspicion that I am in the way. It takes me a while sometimes to remember that, much like yoga, most people are occupied with their own business, and as long as you are not directly in the way or doing something genuinely offensive, they probably do not mind your presence at all, or have not noticed you.

After that she took me to a park with lights, the kind with little lit pathways running everywhere, colored lights and white lights woven through the place. Some of it was clearly meant for children, including dinosaur figures lit up with LED bulbs like Christmas decorations. That did not stop me from pretending one of the dinosaurs was about to eat me. A brief escape for the inner child, who is always trying to slip out from behind the professional engineer routine I present to people I do not know well.

Then we went to get durian.

It arrived split open like something you are not entirely sure a person should be eating. The shell was spiky, the interior pale, and inside were these thick, glossy yellow lobes that honestly looked like oversized grubs. Soft, slightly wrinkled, almost custard-like, just sitting there with total confidence. I had already heard enough about it by then, Black Thorn durian, its reputation, the smell, the strong opinions. People either loved it or talked about it like it belonged in a hazardous materials container.

At that point I was committed. No overthinking, just a straight YOLO approach.

I picked up a piece and it gave a little under my fingers, soft and dense, not quite liquid but close. I took a bite. The first thing that happened was my teeth hit something solid. Not gently. A quick, very clear *thunk*. There was a pit in there, and I had managed to find it immediately.

Once I adjusted and actually tasted it, it was not what I expected. It was rich, almost absurdly rich. Sweet, but not a clean sweetness. There was a savory edge to it, something faintly fermented that had somehow become dessert. The texture was thick and creamy, like a custard that had forgotten to set properly and did not care. It lingered too. The flavor stayed around and kept changing a little after each bite. It was not refreshing. It was not light. This was a commitment.

And somewhere between the weird texture, the pit, and the fact that it looked like something I would normally avoid, I realized I kept going back for another bite. Partly because it was oddly compelling, and partly because there were only two of us at the table and these things were not exactly cheap.
