## Tuesday, May 19 - Hanoi, Round Two

```poem
The train threw me out before the sun felt awake,
I trusted a touchscreen, a tactical mistake,
I chased cash, then pâté, then stepped into disgrace,
And Hanoi welcomed me back right in the face.
```

Hanoi again.

The train woke us at 5:30 a.m., the kind of wake-up that feels less like rising and more like being retrieved. We disembarked and got a Grab to GM Premium Hotel, the one we had somewhat suspiciously scoped out the night before, as if hotel reconnaissance was a perfectly normal evening activity. This time it paid off. They gave us an early check-in, which immediately improved my opinion of the day.

Once we had a room secured, we walked to the lake to catch the city in that early morning mode where everyone seems more disciplined than I am. Around the park, people were out doing Tai Chi, moving slowly and deliberately in the soft morning light. We circled the whole lake and saw a group of dressed-up dancers performing something I assumed was a traditional dance, though I was aware that "I assumed" was doing a lot of work there. It had that public-park energy I keep running into in Asia, where before breakfast half the city has already done something graceful.

Then I stepped in dog shit.

Kate had been walking in front of me. She had seen it. She did not warn me. I would like the record to reflect this.

After that, we went to a café and tried to order ham and cheese croissants, coffee, and a few other drinks. There was a touchscreen ordering system, which seemed modern and efficient in the abstract. In reality, we poked at it long enough to feel slightly stupid before the girl at the counter called us over and told us not to use the touchscreen. So that had been useful. We told her our order the normal way, and then she told us they were cash only. We looked at the total, looked at our wallets, and realized we did not have that kind of cash on us.

So we left to find an ATM.

On the way back from getting cash, we passed a giant line of locals waiting for some kind of food, which is usually all the endorsement I need. We stopped to investigate and saw pâté and some other kind of meat being loaded into sandwiches. That was enough. We decided we had to try one. Ordering happened entirely by pointing at the sandwich the guy before us had received, which worked well enough, although I noticed she did not put as much meat on ours. Fair enough, maybe we had not earned it.

Back at the café, the girl at the counter remembered our order, which was impressive, but she did not remember that we wanted takeaway. So we ended up eating the ham and cheese croissants there and drinking everything on the spot. In the end this was completely fine because it was a comfortable place to sit, and at that hour comfort had become a serious priority.

Later, back in the room, we tried the sandwich. Neither of us liked it much. Maybe it suffered from bad timing because we were no longer very hungry, and maybe a sandwich like that loses something when it is eaten while standing over a hotel desk instead of on the street with traffic going by and smoke in the air. Food can be improved a lot by context, and stripped of a lot by fluorescent hotel-room reality. Also, there is no reason to pretend in private. If you do not like it, you do not like it.

The rest of the morning folded into showers, naps, and the unglamorous but necessary work of unloading photos from the camera to the laptop for backup and review. It was one of those travel stretches where the day had already felt full before most places would even consider serving lunch.
