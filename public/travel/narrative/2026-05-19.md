## Tuesday, May 19 - Hanoi, One Last Round

```poem
We chased the dawn with very noble grace
Then I found dog shit with my actual face
Cash-only breakfast, spa tools, rooftop views
A solid day of wins, confusion, and shoes
```

Hanoi again.

The train woke us at 5:30 a.m., that special kind of wake-up where you are not really rising so much as being peeled out of sleep by logistics. We disembarked and got a Grab to the GM Premium Hotel, the one we had somewhat surreptitiously scoped out the last time we were in Hanoi while trying, if possible, to avoid another Cockroach Incident. This time the place rewarded our caution with an early check-in, which felt almost suspiciously kind.

Once we had a room secured, we went walking toward the lake to catch the city in its early-morning mood. Around the park, people were already moving through their routines with more energy and purpose than I could personally relate to at that hour. We watched groups doing Tai Chi, all controlled motion and patience, and then saw some dressed-up dancers performing what I assumed was a traditional dance. The lake had that morning feeling cities sometimes get, when everything is briefly softer before the traffic and heat fully commit.

And then I stepped in dog shit.

Kate had been walking in front of me, had seen it too, and somehow did not provide the sort of warning one hopes for from a travel companion in active formation. So that was a nice little interruption to the calm lakeside atmosphere.

We ended up at a café to get ham and cheese croissants, coffee, and a few other drinks. There was a touchscreen ordering system, and we put an admirable amount of effort into trying to decipher it, only to finally be summoned over by the girl at the counter and told not to use the touchscreen at all. A perfect modern convenience experience. We gave her our order verbally instead, and then she told us they were cash only. Looking at the total, we realized we did not actually have enough cash for the breakfast we had just carefully failed to order by machine.

So we left to find an ATM. We found one, got cash, and on the way back noticed a giant line of locals gathered around some kind of food stall. A line like that is usually worth respecting. We saw pâté and some other kind of meat going into sandwiches and decided we had to try one. Our ordering method consisted mainly of pointing at the sandwich the guy before us had received, which worked well enough. Though we both noticed she did not put quite as much meat on ours. Fair enough, maybe we had not yet earned premium line treatment.

When we got back to the café, the girl at the counter remembered our order, but not that we had wanted takeaway. So we wound up eating the ham and cheese croissants there and drinking everything in the café instead of carrying it off somewhere else. Which was honestly fine. It was a comfortable place, and after the touchscreen, the cash-only twist, and the ATM detour, I no longer had the energy to insist on the original plan.

Back in the room, we tried the sandwich from the street stall, and neither of us liked it much. Maybe that was because we were not really hungry anymore, or maybe because standing over the hotel desk in a private room had none of the atmosphere that had made it seem compelling in the first place. Street food has a way of borrowing some of its magic from the street itself. Under fluorescent hotel-room logic, it was just a sandwich we did not enjoy. And inside a hotel room, there is no need to perform loyalty to a disappointing snack. If you do not like it, you can just stop eating it.

Later we went back out and somehow found ourselves again at The Orient spa, this time for manicures and pedicures. For me this was another first. I had never had either. My feet were bathed, and because I had apparently signed up for the full experience, out came a little disc grinder and the woman went to work on the bottoms of my feet with unnerving commitment. Every time she ran it over the arches of my feet, I burst into laughter. Completely involuntary. Apparently this is what my dignity looks like in a spa chair.

Dinner was pho. The restaurant had multiple Michelin signs hanging out front, which usually means either confidence or branding or both, and inside you sat wherever there was room for your party. We ended up sharing a table with a Canadian girl taking a gap year before continuing on to more advanced education. Her hand shook a little as she moved spoon and chopsticks toward her mouth, and I found myself idly wondering whether it was medical or hangover-related. The pho itself was very good, in the straightforward way that makes a place popular for a reason. There were small dishes on the table with extra garlic, chilis, and a few other additions, but the whole thing was pleasingly simple. You mostly chose the style by how cooked you wanted the meat when it arrived. We all picked what seemed to be the half-cooked option on the menu.

At some point there were showers, naps, and the practical evening work of unloading photos from the camera to the laptop for backup and review. Travel always has these oddly domestic interludes in the middle of movement, little patches of maintenance between meals and streets and getting from one place to another.

I know I am missing things from this day. My notes clearly were too. But I remember how it ended, and maybe that matters more anyway.

We went back up to the rooftop bar at the GM Premium Hotel and sat there with expensive cocktails, the kind that tasted delicately balanced enough to justify at least part of their price. Below us was the nighttime cityscape of Hanoi, all glow and movement and distance. And sitting there, looking out over it, I realized this would not just be our last night in Hanoi. It would be our last night on this magical multi-nation vacation.

It did seem like a pretty good title.
