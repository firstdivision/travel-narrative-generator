## Tuesday, May 19 - Hanoi, Take Two

```poem
I greeted dawn by train and then by shoe
Which met some dog shit Hanoi thoughtfully threw
We chased our cash, our pho, our final night sky
And paid for cocktails because apparently goodbye
```

Hanoi again.

The train got us up at 5:30am, and we disembarked with all our baggage in that half-functioning early-morning state where you are technically moving, but not yet fully a person. We took a Grab to the GM Premium Hotel, the one we had somewhat surreptitiously scoped out the last time we were in Hanoi while trying, if possible, to avoid another Cockroach Incident. That alone gave the place a certain appeal.

We were granted an early check-in, which immediately improved my opinion of the world. The staff at the front desk spent a surprising amount of time with us, going over what was in the area and what we should see. They were very thorough, right down to where the beef pho was and where the chicken pho was, which felt like exactly the kind of local orientation I could respect.

Once we settled into the room, we resisted the urge to nap, which was probably the right decision, though not one made joyfully. Instead we headed back out into the early morning. Cities in the morning are almost always worth the pain of getting up for. Not the getting up part, obviously. That remains bad. But the atmosphere is so different from the usual nighttime chaos that it feels like being allowed into a separate version of the same place. Different crowds, or maybe just different energy. Softer around the edges.

We walked to Hoàn Kiếm Lake in the early light. It really does sit at the heart of Hanoi like a calm green center the rest of the city has agreed, for now, not to disturb too much. Around the shore, locals were already out doing tai chi and various forms of exercise, moving with the slow confidence of people who had been awake long before me and had probably already made better choices. Visitors drifted beneath the old trees, and the water caught the morning light in a way that made the lake look almost staged, with French colonial buildings, temples, and cafés arranged around it. Turtle Tower stood in the middle, small and elegant, one of those landmarks that somehow looks both iconic and slightly unreal in person.

The lake carries its legend with it. This is the Lake of the Returned Sword, where, according to the story, Emperor Lê Lợi was given a magical sword by a divine golden turtle to help drive out foreign invaders, only for that same turtle to reclaim it from the lake once victory had been won. It is the kind of story that would feel overdetermined anywhere else, but here it just seems absorbed into the place, folded in with the morning walkers, the old trees, the laughter, the conversation, and the occasional motorbike horn drifting in from the streets beyond.

We saw people doing tai chi and walked all the way around the park. At one point we came across a group of dressed-up dancers performing what I assumed was some kind of traditional dance. There was already enough happening around the lake that no one thing seemed to dominate it. It all just layered together.

Then I stepped in dog shit.

Someone, who shall remain unnamed, had been walking in front of me, saw it too, and still failed to provide any kind of warning. A true test of companionship. Still, we all decided on a few practical points. First, we were taking our shoes off when we got inside anyway, whether that meant we were assimilating or had just always been that way. Second, Hanoi sidewalks are not exactly operating-room clean to begin with, so all of our shoes were probably filthy already. I deliberately stepped through a few puddles to try to wash it off. The good thing about boat shoes is that the soles are mostly flat, with no real tread, so at least there were not too many tiny crevices for disaster to settle into.

At a café, we tried to order ham and cheese croissants, coffee, and some other drinks. There was a touch-screen ordering system, and we did our best with it until, just as we finally started to understand what it wanted from us, the girl at the counter called us over and told us not to use the touch screen. A strong start. We gave her our order directly instead, and then she told us they were cash only. We looked at the total and realized we did not have nearly that kind of cash on us.

So we left to find an ATM.

We found one, got cash, and started back. On the way, we noticed a giant line of locals gathered around some kind of food stall, which is usually a useful sign. We went over and saw pâté and some other kind of meat being loaded into sandwiches. That seemed convincing enough. We decided we had to try it, and since we did not have the language for specifics, we ordered by pointing at the sandwich the guy before us had just received. It worked, more or less, though we both noticed she did not put quite as much meat on ours. Fair enough. We had not exactly come in with regular-customer authority.

Back at the café, the girl at the counter remembered our order, but not that we had wanted takeaway. So we ended up eating the croissants and drinking our coffee and other drinks there, which was fine because it was a comfortable place to sit. The croissants were the kind of thing that felt especially good after all that unnecessary cash logistics, buttery and warm and easy to appreciate once the mission had become more complicated than it had any right to be.

Back in the room, we tried the sandwich. Neither of us liked it. Maybe that was partly because we were not as hungry anymore, and maybe it was because standing over the hotel desk in the room stripped away whatever street-food magic had made it look irresistible in the first place. Food can be very dependent on context. In the privacy of a hotel room, there is also no reason to perform bravery for each other. If you do not like it, you do not like it.

Later we went back out and somehow found ourselves at The Orient spa again, this time for pedicures and manicures. For me, this was another first. I had never had either. My feet were bathed, and because I had apparently committed to the full experience, out came a small disc grinder and the woman went to work on the bottoms of my feet with alarming confidence. Every time she passed over the arches, I was sent into helpless laughter. There are probably more dignified ways to receive foot maintenance, but I did not discover any of them that day.

Dinner was at a pho restaurant with multiple Michelin signs hanging out front. Inside, you sat wherever there was room, so we ended up sharing a table with a Canadian girl taking a gap year from college before continuing on to more advanced education. Her hand shook slightly as she brought her spoon and chopsticks toward her mouth, and I found myself wondering, not unkindly, whether it was medical or hangover related. Travel creates a lot of mysteries you are not meant to solve.

The pho was very good, exactly as advertised. The setup was simple: a few styles to choose from, mostly based on how cooked you wanted the meat when it arrived. We all chose what seemed to be the half-cooked option on the menu. Small dishes of extra garlic, chilis, and other additions sat on the table. The broth had that deep, steady warmth that makes you sit up a little straighter after the first sip, and the whole bowl felt less showy than precise, like it knew exactly what it was doing and did not need to explain itself.

After that there were showers, naps, and the practical business of unloading photos from the camera to the laptop for backup and review. Travel is often interrupted by these little clerical rituals. They are not glamorous, but they keep the whole enterprise from dissolving into lost files and bad memory.

I know I am missing some things from this day in my notes. That seems appropriate, honestly. It was one of those days with enough moving parts that some of them inevitably slipped through. But I remember clearly how it ended.

We were back up at the rooftop bar of the GM Premium Hotel, looking out over Hanoi at night and drinking expensive cocktails with delicately balanced flavors that made them feel even more expensive. The city spread out below us in lights and layers, and somewhere in the middle of that view it landed on me that this was not just our last night in Hanoi. It was our last night on this magical multi-nation vacation.

A dramatic realization, maybe, but not inaccurate.

And yes, I did think it would make a good title.
