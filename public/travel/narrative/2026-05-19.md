## Tuesday, May 19 - Hanoi, One More Round

```poem
I greeted Hanoi half-awake and less than neat
Then stepped in a warning I missed beneath my feet
We chased cash, pho, and cocktails with veteran flair
And ended the night slightly polished, still tired, up in the air
```

Hanoi again.

The train woke us at 5:30 in the morning, which is a rude time to become a person again, and we disembarked still carrying that strange overnight-train fog. From there we took a Grab to GM Premium Hotel, the one we had somewhat surreptitiously scoped out the last time we were in Hanoi, partly because I was very interested in avoiding any repeat of the earlier Cockroach Incident. This time luck was with us. We were granted an early check-in, which immediately improved my opinion of the day.

Once we had a room secured, we headed out toward the lake to see the early morning Tai Chi. The city had that washed, pale morning look to it, with people already fully engaged in being awake while I was still negotiating the basics. Around the park, groups were moving slowly and deliberately, and elsewhere we saw some dressed-up dancers performing what I assumed was a traditional dance. I wasn't completely sure what I was looking at, but that felt normal enough. Hanoi often seemed to operate on the principle that you were welcome to observe, but not necessarily to understand everything in real time.

At some point during this wholesome morning walk, I stepped directly in dog shit.

Kate had been walking in front of me, had apparently seen it, and for reasons still unclear did not give me any kind of warning. A simple heads-up would have been nice. Instead I got the full tactile experience.

We ended up at a cafe, aiming for a fairly simple breakfast of ham and cheese croissants, coffee, and some drinks. The place had one of those touchscreen ordering systems that suggests modern efficiency and then immediately starts testing your will to live. We fiddled with it long enough to finally think we had cracked the system, at which point the girl at the counter called us over specifically to tell us not to use the touchscreen. Naturally.

So we gave her the order in person, which seemed to settle things until she told us they were cash only. We looked at the total, looked at the cash we had, and realized we did not have nearly enough of the right kind of paper to participate in this breakfast. So we left, found an ATM, got cash, and started back.

On the way, we saw a giant line of locals gathered for food, which is usually the sort of thing worth paying attention to. They were serving pate and some other kind of meat in sandwiches, and we decided we had to try one. There was no elegant ordering process here. We just pointed at the sandwich the guy before us had gotten and hoped for the best. It seemed to work, although we both noticed she did not put quite as much meat on ours. Fair enough. We had not exactly arrived with regular-customer energy.

Back at the cafe, the girl at the counter remembered our order, which I respected, but not the fact that we wanted takeaway. So we ended up eating there after all, with our ham and cheese croissants and drinks. In the end that was completely fine. It was a comfortable place, cool and calm, and by then I was too tired to object to being seated.

Later, back in the room, we tried the sandwich from the street stall and neither of us liked it very much. Maybe that was partly because we were no longer hungry enough to be generous. Maybe it was because standing over the hotel desk in the room did not have the same magic as eating something right there on the street while scooters rattled past and the air smelled like heat and exhaust and lunch. Food can lose something in transit. Also, a hotel room gives you privacy, and privacy removes the need for politeness. If you don't like it, you don't like it.

After that we went back out and somehow found ourselves once again at The Orient spa, this time for pedicures and manicures. For me this was another first. I had never had either. My feet were bathed, and because I had signed up for the full experience, out came a little disc grinder and the woman went to work on the bottoms of my feet with a level of cheerful determination I had not expected. Every time she passed over the arches, it sent me into helpless laughter. There is probably no dignified way to receive your first pedicure, and I did not find one.

Dinner was at a pho restaurant with multiple Michelin signs hanging out front, displayed with just enough confidence to make clear they knew exactly what they were doing. Inside, you sat wherever there was room, which meant we shared a table with a Canadian girl taking a gap year before going on to more advanced education. As she brought her spoon and chopsticks up, her hand shook a bit, and I found myself idly wondering whether it was medical or hangover-related. Travel makes you weirdly observant about strangers while also giving you almost no useful context.

The pho itself was very good, exactly as advertised. The setup was simple. A few styles, mostly determined by how well cooked you wanted the meat when it arrived. We all chose what seemed to be the half-cooked option on the menu. On the table were small dishes of extra garlic, chilis, and a few other additions. Nothing fussy, nothing overbuilt. Just hot broth, meat at that just-done edge, and the kind of meal that makes the whole table go a little quieter for a few minutes.

Somewhere in the day there were showers, naps, and the practical ritual of unloading photos from the camera to the laptop for backup and review. I know I'm missing things from my notes for this day. That seems fitting, honestly. Some days get recorded in clean detail and some get remembered in fragments, with the feeling of them lingering more clearly than the sequence.

What I do remember very clearly is how it ended.

We were back on the rooftop bar of the GM Premium Hotel, with expensive cocktails in front of us, the kind that tasted delicately balanced in a way that made the price feel slightly less offensive. Below us, Hanoi spread out in the nighttime haze, all lights and rooftops and the restless shape of the city settling into evening. And up there, looking out over it, I realized this was not only our last night in Hanoi but the last night of the entire trip, this long magical multi-nation vacation that had somehow become normal while we were inside it. I remember thinking that would make a good title.

It probably still would.
