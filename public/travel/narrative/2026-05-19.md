## Tuesday, May 19 - Hanoi, Take Two

```poem
Back to Hanoi with luggage and hope,
dodging old mistakes by hotel scope.
I found dog shit before I found my grace,
and cash-only croissants put me in my place.
```

Hanoi again.

The train woke us at 5:30am, that particular kind of wake-up where your body is technically upright but still arguing with reality. We disembarked and got a Grab to GM Premium Hotel, the one we had somewhat surreptitiously scoped out the last time we were in Hanoi, mainly because I was hoping to avoid another Cockroach Incident. This felt like reasonable character growth. They granted us an early check-in, which immediately improved my opinion of the day.

Once we had dumped our things, we walked to the lake to catch the early morning Tai Chi crowd. The park was already alive in that calm but busy way cities sometimes manage before breakfast, full of people moving with purpose that had nothing to do with work. We walked around the whole lake and saw a group of dressed-up dancers performing what I assumed was some kind of traditional dance. I was not confident enough to identify it with any authority, so I just watched. There was music, coordinated movement, and that odd sense of being slightly outside the scene while also being pulled into it.

Then I stepped in dog shit.

Kate had been walking in front of me, had apparently seen it, and still did not give me any kind of warning. A simple heads-up would have been nice. Instead I got that warm, immediate, deeply personal feedback from the pavement itself. Hanoi was happy to have me back.

We ended up at a cafe and tried to order ham and cheese croissants, coffee, and some other drinks. There was a touchscreen ordering system, which we wrestled with for long enough to feel mildly stupid, only to then be summoned over by the girl at the counter and told not to use the touchscreen. Fair enough. We gave her our order the old-fashioned way, and then she told us they were cash only. We looked at the total, then at our cash situation, and realized we did not, in fact, have that kind of cash.

So we left to find an ATM.

After getting money, we were walking back when we noticed a huge line of locals waiting for some kind of food. That usually gets my attention faster than signage ever could. We looked closer and saw pâté and some other kind of meat being packed into sandwiches, and that was enough for us to decide we needed one. We ordered by pointing at the sandwich the guy before us had gotten, which felt efficient if not sophisticated. I also noticed she didn’t put as much meat on ours, which was a little humbling. Tourist tax, but in sandwich form.

Back at the cafe, the girl at the counter remembered our order, though not the part where we wanted takeaway. So we ate the ham and cheese croissants there after all and drank our coffees and whatever else we’d ordered in the air-conditioned comfort of the place. Honestly, it was fine. Better than fine. It was comfortable, and at that point comfort had real value.

Later, back in the room, we tried the sandwich. Neither of us liked it.

Maybe it was because we weren’t as hungry anymore. Maybe food eaten while standing over the hotel desk in a private room just doesn’t have the same force as food eaten immediately on the street, still wrapped, still hot, still surrounded by traffic noise and heat and other people waiting their turn. Outside, it had seemed inevitable that it would be good. Inside, there was no reason to pretend. If you don’t like it, you don’t like it.

After that, the day narrowed into practical comforts: showers, naps, unloading photos from the camera to the laptop for backup and review. Nothing glamorous, just the quiet maintenance that travel keeps demanding. Hanoi had given us Tai Chi, dancers, an early check-in, a cash-only cafe, an overhyped sandwich, and dog shit before noon, which felt excessive but also, in its own way, like a proper return.
