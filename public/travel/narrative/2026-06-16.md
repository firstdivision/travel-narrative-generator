## Saturday, May 16 - Coconut Coffee, Train Tracks, and Bad Financial Decisions

```poem
We chased dry shelter and found a massage rack,
Asked for plain noodles and got more pho back,
Paid martini prices fit for mild despair,
Then slept with a train part screaming underneath the air.
```

I slept in and went back to the hotel buffet for breakfast, which felt like the right kind of laziness. We were leaving that night anyway, so there was no point pretending to be efficient. After that we headed to Hanoi train station to drop our bags in a locker so we could wander the city a little more without dragging our lives behind us.

From there we went to Kong Coffee for another Vietnamese coconut coffee. I was still fully in favor of these. Cold, sweet, thick, slightly absurd, exactly the kind of thing that makes a hot day feel briefly negotiable. We walked around after that, loosely aiming ourselves back toward the old train station and letting the city fill in the space between.

We stopped at St. Joseph's Cathedral, which rises out of central Hanoi in a way that feels faintly unreal, as if a piece of old French ambition had simply been set down in the middle of the streets and left there to weather. It is a large neo-Gothic Catholic cathedral built by the French in the late 1800s during the colonial era, one of the oldest churches in Vietnam and still a major landmark. Its twin bell towers lift above the tangled shopfronts and wires of the Old Quarter with a kind of stern surprise. The building was inspired by Notre-Dame de Paris, with arched windows, ribbed vaults, stained glass, and a weathered stone facade that looked dark and tired in a way I liked. Humidity, rain, pollution, and tropical time had all done their work on it. It looked old honestly, not romantically.

At some point it started to rain, and rain has a way of improving bad ideas by making them feel practical. We found a place called Orient Spa and decided to go in for massages, partly because of the weather and partly because, at that stage, why not. I signed up for a Thai massage and with a confidence I had absolutely not earned, checked the box marked "hard" to indicate how much pulling and stretching I wanted. This turned out to be a mistake. A memorable one. There were several moments during that massage when I felt I was being unfolded by someone with a personal grudge. I had chosen this for myself, which made it harder to complain.

Afterward we went to Train Street, another one of those Hanoi things that gets filed under *must do*, though "must" felt a little strong for sitting inches from railway traffic with a drink. Still, we found a seat at a small cafe right on the tracks and settled in to eat and drink while two trains passed close enough to touch. It was one of those scenes that feels both theatrical and completely normal to everyone around you. Metal, noise, narrow space, people calmly moving chairs out of the way at the last second. Then the train was gone and everyone resumed eating.

I tried to order more noodles for my pho using the trick I had learned in Tokyo for ramen refills, which was my first error. The waitress looked at me with complete and reasonable confusion.

"Just noodles, please."

"Another of this one?" she asked, pointing to my bowl.

"No, just plain noodles."

Another waitress was summoned, which is never a promising sign. We repeated the same exchange with slightly different words and the same useless hand gestures. She eventually left looking satisfied that she understood exactly what we wanted.

What arrived was another full bowl of pho broth and noodles.

Close enough. I ate all of it, including both bowls of broth, which felt less like victory than surrender with chopsticks.

Later we went back to the actual train station and learned that the VIP room did not, in fact, have the showers we had been planning to use before boarding the overnight train. This was the sort of information that would have been helpful earlier, but travel has never been especially interested in timing. Right next to the station was a hotel called The Mango, so we went there and booked a room for three hours. The front desk clerk understood immediately what we wanted without needing much explanation, which suggested this was not an unusual arrangement. We showered, reset ourselves as best we could, and returned to the station VIP room smelling like soap instead of accumulated heat and street air.

There I ordered some gin martinis that were expensive enough to annoy me even before the full damage revealed itself. Later in the trip I would check my credit card statement and discover they had charged me $130 US for two martinis and two non-alcoholic drinks. The only explanation we could come up with was that they had somehow charged a 300% card fee instead of 3%, which is such a stupid theory that it might actually be true. Who knows. The drinks were not worth solving the mystery.

When it was finally time, we boarded the train and were shown to our cabin, which had four single beds, two on the bottom and two that swung down from the top. We had some complimentary drinks and opened the snacks we had brought with us, one by one determining that nearly all of them were gross. The cheddar potato chips were the exception, and they also gave us our favorite new Vietnamese phrase: "Vi Pho Mai!", which we were pretty sure meant cheese flavored. After that, everything became *Vi Pho Mai*, which for reasons that made perfect sense at the time sent us into fits of laughter.

We played a couple games of Monopoly Deal, then got ready to sleep. I had been hoping the night would feel like the last time I slept on a train, years ago as a child on the sleeper from San Francisco to Seattle. In memory that train is soft and rhythmic and vaguely magical, the kind of travel that makes you believe movement itself can be restful.

This was not that.

Some piece of mechanical hardware under our cabin groaned and made a loud CLUNK CLUNK CLUNK every time the train went around a corner, which turned out to be often. Whatever it was, it needed grease in the most obvious possible way. The sound came up through the bed like a personal complaint. So instead of drifting off in nostalgic railway comfort, I lay there in the dark listening to the train argue with itself around every bend.
