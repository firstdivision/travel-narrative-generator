## Friday, May 15 - Coconut Coffee and a Much Better Bar

```poem
We slept past dawn and called that wise,
Then chased cool air like wilted spies,
A market roared, my nerves said no,
So naturally we found a bar below.
```

We woke up at our own pace, which felt like a gift. No 3:37 a.m. alarms today, no violent return to consciousness, just the slow drift into morning and a few texts back and forth until it all resolved into the very practical phrase, "meet downstairs for breakfast."

The buffet was good in the way hotel breakfasts in this part of the world often are, with that distinctly Southeast Asian refusal to pretend breakfast has to be limited by western ideas of what belongs on a morning plate. I had fried eggs, rice, chicken, vegetables, and a caffe latte, and it all made perfect sense. There were plenty of conventional breakfast things around, but I liked the "dinner" options more. At that hour, with travel fatigue still sitting somewhere behind my eyes, rice and chicken felt more useful than pastry.

After that we went to the pool to relax and journal, which was a solid plan right up until I realized I had also sent my bathing suit out to be laundered. Wups. So there I was at the pool, fully committed to the concept of poolside life while being unable to actually participate in the water part of it. I journaled anyway. I also added a cockroach drawing to Kate's book of travel drawings, which felt like the only reasonable artistic response to recent events.

Later we headed out into town and took a Grab into the Old Quarter. Hanoi kept doing that thing where it felt both dense and impossible to fully process, every block busy with scooters, storefronts, and the kind of sidewalk activity that never really qualifies as walking space. We stopped at a jade store, where I picked up a bracelet as a gift for a cousin back home who is watching my plants, house, and truck for me. It felt good to buy something with an actual person in mind instead of just staring vaguely at objects and wondering whether I wanted to carry them for the next several weeks.

We found a cafe for drinks, and this was where I got introduced to the famous Vietnamese coconut coffee, *cà phê cốt dừa*, coffee with coconut. I could immediately see why people get obsessive about it. The version most travelers fixate on is strong Vietnamese robusta coffee with thick coconut cream or coconut milk and sweetened condensed milk, blended with ice into something almost milkshake-like. In Hanoi it often arrives frosty and slushy in a small glass, with the dark, espresso-like coffee poured over pale whipped coconut cream. It looked almost too composed to trust, but drinking it was better than looking at it. Cold, dense, sweet, bitter, creamy. It had that trick of feeling like dessert and caffeine and relief at the same time.

From there we went to the Temple of Literature. Founded in 1070, it is an ancient Confucian temple and Vietnam's first national university, dedicated to Confucius, scholars, and the whole long discipline of learning and civil service. What stayed with me was how quickly the city dropped away once we were inside. Hanoi traffic was still out there somewhere, doing its usual unhinged symphony, but inside the walls the place felt surprisingly quiet.

The complex unfolded gradually through layered courtyards, gates, stone pathways, tiled roofs, red wooden pillars, ponds, and carefully shaped gardens. It really did feel like moving deeper into another century one section at a time. The architecture was restrained rather than flashy, thoughtful rather than showy. Mossy stone, dark wood, incense smoke, soft lantern light. The famous stone turtles holding carved stelae listing successful doctoral scholars from the imperial exams gave the place a weight that was more intellectual than decorative. It symbolized education, discipline, scholarship, prestige. Students still come there before important exams to pray for luck, which made sense. Even as a visitor, you could feel the pressure of history there, but in a calm way. Not loud, not theatrical. Just present.

At some point we were desperate enough for air-conditioning that the hop-on hop-off bus started to seem less like a tourist gimmick and more like a medical intervention. So we got on. It also had the practical advantage of carrying us back toward where we needed to go for our cooking class, which was convenient in the way tired decisions sometimes accidentally become smart ones.

We met our cooking class instructor and two of the other students, Martina and Paul from London. Then we were taken to a wet market and given a quick lesson in how to haggle. Kate did the talking for us, since she had by then become our linguist on this trip, a role she had not officially applied for but was performing with increasing necessity. The market was the usual sensory overload in the best and most exhausting way: damp floors, raw smells, piles of produce, motion everywhere, people clearly operating at a level of purpose the rest of us were only pretending to understand.

After the market we went to Roses Kitchen, where we actually started cooking. We made mango salad, spring rolls, and a dipping sauce that was technically a dipping sauce but felt more like a soup broth. One of the useful principles they taught us was that each sauce or dressing needed five components: sweet, salt, sour, spice, and umami. It was the kind of framework that sounded obvious once someone said it aloud, which I suppose is what good instruction often is.

At the grill, where we were cooking meatballs and pork belly, I was assigned the task of "timer," which meant telling the others to flip the racks every thirty seconds. It was not the most glamorous role in the kitchen, but it did give me a clear purpose, and on some travel days that is enough. The food itself was satisfying in that direct, earned way food is after you've handled ingredients, stood over heat, and smelled everything before it reached the plate. Crisp spring roll wrappers, cool mango, smoke from the grill, pork belly giving off fat and char in little bursts. Nothing abstract about it.

Afterward we went to a night market, and it was complete insanity. People everywhere, stalls everywhere, stores everywhere, noise coming from every angle at once. It was one of those scenes that is easy to admire for about three minutes before your nervous system starts filing formal complaints. I was looking for a gin and tonic, but all I could see were beer stands, and extremely busy ones at that. The whole place felt loud in a way that went past excitement and into static. I needed to get away from the noise and bustle for a while.

Kate saved the day by finding a speakeasy. There was no sign outside. You walked in through an ice cream store, went through a curtain at the back, and then up the stairs, and suddenly there it was: a dimly lit bar with comfortable seats upholstered in some kind of soft red fabric. Exactly what I wanted. The transition was so abrupt it felt almost suspicious, like we had accidentally found a pocket of civilized indoor life hidden behind a joke.

I ordered one of their specialty cocktails, which I think was peach-based, and also a gin and tonic. When the G&T was delivered, the bartender seemed almost offended that anyone would order something so simple in a place making more elaborate drinks. "Who ordered the gin and tonic?" he asked abruptly as he dropped it on the table. Sorry, man. I like them. Maybe he was just having a long night too.

Later we realized we were close to our next Hanoi hotel, the **GM Premiere Hotel**, and decided to scope it out. This was not paranoia so much as quality control after the cockroach episode. We saw they had a rooftop bar, which was as good an excuse as any to walk in. The moment we entered, most of our concern dissolved. It was a nice place, and the bar was nice too, which felt absurdly reassuring.

I had an O Mai drink. I didn't remember exactly what was in it, so later I looked it up, and that seemed to explain the strange appeal of it. Ô mai is made from preserved fruits like plum, apricot, kumquat, peach, or tamarind. The fruit is salted, sugared, dried, and sometimes spiced with ginger, chili, or licorice. The flavor profile is intensely Vietnamese: sweet, sour, salty, slightly medicinal or herbal, sometimes smoky or spicy. In Hanoi, ô mai is a classic Old Quarter snack, and cocktail bars have apparently started using those flavors because they create that weirdly addictive salty-sour edge that cuts through sweetness.

So the cocktail I had was likely fruit-forward, tangy, salted, probably plum- or apricot-based, maybe with preserved-fruit syrup or even a salty rim. The taste landed somewhere between a cocktail, a dried fruit candy, and the kind of sweet-sour snack your brain can't decide whether it loves or hates for the first five seconds. Which, honestly, was part of the fun.

More importantly, the hotel seemed good. Clean, polished, calm. No immediate signs of insect-related betrayal. By that point in the day, that kind of reassurance felt almost luxurious.
