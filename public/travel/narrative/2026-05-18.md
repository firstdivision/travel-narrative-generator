## Monday, May 18 - Maximum Tourism

At 8 a.m. we had breakfast at the upstairs restaurant, another Southeast Asia mixed buffet, the kind of spread that starts to blur together after a while even when it is perfectly decent. Then our driver from yesterday, Thuan, picked us up for a driving tour, one of those days where someone else has already decided what counts as seeing a place.

The first stop was Cat Cat Village, and almost immediately I had the sinking realization that the entire day might be what I started calling max tourism. Cat Cat sits just outside the misty hills of Sapa and feels caught between being a real mountain village and being a carefully constructed outdoor theme park. There were terraced rice fields, waterfalls, wooden bridges, traditional Black H’mong clothing, and more photo spots than seemed strictly necessary for a single valley. Souvenir stalls lined the paths. Ethnic costumes were available for rent. “Instagram” viewpoints appeared at regular intervals like a public service. Visitors shuffled downhill in a slow camera-forward stream. It was beautiful in flashes, especially when the clouds rolled through the valley, but it rarely felt quiet and never really felt untouched.

After that we went to the next stop, and by then there was no point pretending this was anything other than an orchestrated tourism experience. Moana Sapa felt less like a mountain village and more like someone had dropped a Bali-themed Instagram park onto the side of northern Vietnam. You paid an entrance fee and walked through curated photo sets: giant stone hands, fake Heaven Gates, infinity swings, glass viewpoints, oversized statues, flower gardens, all of it arranged against carefully framed mountain backdrops while tourists rotated through each setup waiting for their turn to pose.

It was incredibly photogenic, almost aggressively designed for social media, but at least it knew exactly what it was. Moana Sapa did not pretend to be an untouched cultural experience the way Cat Cat sometimes tried to. It was a manufactured check-in attraction from the start, a mountain photo park with coffee, misty Fansipan views, and enough staged scenery to fill an influencer’s camera roll in an hour. On a clear day, the surrounding landscape genuinely would have been hard to argue with, and even with the artifice, the contrast between all these fantasy props and the actual mountains created a weird, surreal atmosphere that I had to admit was entertaining. I left feeling like I had visited a high-altitude content factory.

At one point I was able to play what I assume was a gravely out-of-tune đàn tranh. The Vietnamese đàn tranh is long and elegant, like a polished wooden canoe crossed with a harp laid flat on a table, traditionally with sixteen strings over a curved body and movable bridges underneath. In skilled hands it sighs and bends and sings. In my hands it made vaguely musical noises, which was generous of it.

Next we went into town, where our guide explained how all the current and under-construction hotels had replaced what used to be fields of bamboo. By then a theme was forming. Tourism had not exactly arrived delicately.

We also met Cici, a woman who initially pretended she was just walking in the same direction as us. Eventually, as expected, she asked us to buy something. When told we had no money, she laughed and delivered what became her catchphrase: “Oh no money? No money no honey, right?” Then she finally peeled away.

Somewhere in town I had what can only be described as a bathroom emergency. The girls suggested maybe I could hold it. I informed them that I did not think that was an option in this case. This train was leaving the station. At some point I said what then became a trip quote: “I fear that my body has betrayed me.” It felt accurate and not especially dignified.

Later we wandered off the beaten path, or possibly just took a wrong turn off the prescribed tourist roads. It led us down toward the river, and we followed it, partly because it looked interesting and partly because it felt like an accidental escape hatch from all the managed scenery. Down there things began to resemble something more real. We ended up between the river and a few rice paddies, one of which was being tended. A paraglider circled down over us in lazy loops from above, a reminder that even here, with mud and water and fieldwork in front of us, tourism was still hanging in the air. There was also a line of children walking down by the river. We could not tell whether they were playing or, more likely, collecting things to sell.

And we were hot. Truly hot. Rotisserie-chicken hot, like the birds we had seen cooking over charcoal back in Cat Cat.

We had lunch back in main Sapa town at a place called Little Sapa. It felt exactly like the kind of restaurant travelers stumble into after a freezing, fog-soaked trek and then keep talking about for the rest of the trip. The relief started before the food even arrived. Giant portions, low prices, hot local food, and the general feeling that whatever happened that morning, at least now you were sitting down. People rave about dishes there for a reason. Wild pig, venison, sizzling beef noodles, spring rolls, salmon hot pot, even burnt banana with rum. It was one of those unassuming places that makes no effort to look important and then quietly justifies itself.

After lunch we shopped along the same street. We found an art shop and each bought something, including a Smoking Lady painting.

Back in the car, we headed to Love Falls. Love Waterfall, Thác Tình Yêu, sits hidden in the forests outside Sapa near the base of the Fansipan range, and reaching it required a scenic walk through bamboo groves, pine forest, and misty mountain trails alongside small streams and rushing water. The falls drop about 100 meters down dark rock into a cold turquoise pool. The air there was noticeably cooler, with mist hanging in the trees and the sound of water echoing through the valley before the waterfall even came into view. According to local legend, it was named after a fairy who fell in love with a flute-playing woodcutter and met him there in secret. Tourist attraction or not, it felt calmer and less commercialized than Cat Cat Village or Moana Sapa. In the wet weather and fog, the whole area had that almost prehistoric feeling: wet stone, jungle smells, cloud drifting through the trees, water roaring somewhere ahead.

Back at the car, we had to wake Thuan up, but soon we were on our way again to the next spot: Rong May Glass Bridge, also called the Dragon Cloud Glass Bridge. It felt like somebody had taken an extreme amusement park ride and bolted it directly onto the side of a mountain above the clouds. High on the Sky Gate of O Quy Ho pass, the attraction launches you out over a massive valley on transparent glass panels more than 2,000 meters above sea level. The first few steps were pure brain confusion. My eyes saw empty air under my feet while the rest of me refused to accept that the glass was solid.

The whole place was built for adrenaline and social media and did not bother to hide it. Glass elevators climbed the cliffside. There were rainbow staircases, suspension bridges, death swings, rope bikes, drone videos, and endless photo platforms dangling over impossible views. The valleys and jagged peaks around Sapa faded into layers of blue and cloud. At the same time, the human part of the scene was almost better: terrified tourists inching across transparent flooring while influencers posed dramatically five feet away, apparently untroubled by gravity.

We each bought into it a little more than I expected. Two of us lay down on the glass. The third succeeded in simply making it all the way to the end of the bridge, which was honestly a respectable achievement. We also recorded a Happy Birthday video for someone back home, because apparently that was the setting we chose for a birthday greeting.

The next stop was something called the lonely tree. It was, literally, a small tree surrounded by a deck that you could walk out on and, yes, take your picture with. There were a couple of other Instagram setups too, including a swing and another deck reaching off toward the view. We took a few photos and moved on.

And yes, these places all cost money. Not just the big ones. Even the lonely tree required paying, after stepping under a highway guardrail to reach the ticket booth, which felt like a quietly perfect summary of the whole thing. The prices were favorable to us, sure, but there were moments when it still felt faintly ridiculous to hand over money for access to a tree.

That was our last stop. Soon Thuan was taking us back to Lao Cai, where our overnight train waited to carry us back to Hanoi. Sapa gave us some magnificent views on the way out. The sun was setting, golden hour had arrived, and for a few moments I considered asking the driver to pull over so I could take photos. But sometimes in a car you do not make the decision quickly enough. The good light appears, the perfect angle arrives, and by the time you realize it, you are already past it.

Back in Lao Cai we stopped at an ATM to get cash for the driver, then took showers and settled in for drinks and dinner in the VIP room. While we were waiting, the driver returned carrying a rolled-up package. One of the paintings had been left in the car. Many thanks were exchanged, and then it was time to board the train.

The new train car was not as nice as the last one. It seemed older. Two rooms down there was a group of girls having a very good time, long peals of laughter coming from their compartment. The train conductor informed us that they were from Kuala Lumpur.

We were tired. After changing into pajamas, we settled in for a couple games of Monopoly Deal.

When we finally went to sleep, we were pleased to notice that this train car, while not as nice as the other one, had apparently received better undercarriage maintenance. There were no KLUNK KLUNK KLUNK sounds coming from underneath us this time.

But it was hot.

You cannot get everything.
