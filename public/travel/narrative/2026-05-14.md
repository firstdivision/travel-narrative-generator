## Thursday, May 14 - Temples, Tuk-Tuks, and One Very Bad Hotel

```poem
We chased the sunrise under a stubborn gray sky
Trusted sweet coffee and ice with a practical why
Then fled one roach toward a toilet fit for a king
Travel, as usual, was not a seamless thing
```

The alarms started going off at 3:37am for our temple tour, which felt like an unreasonable hour even by travel standards. I did a quick self-diagnostic on my cold and decided I was going to go instead of staying behind. At that moment I seemed fine enough. My nose had stopped dripping. Maybe I was actually getting better, or maybe I was just being held together by residual cold medicine and optimism. Hard to say.

The bus arrived at 4:45 and we climbed on in the dark, still half asleep, committed now.

We reached Angkor Wat in time for the famous sunrise, one of the main reasons we had signed up for the early morning tour in the first place. Angkor Wat is enormous, a 12th-century temple complex and the largest religious monument in the world, built by the Khmer Empire under King Suryavarman II. It began as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu and later became a Buddhist site. Even knowing those facts in advance did not really prepare me for the scale of it. The place felt less like a building and more like an ancient stone city rising out of the jungle, all symmetry and weathered gravity, its lotus-shaped spires lifting over the moat and the galleries lined with intricate carvings of mythology, battles, apsaras, and scenes of Khmer life. It was designed to represent Mount Meru from Hindu cosmology, which sounded exactly like the kind of thing a guide tells you while your brain, at that hour, is trying not to slide quietly out of your ears.

And the sunrise itself? Overcast. Completely overcast. So we had dragged ourselves out of bed in the middle of the night to watch a cloud bank gradually become a brighter cloud bank. Very spiritual. Still, there was coffee nearby, so I got one. It was very sweet, but it was hot and contained precious caffeine, and at that hour I was not in a position to be critical.

We walked through Angkor Wat and other nearby temples after that, with our guide narrating history and facts the entire way. I listened sincerely and forgot things almost immediately, which felt rude but inevitable. Some places still managed to cut through the fog anyway: the long corridors, the worn stone passages, the sudden quieter corners that still felt mysterious despite the crowds. Even with other tourists everywhere, there were moments when the place seemed to hold on to something older and less available.

Our tour guide was named Money, and the driver was Mr Moon, which together made them sound less like a guide team and more like characters from a fable.

By lunch, my legs were done negotiating. We stopped at Khmer Family Resturant, where noodles and a pineapple smoothie brought me back to life. The smoothie was excellent, though I spent part of the time wondering whether this was "good ice" or the kind that would make my intestines file a formal complaint later. I decided it was probably safe. Surely a tour bus would not regularly deliver people to a place known for making their guts explode. That seemed bad for business.

After lunch we saw more temples, and at one point some monkeys playing around a pond area, which was the sort of thing that briefly resets your mood no matter how tired you are.

Back at the resort, we went to see Jodi and picked up some clothes Kate had altered. I was now sockless with my boat shoes, which meant the "Canadian Grandpa" phase of the trip had finally ended. We walked around the corner to another small market under a tin roof, and two of us found artwork to buy there. By then we were tired enough that walking had lost a lot of its charm, so we finally took a tuk-tuk. The driver offered $3 to go to the hotel or $5 for a tour of Siem Reap. We took the tour because, at that point, why not.

Back at the hotel we went to the pool for a quick cool-off and lunch, and it made me wish we had another day there. But we were flying to Vietnam that night, so Siem Reap was already turning into something we were leaving.

After quick showers, we packed and were soon in the van to the airport. There I had my first encounter with a squatty potty, the kind with two foot pads and a hole in the middle. When in Rome, or in this case, when in an airport bathroom trying to maintain dignity with a backpack on.

Our flight was delayed, and we all decided that this had to be the hiccup for this leg of the trip. Surely this was it. Surely it could get no worse than a delay. It had that *Apollo 13* feeling. Looks like we’re gonna have our glitch for this mission.

Then we landed in Hanoi.

We got a Grab to our hotel, Hanoi Garden Hotel, and during the ride the driver kept asking his phone for directions and drifting out of his lane. Later all three of us agreed he seemed like he might have been high or something. It was not a reassuring first impression.

The second first impression was worse.

We arrived at the hotel and walked into a lobby that was hot and very musty-smelling. Not good. Then we were let into our room, and both girls shrieked at exactly the same time.

Cockroach.

I trapped it under a wastebasket, and in the process also found a big wad of black hair, which really completed the atmosphere in a way nobody had asked for. At that point we all agreed we no longer wished to stay there. No debate, no brave attempts to rationalize it, no "maybe it's fine." We left for the Shereton Hotel without even getting reservations first. We just wanted out.

The Shereton lobby was clean and cold, and after the heat and smell of the first place, it felt almost suspiciously civilized. The hosts were very helpful and got us two rooms. I ended up in my own room with a king-sized bed and one of those robot toilets with a motorized lid, heated seat, and controls for bidet and deodorizer functions. Quite a jump from the previous accommodation, which had offered a more stripped-down relationship with nature.

I took a long shower and sent out all my clothes to be washed.

By the end of the day, I had started before dawn in one of the oldest temple complexes in the world and ended it in Hanoi with a luxury toilet whirring quietly beside me. Travel does have a way of refusing any consistent tone.
