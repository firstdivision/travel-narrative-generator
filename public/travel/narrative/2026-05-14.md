## Thursday, May 14 - Temples, Tuk-Tuks, and Tactical Retreats

The alarms started at 3:37am for our temple tour, which felt like a hostile suggestion from a version of myself who had been far too optimistic the night before. I did a quick self-diagnostic on the cold situation and decided to go for it instead of staying behind. At that moment I seemed fine enough. My nose had stopped dripping, and I chose to believe that meant recovery instead of the more obvious possibility that cold medicine was simply doing an excellent impression of health.

The bus arrived at 4:45 and we got on in the dark, that peculiar hour when nobody is really conversational and everyone is just quietly committed to their own level of fatigue.

We arrived at Angkor Wat, which somehow still manages to feel oversized even when you already know the facts. It is an enormous 12th-century temple complex, the largest religious monument in the world, built by the Khmer Empire under King Suryavarman II. It began as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu and later became a Buddhist site. The place is famous for its lotus-shaped spires and its broad reflective moat, and the stone surfaces are covered in intricate carvings of mythology, battles, apsaras, and scenes of Khmer life. Everything about it is symmetrical and symbolic, meant to represent Mount Meru from Hindu cosmology. It sits near Siem Reap inside the larger Angkor Archaeological Park, and even before seeing much of it, I understood why sunrise at Angkor Wat is one of those iconic Southeast Asia experiences people talk about so reverently. The temple really does feel less like a single building and more like an ancient stone city pulled out of the jungle.

Of course, the sunrise itself was overcast, which was mildly rude given that this was one of the main reasons we had signed up for an early morning tour in the first place. Still, there was coffee nearby, so I got one. It was very sweet, the kind of sweet that seems less brewed than negotiated, but it was hot and contained precious caffeine, which was enough. Sometimes standards adjust themselves before dawn.

We walked through Angkor Wat and other nearby temples while our tour guide gave us a steady stream of history and facts the entire way, most of which I knew I would forget almost immediately. I tried to hold onto details while also just looking. Even with crowds, there were corridors and weathered stone passages that still felt quiet, mysterious, almost separate from the rest of the morning. The stone had that worn, softened look that only centuries can give it. It was easy to see how the place remains so iconic even under a flat gray sky.

Our tour guide’s name was Money, and the driver was Mr Moon, which together made the whole operation sound either wonderfully memorable or very slightly invented. But they got us around all day, so I had no complaints.

By lunch we were tired in the legs in that specific temple-tour way, where your body starts treating stairs as a personal attack. We stopped at Khmer Family Resturant, where I had noodles and a pineapple smoothie. The noodles and the cold sweetness of the smoothie brought me back to life a little. I did have a moment with the ice, wondering whether this was good ice or the sort that would make my intestines complain loudly later. In the end I decided that a tour bus probably would not make a regular stop at a place known for detonating foreigners’ digestive systems, so I drank it and hoped for the best.

After lunch we saw more temples, and at one point some monkeys playing around a pond area, which added a bit of chaos to the stone-and-history rhythm of the day. By the time we were dropped back at the resort, I was fully in that tired but still moving state that travel creates so efficiently.

We went back to see Jodi to pick up some clothes that Kate had altered. An important development followed: I was now sockless with my boat shoes, which meant no more Canadian Grandpa. A subtle but necessary shift in personal branding.

After that we walked around the corner to another small market under a tin roof, the kind of place where heat gathers and hangs there. Two of us found artwork to buy. By then we were tired enough that walking had started to feel optional, so we finally took a tuk-tuk. It was $3 to go to the hotel or $5 for a tour of Siem Reap. We took the tour because, at that point, why not? If you are already tired and being moved around by small vehicles all day, an extra loop around town for two more dollars starts to sound like very sensible economics.

Back at the hotel we went to the pool for a quick cool-off and lunch, and mostly wished we had another day there. It had that frustrating end-of-stop feeling, where you only really settle in right before you have to leave. But we had a flight to Vietnam that night, so after quick showers we packed and were soon riding in the van to the airport.

It was the same driver who had taken us from the airport to the hotel when we first arrived, the one I had forgotten to tip. I had felt bad about that, so this time we gave him the cost of the ride plus a fairly considerable tip on top, hoping to make up for my forgetfulness from the day before. Travel offers a lot of chances to reveal your character, and just as many chances to correct it a little later if you miss the first one.

At the airport I had my first encounter with a squatty potty, the kind with two foot pads and a hole in the middle. When in Rome. Or in this case, not Rome at all.

Then our flight was delayed, and we decided this was clearly the hiccup for this leg of the trip. Surely it could get no worse than that. We even treated it like the Apollo 13 line: looks like we’re gonna have our glitch for this mission. It was a comforting theory for a while.

We landed in Hanoi and got a Grab to our hotel, the Hanoi Garden Hotel. On the way, the driver kept asking his phone for directions and drifting out of his lane often enough to become memorable. Later, all three of us agreed that he seemed like he might have been high or something. It is not ideal to arrive in a new city after a delayed flight while wondering whether your driver is improvising both navigation and consciousness.

We got to the hotel and walked into a lobby that was hot and musty-smelling, which was not a promising opening note. Then we were let into our room and both girls shrieked at exactly the same time.

Cockroach.

I trapped it under a wastebasket and, in the process, also found a big wad of black hair, which did not help the room’s case. At that point we all agreed we no longer wished to stay there. No debate, no brave compromise, no late-night attempt to lower expectations. We just left for the Shereton Hotel without even making reservations first. We wanted out.

The contrast was almost absurd. The Shereton lobby was clean and cold, and the hosts were very helpful and able to book us two rooms. Suddenly the night shifted from grim and musty to expensive and deeply necessary. I ended up in my own room with a king-sized bed and one of those robot toilets with a motorized lid, heated seat, and controls for the bidet and deodorizer functions. It felt like I had traveled, in the span of an hour, from mild existential lodging crisis to the future.

I took a long shower and sent out all my clothes to be washed. After a day that began before dawn at Angkor Wat and ended in Hanoi fleeing a cockroach, that felt like the correct final act.
