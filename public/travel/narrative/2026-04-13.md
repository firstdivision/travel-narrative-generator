## Monday April 13 to Friday April 17 - Workweeks, Blankets, Beetles

```poem
Work fed the week while my weekends stayed bright
I chased fiber, blankets, and ibuprofen at night
A beetle revolt canceled pasta with flair
So I dined on snacks, then floated above the hot air
```

Work was work. That was the small sacrifice that made the weekends possible, the trade I had apparently agreed to. With an operational laptop, I was finally able to do real work, which mostly meant finding myself back in the same rabbit hole of the unsolvable. They are literally called "pipelines," which felt a little too honest. More often than not, they led to dead ends, or just looped me in circles around the metaphorical computing drainpipe.

Still, another universal constant held up. The people you meet are the good part. Just like interstate travel, inter-country travel kept proving that by an overwhelming majority, we all want roughly the same things: friends, food, family. In my case, at least for now, it was a temporarily adopted work family. There were jokes, stories, shared meals, and the reliable excitement that appears whenever food enters the conversation.

There were many lunches, including the standing Thursday lunch, but somewhere in the middle of all that rice and noodles my body started filing complaints. Every meal with rice or noodles sounds excellent until it becomes your actual life for days in a row. I found myself going to Village Grocer for salad fixings, eggs, avocado, tortilla chips. Maybe it was culinary homesickness. Maybe it was just fiber calling from deep within. Either way, I listened.

My Airbnb was also missing what I would consider a critical feature, a comfy blanket. After work I wanted to burrito myself in something soft and convincing. I noticed, a little too late, that this was yet another food-adjacent metaphor from home. There was a mall nearby, because of course there was. They still have malls here, and lots of them. This one was called DPULZE. I bought a fuzzy blue blanket, hand towels that were also missing from the bathrooms, and then wandered around inspecting this particular consumer ecosystem. I found a clothing store and bought a nice shirt that might come in handy. There was also a Japanese bakery where I picked up a flossed chicken roll and some garlic bread.

Back at the apartment, I started to make pasta. Salted water boiling, remains of a bag dumped into the pot, basic weekday competence. Then I saw the black things. Some were in the water, some still in the bag, and they were moving. Some sort of flower-eating beetle, as far as I could tell. Even knowing it probably would have been technically fine did not help. I dumped the whole batch into the trash. No pasta tonight, apparently. Less "No Sugar Tonight" by Mother Nature, more no pasta tonight, courtesy of beetles.

Dinner became the garlic bread I had bought earlier and whatever else I could scrounge from my chronically understocked kitchen, which at that point meant apples, peanut butter, and a blueberry yogurt. Not elegant, but functional.

At some point in the week I also had to figure out how pharmacies worked here. I went into a CVS-like store in search of ibuprofen and found nothing but shelves of paracetamol, in other words acetaminophen. I gave up browsing and asked. Ibuprofen, it turned out, was in the pharmacy, which here meant behind the counter. Small things like that are what remind you, very efficiently, that Toto is not home anymore.

Other dinners were better. I kept exploring the restaurants around Tamarind Square, and one of them produced a fantastic plate of Thai chicken and rice. It was a welcome sit-down meal, especially after the more casual kind of dining that begins with scanning a QR code at the table. The plate came with a whole bone-in chicken thigh, rice obviously, a small salad on the side, and some kind of broth. I poured the broth over the rice because I wasn't entirely sure what else I was supposed to do with it, and that turned out to be a solid decision.

One useful accomplishment that week was finally inspecting the rooftop pool in my apartment building. But first, pools need snacks. Require them, really. That meant I got to check out the little minimart I had only recently discovered on the 10th floor, the level that connects the two towers of the apartment complex. It had the necessities and, more importantly, the non-essentials I was looking for. I chose one familiar thing and one unfamiliar one. The familiar was sour cream and onion Pringles, the comfort-food safety pick. The new one was "O'mas Snek Mi," snack noodles, flavor "Perisa Kari BBQ," which I was fairly certain meant barbecue curry. At the pool, between cooling swims and staring out beyond the edge of the infinity view, both the noodles and the Pringles kept quietly disappearing.

My final mission of the week was stranger, but more satisfying. I wanted to recreate the ginger coconut drink we had discovered in Puerto Rico, of all places. My Malaysian coworkers looked into it a bit and figured out that it was made in China, but as far as we could tell it was not sold anywhere in Malaysia. Business opportunity, probably. Immediate problem, definitely. I was still craving it, so I decided to make my own version.

I bought a bottle of ground ginger, a few different kinds of coconut powder, and some sweetened condensed milk, then got to work as the least qualified mad scientist in the building. The recipe I landed on was not elegant: a lot of coconut powder, what felt like too much, an equally absurd amount of ginger powder, enough that I began wondering how old the ginger might be, two pink plastic Barbie-looking bread knives' worth of condensed milk, because that was the measuring system available to me in this woefully understocked Airbnb kitchen, then hot water to fill and stir.

It was actually pretty close. Not the real thing, and I would have preferred the real thing, but it scratched the itch. In a crunch, that was enough.
