## Monday April 13 to Friday April 17 - Pipelines and Pool Snacks

Work was work. That was the trade, a small sacrifice that let me fill the weekends with things that actually felt new. With a functioning laptop, I was finally able to do real work, which in theory sounded promising. In practice, I still found myself down the never-ending rabbit hole of the unsolvable. They are literally called “pipelines,” which felt almost insulting, because more often than not they led to dead ends and endless loops around the metaphorical computing drainpipe.

Still, one of the constants of travel held up. The good part was the people. Just like interstate travel, inter-country travel kept proving the same thing by overwhelming majority, that most of us want roughly the same setup: friends, food, family. In my case, at least for now, that meant a temporarily adopted work family. There were jokes, stories, shared meals, and the very reliable excitement that appears whenever food enters the conversation.

There were many lunches. The standing Thursday lunch. Plenty of eating, generally welcome, though eventually my body started filing formal complaints. Every meal seemed to involve rice or noodles. That sounds ideal right up until it becomes your actual life for long enough. At some point I needed greens with real urgency, so I went to Village Grocer for salad fixings, eggs, avocado, and tortilla chips. Maybe it was culinary homesickness. Maybe it was just fiber deprivation. Either way, I was no longer in a position to be picky.

My Airbnb was also missing a critical feature: the comfy blanket. After work, I needed to burrito myself in something soft and vaguely protective. Yet another comfort from home, apparently. So I went to a mall, because they still very much have malls here, and lots of them. This one was called DPULZE. I bought a fuzzy blue blanket, plus hand towels because the bathrooms were somehow missing those too. Then I wandered. I found a clothing store and bought a nice shirt, which seemed likely to come in handy. There was also a Japanese bakery, where I picked up a flossed chicken roll and a garlic bread, both of which felt immediately like good decisions.

Back at home I started to make pasta, boiling salted water and dumping in the remains of a bag I had. Then I noticed unexpected black things. They were in the pot, and also still in the bag. Worse, they were moving. Some kind of flower-eating beetle, apparently. I know, rationally, it probably would have been fine. I was not interested in testing that theory. I dumped the whole batch into the trash. No pasta tonight, which made me think of “No Sugar Tonight” by The Guess Who, except with more beetles and less dignity.

Dinner became the garlic bread I had bought earlier, plus whatever I could scrounge from my chronically understocked kitchen, which turned out to be apples, peanut butter, and a blueberry yogurt. Not glamorous, but technically dinner.

I also went looking for ibuprofen, which turned into one of those tiny logistical missions that remind you you’re not at home. In a CVS-like store I found shelf after shelf of paracetamol, or acetaminophen, and no ibuprofen at all. Eventually I gave up and asked. Ibuprofen, it turned out, was in the pharmacy here, meaning behind the counter. It was a small thing, but small things are often the ones that make the point most clearly. Toto was not home anymore.

Other dinners this week came from more exploration around Tamarind Square. One of the better ones was a fantastic plate of Thai chicken and rice, and I was unusually grateful for it because it was a real sit-down meal, not the more casual QR-code-on-the-table routine. The plate came with a whole bone-in chicken thigh, rice obviously, a small salad on the side, and some sort of broth that I ended up dumping over the rice because I wasn’t entirely sure what else to do with it. It worked. The chicken was excellent, the meal had the satisfying feeling of being properly assembled, and for a little while I forgot I had ever wanted salad.

Another overdue accomplishment was finally inspecting the rooftop pool in the apartment building. But first, pools need snacks. Require them, really. That gave me an excuse to check out the little minimart I had only recently discovered on the 10th floor, connecting the two towers of the complex. It had both necessities and exactly the kind of non-essentials I was after. I picked a familiar option, sour cream and onion Pringles, comfort food in canister form, and something new: O'mas Snek Mi, snack noodles, in *Perisa Kari BBQ*, which I was fairly sure translated to barbecue curry flavor. At the pool, between cooling swims and staring out past the edge of the infinity, both the noodles and the Pringles quietly disappeared in alternating turns. It felt like a proper small luxury.

My final mission for the week was an attempt to recreate the ginger coconut drink we had discovered, improbably enough, in Puerto Rico. My Malaysian coworkers helped me investigate, and as far as we could tell the product was made in China but not actually sold anywhere in Malaysia. There was probably a business opportunity in that, but my concern was more immediate and less entrepreneurial. I wanted the drink.

So I assembled ingredients: a bottle of ground ginger, a few kinds of coconut powder, and some sweetened condensed milk. The Airbnb kitchen remained woefully understocked, so the process had a certain improvised-laboratory quality to it. The recipe, if it deserved that title, was a lot of coconut powder, honestly what seemed like too much, an equally absurd amount of ginger powder that made me start wondering how old the ginger might be, and two pink plastic Barbie-looking bread knives’ worth of condensed milk, because spoons were apparently too much to ask of this kitchen. Then I added hot water and stirred.

It was actually pretty close.

Not the real thing, not quite, but close enough to scratch the itch. In a pinch, that was more than good enough.
