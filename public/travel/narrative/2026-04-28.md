## Tuesday, April 28 - New Wheels, Modest Soup

```poem
My old bag groaned like it hated the trip
The new one rolled off with barely a slip
I spun it in circles like a kid in a store
Then ate decent chicken and wanted the airport more
```

Tonight we headed to a mall because I needed to buy new luggage to replace the wounded suitcase I had been dragging around. It felt like a practical errand, not an especially glamorous one, which is probably why it ended up being oddly memorable.

We found a store with a lot of options, including Malaysian brands, which seemed like the appropriate thing to do. Very quickly, buying a suitcase turned into a small performance. The store employees multiplied until there were four of them, each appearing with another case, another feature, another reason this one might be the one. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. This one has TWO zippers. It had the cheerful intensity of retail theater, and I was tired enough to find the whole thing both helpful and faintly absurd.

Eventually I settled on one.

The clerks insisted on wheeling the suitcase to the register for me, then instructed the cashier to give me a discount, which I appreciated both financially and emotionally. It is hard not to like people when they are this invested in your luggage situation.

I liked the new hardcase style of the carry-on immediately. I liked the four casters too. They glided smoothly over the mall’s tiled walkways with a neat little click clack, click clack, like a single-car tiny train. Much less embarrassing than the loud noises and awkward ambulations of my old suitcase, which had been announcing to the world everywhere we went that it did not want to be there.

On the way to somewhere else in the mall, I kept playing with the new suitcase. I was trying to see if I could get it to do a full 360 on its wheels. It took a few attempts, but on about the third try I managed it. She smiled at me and said, “You are still a child inside?”

“Yes, sometimes I am,” I replied.

I think it’s a good quality.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, perfectly fine in fact. Warm, simple, competent. The kind of meal that does its job without trying to impress anyone. But the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport still hung over it, which was slightly unfair and also completely true. Some meals don’t get judged on their own terms. They get judged against whatever you’re still thinking about while you eat them.
