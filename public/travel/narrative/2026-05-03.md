## Sunday, May 3 - Kopi, Temples, and Mall Miles

```poem
I missed the run but made the coffee call
A noble upgrade, honestly, from none at all
We prayed, we walked, we browsed what I can't buy
Then fed ourselves in malls and called it by-and-by
```

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore, I was supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run, and I missed that one completely. A 9 a.m. breakfast, though, felt like a far more humane arrangement. I was willing to be ambitious, but not *that* ambitious.

We all met at Luck Bros Kopi, tucked beside the bustle of Petaling Street. It felt like a polished version of the Malaysian coffee shop experience, the kind of place that takes something already good and gives it a careful shine without draining the life out of it. There was strong kopi, fragrant nasi lemak, and a menu that moved easily between Chinese, Malay, and Peranakan influences. It made breakfast feel slightly ceremonial, or at least more thoughtful than my usual strategy of finding caffeine first and asking questions later. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes.

Afterward we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe they had to rent them, I never quite figured that out, and we all went inside without our shoes. One of the first things I noticed was a set of those prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I had already become weirdly fond of. There is something about that small open flame floating in oil that keeps catching my attention. I made a mental note to figure out where to buy them. The ceiling here was especially intricate, covered in mosaic work that pulled my eyes upward and held them there longer than I expected.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. Despite being a Chinese temple in my notes as I first experienced the turn in the street, it is in fact the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur, founded in 1873. Inside photography was not allowed, so I don't have any pictures, which may have helped me pay better attention. The entrance tower rises 22.9 meters and is covered with more than 200 sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings in the traditional Dravidian style of South India. During Thaipusam, it serves as the starting point for the huge pilgrimage procession to Batu Caves. Even without a camera, it was easy to feel the weight of history in the place.

After that we headed toward KLCC mall, which felt like a very modern answer to the morning's incense and mosaics. We stopped in a bookstore, where I found a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. I did the conversion back to USD and it remained, with impressive consistency, far too expensive for me. Also, even if I had lost my mind and bought it, where exactly was I supposed to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers. The contrast was sharp in a way Kuala Lumpur seems to enjoy: glass and steel looming over wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park where children were splashing around. It was less a simple park than a carefully arranged pause in the city, with enough shade and greenery to make the surrounding towers feel slightly less severe.

We took a short train ride to TRX, where there was another mall, larger of course, because apparently the answer to one mall is always a second, bigger mall. Inside we visited the Apple Store and all looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at the Airbnb because the battery had died, but also because Apple Pay does not work there for me, which is mostly what I use it for at home anyway. A watch that can't pay for things starts to feel a bit decorative.

We also got lunch there, and many things were ordered. Three of us shared a bowl of Caesar salad, and the raw vegetables felt genuinely welcome after days of heavier meals. There was also steak and some spaghetti, an only-in-a-mall kind of spread, but at that point full stomachs mattered more than thematic consistency.

Content and tired, we all left and headed home. We did have work tomorrow, after all. The day had covered a lot of ground without ever feeling rushed: coffee, temples, towers, trains, watches, salad. A pretty solid way to spend a Sunday before returning to whatever Monday was planning.
