## Tuesday April 7 - Badges, Blue Screens

```poem
I woke before reason, my brain on cheap patrol
Chased a borrowed laptop through each security hole
One machine died theatrically, in a bright blue loop of spite
Then Malaysia wrapped me in warm night and ended the fight
```

I woke up earlier than I needed to for work, which felt unnecessary but apparently my brain had other plans. I had been supposed to meet someone at the office in the afternoon, but once I was awake I figured I might as well go in that morning and pick up my laptop first. Then I could spend the day setting it up, which always takes longer than anyone admits.

That plan started to unravel as soon as my Grab pulled up outside the office. There was a gate, and inside the gate was a security officer, and the security officer was not interested in letting me in without my badge, which I had not realized was still back in Austin, Texas. Not ideal.

He asked for my badge number, so I stood there digging through far too many Teams conversations on my phone until I found one where I had sent the number to a coworker. I gave it to him, he looked me up in the system, then held up his phone and showed me a picture of myself.

Yeah, that was me. I just had longer hair back then.

Once I had been sufficiently "authenticated," the Grab and I were finally allowed into the super secure secret parking lot, and I was dropped off in front of the building. That only created the next problem, which was getting past the main lobby. The security guards there informed me, with the kind of calm that usually means trouble, that this was going to be a problem. As far as they knew, I could not get a new badge there.

After a lot of back and forth, they eventually landed on a solution. I needed to email the security office, explain the situation, and then they would be able to print me a new badge. Simple, in the way things are only simple after fifteen minutes of confusion.

I knew I needed to get to the fourth floor to pick up the laptop being loaned to me while I was in Malaysia, so I walked up there and got lucky. Someone was sitting outside the secure doors. After a short conversation, she escorted me into the tech office, where I was finally handed a laptop and started the setup process. It was basically everything from the beginning, Windows included, the full experience.

I got tired of sitting in the cold, empty office outside the tech area, so at the first chance I took my new laptop back to Tamarind Square. I grabbed another Grab, set up camp at my new favorite coffee shop, ordered a delicious iced café latte, and resumed the long crawl of installations, logins, and permissions. Eventually I managed to do some actual work, opening Outlook and Teams and responding to messages. I still could not do any development, not yet anyway, but at least things were moving.

Then the screen turned blue.

There it was, the familiar Windows announcement that everything was broken and it would now reboot. The laptop restarted, then restarted again, then again, and again. Eventually it tried to enter some kind of self-diagnosing repair utility. Fine, I thought, go for it. It claimed to fix the problem, then promptly rebooted again and again. There was clearly something very wrong with this laptop.

So now I had to go back to the office and get another one.

By then it was early afternoon, and the person I had originally planned to meet was already there. After another round through the super secure parking lot gate and the super secure lobby, I met her, and together we went back up to the tech office, where I swapped my new but very broken laptop for another new laptop. At that point I was mostly hoping the universe would stop being funny and give me one that actually worked.

My new friend from work took the chance to give me a full tour of the building, all four floors of it. Here was the cafeteria. Here were the elevators at both ends, and the elevator in the middle. Here were the bathrooms, with the useful warning that the male and female ones switched sides depending on which side of the building you were on, and that I did not want to accidentally walk into the wrong one, which had apparently happened before. It was exactly the sort of practical detail that matters more than any polished corporate orientation.

She also showed me where the team worked, and where I could set up my laptop that day and for the next four weeks, which was how long I would be working there.

Soon it was time to leave. I took another Grab back to Tamarind Square and did a little shopping at Village Grocer, yogurt, apples, and bananas, the kind of plain food that feels right at the end of an administratively absurd day. I carried it back to my apartment under the dark sky, walking through the warm, heavy Malaysian air, thick with that human nighttime feeling cities have when everyone has not quite settled down yet.

I ate, and I was tired.

Very quickly, the urge to sleep beat out the urge to do anything else.
