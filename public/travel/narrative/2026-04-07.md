## Tuesday April 7 - Badges, Blues, and Reboots

```poem
I woke before reason, thanks brain, what a gift
Then chased down a laptop through layers of bureaucratic drift
It blue-screened with gusto, a corporate mechanical flop
By night I had yogurt, warm air, and the strength to stop
```

I woke up earlier than I needed to for work, which felt unnecessary even at the time. Stupid brain. Since I was already awake, I reconsidered the day’s plan. I had been supposed to meet someone at work in the afternoon, but it occurred to me that I might as well go in that morning and pick up my laptop first. That would leave the rest of the day for setup, which always takes longer than anyone admits.

The first obstacle appeared before I even got properly started. My Grab pulled up outside the office and stopped at a gate, the kind that suggests serious operations are happening beyond it, or at least that someone wants the place to seem that way. Inside the gate sat a security officer, and he would not let me in without my badge, which I had not realized was still back in Austin, Texas.

So there I was, outside the gate, searching through an absurd number of Teams conversations on my phone, trying to find the one message where I had once sent my badge number to a coworker. Eventually I found it. I gave the number to the guard, he looked me up in the system, then showed me a picture of myself on his phone.

Yes, that’s me, I told him. I just had longer hair back then.

That was apparently sufficient. Now that I was "authenticated," both my Grab and I were allowed into the super secure secret parking lot, and I was dropped off in front of the building. Naturally, getting through the gate did not mean I could get into the building itself. At the main lobby, the security guards informed me that this was going to be a problem. As far as they knew, I could not get a new badge there.

After a lot of back and forth, the answer changed. Oh, wait. I had to email a security office and explain the problem, and then someone would be able to print me a new badge. Simple, in the way things are simple only after twenty minutes of contradictory information.

I knew I needed to get to the fourth floor to pick up the loaner laptop I’d be using while I’m in Malaysia, so I went upstairs and hoped for an opening. Fortunately, someone was sitting outside the secure doors. After a brief conversation, she escorted me into the tech office, where I was finally handed the laptop and began the setup process. It was basically starting from zero, setting up Windows from scratch, then everything else on top of that.

After a while I got tired of sitting in the cold, empty office outside the tech room. The place had that over-air-conditioned corporate stillness that makes time feel stalled. At the first decent opportunity, I took the new laptop back to Tamarind Square. Another Grab, and then I set up camp at my new favorite coffee shop to continue the installations and, ideally, do some actual work.

With another excellent iced café latte in front of me, things briefly felt under control. I kept installing software and was at least able to do the basic corporate rituals, opening Outlook and Teams, responding to messages, proving digitally that I existed. I still could not do any development, not yet anyway, but it felt good to be inching toward usefulness.

Then the screen turned blue.

Not metaphorically. The full Windows *something has gone terribly wrong* blue. The laptop announced that it would reboot, which it did, and then did again, and again, and again. Eventually it tried to launch some self-diagnosing repair utility. Fine, I thought, go for it. It claimed to have fixed the issue. Then it rebooted again. And again. There was clearly something very wrong with this laptop.

So now I had to go back to the office and get another one.

By then it was early afternoon, and the person I had originally planned to meet was there. After another trip through the super secure parking gate and the super secure lobby, I finally met her, and together we went back up to Texas Central, where I turned in my very new and already very broken laptop and got another new one. I hoped, not for the first time, that the universe might allow my employer to provide me with a laptop that was not broken straight out of the box.

My new friend from work used the moment to give me a full tour of the building, all four floors. Here was the cafeteria. Here were the elevators at both ends. Here was the elevator in the middle. Here were the bathrooms, but watch out, because the men’s and women’s switched sides depending on which side of the building you were on, and apparently people had, on occasion, walked into the wrong one. Useful information, delivered with the tone of someone who had seen things.

She also showed me where the team works and pointed out a place where I could set up my laptop, both for that day and for the next four weeks, which is how long I’ll be working there.

Soon it was time to leave. I took another Grab back to Tamarind Square, stopped at Village Grocer, and bought simple food, yogurt, apples, bananas. Nothing ambitious. I carried it back to my apartment through the dark, under a night sky that did nothing to reduce the warmth. The air felt heavy and human, the kind that wraps around you instead of moving past you. I ate, and by then I was tired enough that sleep defeated every other possible plan without much resistance.
