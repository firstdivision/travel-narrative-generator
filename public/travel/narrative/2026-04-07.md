## Sunday, April 7 - Butter Flights and Berets

```poem
Too early for taxis, too late to feel slick
I dressed for control, then forgot every trick
A guard made me legal, a driver made me sprint
By night I was couch-folded, asleep in a squint
```

It turned out I didn't need a cab that early after all.

Haneda was nearly empty, security so quiet that I should have glided through it. I did not. For reasons that still do not flatter me, I somehow decided I did not need to remove my watch, wallet, or belt. So instead of the smooth airport performance I had apparently imagined for myself, I got to do the familiar little routine of apologetic incompetence while they re-scanned my things and I walked back through like a man publicly learning basic travel procedure.

After that, I spent some time in the duty free fragrance and skin-care shop, texting Kate as my remote consultant. We considered options, weighed the prices, and arrived at the very sensible conclusion that all of it was still too expensive and not worth it. A victory for restraint, if not for glamour.

Boarding started on time. They announced there would be no wifi, which felt faintly medieval, but the flight itself was smooth in a way that made the comparison unavoidable. Compared to the American Airlines flight, this one was butter. The food was also good, actually memorable good, noodles with some kind of sauce, then chicken curry with rice. See, AA? I remembered this meal because it was noteworthy. Maybe *you* should take some *notes*, American Airlines.

With no wifi to distract me, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and started browsing the movie options. I found two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, which felt like an unexpectedly decent way to keep nudging myself further into the region instead of retreating into something familiar. It seemed right to stay with the geography, at least emotionally, while my body kept crossing it.

Landing was less serene. I had to run for the next flight, which involved a shuttle to a different terminal and then another security check right at the gate. The gate area itself was sealed behind glass walls, self-contained in a way I had not been expecting. By then I was sweaty from hurrying and mildly disoriented, but everything worked out, and before long I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.

Kuala Lumpur airport struck me immediately as competent. Clean, plenty of signs, mostly clear. I did manage to follow one sign toward "e-rides" only to discover that it was not, in fact, the right one. Fortunately the Grab app had built-in airport navigation and guided me down two escalators to the correct pickup spot, which felt oddly intimate, being led through a foreign airport by my phone.

At a certain point I reverted to one of my standard travel methods, the same one I had used walking through Tokyo in the rain. Do what others do. Find the people who look confident and copy them. Or, in plainer terms, fake it till you make it.

The pickup area had a board of monitors divided into sections A and B, green and red. I stood there watching it long enough to realize the numbers on the screen were not some bizarre random-letter bingo system. They were license plates for the e-ride cars entering the pickup area. New arrivals appeared on the right and drifted left until they disappeared, presumably with passengers now inside. Once I understood the system, it seemed obvious. Before that, it looked like an administrative prank.

So I watched the board and the little car icon in the Grab app until my moment arrived. The car was here. I stepped out into a sea of people and was immediately disoriented, trying to locate both the right pickup area and my car, which was supposed to be red. Then I saw the plate number. It matched. I waved. The driver saw me and waved back, but not in a friendly, conversational way. His wave meant: *Get in here now*. Then he said it out loud.

"Hurry. Get in."

My first experience with Grab no-wait zones. They are not kidding. Be ready to get in.

Then came that particular feeling I always like at the start of a new country, wide-eyed and slightly dazed, staring out the window as if every overpass and roadside sign might explain something important. There was traffic, and the driver threaded through it with real confidence, weaving in and out of lanes to get me there faster. Or possibly for his own amusement. In any case, not long after that I was dropped off at what would be my home for the next five weeks, Tower #2 of Eclipse @ Pan'gaea.

And immediately I was thrown into the next adjustment.

There were guards at the building. Not only at the main gate, but also in the car drop-off area, basically the main lobby. I would learn quickly that this was completely normal in Malaysia. The same guards, in the same uniforms with berets on their heads, were in grocery stores, malls, seemingly everywhere. But when you are not expecting it, the first encounter has a faintly startling quality, like you have arrived in a place with rules everyone else already understands.

My Airbnb host's instructions said to show a QR code to the guard. So I did, and he seemed to have no idea why I was showing him a QR code. Instead he produced a logbook and had me fill out my details. Name, phone number, passport number. I paused at passport number, then filled it in anyway. Date, signature. When I handed the pen back, he smiled and said something that sounded like, "now you are new man." At least that is what I heard. Over the next couple of days I would decide, perhaps unilaterally, that some kind of bond had formed between us. Later he would come over and ask about my work.

"Computers," I would say.

"Ahh, hardware?"

"No, software actually."

He would nod, satisfied.

For now, he let me into the elevator lobby behind the key-carded doors and pointed me toward the mailroom. Right, the keys. I had nearly forgotten. They were in the mailbox, which I opened with the code I had been given.

I took the elevator to 19, made two right-hand turns, and stood in front of the double doors that marked the entrance to my new place. The outer door was more like a gate, and then a couple of feet behind it, across a little landing, was the main door. I worked out which keys opened which locks and let myself in.

You never really know what to expect with an Airbnb. They are often under-decorated in a generic, bare-essentials kind of way. Or the Bare Necessities, if we are borrowing from a certain animated movie. This one was even more spartan than I had expected. A dual washer-dryer next to a medium-sized fridge. Plain white countertops. A two-burner glass-top electric stove, complete with the usual scorch marks and scratches that seem to appear on all glass cooktops once they pass a certain age. A microwave. A small utilitarian table with four mismatched chairs. A similarly simple living room. Two bedrooms, two bathrooms. To my American preconceptions, it looked barren at first. So bare, in fact, that I texted a few messages to my future travel companions warning them about the state of it.

But even then I could feel that first judgment beginning to loosen. Over the coming days and weeks I would come to like the simplicity. I would buy a few things I thought it needed, especially the all-important comfy couch blanket so I could burrito myself after work, and some hand towels for the bathrooms. I would even put a little elbow grease into some shower grout. In the end, I would dig it. Or at least I would eventually.

I texted the host to let him know I was in and asked where I might get a few basic supplies for the night, things like water and orange juice. I also mentioned that there was a bag of garbage left behind by the cleaning crew, presumably. He replied immediately and said he would come pick it up and bring me supplies. Soon he arrived with a case of bottled water and two things of orange juice. I thanked him. He responded with a quick Malaysian-style bow, hands together, and then I was on my own again.

Not long after that, I fell asleep on the couch under one of the blankets from the bedroom.
