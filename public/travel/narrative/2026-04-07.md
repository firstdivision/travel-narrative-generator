## Sunday, April 7 - Butter Smooth, Gate Sweat

```poem
I hired dawn panic for a trip I did not need
Then fumbled through security at a slower, clanking speed
One flight served curry, films, and competence in the air
The next began with a sprint and sweat trapped behind glass somewhere
```

It turned out I did not need a cab nearly that early.

At Haneda, security was basically empty, and I got through fast, or I would have if I had remembered how airport security works. For some reason I seemed to think I was exempt from removing my watch, wallet, and belt. I was not. So there was a brief, familiar ritual of standing there feeling like a person who had absolutely no business being surprised by this, apologizing silently while they rescanned everything and I walked back through with less metal attached to my body.

With time to kill after that, I drifted into the duty-free fragrance and skin-care shop and texted Kate for live guidance. It became a long-distance consultation conducted under bright retail lighting, with me hovering over expensive bottles and sending updates like I was reporting from the front. In the end, we landed on the obvious conclusion, it was still too expensive and not worth it.

Boarding started on time. They announced there would be no wifi, which felt mildly bleak for a few seconds, but the flight itself was so smooth that I stopped caring. Compared to the American Airlines flight, this one felt like butter. Calm, competent, almost suspiciously easy.

The food was good too, actually memorable, which is more than I can say for most plane meals. There were noodles with some sort of sauce, and a chicken curry with rice. I remember them because they were noteworthy, and that alone felt like a small indictment of American Airlines. See, AA? I remembered this meal because it gave me something to remember. Maybe you should take some notes.

With no wifi, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and scrolled through the movie options. I found two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, and watched them as the hours passed. It felt like a decent way to stay mentally inside the region instead of floating in that sealed-off in-between space that long flights create, where you are nowhere in particular and somehow still expected to choose between six kinds of action movie.

Landing did not lead to any graceful transition. I had to run for the next flight, which involved a shuttle to a different terminal and then another security check right at the gate. The whole gate area was self-contained behind glass walls, something I had not expected at all. By then I was sweaty from the run and moving with the kind of focused airport urgency that makes everyone look slightly annoyed and faintly unreal. Still, all was well. A little scrambled, a little damp, but soon enough I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.
