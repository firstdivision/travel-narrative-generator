## Sunday, April 7 - Running on Arrival

```poem
I left too early, naturally, for an airport in no mood to fight
Lost to my own belt and watch in security's fluorescent light
Then chased three flights, one Grab, and a guard who made me sign my fate
By night I owned a blanket fort, and Malaysia could kindly wait
```

I found out almost immediately that I had not, in fact, needed a cab quite that early.

Haneda was nearly empty, security quiet enough that I should have sailed through it without incident. I did not. For reasons I still cannot fully defend, I went in apparently believing my watch, wallet, and belt could remain on my person without consequence. So instead of a smooth pass, I got to perform the familiar little ritual of, *sorry, I'm an idiot*, collecting myself, removing the obvious metal objects, and walking back through while they rescanned everything.

After that I killed some time in the duty free fragrance and skin-care shop, texting Kate like she was remote support for luxury indecision. We consulted, compared, reconsidered, and reached the sensible conclusion that it was still too expensive and not worth it.

Boarding started on time. Then came the announcement that there would be no wifi, which felt primitive for about ten seconds, until the plane itself proved so much better than the American Airlines flight that comparison became unavoidable. This one was smooth as butter.

The food helped too. Noodles with some kind of sauce, then a chicken curry with rice, both actually good, and memorable in a way airplane food rarely is. See, AA? I remembered this meal because it was noteworthy. Maybe *you* should take some *notes*, American Airlines.

With no wifi to distract me, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and went looking through the in-flight options. I found two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, and watched them as a way of staying mentally pointed in the right direction, farther into Asia rather than back out of it. It felt like an oddly fitting in-between activity, sitting in a sealed tube between countries, watching stories rooted in a region I was only just beginning to move through.

Landing did not lead to any graceful transfer. I had to run for the next flight, including a shuttle to a different terminal and another security check right at the gate. The gate area was sealed off behind glass walls, self-contained in a way I had not expected, and by the time I got there I was sweaty and trying not to look as rushed as I felt. Still, all was fine. Soon enough I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.

Kuala Lumpur airport struck me as immediately competent. Clean, well-signed, orderly, though not so orderly that I didn't still manage to follow a sign to "e-rides" and end up in the wrong place. Fortunately Grab had built-in airport navigation, which felt almost absurdly helpful. The app calmly walked me down two escalators and to the correct pickup area, as if it had done this exact rescue a thousand times before.

My method, as usual in places where I am not entirely sure what I am doing, was to do what others did and assume they knew more than I did. Find the confident people and copy them. Fake it till you make it, but with luggage.

At the pickup area there was a bank of monitors split into A and B, green and red, and for a while I stared at it as if it were some kind of deranged airport bingo system. Eventually I understood that the numbers were license plates for the e-ride cars entering the zone. New arrivals appeared on the right, then drifted left until they vanished, presumably because they had collected their passengers and escaped.

So I watched the board and the little car icon on the Grab app, back and forth, until my moment came.

The car had arrived. I stepped out into a sea of people and was instantly disoriented, trying to find the correct pickup area and the red car that was supposed to be mine. Then I spotted the plate number. It matched. I waved. The driver saw me and waved back, though not in any warm human sense. His wave meant, very clearly, *get in here now*, and then he said exactly that. "Hurry. Get in."

My first experience with Grab "no wait" zones. They are not kidding. You should already be halfway into the vehicle emotionally before the car even stops.

Then it was new-country time, that wide-eyed phase where every view out the window feels charged simply because it is not yet familiar. There was traffic, of course, and the driver worked through it with aggressive confidence, weaving in and out of lanes to get me there faster. Or possibly for his own entertainment. Either way, before long I was dropped at what would be home for the next five weeks, Tower #2 of **Eclipse @ Pan'gaea**.

And immediately I was thrown into the next system.

There were guards at the building. Not just at the main gate, but in the car drop-off lobby too. I would learn quickly that this was normal in Malaysia. The same kinds of guards, same uniforms, berets and all, turned up in grocery stores, malls, seemingly everywhere. But the first time you encounter it, it has a slightly startling quality, like you have entered a place with rules everyone else already understands.

My Airbnb instructions said to show a QR code to the guard. So I did, and he seemed to have no idea why I was showing him a QR code. Instead he produced a logbook and had me fill in my details. Name, phone number, passport number. That one gave me pause. I stared at the line for a second, then wrote it anyway. Date. Signature. When I handed the pen back, he smiled and said something that sounded to me like, "now you are new man."

That was what I heard, anyway.

Over the next couple of days I would start to feel, at least in my head, that some small bond had formed between us. Later he would come over and ask about my work. "Computers," I would say. "Ahh, hardware?" he would ask. "No, software actually." He would nod, satisfied.

For now, he let me through the key-carded doors into the elevator lobby and pointed me toward the mailroom. Right, the keys. I had nearly forgotten that part. They were in the mailbox, exactly where the host had said, and the provided code opened it without drama.

Up to 19. Two right turns. Then I was standing in front of the double-door entrance to my new place. The outer door was more like a gate, and two feet behind it, across a little landing, was the main door. I worked out which key did what and let myself in.

Airbnbs are almost always under-decorated in one way or another, committed to the bare essentials and not much beyond that. This one was more spartan than I had expected. There was a combined washer-dryer next to a medium fridge, plain white countertops, a two-burner glass-top electric stove complete with the usual scorch marks and scratches that seem to appear on every glass cooktop after a certain age, and a microwave. To my American preconceptions it looked barren at first. A small utilitarian table, four mismatched chairs, a similarly simple living room, two bedrooms, two bathrooms.

It felt sparse enough that I texted a few warnings to my future travel companions about the state of the place.

Still, even then I could sense it might grow on me, and over the coming days and weeks it did. I would end up buying a few things it seemed to need, especially the all-important comfy couch blanket so I could burrito myself after work, plus hand towels for the bathrooms. I would also eventually put some elbow grease into the shower grout. But even on first inspection, with all its plainness, I could tell there was something workable about it.

I texted the host to say I was in and asked where I might get a few basic supplies for the night, water and orange juice, and also mentioned that there was a bag of garbage left behind, presumably by the cleaning crew. He replied immediately and said he would come pick it up and bring me some supplies.

Soon he arrived with a case of bottled water and two things of orange juice. I thanked him. He gave a quick Malaysian-style bow with his hands together.

Then I was alone again.

Not for long, though. I took one of the blankets from the bedroom, stretched out on the couch, and fell asleep there.
