## Sunday, April 7 - Butter Flights and Berets

```poem
Too-early taxi, too-empty gate, I fumbled through the scan
No wifi, good curry, and films to help revise my Asia plan
A Grab man yelled "Hurry!" while traffic braided lane by lane
Then I met my spartan flat, and fell asleep without complaint
```

It turned out I had not needed a cab that early after all. Haneda security was nearly empty, and I should have breezed through it, except for the part where I forgot, somehow, that watches, wallets, and belts do in fact count as things you remove before going through security. So there was a brief little performance of *sorry, I am clearly an idiot*, while they rescanned my things and I walked back through.

With time to spare, I wandered into the duty free fragrance and skincare shop and texted Kate for guidance like I was consulting a remote command center. We talked through options, prices, whether any of it was actually worth buying. In the end, the answer was no. Still too expensive, still not worth it. The airport got my attention, but not my money.

Boarding started on time. They announced there would be no wifi, which felt mildly primitive, but the flight itself was so smooth that I stopped caring. Compared to the American Airlines flight, this one was butter. The food was actually good, memorable good, which is rare enough on a plane that it deserves to be said plainly: noodles with some kind of sauce, and a chicken curry with rice. I still remember that meal, which is more than I can say for almost anything American Airlines ever handed me in foil.

See, AA? It can be done. Maybe *you* should take some *notes*.

With no wifi to distract me, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and started browsing the movie options. I found two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, and watched them as the hours passed. *Past Lives* had that quiet, melancholy pull of a story about timing, migration, and the versions of ourselves that keep existing somewhere else even after we leave. *People and Meat* was rougher, stranger, more grounded in ordinary life and appetite, the body and its habits, the things people consume and the ways they live around one another. Watching both somewhere between countries felt like a decent way to keep leaning into Asia rather than just passing over it, to sit still for once and let a place's sensibilities come through a screen instead of a checklist.

When we landed, the calm ended abruptly. I had to run for the next flight, which involved a shuttle to a different terminal and then another security screening right at the gate. The gate area itself was sealed behind glass walls, self-contained and vaguely futuristic in the stressful way airports sometimes specialize in. I had not expected any of this. By the time I got through, I was sweaty from the run and feeling vaguely hunted by the clock, but all was fine. Soon enough I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.

Kuala Lumpur airport was nice in the practical way that matters when you have just arrived tired and slightly overstimulated. It was clean, and there were plenty of signs, although one of them sent me toward "e-rides" only for me to discover it was not the right one. The Grab app, however, turned out to be more useful than the airport signage. It had built-in navigation and guided me down two escalators and to the correct pickup area like a tiny, efficient sherpa in my pocket.

My strategy was the same one I had used walking through rainy Tokyo. Do what others do. Find the people who look like they know exactly where they are going, and copy them. Fake it till you make it, except with luggage.

At the pickup area there was a bank of monitors split into sections, A and B, green and red. At first I stared at it as if I were watching some bizarre airport bingo game involving random letters and numbers. Then, after a while, it clicked. Those were license plates. The screens were showing the e-ride cars that had entered the pickup area, new ones appearing on the right and drifting left until they vanished, presumably because they had acquired passengers and escaped.

So I watched the monitor, and I watched the little car icon in the Grab app, and eventually the moment came. My car was here. I stepped out into a sea of people and immediately lost my bearings, which felt right. I searched for the correct pickup area and for the car, which was supposed to be red. Then I saw the plate number. It matched. I waved. The driver saw me and waved back, but not in any warm, social sense. His wave clearly meant, *get in here now*. Then he said it out loud.

"Hurry. Get in."

My first encounter with a Grab no-wait zone. They were not kidding. You needed to be emotionally and physically prepared to enter a moving system with conviction.

Then came that familiar and useful feeling, new country time. I sat wide-eyed in the back seat looking out the window at everything, trying to absorb a place in one pass even though that is impossible. There was traffic. The driver wove through lanes with enough confidence that I could not tell whether he was optimizing the route or entertaining himself. Either way, he got me where I needed to go, and soon I was being dropped off at what would be home for the next five weeks, Tower #2 of **Eclipse @ Pan'gaea**.

And immediately, another adjustment.

There were guards at the building. Not just at the main gate, but in the car drop-off lobby too. I would learn quickly that this was completely normal in Malaysia, the same uniforms, the same berets, the same watchful presence in grocery stores, malls, seemingly everywhere. But when you were not expecting it, it was a little jarring.

The Airbnb instructions had told me to show a QR code to the guard, so I did. He seemed to have no idea why I was showing him a QR code. Instead he handed me a logbook and asked me to fill in my details. Name, phone number, passport number. I paused at that one, just long enough to register my discomfort, then filled it out anyway. Date. Signature. I handed back the pen, and he smiled and said something that sounded like, "now you are new man." That is at least what I heard, and I decided it was good enough.

He let me into the elevator lobby behind the key-carded doors and pointed me toward the mailroom. Right, the keys. They were in the mailbox, exactly where the host had said they would be, and the code worked.

I took the elevator to 19, made two right-hand turns, and stood in front of the double-door entrance to my new place. The outer door was more like a gate, and then a couple of feet behind it, with a small landing in between, was the main door. I sorted out which key opened which lock and went in.

You never really know what you are getting with an Airbnb. They are often under-decorated in that generic, *bare essentials* way, and this one was a little more spartan than I had expected. The dual washer-dryer sat next to a medium-sized fridge. The countertops were plain white. There was a two-burner glass-top electric stove, complete with the scorch marks and scratches that seem to be the natural fate of all glass cooktops after a certain age. A microwave. A small practical table with four mismatched chairs. A similarly simple living room. Two bedrooms. Two bathrooms.

My first, very American reaction was that it looked barren.

So barren, in fact, that I texted a few warnings to the travel companions who would be joining me later. But even in that first look I could sense that my reaction had more to do with my own expectations than with the apartment itself. Over the coming days and weeks I would come to like the simplicity. I would add the things I felt it needed, the all-important comfy couch blanket so I could burrito myself after work, some hand towels for the bathrooms, a little elbow grease on the shower grout. But even then, on day one, there was something workable about it. Not charming exactly, not yet. Just honest.

I texted the host to let him know I was in and asked where I could get some basic supplies for the night, water and orange juice, and also mentioned that there was a bag of garbage left behind, presumably by the cleaning crew. He replied immediately, said he would come pick it up and bring me supplies. And he did. Soon he was at the door with a case of bottled water and two things of orange juice. I thanked him, we exchanged a quick Malaysian-style bow with hands together, and then I was alone.

Not long after that, I fell asleep on the couch under one of the bedroom blankets.
