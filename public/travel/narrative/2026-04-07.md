## Sunday, April 7 - Butter, Sweat, Bingo

```poem
I beat the dawn for a flight I did not need to chase
Then undressed for security with embarrassed grace
I learned that airport numbers were not a gambling show
And ran myself half-drenched just to board a very short hop below
```

It turned out I did not need a cab *that* early after all. Haneda security was almost empty, the kind of empty that makes you feel briefly efficient and clever, right up until you do something dumb. I did not think I needed to remove my watch, wallet, or belt, so instead of gliding through, I got to do the quiet little ritual of, sorry, I’m an idiot, while they rescanned everything and I walked back through properly. Not a disaster, just a small self-inflicted delay at an hour when my brain was apparently still off-duty.

With time to spare, I drifted into the duty free fragrance and skin-care shop and texted Kate like she was a remote consultant on retainer. I sent photos, asked questions, waited for judgment. After some back and forth, we arrived at the sensible conclusion that it was still too expensive and not worth it. International travel does occasionally offer glamour, but mostly it offers the chance to overpay for moisturizer under very bright lights.

Boarding started on time, and they announced there would be no wifi. Normally that would feel like a minor betrayal, but the flight itself was so much smoother than the one on American Airlines that I found myself willing to forgive a lot. Smooth as butter, really. Even the food was good, actually memorable good: noodles with some sort of sauce, then chicken curry with rice. I remembered the meal because it was noteworthy, which is more than I can say for most airline food. See, AA? Maybe *you* should take some *notes*, American Airlines.

With no wifi to distract me, I plugged the provided wired earbuds into the seatback and started scrolling through the movie options. I landed on two Korea-based films, *Past Lives* and *People and Meat*, which felt like a fitting way to stay mentally in the region even while suspended above it. It was a strange modern kind of travel, sealed in a metal tube, eating curry, watching stories from nearby countries, trying to convince myself that this somehow counted as a deeper continuity and not just airline entertainment with better thematic alignment.

Landing did not bring much time to reflect. I had to run for the next flight, which involved a shuttle to a different terminal and then another security check right at the gate. The gate area itself was sealed behind glass walls, self-contained in a way I had not expected. I arrived sweaty from the run and mildly rattled, the familiar airport feeling of having missed some important memo that everyone else received. But all was well. Soon I was in my seat for the very short flight to KL.

Kuala Lumpur airport struck me immediately as good in the ways that matter when you are tired. Clean, clear, plenty of signs. Even so, I managed to follow one sign to “e-rides” only to discover it was not the *right* one, which felt predictable. Fortunately, the Grab app had built-in airport navigation and guided me with surprising competence, down two escalators and to the correct pickup spot. I trusted it more than I trusted myself, which was fair.

By then I was back to one of my favorite travel techniques, the same one I had used walking through Tokyo in the rain: do what others do. Find the people who look confident and copy them. Assume they know what *they’re* doing, and borrow that certainty until your own catches up. Fake it till you make it, except with more luggage and worse posture.

At the pickup area there was a board of monitors split into sections A and B, green and red, and for a while I stared at it as if it were some bizarre random-letter bingo game. Only after watching it for a bit did I realize the numbers were license plates for the e-ride cars entering the pickup area. New arrivals appeared on the right, then drifted left until they disappeared, presumably with passengers inside. Once I understood it, it seemed obvious. Before that, it just looked like the airport was running a small, opaque lottery for exhausted travelers.
