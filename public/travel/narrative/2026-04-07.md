## Tuesday April 7 - Badges, Blue Screens

```poem
I woke before reason, because brains like to cheat
Chased one borrowed laptop through layers of heat
Security loved numbers more than my face or my plea
Then Windows performed its small tragedy for me
```

I woke up earlier than I needed to for work, which felt unnecessary in the specific, irritating way only my own brain can manage. I had been supposed to meet someone at work in the afternoon, but once I was awake and functional enough to think, it occurred to me that I might as well go pick up my laptop in the morning and spend the day setting it up. That process always takes a while, and there was no point pretending otherwise.

Unfortunately, the day immediately became about access.

My Grab pulled up outside the office, where there was a gate, and inside the gate there was a security officer, and the security officer was not interested in letting me in without my badge. This would have been a reasonable policy if my badge had not still been in Austin, Texas, which was not especially useful to either of us.

He asked for my badge number instead. So I stood there scrolling through far too many Teams conversations on my phone, the modern administrative archive of every bad decision, until I found a message where I had once sent my badge number to a coworker. I gave it to the guy at the gate. He looked me up in the system, then showed me a picture of myself on his phone to confirm I was, in fact, me. I told him yes, that was me, I just had longer hair back then.

That was apparently enough. Once I was officially authenticated, the Grab and I were allowed into the super secure secret parking lot, and I was dropped in front of the building.

Naturally, getting through the gate did not mean I could get into the building.

At the main lobby, the security guards informed me, with the sort of calm that suggests they had seen this movie before, that this was going to be a problem. As far as they knew, I could not get a new badge there. After more back and forth, they finally landed on a solution: I needed to email some security office, explain the problem, and then they would be able to print me a new badge. It all had the usual corporate feeling of a system designed by several different people who had never met.

I knew I needed to get to the fourth floor to pick up the loaner laptop I would be using while I was in Malaysia, so I went upstairs and hoped for improvisation. Thankfully, someone was sitting outside the secure doors. After a short conversation, she was willing to escort me into the tech office, where I was finally handed the laptop and could begin the long, dull ritual of setting it up. It was essentially a fresh Windows setup, which is to say, everything.

I got tired of sitting in the cold, empty office outside the tech area. It had that drained corporate chill that makes you feel like a forgotten contractor in a science fiction film. So at the first opportunity I took the new laptop back to Tamron Square. I grabbed another Grab, set up camp at my new favorite coffee shop, and kept going there instead.

With another good iced café latte in front of me, I resumed the software installation process and managed to do a little real work. I opened Outlook, opened Teams, started responding to messages. I still could not do any development, not yet anyway, but at least things were moving in the right direction. Or seemed to be.

Then the screen turned blue.

There it was, the classic Windows announcement that everything was broken and it was going to reboot now. The laptop restarted, then restarted again, then again. It eventually tried to launch some self-diagnosing repair utility, and at that point I was willing to let it believe in itself. It claimed it had fixed the issue. Then it rebooted again. And again.

There was clearly something very wrong with this laptop.

Which meant I now had to go back to the office and get another one.
