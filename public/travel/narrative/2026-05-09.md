## Saturday, May 9 - Gates, Boats, and One Unimpressed Snake

```poem
We chased a gate that wasn’t boarding at all
And sacrificed hot tea to airport protocol
By sunset I was wave-drunk, sun-soft, and awake
Then came home to share the patio with a snake
```

We woke up and our Grab was already waiting for us. It was time to leave Malaysia and start the next leg, to Thailand this time. We were headed for Koh Lanta, an island tucked down at the end of a peninsula in Krabi province along Thailand’s southwestern coast on the Andaman Sea.

That sea had been sitting in my head as both geography and promise. Thailand to the east, Myanmar to the north, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands of India to the west, the Strait of Malacca to the south. One of those sea lanes that quietly runs the planet while most of us are busy misreading airport signs. To get from the Andaman Sea into the broader Indian Ocean, you just keep going west or southwest past the Andaman and Nicobar chain. No dramatic border, no curtain lift, just one body of water easing into another. But at that moment it was less a lesson than a direction. It was simply where we were going.

Our Grab driver, on the way to the airport, told us it would probably be raining in Thailand. Thanks, dude.

Airport security was easy enough, except for the part where I forgot there was a second, actual security check before the gate. This meant that instead of doing the obvious thing, which is usually rule number one of flying and heading straight to the gate first, I confidently led everyone into another part of the airport while declaring that there was not much available down by our gate.

This is the kind of confidence that travel rewards only when it feels like being generous.

We did find a good place to eat, and they ordered Teh Halia. It arrived hot enough to fog your glasses the second it touched the table, sweet and spicy and creamy in a way that felt oddly medicinal, but in a comforting way. *Teh* is tea, *halia* is ginger in Malay, so yes, ginger tea, but in practice something stronger and softer at the same time, black tea pulled together with fresh ginger, milk, and enough sugar to round off the sharpness.

Then while ordering, we looked up and saw the board for our flight flashing **NOW BOARDING**.

Our flight was not due to leave for another three hours.

So we entered a slight emergency mode, that specific airport state where you are fairly sure the sign is wrong, very sure in fact, but also aware that airports operate on their own chaotic theology and maybe this is how you get stranded.

We hurried over to the entrance to concourse L, where our gate was, and there it was: the second security line I had forgotten about. Which meant the drinks had to go.

“Andrew... you said there was nothing down here. Now we have these teas that we will need to pour out.”

I chugged my latte. They tried to do the same with their teas and mostly just scorched their mouths. A solid little victory for nobody.

Once we got through security, minus the drinks, I was mostly right. There was not much there. A Burger King and an OldTown White Coffee.

OldTown White Coffee makes immediate sense in Malaysia for the same reason diners or Waffle House or Tim Hortons make sense elsewhere. It is familiar, reliable, affordable, and everywhere. But there is also the deeper Malaysia-specific thing underneath it. OldTown built itself around Ipoh white coffee, one of the country’s signature styles, traditionally roasted with margarine or palm oil, brewed strong, then mixed with condensed milk. Smoother, sweeter, less bitter, very easy to drink in tropical heat. It takes something nostalgic and local and turns it into a chain without entirely sanding the feeling off it.

In any case, the flight was not boarding. Of course it wasn’t. So we waited the customary amount of time before we actually boarded and then got on the plane and headed to Thailand.

Customs and immigration in Thailand were easy-peasy. No line at all. There are, apparently, occasional benefits to being near the front of the plane.

The driver from Pimalai Resort was there to meet us, and once we got into the van, the day changed shape. Anyone who has spent time on the water knows that feeling when a car starts heading toward the coast. You come over a rise and there it is, the road dropping downhill toward salt water, maybe narrowing, with random cars parked along the side, small boats on trailers half in the bushes, a ferry terminal appearing like a practical little promise. Something in me always relaxes at that sight. Salt water in the blood is probably an affliction, but I have never wanted curing.

As the scene opened up in front of us, we all said versions of the same thing.

Then the van turned off before the ferry road and dropped us at a comfortable gazebo by the water. A long pier reached out toward a floating concrete dock, and all of us were immediately drawn toward it like dehydrated people toward anything blue.

After a short wait, the boat arrived. It had a Gilligan’s Island sort of look to it, a motor-cruiser converted into a passenger ferry. The ride to the other side took about five minutes total, which felt rude, honestly. We all wished it had taken longer.

Then another van, another forty minutes, and we reached the resort.

It was very, very nice. Hushed tones, warm woods, flowered necklaces placed around our necks, and a cold citrus drink with a straw made from some kind of plant. The ice clinked in the glass while the host settled us into comfortable seats and went over things in that calm resort voice that suggests nothing bad has ever happened anywhere nearby.

We had an elephant trip booked for the next morning, but she told us there was also a boat trip we could join instead if we wanted. We said that sounded nice. She disappeared with that information and came back a few minutes later to say she had managed to move the elephant trip to Monday, and that weather permitting we would be able to do the boat trip as well. A surprisingly efficient rearrangement of our future.

Then we were taken to the room, which was absurdly nice. Two buildings connected by a private infinity pool, with the bedrooms on one side and a common room or living room on the other. The whole thing had that polished quiet that makes you instinctively lower your voice, as if the architecture itself would prefer not to be disturbed.

We went out to get situated and inspect the grounds, but really we were hungry. Especially me, since I had not eaten much. We found one of the restaurants and ordered lunch, and I immediately attacked the bread and butter like I had been wronged personally by the day’s logistics. Then I got into a plate of rice with stir-fried beef and peppers, spicy and savory and exactly what I needed.

The beef came in thin slices, flash-seared in a violently hot wok with onions, chilies, and sweet bell peppers that still kept a little crispness. I ate one of the hot chilies and decided one was plenty. The beef was tender, the rice caught all the salty, peppery juices underneath, and there was also warm bread with butter, sea salt, and a black pepper grinder. It all vanished. I felt significantly more human afterward.

The Pink Gin and Tonic may have helped too. I cannot remember what was in it. Which probably means it was really good.

After that we went to the beach. The water had enough salinity to make floating feel almost suspiciously easy. There were waves too, and not decorative resort waves either, but actually body-surfable ones. A quick stop at the beach cabana got us two boogie boards, and I slowly remembered how they worked. At one point I managed to carve into a wave, but the break was quick and right on the beach, so I turned parallel to shore and rolled the board over the top of me in a kind of 360 barrel-roll move.

I was in heaven.

Not my usual ocean, not my usual coastline, but I did not care. I was back in the water and exactly where I wanted to be.

We had drinks on the beach after that. Buy one get one free. Gin drinks, piña coladas, that kind of late-afternoon generosity that feels almost engineered to make you forget every airport decision that came before it.

When it got dark, we headed back to the room. We ordered a buggy, which is what the resort calls the extra-small lorry-style trucks with rows of seats they use to shuttle people around the property.

And then, because the day had apparently decided to stay interesting, we got back and found a snake on the patio by our pool.

This, I suppose, is the tradeoff in a jungle climate. The wildlife has not agreed to the resort concept. It does not care about room rates or polished wood or private infinity pools. We are in *their* house, really.

The hotel was quick to send someone with a snake hook. I offered to help by lighting the snake with both our phones, and he gladly accepted. A practical little collaboration between hospitality and basic reptile management.

With the patio cleared of wildlife, I took the opportunity to get into our private infinity pool, looking out over the water below. It felt like the proper ending to the daylight part of the day, quiet and warm and slightly surreal.

Later we ordered room service: chicken satay and mango sticky rice.

Then I had a shower with deeply satisfying hot water, an experience one of my fellow travelers did not get to enjoy. Her shower not only ran out of hot water, but the pressure dropped to nearly zero at the worst possible moment, which was when she had shampoo in her hair. Luxury remains committed to occasional acts of sabotage.

One thing we kept noticing was that somehow I got both the responsibility and the credit for everything in the resort. We figured it was because I was a man, even though the credit card used for the room belonged to Kate and she had made the reservation. Still, somehow I was always the one being asked to sign.

By the end of the night my brain had drifted into the sort of thoughts travel seems to shake loose. Aren’t continents really just large islands? Where exactly is the line between an island and a mainland and a continent? And then, for some reason, I remembered that old lesson about breathing, the one about how in every breath you take, you almost certainly inhale at least one molecule that was once breathed by some historical person. Julius Caesar, Shakespeare, Einstein, whoever. It is either comforting or ridiculous. Maybe both.

Anyway, we had made it to Koh Lanta. There had been fake boarding, sacrificed tea, a five-minute ferry ride that should have been longer, a resort arrival in full soft-focus mode, hot chilies, beach drinks, body-surfable waves, and a snake on the patio.

A fairly complete day, really.
