## Saturday, May 9 - Security Theater and Salt Water

```poem
We chased a gate that wasn't going anywhere fast,
Sacrificed our drinks to airport rules at last,
Then five sweet ferry minutes fixed the whole display,
And a snake dropped by the villa to end the day.
```

I woke up and our Grab was already waiting for us. Time to leave Malaysia. Next stop, Thailand.

Somewhere tucked up at the end of a peninsula is an island called Koh Lanta, in Krabi province, along Thailand’s southwestern coast on the Andaman Sea. I found myself thinking about that body of water in the abstract way travel sometimes makes you do, as if geography becomes more real when you are physically moving toward it. The Andaman Sea is bordered by Thailand to the east, Myanmar to the north, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands of India to the west, and the Strait of Malacca to the south. The Strait of Malacca, one of those places that sounds faintly academic until you remember it is one of the most important shipping lanes on Earth, connects the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea and the Pacific. To get from the Andaman Sea into the broader Indian Ocean, you just keep going west or southwest past the Andaman and Nicobar chain. No dramatic line. No grand threshold. It is basically a northeastern arm of the Indian Ocean.

At that point, though, it was less a geographical reality than just "where we are going."

Our Grab driver, on the way to the airport, told us it would probably be raining in Thailand. Thanks, dude.

Airport security was easy, at least at first. The problem was me forgetting there was a second, real security check before the gate. So instead of doing what is usually rule number one, go directly to the gate and figure everything else out later, I confidently led everyone into another area of the airport while declaring that there was not much available down by our gate.

Overconfidence has a very specific tone when it is still technically calm.

We found a nice place to get food, and they ordered Teh Halia. It is the kind of drink that feels medicinal in the best possible way, sweet, spicy, creamy, and hot enough to fog your glasses the second it lands on the table. The name is simple. *Teh* means tea. *Halia* means ginger in Malay. Literally ginger tea, but in practice it is usually strong black tea pulled together with fresh ginger, condensed milk or evaporated milk, and enough sugar to make it comforting instead of sharp.

Then while we were ordering, we saw the signboard for our flight say **NOW BOARDING**.

Our flight did not leave for three hours.

We entered a slight emergency mode. I was fairly sure, very sure in fact, that the flight was not really boarding, but airports occasionally like to test your confidence just to keep things balanced.

We got to the entry to Concourse L, where our gate was, and there was another security line. That was the moment my earlier airport wisdom came back to collect its fee.

"Andrew... you said there was nothing down here. Now we have these teas that we will need to pour out."

I chugged my latte. They tried to do the same with their teas and scorched their mouths, which felt like an unnecessarily punitive consequence for trusting me. Eventually we got through security without their drinks, and I was mostly right. There really was not much there. A Burger King and an OldTown White Coffee restaurant.

OldTown White Coffee made sense to me in the way certain chains do when they have become more than a chain. It is popular in Malaysia for some of the same reasons diners, Waffle House, or Tim Hortons become fixtures elsewhere. Familiar, reliable, affordable, everywhere, and tied to a specific comfort-food identity. But there is also a more Malaysia-specific layer to it. OldTown built itself around Ipoh white coffee, one of Malaysia’s signature coffee styles. Unlike darker roasted Western coffee, the beans are traditionally roasted with margarine or palm oil and brewed strong, then mixed with condensed milk. The result is smoother, sweeter, less bitter, and very easy to drink in tropical heat. Ipoh white coffee became nostalgic because kopitiams, traditional coffee shops, had been social hubs for generations. OldTown basically industrialized and modernized that feeling.

The flight, of course, was not boarding. We waited the customary amount of time before actually boarding, then got on the plane and were finally on our way to Thailand.

Customs and immigration in Thailand were easy-peasy. No line at all. I suppose there are benefits sometimes to being near the front of the plane.

The driver from Pimali Resort was there to meet us, and we got in the van and headed off. Anyone who has spent time on the water knows the specific feeling of being in a car that is headed toward salt water. You come up over a hill and there it is. The road tips downhill toward the water now visible in front of you. It narrows. There are random cars parked along the sides, small boats on trailers, maybe half in the bushes, waiting to be launched again one day. Sometimes there is a ferry terminal waiting to take you somewhere else. It relaxes something in me immediately. If you are unlucky enough, or lucky enough, to have salt water in your blood, it works like a kind of affliction. Not serious enough to cure. Just enough to keep drawing you back.

As we watched this scene develop in front of us from the van, we each said basically the same thing in different ways.

Then the van turned off the ferry road and deposited us into a comfortable gazebo by the water, with a long pier reaching out into it and a floating concrete dock at the end. We were drawn toward it like thirsty desert travelers toward an oasis, except with considerably more luggage and less dignity.

After a short wait, the boat arrived. It looked like a Gilligan's Island-esque motor-cruiser converted into a passenger ferry. The ride to the other side took a grand total of about five minutes. All of us wished it had taken longer.

Then another van ride, about forty minutes more, and we were at the resort.

It was very nice. Hushed tones. Warm woods. Flowered necklaces placed around our necks, then a refreshing citrus-based drink with a straw made from some sort of plant. The ice clinked in the glass while the resort host led us to comfortable seating. We had an elephant trip booked for the next morning, but she told us there was also a boat trip we could join instead, if we wanted. We said that would be nice. A few minutes later she returned and told us she had been able to move the elephant trip to Monday, and that weather permitting, we would be able to do the boat trip as well.

We were taken to the room. It was very, very nice. Two buildings connected by a private infinity pool, with the bedrooms on one side and a common room or living room on the other.

We went out to check the grounds and get situated, but mostly we were hungry. We found one of the restaurants and had a much-needed lunch, especially for me since I had not eaten much. I devoured the bread and butter. Then absolutely crushed the rice and stir-fry beef with peppers. Thin slices of beef flash-seared in a screaming hot wok with onions, chilies, and sweet bell peppers that stayed bright and a little crisp. One of the chilies was hot enough that after eating it I decided one was enough. The beef was tender, everything spilled over fragrant rice, and the salty, peppery juices collected underneath where the rice could soak them up properly.

There was also bread. Warm bread with butter, sea salt, and a black pepper grinder.

It all disappeared, and I felt much better. The Pink Gin and Tonic may have helped too. I cannot remember what was in it. It must have been really good.

After that we went to the beach and swam in the high-salinity water that made floating easy. There were waves, and not just decorative ones either. We discovered they were body-surfable, and a quick stop at the resort beach cabana got us two boogie boards. I slowly remembered how those things work. At one point I was able to actually carve into a wave, but they were breaking quickly and right on the beach, so I turned parallel to shore and rolled the board over the top of me in a kind of 360 barrel-roll move. I was in heaven, back in the water. Not my normal ocean, but I did not care. I was exactly where I wanted to be.

We had drinks on the beach too, buy one get one free, gin drinks and piña coladas, the sort of arrangement that feels suspiciously generous until you remember where you are and decide not to question it.

When it got dark, we headed back to the room. We ordered a buggy, which is what the resort calls the extra-small lorry-style trucks with rows of seats in back that shuttle people around the property.

Back at our rooms, there was a snake on the patio by our pool.

I suppose that is the tradeoff for a jungle climate. The wildlife has not yet been informed that this is a very nice resort built for humans by humans. We are in *their* house, really. The hotel was quick to send someone with a snake hook, and I offered to illuminate the snake with both our phones, which he gladly accepted.

With the patio now temporarily free of wildlife, I took the opportunity to get in our private infinity pool, which looked out over the water below.

Later we ordered room service: chicken satay and mango sticky rice.

Then came the shower, with delicious hot water, an experience one of my cousins did not get to enjoy. Her shower not only ran out of hot water, but the water pressure dropped to near zero at exactly the least opportune time, when her hair was full of shampoo. There are failures of infrastructure, and then there are targeted acts of malice.

For some reason I got both responsibility and credit for everything in the resort. We think because I am a man, even though the credit card used for the room belonged to Kate and she made the reservation. Still, I was the one always being asked to sign for things.

Late in the day, my brain wandered into island logic. Aren't continents really just large islands? Where exactly is the line between island and mainland and continent? Travel tends to produce that sort of thought, somewhere between actual curiosity and low-grade exhaustion.

I also remembered that old school lesson about the sheer number of oxygen molecules in a breath, and the idea that in every breath you take, you almost certainly inhale at least one molecule once breathed by some historical person, Julius Caesar, Albert Einstein, William Shakespeare, whoever you like. One of those facts that is either profound or just what happens when the atmosphere is old and you are tired.

Either way, by the end of the night I had left Malaysia, made it to Thailand, crossed a tiny stretch of water that should have lasted longer, body-surfed in the Andaman Sea, and helped spotlight a snake off a luxury patio. That felt like a full day.
