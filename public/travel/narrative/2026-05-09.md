## Saturday, May 9 - Boarding Panic and Snake Duty

```poem
We left with tea and premature gate alarm,
A boarding sign with all the wrong kind of charm.
By night I held a flashlight for a jungle snake,
Travel remains a series of choices I probably shouldn’t make.
```

We woke up and the Grab was already waiting for us.

It was time to leave Malaysia and start the next leg: Thailand. We were headed for Koh Lanta, an island tucked away at the end of a peninsula in Krabi province, off Thailand’s southwestern coast on the Andaman Sea. On paper, that sounded clean and simple. In my head, it turned briefly geographical. The Andaman Sea sits there as a northeastern arm of the Indian Ocean, bordered by Thailand to the east, Myanmar to the north, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands of India to the west, and the Strait of Malacca to the south. The Strait of Malacca, one of the planet’s busiest shipping routes, connects the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea and the Pacific. If you keep sailing west or southwest past the island chain, there is no dramatic line where one body of water ends and another begins. It just opens out.

At that point, though, all of that was mostly academic. What mattered was that this was where we were going. Our Grab driver, on the way to the airport, offered the useful forecast that it would probably be raining in Thailand. Thanks, dude.

Airport security was easy, which should have been a warning that I was about to create my own problem. I had forgotten there was a second, more real security check before the gate. Instead of following the usual rule of flying, which is get to the gate first and do anything clever later, I confidently led my companions into another part of the airport while announcing that there was not much available down by our gate. This was based on nothing except my own overconfidence, always a reliable travel resource.

Still, the detour did produce food, which is how bad decisions sometimes survive. They ordered Teh Halia, and it arrived with that immediate medicinal comfort certain hot drinks have when you are tired enough to trust them. Sweet, spicy, creamy, and hot enough to fog your glasses as soon as it hit the table. *Teh* means tea. *Halia* means ginger in Malay. Literally ginger tea, but in practice it was stronger and softer than that sounds, black tea pulled together with fresh ginger, milk, and enough sugar to make it soothing instead of severe.

Then, while ordering, we looked up and saw the signboard for our flight: **NOW BOARDING**.

Our flight did not leave for another three hours.

We entered what I can only call slight emergency mode, the kind where nobody is fully panicking yet but everyone starts moving faster and talking in clipped sentences. It felt unfair, honestly. We had barely settled into the illusion that we had time. The airport, apparently, had other ideas.

By the time we reached Koh Lanta, the mood had shifted completely. We were in jungle climate now, in a very nice resort that still had not managed to persuade the surrounding wildlife that humans were in charge. There was a snake on the patio of our pool.

That, apparently, was part of the arrangement.

I guess this is the tradeoff for staying somewhere lush and beautiful and slightly removed. The hotel may have built a lovely resort for humans, but we were still in *their* house, really. The staff was quick about it. Someone arrived with a snake hook, calm and practical, which is exactly the energy you want in a person dealing with a snake near your pool. I offered to help by illuminating the scene with both our phones, and he gladly accepted. It was not a role I had expected to play that day, but there I was: unofficial assistant to snake relocation.

Once the patio was free of wildlife again, I took the opportunity to get into our private infinity pool, which looked out over the landscape beyond. The whole place had that strange resort contrast I never quite get used to, where everything is polished and peaceful until a reptile reminds you what the setting actually is.

Later I had a shower with genuinely delicious hot water, one of those hotel showers that feels less like hygiene and more like emotional repair. One of my fellow travelers was less fortunate. Her shower not only ran out of hot water, but the water pressure dropped to nearly zero at the exact wrong moment, with her hair full of shampoo. Travel can be very glamorous for about six seconds at a time.

There was one more detail about the resort that became obvious almost immediately. I seemed to get credit for everything simply because I was a man, even though the credit card belonged to Kate and she had made the reservation. It was one of those small, annoying pieces of social theater that is too mundane to qualify as shocking and too obvious not to notice. I did not earn the authority. I was just apparently issued it on sight.

So that was the day: leaving Malaysia, a premature boarding scare, ginger tea under fluorescent airport light, then arrival in Thailand by way of a resort patio snake and a pool in the jungle. Not elegant, exactly. But definitely travel.
