## Wednesday, May 6 - Huskies, Heat, and Noodles

```poem
We chased cold dogs through a city of steam
A nervous start turned oddly serene
Then hunger won, as it usually does
And noodles arrived without making a fuss
```

Fresh off the blocks from the Catfe, we decided to go to a place called Huskiss that night, basically the same concept but with husky dogs. I am a dog person. She is, apparently, not so much. In fact, somewhere along the way she informed me that she was afraid of dogs, which felt like useful information to receive while actively en route to a room full of large, enthusiastic huskies.

When we got there, I could see the nerves immediately. She was not exactly thrilled by the sight of these big dogs coming up to say hello with the confidence of animals who know the whole place has been built around their charm. But the dogs did what dogs tend to do. They kept coming over, fluffy and insistent and impossible to take too seriously for long, and soon enough they wore her down. Before long she was smiling, petting the black-and-white huskies, the fear giving way to that slightly disbelieving expression people get when they realize they are actually enjoying the thing they were bracing themselves against.

In a tropical city where the afternoon heat can feel like a wet blanket, Huskiss was an unexpected encounter with the Arctic, or at least the air-conditioned fantasy version of it. For an hour we were surrounded by a pack of impossibly fluffy huskies who seemed equally interested in posing for photos, demanding attention, and sprawling across the floor in search of the next belly rub. It was quirky, a little ridiculous, and hard to leave without smiling. The dogs had that peculiar mix of dignity and total shamelessness that makes them easy company.

But leave we had to. Our hour was up, they were closing, and more importantly, we were hungry.

So it was back to Google Maps, that great modern machine for confidence followed by second-guessing. I found a place called Noodle Art and we headed that way. Like many experiences I would keep having in KL, and in Southeast Asia more generally, I later realized that where there is one restaurant, there are probably many more packed beside it, behind it, upstairs from it, or circling a parking lot nearby. That turned out to be true here too. We made the rounds down the street, then wandered into a parking lot next door where a ring of food trucks surrounded a cluster of tables, all of it humming with options and the mild paralysis that too many options tends to produce when you are already hungry.

In the end we circled back to the original plan and chose Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specialized in the comforting noodle dishes of Sarawak, bringing flavors from East Malaysia into the busy food district of Petaling Jaya. The place felt bright and casual, more neighborhood favorite than trendy hotspot, which was exactly right for the mood. Handmade noodles, Sarawak laksa, kolo mee, and other regional specialties were the center of the menu. It felt like a chance to eat a side of Malaysian cuisine less familiar on the peninsula, simple and satisfying food built around springy noodles, aromatic broths, and recipes tracing back to Kuching and the towns of Sarawak.

After the air-conditioning, the dogs, the indecision, and the slow drift of evening hunger, that kind of meal sounded about as correct as anything could.
