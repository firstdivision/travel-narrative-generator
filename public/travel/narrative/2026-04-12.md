## Sunday, April 12 - Beef Balls and Dumplings

```poem
I slept in late, a scandal by my recent dawn patrol code
Then coded through the daylight, happily off the road
I feared I’d ordered mystery parts in noodle-bowl suspense
But dumplings saved the evening, with greasy common sense
```

I woke up at 7 AM, which counted as *late* for me lately. That word still amused me more than it probably should have. Maybe it meant I was finally adjusting back to something like a normal work and sleep schedule. Yesterday I had apparently gone downtown too early, so today I planned to do the opposite and linger around the apartment, drinking my coffee powder stuff from Village Grocer and doing very little of consequence. The important things.

With not much else pressing, I decided to work on some journal-writing software, in fact the very thing you are probably reading this on right now. That swallowed most of the late morning and afternoon. Software can do that when it is fun. It has the same time-warp effect as playing CIV, where you look up and realize half the day has quietly disappeared while you were happily fiddling with systems and text.

Around 4:30 I took a quick shower and headed down to the main entrance of my apartment building to grab a Grab. By now I knew the routine. Grab to the train, top up my `Touch 'n Go` card because it was running low, take the yellow train to TRX station, change there, then get off at Pasar Seni. At the station, when I went to add money to the card, the man asked how much, and I had one of those brief internal shutdowns where the simplest question suddenly feels unreasonably complex. I blurted out 50. It seemed like a decent number, somewhere between zero and 100, why not. He raised an eyebrow in a way that strongly suggested, *Fifty? Really?* I felt like Captain Kirk in *The Voyage Home*, selling his glasses for $100 with no idea whether he was making a brilliant deal or being robbed in plain sight.

When the train rides were over, I came out at my now-familiar station and made a beeline for the blue pin I had dropped yesterday for the "yellow tents" I'd seen. Only when I got there, it looked nothing like it had the day before. There were a few yellow umbrellas, but not nearly as many. There had been a thunderstorm earlier, and I started wondering if the rain had made everyone pack up and leave, or if this was just one more example of a place changing shape depending on the hour and the weather and some local logic I did not yet understand. This was one of those moments when traveling alone felt slightly limiting. Internet comments and search results can get you to a location, but they do not always give you the actual story.

I searched the area anyway, going up one street and down another, hoping maybe my charcoal chicken strip guy had simply relocated a block away. No luck. Eventually I gave up on that mission and went back to a beef noodle place I had passed while speed-walking toward my original blue pin. I had dropped another blue pin by that restaurant because it had been full of people, and from the sidewalk I could see into the kitchen. What I saw there had looked promising.

I was seated immediately and handed a menu that was refreshingly uncomplicated. There were beef balls, beef, pork, and noodles. You could get the noodles dry or wet. That was basically the whole system. When it was my turn, I ordered beef balls and noodles, dry. I was not even sure why I said *dry*, because in my head I had imagined soup. But I think some YouTube food video I had watched before this trip had planted the idea that dry noodles were the move, so there I was, obediently making a decision I barely understood.

While I waited, I looked back at the menu photo. The meatballs looked a little strange. When the food arrived, the same thought came back immediately. These "meatballs" looked very smooth and oddly walnut-shaped. Suspiciously smooth, really. I found myself wondering what kind of balls these actually were.

I tried one.

The texture was odd, firm and sort of bouncy. I cut one open with my fork and found that it seemed layered somehow, not crumbly the way I expected a meatball to be. At that point I became fully convinced that these were, in fact, *balls*. Well, I had wanted food adventure, and apparently I had gotten it.

Later that night, back at the apartment, I searched for recipes for Chinese beef ball noodle soup and found out they really were meatballs. They are made by blending the beef mixture until it is nearly foamy, then forming it into balls and boiling them, which explained the shape and the texture that had me so confidently misunderstanding my dinner. In the moment, though, sitting in that restaurant, I had been wondering why no one had taken one look at this very obvious American and warned him that what he had ordered was not exactly what he thought. Of course the reason no one did that was because I had, in fact, ordered exactly what the menu said, and I was just being ignorant.

When I left the restaurant, dusk had arrived. *Camera hours.* That stretch from golden hour into early night when everything looks a little more composed than it really is, and photographs get an unfair advantage. I walked without much purpose, partly because I wanted to explore, partly because I wanted to put some distance between myself and the ball episode, which at that point was still unresolved in my mind.

I ended up in what I think was called India Town. It felt fairly chaotic, but in a tired end-of-day way. People seemed to be closing shops rather than opening them, pulling things in instead of putting things out. I took a few photos and kept moving. Eventually I drifted back toward the train, and then, just like the other day, I turned a corner and there was the entrance to the Chinatown shops. I walked through, mostly because it felt familiar now, which is amazing what one previous visit can do. The sun was setting this time, and the whole area had shifted with it.

At the far end I finally saw what I had been hoping to find. Food stalls lined the edges of the street, left and right, and directly ahead there was a charcoal grill station. Across from it I spotted another stall selling fried dumplings, and suddenly I was very hungry in a focused, practical way.

The dumpling stall was run by an older woman and a man I assumed was her husband. She took my order, seven pork and chive dumplings for exactly 10 ringgit. I was suddenly very pleased with myself for having remembered to carry cash. I sat and watched the man cook them, frying one side, flipping them, then frying the other. They came in a little plastic clamshell case, dumplings on one side, a soy-based sauce heavy on ginger and spice on the other. A long toothpick was the utensil, which worked well enough if you did not expect elegance from the experience.

I ate them quickly, then went back and ordered seven more, this time chicken and chive. Those disappeared just as fast. The pork ones were my favorite, but the chicken ones were my second favorite. Yes, that was a Brian Regan quote, or at least a thinly veiled one.

Satiation, finally.

I took a few more photos and decided it was time to head back. I was walking toward where I knew Central Market had to be when, like a lighthouse in mist, a sign emerged: "Pasar Seni." Was it *the* Pasar Seni, my stop? It was. I had found the secret entrance. I went in, now well rehearsed, reversed the train sequence, and took a Grab home.

Then it was a shower, clean clothes, and the lingering comfort of dumplings. It had been a good day. Tomorrow brought another day of work, my first full week.
