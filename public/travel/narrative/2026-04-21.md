## Tuesday, April 21 - Whopper Waiting Game

```poem
A Whopper crossed an ocean and met me after work
While fries arrived with chili sauce, doing ketchup's old job with a smirk
I worried the Korean host would judge my patriotic retreat
Then ate the burger anyway, because skepticism still likes to eat
```

Apart from work, the main event of the day had been waiting in the wings for me like some slightly ridiculous main attraction. The grand champion, the star, the thing I had been circling around since I got here, was the Burger King Whopper.

My coworkers had all asked me, I think individually, whether I had tried Burger King yet, or fast food in general. They were genuinely curious what I would think, and especially what differences I would notice between Burger King in Malaysia and Burger King in the United States. One thing they told me to expect was that the fries would not come with ketchup, but with some kind of chili sauce instead, maybe tomato-based, maybe spicy, maybe sweet. That alone was enough to make me curious.

The Burger King in Tamarind Square was, like a suspicious amount of the food I have tried here, right next to the Korean restaurant where I ate last night. Standing in the doorway was the same host who had welcomed me in so enthusiastically yesterday, and as I walked toward Burger King I felt a faint, irrational guilt. One of those private anxieties that appears fully formed and immediately starts making accusations. You wonder if anyone is looking at the American and thinking, *I knew it. He can't handle the spicy food. He refuses to try anything unfamiliar.* In reality, of course, the host probably did not care at all. He may even have been planning to get Burger King himself after his shift. That was another thing my coworkers had told me, fast food here is expensive and more of a treat than an everyday fallback.

Inside, I ordered the Whopper from a touchscreen kiosk in the lobby. Burger King still asks you to build the thing piece by piece, or at least confirm the components, and I had to pause and reconstruct from memory what a normal Whopper was supposed to be. Beef patty, cheese, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, ketchup. Mustard was an option, but I skipped it because I did not remember mustard being part of the standard Whopper, and it had been a long time.

When the tray arrived, it looked mostly as expected. The fries were the slightly larger kind, not quite steak fries, but definitely thicker than shoestrings. The first bite told the story immediately. They had that texture of frozen fries you buy at the store and bake in your oven at home, not the deeply satisfying crispness of something that has spent quality time in hot oil. Not tragic, just recognizable.

The burger itself was about like I remembered. I had been hoping, maybe unfairly, that it would be somehow different, something surprising or revealing, maybe improved in some strange and specifically Malaysian way. Maybe more like the hipster burger places that now seem to populate, or possibly litter, the United States. But it was a Whopper. And I had to admit, it tasted good.

Maybe some part of the American food palate had traveled with me after all.
