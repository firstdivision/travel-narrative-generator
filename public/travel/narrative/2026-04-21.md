## Tuesday, April 21 - The Whopper Question

Apart from work, the main event of the day, the grand champion, the thing that had been waiting in the wings for me to finally step into the ring with it, was the Burger King Whopper.

My coworkers had each, I think individually, asked whether I had tried Burger King yet, or fast food in general. They were genuinely curious about what I would think, and especially about the differences between the Malaysia and United States versions. One of the things they told me to expect was that the french fries would not come with ketchup, but instead with some sort of chili sauce, maybe tomato-based, maybe spicy, maybe sweet. The description was vague enough to make it more interesting.

The Burger King in Tamarind Square was, like much of the food I had tried so far, right next to the Korean restaurant where I had eaten the night before. Standing in the doorway was the same host who had enthusiastically welcomed me in yesterday, and now I felt a little guilty walking toward Burger King instead. It was one of those absurd private anxieties. You start wondering if anyone is looking at the American and thinking, oh, I bet he can’t handle spicy food, I bet he’s unwilling to try anything unfamiliar. In reality, of course, the host almost certainly did not care. Maybe he was even planning to go to Burger King after his shift. My coworkers had also told me that fast food here was generally expensive, more of a treat than an everyday fallback.

I ordered the Whopper on a touchscreen in the lobby, which somehow made the whole thing feel even more formal. At Burger King, after all, you are meant to have it your way. I selected what I remembered as the traditional Whopper, beef patty, cheese, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, ketchup. Mustard was an option, but I left it off because I didn’t remember mustard being part of the standard build. It had been a long time.

When the tray arrived, it looked about how I expected. The fries were a little larger than shoestrings, not quite steak fries, just thick enough to suggest substance. But the first bite gave them away. They had that unmistakable frozen-fry texture, the kind you buy at a grocery store and bake in your oven at home when you want something that resembles a french fry in theory. They were not bad, exactly. They just had none of that deep-fried conviction.

The burger itself was more or less what I remembered. I had secretly hoped it would turn out to be something different, something startling, some alternate-universe Whopper that would force me to rethink the whole category. Maybe it would edge toward the sort of hipster burger places now popping up, or really, littering, the United States. But no, it was a Whopper.

And I had to admit, it tasted good.

Maybe some part of the American food palate was still traveling with me.
