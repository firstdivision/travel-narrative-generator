## Tuesday, April 21 - Whopper in Waiting

```poem
All day the Whopper lurked, patient, greasy, grand
While coworkers studied me like a field report from another land
The fries arrived, but the chili sauce slipped out of sight
So my great fast-food quest ended decent, and mildly spiteful, by night
```

Apart from work, the main event of the day had been waiting in the wings with almost embarrassing importance: the Burger King Whopper.

My coworkers had each, I think, asked me individually whether I had tried Burger King yet, or fast food in general. They were genuinely curious about what I would think, and especially about the differences between Burger King in Malaysia and in the United States. One thing they told me to expect was that the french fries would not come with ketchup, but with some kind of chili sauce instead, maybe tomato-based, spicy, maybe sweet. That alone was enough to make me curious.

The Burger King in Tamarind Square was, like so much of what I had eaten lately, right next to the Korean restaurant where I had dinner the night before. Standing in the doorway was the same host who had welcomed me in so enthusiastically yesterday, and for a moment I felt oddly guilty walking past him on my way to Burger King. It was one of those private anxieties that appears fully formed and not especially rational. You wonder if they are watching the American and thinking, oh, I bet he can’t take spicy food. I bet he’s unwilling to try new and unusual things he hasn’t seen before. In reality, of course, the host probably did not care at all. Maybe he was even planning to go to Burger King himself after his shift. My coworkers had also told me that fast food here is expensive, more of a treat than an everyday fallback.

Inside, I ordered the Whopper from a touchscreen kiosk in the lobby. Burger King still wanted me to have it my way, so I had to pick the components one by one. I went with what I remembered as a standard Whopper: beef patty, cheese, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, ketchup. Mustard was an option, but I skipped it because I didn’t remember mustard being part of the usual setup. It had been a long time.

When the tray arrived, it looked more or less like I expected. The fries were the thicker kind, not quite steak fries but definitely larger than shoestrings. One bite in, I could tell they were the frozen variety, with that specific texture of store-bought oven fries, as if they had come out of a bag in someone’s freezer rather than out of a vat of deep fry oil with serious intentions.

And the chili dipping sauce was missing. I had been denied.

Tomorrow I would ask a coworker about this, and she would tell me, "Oh, you probably have to ask for it." Well then. Maybe I would have to make another trip to Burger King just to track down this mysterious, elusive sauce.

The burger itself tasted about like I remembered. I had been hoping, perhaps unfairly, that it would turn out to be something notably different, something surprising. Maybe closer to the kind of burger served at all the hipster burger places that now seem to litter the United States. But no, it was a Whopper. To be fair, it was a good Whopper. I had to admit that. Maybe some part of my American food palate had traveled with me after all.
