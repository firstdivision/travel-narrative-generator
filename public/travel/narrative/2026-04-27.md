## April 27 to May 9

This title is a little different than the others because it spans many days.

I had stopped journaling during this stretch, but for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started hanging out more and more, going places every night after work. I found myself with neither the time nor much real desire to write things down. I would rather spend it with her. So this became a kind of mega post for that time.

## Monday, April 27 - Sunset Stories

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I'm sure there was a breakfast, but I don't remember what it was. Maybe I was too tired for that and just got a latte and a Grab to work instead.

After work, though, we went to a park nearby. It was a nice park, with wide curved pathways that felt made for walking slowly, talking without much urgency, and making up stories just because the evening allowed it.

It was sunset, and we took turns finding more sunset pictures to take while also asking each other questions, the kind that start simple and somehow keep going.

After meeting a cat, we made up a story about two people who get locked into the park because they lost track of time and the gates are closed:

[do not rewrite this part!]

    The Magic Tree in the Park:

    After finding the gates closed and being unabel to leave, the couple finds a tree and crawl under it for shelter as the sun goes down.  But then they hear a noise, startled and scared at first, they soon see it is the cat they had met earlier.  They are not frightened any more but are soon startled when the cat speaks to them!

    "Thank you for the pets earlier! They were magnificent!" the cat says.

    Still too startled to talk the couple stares at the cat in disbelief.  Then another animal arrives.  A squirrel! And this one talks too!

    "Oh cat! Leave those two alone!" the squirrel says.

    "I am not bothering them, I was thanking them!" cat replies.

    No longer scared the couple talks with the animals all night long.  An owl, a tiny ant, a moth who flutters by and stays a while to talk, before flying away again.

    The sun rises, and the couple who had fallen asleep sometime late into the night wake up to the cat and squirrel are sleeping next to them.  The cat softly purring even as he sleeps.  Soon they awake too.

    All say goodbye, but the couple promise to return again.  And they do.  They return many nights, for many years.  Later they bring their kids to meet the animals in the park.  Cat is old now, his hair slightly grey, but he introduces his wife and kittens as well.

    The magic tree still stands in the park. And if you are patient enough, and kind enough, maybe you can get a cat to talk to you too.

For us, back in reality, we were not locked out of the park. We got into the car and drove home, taking pictures of the sunset as we did.

## Tuesday, April 28 - Luggage, Chords, and a Tiebreaker

That night we headed to a mall because I needed to buy new luggage to replace my wounded one.

Before we found the luggage store, we found a music store. I was pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and sure enough, there it was: a Yamaha store selling guitars and pianos. I played a couple songs on a guitar, forgetting the words for one of the verses completely, which is apparently the traditional reward for not practicing in quite a while. I also played a piano song that belongs to my personal category of standard chords to play when I first sit down at a piano. Every musician has some version of this, I think. I am certainly no exception.

Eventually we found a store with a lot of options, including Malaysian brands, which seemed like the appropriate thing to do. The employees gathered around me until there were four of them, bringing suitcase after suitcase out to demonstrate the virtues of each one. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. There was a sales rhythm to it that was almost impressive. Eventually I settled on one. I liked the new hardcase style of the carry-on.

There was also bowling. I had not bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how much fun it is once I admit to myself that I am not very good at it. We originally planned on playing one game, but it turned into two. By then we had each won one, so obviously we needed a tiebreaker. I won that one, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was probably closer to a tie.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, though the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport still overshadowed it a bit. Some meals are unlucky enough to arrive after a better one.

## Thursday, April 30 - Shared Plates After Work

At work, my new work friends treated me to lunch. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered a whole table of food to share, what I call homestyle. In other words, the plates and bowls go in the middle and everyone takes what they want. There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I was not sure what it was called, though some quick Googling later suggested maybe Peranakan-style curry. Whatever its proper name was, I liked it a lot, and I think I may have earned some points for handling the heat.

Afterward my friend and I met up near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think it was a variation of another chicken and rice dish, but when I saw a plate of french fries go by, I could not resist and ordered those too. Sometimes restraint loses very quickly to fries.

## Friday, May 1st - Wet Socks and Velvet Ropes

Tonight was a double-header.

First we went to Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks: a series of limestone caves and cave temples rising from a hillside just outside Kuala Lumpur. Visitors are greeted by the towering golden statue of Lord Murugan before climbing the colorful 272-step staircase to the main cathedral cave. Inside, the cavern opens toward the sky through natural gaps in the rock, and the light falls in dramatic shafts over shrines, worshippers, and the occasional troop of monkeys. It is a major pilgrimage site, not just a tourist stop, and the scale of it makes that obvious.

After we made our way up the first set of steps, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend, who had been there before, said she would wait there if I wanted to go do the second section. I wasn't sure why.

Then I found out why.

The second part required taking off shoes, even though I saw plenty of people who did not. Being a rule follower, I took mine off. For some reason I also decided to leave my socks on. It had worked well at the temple at the base of the stairs. Why wouldn't it work here, in this cave, the one with water dripping down the walls?

As I climbed the second set of stairs, I tried to dodge the puddles, but you can only dodge so much. My socks got wetter and wetter, and still I carried on, because apparently I like committing to bad ideas once I start them. I made it to the top, to the inner part of the temple, but by then I was no longer enjoying the experience because my socks had become the whole experience. Then I accidentally stepped in a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my spiritual ascent. I tried not to think about it.

I went back down to meet my friend.

"I maybe should not have left my socks on," I said, showing her the dark, wet bottoms of what had once been white socks.

She smiled at me knowingly while I put my shoes back on.

We descended the stairs and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold." The cold ones were only about one ringgit more, which made this one of the easiest financial decisions of the trip. We drank them, then tried to scoop some of the coconut meat out. We were somewhat successful, which felt fair.

Back at the car, I was taking off my shoes and my nasty socks and saying I thought I would just wear the shoes by themselves for the rest of the day. Thankfully, she had spare socks with her. Maybe she knew I would do this. Maybe everyone who goes into Batu Caves comes out wishing they had a fresh pair of socks. In any case, they were clean, new, warm, and soft. A true rescue.

Then we got back in the car and headed to Genting Highlands, with my job being to pick new music along the way.

Genting Highlands is perched high in the Titiwangsa Range about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, where cooler temperatures and misty forest make a sharp break from the tropical heat below. It has hotels, casinos, shopping, entertainment, and theme parks. The whole place feels part hill station, part resort city, part fever dream. Many visitors arrive by scenic cable car over dense rainforest and into the clouds.

We were hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. In the moment I thought it was the same place I had gone to in Tokyo, but later research showed the one in Tokyo was Ichiran. Close enough for a tired brain, apparently. In any case, the ramen was very good, warming in the mountain air, and exactly the kind of thing I wanted before the next part of the adventure.

After that we headed straight for the cable car and were immediately confronted with a gigantic line. The kind that folds back and forth and back and forth, what I call a Disney line. There were actually two of them, one for tickets and one for getting on the cable car. We also discovered there were VIP tickets online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible.

I looked up the tickets online. They were not that much more expensive than the regular ones. Add to cart, two tickets please.

We showed the QR codes to an attendant. A red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway with windows looking out over the full line snaking back and forth. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich.

The cable car delivered us to the top of the mountain, where there were attractions and a mall. Inside, the bright neon lights of stores and carnival rides made a strange contrast with the green, misty hills outside. And yes, carnival rides. Ferris wheel, games, all of it inside this mountain-top mall, because apparently that is a normal sentence here.

We played some games. There was an archery place, and video games where we played Halo in those racecar-seat setups with giant screens and tracked guns.

We also played two basketball games. The first one went fine, twenty-something balls sunk, respectable enough. The second one went worse. We had chosen a different machine, and this one had about half as many balls in it. I remain suspicious that previous players had redistributed the inventory in favor of the other machine.

With our final bit of balance on the plastic game card, we could only find one game cheap enough to play. It was a Street Fighter-style game, and it turned out to be the best value of the night. I made it through multiple fights on a single play before eventually meeting an opponent who absolutely clobbered me in all three bouts. I never had a chance, but it was still money well spent.

Afterward we went back to the cable car on the go-down side, but stopped at a midpoint landing to check out a temple.

Chin Swee Caves Temple rises from the mist below Genting's casinos and hotels and feels like a different world entirely, a mountainside sanctuary of pagodas, incense, and towering Buddha statues above a sea of rainforest. Reached by cable car or winding road, it offers some of the most dramatic views in the highlands and a welcome stretch of calm after Genting's constant buzz. The complex has a towering Buddha statue, a nine-story pagoda, prayer halls, gardens, and sweeping views over the surrounding mountains.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down. Walking back to the car, I saw a candy store and decided to step in. They had chocolates of all varieties, including durian-flavored ones. I could not resist buying a few of those for my cousins when they arrived, along with safer choices like almond, sea salt, and mint.

Leaving Genting was like leaving a ski resort. Cars everywhere, everyone trying to go home at exactly the same time. We both admitted we had headaches. Maybe it was the altitude, maybe just the length of the day. Eventually the traffic cleared, and we made the drive back to Kuala Lumpur, which took a little more than an hour and a half.

## Sunday, May 3 - Breakfast Walkabout

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and then a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore, I was supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run, which I missed. The 9 a.m. breakfast meet time sounded considerably more realistic.

We all met at Luck Bros Kopi. Tucked beside the bustle of Petaling Street, it serves a polished version of the Malaysian coffee-shop experience. Strong kopi, fragrant nasi lemak, and a menu that jumps between Chinese, Malay, and Peranakan influences make it feel like a small celebration of the country's food culture under one roof. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes.

Afterward we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe rented them, I was never quite sure, and we all headed inside without shoes. One of the first things I saw were more of those prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick that I like so much. I made a mental note to figure out where to buy them. The ceiling had an especially intricate mosaic that held my attention for a while.

Around the corner was the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. This one did not allow photography inside, so I have none, but it was pretty cool in there. The temple was founded in 1873 and is the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur. Its 22.9-meter entrance tower is covered with more than 200 sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings in the traditional Dravidian style of South India. During Thaipusam, it serves as the starting point for the massive pilgrimage procession to Batu Caves.

Afterward we headed toward KLCC mall. There we stopped in a bookstore where I found, but did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even after converting it back to USD, it remained a little too expensive for me. Also, where exactly was I supposed to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park we visited, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but with wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children.

Then we took a short train ride to TRX, where there was another, larger mall. Inside we visited the Apple Store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at my Airbnb because the battery was dead, and also because Apple Pay did not work on it there for me, which is annoyingly the thing I use it for most back home.

We also got lunch, with many things ordered. A bowl of Caesar salad was shared between three of us, a welcome arrival of raw vegetables. There was also steak and some spaghetti.

Content and full, we all left and headed home. We did have work tomorrow, after all.

## Monday, May 4 - Park Loops and Stray Dogs

I was dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out. I had to get up to get the car out of the garage anyway, so I figured I might as well go for a run.

In the park I met multiple stray dogs and made sure to give a couple of them ear scratches. One dog did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to prevent me from walking, which is a very effective emotional strategy.

There were also large ponds with paved walkways, and even an elevated walkway over some kind of wetlands along the pond's edge. I ran laps along these until I no longer felt like running, which seemed like a reasonable stopping metric, and then ran along the road back to my Airbnb.

## Tuesday, May 5 - Shakshuka and the Catfe

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before going to work. There I found that the new restaurant I had talked to the owner about, the one where I had been given a free coffee, was open. I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as Eggs Benedict, maybe more, and I also needed to compare this one to the one I had at The Botanist, which had been, yes, eggcellent.

This one came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the one from The Botanist, and the toast was good but a little underdone, closer to bread in some places. But it was also their first day open, and based on the conversations I'd had with the owner, I thought they would get things straightened out. The service was superb, and so was the latte. Overall it was a very good rendition of shakshuka, but that day at least, I had to give the nod to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night. We decided on going to a cat cafe, or as it was apparently styled, a "Catfe." A cafe where you get to play with cats. Sign me up.

We arrived and, after having our hands sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz, were immediately surrounded by cats. Most of them seemed either asleep or uninterested in us, which felt like strong evidence that these were real cats.

We got our drinks, which thankfully came with lids. I do like cats, but not enough to welcome their hair into my coffee. Eventually we found some cats that were interested in us, perhaps helped along by treats.

We spent a long time in there, long after our coffees, my latte and her matcha tea, had gone cold. We both very clearly did not want to leave our furry friends.

After leaving, I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat. There were lots of restaurants along the street where the Catfe was, but none of them were calling to us. I did find a Chinese grocery and stepped inside to see if I could find any ginger coconut drink. I even tried asking one of the clerks, but she only shook her head no.

So we got back in the car and I started looking at maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though I don't remember for sure because when we got there they were only serving drinks or something. But next to it was BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food. Tucked away behind an unassuming entrance near Jalan Ampang, TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU felt like the kind of Japanese whisky bar you might stumble into in Tokyo rather than Kuala Lumpur: intimate, dim, and quietly refined.

We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to be an unusual move for a Japanese whisky bar, because the woman taking our order went back and forth between us a couple times to confirm it. Eventually she brought out a younger man, who I think was a chef and spoke better English. We finally established that yes, we really did want the non-alcohol versions. I think mine was a mojito.

We ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu, true to its current level of hype, stole the show so thoroughly that I ordered another plate of it. It was so good that my photo does not do it justice, partly because I took the picture in about two seconds, too eager to eat.

## Wednesday, May 6 - Huskies and Noodles

Fresh off the success of the Catfe, we decided to go to a place called Huskiss, the same concept but with husky dogs. I am a dog person. She, not so much. In fact, on the way there she informed me that she is afraid of dogs.

When we got there I could see she was nervous and not especially happy about the large dogs coming up to say hello. But soon enough the dogs won. Before long she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

In a tropical city where the afternoon heat can feel like a wet blanket, Huskiss was an odd little encounter with the Arctic. For an hour we were surrounded by impossibly fluffy huskies, all of them interested in some combination of attention, photos, and sprawling dramatically across the floor in search of the next belly rub. It was quirky, a little ridiculous, and hard not to enjoy.

But leave we must. Our hour was up, they were closing, and we were hungry.

Back onto Google Maps I found a place called Noodle Art, and we headed in that direction. As with many experiences in KL, and Southeast Asia in general, where there is one restaurant there are probably many more. That was true here too. We made the rounds down the street and even into a parking lot next door where a ring of food trucks surrounded tables, but in the end we chose the original plan: Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specializes in the noodle dishes of Sarawak, bringing flavors from East Malaysia to the busy food district of Petaling Jaya. Handmade noodles, Sarawak laksa, kolo mee, and other regional dishes are served in a bright, casual setting that feels more neighborhood favorite than trend piece. It was a chance to try a side of Malaysian cuisine that was less familiar to me, built around springy noodles, aromatic broth, and a kind of straightforward comfort.

## Thursday, May 7 - Soup, Confusion, and Late-Night Arrivals

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a bowl of rich beef-bone soup built around a massive marrow-filled leg joint known as a gearbox. The broth is slow-simmered with spices, herbs, and aromatics until it turns deep and savory, slightly peppery, with tender meat clinging to the bone and marrow soft enough to scoop out like some absurd gift. It is a gloriously messy meal, part soup and part excavation.

Ordering did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. It was all done in Malay, so I obviously did not understand anything either person said. After some back and forth, we sat down and our two bowls arrived. Then the source of the confusion became clear. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but mine came with rice. I had been hinting lately that I was getting a little tired of rice, which was why we had ordered noodles in the first place. She asked and offered to go get me noodles instead, but I decided it was fine as it was. Besides, I was hungry, and it looked pretty good.

I think the beef in my soup may not have been cooked quite long enough. What I had expected to be fully fall-off-the-bone was, in parts, fairly tough. A few pieces were cooked very well and were tender and excellent, and if all of it had been like that this would have been an A+ dinner. As it was, still very good. A solid B.

After the meal I was given the job of paying for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a different place. When I got to the counter, the man said "lapan," the Malay word for eight.

We both stared at each other for a while until he realized that I clearly am an idiot and cannot even count to ten in the local language.

Then he started counting on his fingers. "One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight!"

Aha.

I handed him a ten-ringgit bill, and he gave me two in change.

On our way out we also ordered some to-go for my two cousins, who would be arriving that night. There was additional confusion when we tried to order just broth and noodles, no meat. They either did not understand or pretended not to and gave us meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my Airbnb, my two cousins texted that they were at the airport. Yay. Then they texted that they were confused about how this whole Grab thing worked. Oh no. Then another text came saying they had managed to order a Grab and were in it, but they had selected pay with cash and did I perhaps have any?

They finally arrived, and we all went back up to the Airbnb, where I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, leaving out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to power through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. It was getting toward one in the morning by the time we finally finished. Very late for the two locals. Maybe not quite so late for the newcomers.
