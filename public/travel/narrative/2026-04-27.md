## Monday, April 27 - Sunset Stories and a Bowling Tiebreaker

```poem
Back from Singapore, slightly blurred at the seams
Fueled by a latte and half-remembered schemes
We chased the sunset, then argued with pins
Which is how a Monday quietly wins
```

The journal days from this point through May 7 are a little different. I had stopped writing in my paper notebook around then, and it was for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to in earlier entries and I started spending more and more time together after work, going places most nights. She became more than just a friend. I found myself with neither the time nor much real desire to stop and write things down in a notebook. I wanted, more honestly, to spend as much time with her as possible.

Anyway. As they say. On with the show.

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I am sure there was breakfast, but I don't remember what it was. Or maybe I was too tired for that and just got a latte and a Grab to work, which feels at least as plausible as anything else from that foggy part of the day.

After work, we went to a park nearby. It was a nice park, the kind with wide, curving pathways that seem designed for walking slowly without admitting that the whole point is to keep talking. The light was dropping into sunset by then, and we kept taking turns spotting new angles for photos, stopping every few minutes because the sky had changed again or the trees looked different from ten feet over. We asked each other questions, the real kind and the casual kind, and let the evening stretch out.

At some point we met a cat, and from there things developed in the very reasonable direction of inventing an entire story about two people getting locked in the park after losing track of time.

The title, naturally, was *The Magic Tree in the Park*.

In our version, the couple discovered the gates were closed and, unable to leave, found a tree and crawled under it for shelter as the sun went down. They heard a noise and got scared at first, only to realize it was the same cat they had met earlier. Then the cat spoke.

"Thank you for the pets earlier! They were magnificent!"

Because apparently once you have been walking at sunset long enough, this seems like exactly what a cat would say.

A squirrel arrived too, and this one talked as well.

"Oh cat! Leave those two alone!" the squirrel said.

"I am not bothering them, I was thanking them!" the cat replied.

From there the couple spent the whole night talking with the park animals. An owl. A tiny ant. A moth that fluttered by, stayed long enough to talk, then left again. By sunrise, the couple had fallen asleep under the tree, with the cat and squirrel sleeping beside them, the cat softly purring even in sleep. In the morning they all said goodbye, but promised to meet again, and they did, many times, over many years. Later they even brought their kids to meet the animals. The cat had grown old by then, his fur slightly gray, and he introduced them to his wife and kittens.

The magic tree still stood in the park. And if you were patient enough, and kind enough, maybe you could get a cat to talk to you too.

It was ridiculous and cute and exactly the kind of story that feels right when you're walking in warm evening air with nowhere urgent to be.

Afterward we went to a mall. I think it was IOI.

We "found" a music store there, though really I was fairly sure there was one because I had looked it up on Google Maps beforehand and wanted to track it down. So this was less spontaneous discovery and more me pretending I had stumbled onto something. It was a Yamaha store, selling guitars and pianos. I played a couple of songs on guitar and, in keeping with long musical tradition, completely forgot the words to one of the verses. Some things survive any amount of time away from practice. I also sat down at a piano and played the standard chords I always seem to reach for first, the ones my hands go to automatically whenever I sit at a keyboard. Every musician has some version of that, I think. I am certainly no exception.

There was also bowling.

I hadn't bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how much fun it is once I fully accept that I am not especially good at it. That part helps. We had originally planned to play one game, which was obviously optimistic. One turned into two. By then we had each won one game, which meant, with the sort of flawless logic that only applies to bowling and other unnecessary competitions, that we needed a tiebreaker. I won that one, but only by a very narrow margin. We both agreed it was probably closer to a tie.

It was my first day back from Singapore, and by the end of it the details that stuck were not the practical ones. Not breakfast, not work, not whatever tired machinery got me through the morning. What stayed with me was the park at sunset, the made-up talking cat, a Yamaha guitar under fluorescent mall lighting, and the particular pleasure of an evening that kept finding one more thing to do before it was over.
