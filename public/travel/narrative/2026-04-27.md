## April 27 to May 9

This title is a little different than the others because it spans many days. I had stopped journaling during this stretch, but for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started hanging out more and more, going places every night after work, and I found myself with neither the time nor much desire to write things down. I wanted to spend that time with her instead. So this became a kind of mega-post for that stretch of days.

## Monday, April 27 - Sunset Stories

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I am sure there was breakfast, but I do not remember what it was. Or maybe I was too tired for breakfast and just got a latte and a Grab to work, which honestly sounds plausible.

After work, we went to a park nearby. It was a nice park, with wide curved pathways that seemed made for walking slowly, talking without urgency, and making up stories just to see where they went. Sunset was coming on, and we kept taking turns finding new angles for sunset photos while also asking each other questions, the kind that start light and somehow become more revealing without announcing themselves.

After meeting a cat, we made up a story about two people who get locked in the park after losing track of time.

The story was called *The Magic Tree in the Park*. In it, the couple found the gates closed and had no way out, so they took shelter under a tree as the sun went down. Then they heard a noise and, after a moment of alarm, discovered it was the same cat they had met earlier. Only now the cat could talk.

"Thank you for the pets earlier! They were magnificent!" the cat said.

Then a squirrel arrived and told the cat to leave them alone. The cat insisted he was only being polite. Soon the couple spent the whole night talking with the animals of the park: an owl, a tiny ant, a moth that fluttered in and stayed to talk for a while before flying off again. By morning, the couple had fallen asleep under the tree, and the cat and squirrel were sleeping beside them, the cat purring softly even in his sleep. When everyone woke, they said goodbye, but the couple promised to return. And they did, many nights, for many years. Later they brought their children, and by then the cat was old, his fur slightly gray, and he introduced them to his wife and kittens. The magic tree still stood in the park, and if you were patient enough, and kind enough, maybe a cat would talk to you too.

Back in reality, we were not locked in the park. We got into the car and drove home, still taking sunset pictures as we went.

## Tuesday, April 28 - Guitars, Luggage, and a Tie That Wasn't

That night we headed to a mall because I needed to buy new luggage to replace my wounded one.

Before we found the luggage store, we found a music store. I had been pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and there it was: a Yamaha store with guitars and pianos. I played a couple of songs on guitar, forgetting the words to one of the verses completely, which is tradition for anyone who has not practiced in quite a while. I also sat down at a piano and played my standard chords, the ones I always play first when I sit at a piano. Every musician has some version of this, I think. I certainly do.

Eventually we found a store with plenty of suitcase options, including Malaysian brands, which felt like the appropriate move. The employees quickly multiplied until there were four of them bringing out suitcase after suitcase and presenting the virtues of each one. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. It was a full production. I finally settled on a hardcase carry-on that I liked.

There was also bowling.

I had not bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how fun it is once I accept that I am not actually good at it. We originally planned to play one game, but one became two. By then we had each won one, so obviously we needed a tiebreaker. I won that one, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was probably closer to a tie.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, but I have to admit it suffered from being compared, unfairly, to the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport.

## Thursday, April 30 - Curry Points

At work, my new work friends treated me to lunch. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered a whole table of food to share, what I call family style, meaning the plates and bowls go in the middle and everyone takes what they want. There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I am not sure what it was called. Some quick Googling suggested maybe Peranakan-style curry. In any case, I liked it a lot, and I think I may have earned some points for being able to handle the heat.

Afterward, my friend and I met near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think I had some variation of another chicken-and-rice dish, but then I saw a plate of french fries go by and lost all discipline. So I ordered fries too.

## Friday, May 1st - Wet Socks and VIP Mountain Energy

We had a double-header planned that night. First was Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous landmarks. The towering golden statue of Lord Murugan stood at the entrance, and the colorful staircase rose steeply upward toward the cave temple. It was one of those places that is both heavily visited and still obviously sacred, which changes the atmosphere in a way even the tourists cannot quite flatten.

After we made our way up the first set of stairs, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend, who had been there before, said she would wait there if I wanted to do the second section. I was not sure why.

The second part required taking your shoes off, even though I saw plenty of people not doing that. But I am, annoyingly, a rule follower, so off came the shoes. For some reason I also decided to leave my socks on. This had worked fine at the temple at the base of the stairs. Why would it not work here, in the cave with water dripping down the walls?

As I climbed the second set of stairs, I tried to dodge puddles, but there is only so much dodging available to a person in a wet cave. My socks got wetter and wetter. I made it to the top and into the inner portion of the temple, but by then I was no longer enjoying myself. Wet socks are not spiritually enhancing. Then I accidentally stepped into a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my interest in the second section.

When I got back, I told her, "I maybe should not have left my socks on," and showed her the dark, wet bottoms of what had once been white socks. She smiled at me in the way people do when they saw this outcome coming from the beginning.

We went back down the stairs and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold," and the cold ones were only about one ringgit more, so there was really no decision to make. We drank them and then tried to scrape out some of the coconut meat. We were successful, somewhat.

Back at the car, I took off my shoes and disgusting socks and announced that I would probably just wear the shoes without socks for the rest of the day. Thankfully, she had spare socks with her. Maybe she knew I would do this. Maybe everyone who enters Batu Caves eventually emerges needing a fresh pair of socks. Either way, they were clean, new, warm, and soft, and I was absurdly grateful for them.

Then we headed to Genting Highlands, and on the drive my job was to pick the music.

Genting Highlands is a mountain resort destination high in the Titiwangsa Range, and the cooler air really did feel like a break from Kuala Lumpur. We were hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. At the time I thought it might be the same place I had been to in Tokyo, but later research revealed that the Tokyo place had been Ichiran. Not the same. Still, the ramen was very good, warming and solid in the exact way mountain food should be.

After that we went straight to the cable car and were immediately confronted by a gigantic line. The kind that snakes back and forth so many times it starts to feel philosophical. A Disney line. Actually two Disney lines, one for tickets and one for boarding. Then we discovered there were VIP tickets online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible.

I looked them up. They were not much more expensive than the regular tickets. Add to cart, two tickets please.

We showed the QR codes to an attendant, a red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway while looking through windows at the massive line curling back and forth. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich.

The cable car took us to the top of the mountain, where there were attractions and a mall, because apparently that is the kind of sentence Genting makes possible. Inside, the bright neon lights of stores and carnival rides made a strange contrast with the misty green hills outside. And yes, actual carnival rides. Ferris wheel, games, the whole thing, all inside a mountain-top mall.

We played a few games. There was archery, and one of those Halo shooting games with racecar-style seats, oversized screens, and guns that track your movement. We also played two basketball games. The first went fairly well, with twenty-something baskets, but the second was noticeably worse because we chose a different machine and this one had about half as many balls. I remain suspicious that previous players had redistributed the balls to improve their odds elsewhere.

With the last bit of money on the plastic game card, we searched for something cheap enough to play and found a Street Fighter-style game. It turned out to be the best value of the night. I made it through several fights on one play before reaching an opponent who absolutely clobbered me in all three bouts. I had no chance, but it was money well spent.

On the way down, we stopped at the midpoint landing to see Chin Swee Caves Temple. Suspended in the mist below the casinos and hotels, it felt like stepping into a completely different place. Pagodas, incense, Buddha statues, mountain views, and a welcome pocket of calm after all the neon and queue-skipping energy above.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down, and while walking back to the car I spotted a candy store and went in. They had chocolates in all kinds of flavors, including durian, which I could not resist buying for my cousins when they arrived. I also bought more normal flavors too: almond, sea salt, mint.

Leaving Genting was like leaving a ski resort after the lifts close. Cars everywhere, everyone leaving at once. We both admitted we had headaches, maybe from the altitude, maybe just from the length of the day. Eventually the traffic loosened and we made the hour-and-a-half drive back to Kuala Lumpur.

## Sunday, May 3 - Kopi, Temples, and Air-Conditioned Retail Wandering

I woke early to meet coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore, I had been supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run and missed it. The 9 a.m. breakfast meet time sounded much more achievable.

We met at Luck Bros Kopi, near the bustle of Petaling Street. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes.

After breakfast we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe rented them, I am not entirely sure, and we all went inside without shoes. One of the first things I noticed was more of those little prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I like so much. I made a mental note to try to find where to buy them. The ceiling had an especially intricate mosaic that kept pulling my eyes upward.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. No photography was allowed inside, so I do not have any pictures, but it was a very cool interior. The place has a weight to it, a density of detail that pictures probably would not have captured properly anyway.

After that we headed toward KLCC mall. Along the way we stopped in a bookstore where I found, but definitely did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even after converting it to USD, it remained firmly in the category of not happening. Also, where exactly was I going to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but with wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children.

Later we took a short train ride to TRX, where there was an even larger mall. We went into the Apple Store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at the AirBnB with a dead battery, and anyway Apple Pay does not work there for me, which is most of what I use the watch for back home.

We had lunch there too. A Caesar salad was shared among three of us, and the raw vegetables felt genuinely welcome. There was also steak and some spaghetti. We left full and content and headed home, because we all had work tomorrow after all.

## Monday, May 4 - Stray Dog Interruption

I got dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out. Since I already had to get up to move the car out of the garage, I figured I might as well go for a run.

In the park I met multiple stray dogs and made sure to give a couple of them ear scratches. One dog in particular did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to stop me from walking away. It was a pretty effective strategy.

The park had large ponds with paved walkways and even an elevated path over wetlands along the water's edge. I ran laps until I did not feel like running anymore, which is still how I measure these things, and then ran along the road back to my AirBnB.

## Tuesday, May 5 - Shakshuka, Cats, and Unexpected Wagyu

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before work. The new restaurant I had talked to the owner about, the one where I had been given a free coffee, was finally open, so I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as eggs Benedict, maybe more, and I also needed to compare this one to the version I had at The Botanist, which had been eggcellent. This one came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the one at The Botanist, and the toast was good but a little underdone, edging into just bread in places. But it was also their first day open, and based on my conversations with the owner I suspected they would get the details sorted out. The service was superb, and so was the latte. Overall it was a very good shakshuka, but for now I still had to give the nod to The Botanist.

After work, it was time for another date night. We decided to go to a cat cafe, or rather a "Catfe," which is the kind of branding choice I am willing to accept when the premise is coffee and cats. No need to ask me twice.

When we arrived, our hands were sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz and then we were immediately surrounded by cats. Most of them were sleeping or entirely uninterested in us, which felt like proof that these were real cats. We got our drinks, with lids thankfully. I like cats very much. I do not like cat hair in drinks quite as much.

Eventually we found some cats who were interested in us, perhaps with a little encouragement from treats. We spent a long time there, long after the drinks had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea both abandoned in favor of furry company. Neither of us seemed to want to leave.

Afterward I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat. There were many restaurants along the same street as the Catfe, but none of them were calling to us. I did find a Chinese grocery and went inside to see if I could find any ginger coconut drink. I even tried asking one of the clerks, but she just shook her head no.

So we got back in the car and I started looking at maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though I do not remember exactly because when we got there they were only serving drinks or something along those lines. Next to it, though, was TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food.

We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to cause a surprising amount of confusion for a Japanese whisky bar. The woman taking our order went back and forth between us a couple of times to confirm, and eventually a younger man, who I think was a chef and spoke better English, came out to make sure that yes, we really did want the non-alcoholic versions. I think I had a mojito.

We ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu stole the show in the most predictable and justified way possible. I liked it enough to order another plate. It was so good that the photo I took does not do it justice, partly because I took it in about two seconds and then got back to eating.

## Wednesday, May 6 - Huskies in the Tropics

Fresh off the success of the Catfe, we decided to go to Huskiss, the same general concept but with husky dogs.

I am a dog person. She is not, or at least not naturally. On the way there she informed me that she was afraid of dogs.

When we arrived, I could tell she was nervous and not exactly thrilled by the large dogs coming over to greet her. But the dogs wore her down in the way friendly dogs often do. Before long she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

It was, objectively, a slightly ridiculous place. In a hot tropical city, there we were spending an hour with a pack of fluffy Arctic-looking dogs who seemed equally interested in attention, photos, and sprawling on the floor for belly rubs. It was hard not to enjoy.

Eventually we had to leave because our hour was up and they were closing, but also because we were hungry. Back to Google Maps. I found a place called Noodle Art and we headed that way.

As often happens in Kuala Lumpur, one restaurant turned out to mean many restaurants. We walked up and down the street and even checked a parking lot next door where food trucks formed a ring around shared tables. After all that, we ended up choosing the original plan after all: Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specialized in Sarawak noodle dishes, bringing flavors from East Malaysia into a busy Petaling Jaya food district. It was the kind of place that felt casual and neighborhood-driven rather than polished or trendy, built around springy noodles and aromatic broths.

## Thursday, May 7 - Soup Logistics and Midnight Cousins

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a bowl of beef-bone soup centered on a massive marrow-filled leg joint known as a gearbox. It is the kind of dish that arrives with a sense of ceremony even if nobody intends one, rich broth, tender meat, and marrow you can scoop out if you are willing to get a little messy.

Ordering did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. It was all done in Malay, so I obviously understood none of it. After some back and forth we sat down, and when the bowls arrived the source of the confusion became clear. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but it came with rice instead. I had been hinting lately that I was getting tired of rice, which was the whole reason we had ordered noodles. She offered to go get me noodles instead, but I decided it was fine as it was. Also, I was hungry, and the soup looked pretty good.

The beef in my bowl seemed like it had maybe not been cooked quite long enough. I had expected everything to be fully in that fall-off-the-bone category, but some of it was fairly tough. A few pieces were cooked the way I had hoped, very tender and rich, and those were excellent. If the whole bowl had been like that, it would have been an A+. As it was, I would call it a solid B. Still good, just not quite the full promise of the menu.

After the meal I got sent to pay for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a different place. At the counter, the man said "lapan," the Malay word for eight. We stared at each other for a while until he realized he was dealing with someone who could not even count to ten in the local language.

Then he started counting on his fingers. "One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight!"

Aha.

I handed him a ten-ringgit bill and he gave me two in change. We had achieved communication through elementary arithmetic.

On the way out we also ordered takeaway for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. We wanted just broth and noodles, no meat. This was either not understood or perhaps strategically ignored, because they gave us meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my AirBnB, my cousins texted that they were at the airport. Great. Then came the second text, which was less great: confusion about how Grab worked. Then another update that they had successfully ordered one, but had selected pay with cash and did I happen to have any?

They finally arrived, and we all went upstairs. I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, leaving out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. By the time we were done it was getting close to one in the morning, very late for the two locals, maybe not quite as late for the new arrivals.
