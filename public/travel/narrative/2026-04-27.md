## April 27 to May 9

This title was a little different than the others, and for once there was a reason beyond my usual inconsistency. I had more or less stopped journaling during this stretch. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started spending more and more time together, usually every night after work, and I found myself with neither the time nor much desire to write things down. I wanted to spend that time with her instead. So this became a kind of mega-post, stitched together from memory, appetite, and whatever details still survived.

## Monday, April 27 - Sunset Stories

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I am sure there was breakfast involved at some point, but I honestly do not remember what it was. Maybe I was too tired for food and just got a latte and a Grab to work, which sounds plausible and not especially noble.

After work we went to a park nearby. It was a nice one, with wide curved pathways that seemed built specifically for walking slowly, talking without urgency, and making up stories because the evening was too soft to waste on normal conversation. It was sunset, and we kept taking turns finding new angles for sunset photos while also asking each other questions, the sort that are half curiosity and half excuse to keep walking.

At some point we met a cat, and from there we ended up inventing a whole story about two people who lose track of time, get locked inside the park after the gates close, and have to shelter under a tree.

The title was *The Magic Tree in the Park*.

In our version, the couple crawled under the tree as night fell, heard a noise, panicked briefly, and then realized it was the same cat from earlier. The cat thanked them for the pets. A squirrel arrived and scolded the cat for bothering them. Then the whole night opened up into this small ridiculous world where animals talked freely: an owl, a tiny ant, a moth that fluttered in and stayed for a conversation before flying off again. By morning the couple had fallen asleep under the tree, the cat and squirrel sleeping beside them, and when they all woke up they said goodbye and promised to return. They did return, many times, for many years, and later brought their kids back to meet the animals. The cat, old now and a little gray, introduced his wife and kittens. The magic tree still stood there, and if you were patient enough, and kind enough, maybe a cat would talk to you too.

Back in reality, we were not locked in the park. No enchanted overnight woodland residency for us. We got into the car and drove home, still taking pictures of the sunset as we went.

## Tuesday, April 28 - Suitcase and Spare Frames

The next night we went to a mall because I needed new luggage to replace the wounded piece I already had, which had apparently given all it could give. Before we found the luggage store, though, we found something much more distracting: a music store. I had been pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and there it was, a Yamaha store with guitars and pianos.

So naturally I sat down and played. A couple songs on guitar, one of them derailed by the complete disappearance of one verse from my memory, which felt very on-brand for someone who had not practiced in a while. Then I played a piano song with my usual standard chords, the ones I always default to when I first sit at a piano. Every musician has a warm-up ritual, I think. Mine is apparently part muscle memory, part bluff.

Eventually we did find a store with lots of luggage options, including Malaysian brands, which seemed like the appropriate move. The staff multiplied quickly until there were four employees showing me suitcase after suitcase, each one presented with the kind of serious respect usually reserved for major appliance purchases. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. This one, presumably, could survive atmospheric reentry. I finally settled on a hardcase carry-on, which felt modern and practical and much less doomed than my previous one.

There was also bowling. I had not bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how much fun it is once I fully admit that I am not very good at it. We had planned on one game, but of course that became two. By then we had each won one, so there was obviously no choice but a tiebreaker. I won that one, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was probably closer to a tie, which was both generous and fair.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, but the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport still hovered over it like an unfair comparison.

## Thursday, April 30 - Curry at Lunch, Fries at Dinner

At work, my new work friends treated me to lunch at a restaurant near the work building. We ordered a whole table of food to share, what I think of as homestyle: plates and bowls in the middle, everyone helping themselves. There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I was not entirely sure what it was called. My later Googling suggested maybe Peranakan-style curry. Whatever it was, I liked it a lot, and I may also have earned a few points by handling the spice without visible collapse.

Afterward my friend and I met up near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think I had some variation of another chicken and rice dish, but then I saw a plate of french fries go by and any plan I had immediately lost authority. So I ordered fries too. There are principles, and then there are fries.

## Friday, May 1st - Wet Socks and Velvet Ropes

Tonight was a double-header.

First we went to Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks, just outside Kuala Lumpur. The limestone caves and cave temples rise out of the hillside in a way that already feels theatrical before you even factor in the towering golden statue of Lord Murugan and the 272 colorful steps leading up to the main cave. Inside, the cavern opens toward the sky through gaps in the rock, with light coming down in shafts onto shrines, people, and the occasional monkey. It is a real pilgrimage site, not just a place where tourists point their phones upward.

After we made our way up the first staircase, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend, who had been there before, said she would wait if I wanted to do that part. I was not sure why.

Soon I found out.

For the second part, you were meant to remove your shoes, even though I saw plenty of people who did not. But I am, regrettably, a rule follower in these situations, so I took mine off. For some reason I also decided to keep my socks on. It had worked well enough at the temple at the base of the stairs. Why would it not work here, in the cave with water dripping down the walls?

As I climbed the second set of stairs, I tried to dodge puddles, but there is only so much dodging available when the floor is largely committed to being wet. My socks got wetter and darker with every step. I made it to the top, to the inner part of the temple, but by then I was no longer spiritually available for the experience. Then I stepped in a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my interest.

When I got back to her, I admitted the obvious.

"I maybe should not have left my socks on."

I showed her the bottoms, dark and soaked where they had once been white. She gave me the kind of knowing smile reserved for mistakes that were both avoidable and completely predictable, and I put my shoes back on.

We went back down and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold," and the cold ones were only about one ringgit more, which made this the easiest financial decision of the day. We drank them and then tried to scoop out the coconut meat. We were successful, somewhat.

Back at the car, I took off my shoes and my now fairly offensive socks and announced that I would probably just wear the shoes without socks for the rest of the day. She, thankfully, had a spare pair with her. Maybe she knew I would do something like this. Maybe everyone leaves Batu Caves wishing they had emergency socks. Either way, they were clean, soft, warm, and genuinely heroic.

Then we drove to Genting Highlands, with my job being to choose the music along the way.

Genting Highlands is perched up in the Titiwangsa Range about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, a mountain resort area where the air is cooler and the forests are misty and everything feels like it has been moved a few climate zones away from the city. People also arrive by cable car, which glides over rainforest and up into the clouds. It has hotels, casinos, shopping, entertainment, theme parks, and an energy that somehow combines hill station and resort city and giant enclosed distraction machine.

We were hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. In the moment I thought it might be the same place I had gone to in Tokyo, but later research showed that the Tokyo one had been Ichiran. Close enough to confuse a tired person, not close enough to be correct. In any case, the ramen was very good and exactly the right thing in the cool mountain air.

After that we went directly to the cable car and were immediately confronted with an enormous line, one of those back-and-forth queue mazes I think of as a Disney Line. Actually there were two such lines, one for tickets and one for boarding. Then we discovered there were VIP tickets online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible.

I looked them up. They were not much more expensive than regular tickets. Add two to cart, please.

We showed the QR codes to an attendant, a red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway beside windows looking out onto the full serpentine misery of the regular line. So this, I thought, is what it feels like to be rich.

The cable car took us to the top, where there were attractions and a mall. Inside, the bright neon lights of shops and carnival rides looked almost absurd against the green, misty hills outside. But yes, carnival rides. Ferris wheel, games, the whole thing. Right there in a mountain-top mall, because apparently subtlety had not been invited.

We played some games. There was an archery place, and a Halo game with racecar-style seats, huge screens, and guns that tracked your movements. We also played two basketball games. In the first one we did all right, hitting twenty-something shots. In the second, we chose a different machine and did noticeably worse. I maintain that this was not entirely our fault. That machine had about half as many balls in it. I suspect previous players had redistributed inventory in favor of the neighboring game.

With the last bit of money left on the game card, we could only afford one cheap game, a Street Fighter-style machine. It turned out to be the best value of the lot. I kept progressing through multiple fights on a single play until eventually an opponent absolutely destroyed me in all three rounds. I had no chance at all, but it was still money well spent.

On the way back down, we stopped at a midpoint landing to see Chin Swee Caves Temple. Dropping out of the whole casino-and-mall atmosphere and into that temple in the mist felt like changing channels entirely. Pagodas, incense, a towering Buddha statue, prayer halls, gardens, and sweeping views out over rainforest-covered mountains. It was calmer, quieter, and somehow more proportionate to the setting.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down. Walking back to the car, I saw a candy store and went in. They had chocolates of all kinds, including durian-flavored ones, and I could not resist buying some for my cousins for when they arrived. I also bought safer options like almond, sea salt, and mint, in case my gift strategy had gone too deep into sabotage.

Leaving Genting was like leaving a ski resort, if ski resorts were in Malaysia and everyone decided to leave at exactly the same time. Cars everywhere. Traffic inching along. We both admitted we had headaches. Maybe altitude, maybe just the long day, maybe the combination of caves, cables, neon, and arcade acoustics. Eventually the traffic cleared, and we drove the hour and a half or so back to Kuala Lumpur.

## Sunday, May 3 - Temples, Towers, and a Pricey Book

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and a walkabout. The weekend I had gone to Singapore, I was supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run and missed it. I have no defense. But a 9 a.m. breakfast was a much more realistic relationship with the morning.

We met at Luck Bros Kopi, tucked beside the bustle of Petaling Street. It felt like a polished version of the Malaysian coffee-shop experience, with kopi, nasi lemak, and a menu that moved easily across Chinese, Malay, and Peranakan influences. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes. This remains true across borders, schedules, and all evidence that I should probably branch out.

After breakfast we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe rented them, I was never entirely sure, and we all went inside without shoes. One of the first things I noticed was another set of those prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I liked very much. I made a mental note to try to figure out where to buy them. The ceiling had an especially intricate mosaic that kept pulling my attention upward.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. No photography was allowed inside, so I do not have pictures, but it was impressive. Founded in 1873, it is the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur, with a 22.9-meter entrance tower covered in more than 200 sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings in the Dravidian style of South India. It also serves as the starting point for the Thaipusam procession to Batu Caves, which made seeing it just after that visit feel nicely connected.

Afterward we headed toward KLCC mall. We stopped in a bookstore where I found, but definitely did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even after converting to USD, it remained completely unreasonable for me, and besides, where exactly was I going to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but with wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children. Then we took a short train ride to TRX, where there was another, larger mall. Inside we visited the Apple Store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at the Airbnb with a dead battery, and in any case Apple Pay was not working for me there, which was unfortunate because that is mostly what I use it for back home.

We also got lunch. A lot was ordered. Three of us shared a Caesar salad, and the raw vegetables felt like a real contribution to my well-being. There was also steak and spaghetti. It was one of those meals where the table slowly fills up and nobody tries especially hard to justify the combination.

After that, full and content, we all headed home. We did have work tomorrow after all.

## Monday, May 4 - Stray Dogs and a Park Loop

I got dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out earlier. I had to get up to move the car out of the garage anyway, so I figured I might as well go for a run.

The park had large ponds, paved walkways, and even an elevated walkway over wetlands along the pond's edge. I ran laps along all of it until I did not feel like running anymore, which is usually the most honest metric. Along the way I met multiple stray dogs and made sure to give a couple of them ear scratches. One of them did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to stop me from walking away, which was manipulative, effective, and very hard to argue with.

Eventually I ran back along the road to my Airbnb.

## Tuesday, May 5 - Shakshuka, Cats, and Wagyu

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before work. The new restaurant I had previously talked to the owner about, the one where I had been given a free coffee, was finally open, so I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as Eggs Benedict, maybe more, and I was immediately comparing this one to the version I had at The Botanist, which had been, yes, "eggcellent."

This version came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the one from The Botanist, and the toast was good but slightly underdone, closer to bread in some spots than toast. But it was also their first day open, and based on my earlier conversations with the owner, I suspected they would sort things out. The service was superb, the latte was excellent, and overall it was a very good shakshuka. Still, on this particular day, I had to give the edge to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night. We decided to go to a "Catfe," a cafe where you play with cats. I did not require convincing.

We arrived, got our hands sanitized with a quick spray-bottle mist, and were immediately surrounded by cats. Most of them were either asleep or pointedly uninterested in us, which was reassuring evidence that these were in fact real cats and not some hospitality-trained imitation species. We got our drinks, which thankfully came with lids. I like cats very much. Cat hair in coffee, less so. Eventually we found some cats that were interested in us, perhaps with a little help from treats.

We stayed a long time, well after both drinks had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea. Neither of us seemed to want to leave.

Afterward I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat. There were plenty of restaurants along the same street as the Catfe, but none of them were really calling to us. I ducked into a Chinese grocery to see if I could find any ginger coconut drink and even tried asking one of the clerks, but she just shook her head no.

So we got in the car and I started looking at maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though I do not fully remember because when we got there they were only serving drinks or something along those lines. Next to it, though, was TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food.

It felt tucked away and a little hidden, the sort of Japanese whisky bar you would expect to stumble into in Tokyo rather than Kuala Lumpur. The room was intimate, with the feel of a late-night place where conversation naturally slows down. We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to cause some confusion. The woman taking our order went back and forth a couple of times to confirm, and then brought over a younger man who I think was a chef and spoke better English. Eventually we all established that yes, we really did want the non-alcoholic versions. I think I had a mojito.

We ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu absolutely stole the show, enough that I ordered another plate. They were so good that the photo I took does not do them justice, because I took it in about two seconds, far more interested in eating than in documentation.

## Wednesday, May 6 - Huskies and Noodles

Fresh off the success of the Catfe, we decided to try a place called Huskiss, basically the same idea but with husky dogs. I am much more of a dog person. She, as it turned out, was not. In fact, on the way there she informed me that she was afraid of dogs.

When we got there I could see she was nervous, and not especially pleased by the large dogs immediately coming over to say hello. But dogs, especially fluffy persistent ones, can wear down resistance with alarming efficiency. Before long she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

It was a strange and very KL kind of experience, being in a tropical city where the heat outside can feel like a wet blanket and then spending an hour with a room full of Arctic-looking dogs. Slightly ridiculous, very charming.

But leave we had to, partly because our hour was up and they were closing, and partly because we were hungry. I went back to Google Maps and found a place called Noodle Art. We headed that way, and as often happens in KL and in Southeast Asia generally, one restaurant turned out to mean many restaurants. The street had options everywhere, and there was even a parking lot next door with a ring of food trucks around tables. We made the rounds and then, after all that scouting, went with the original plan anyway and ate at Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specializes in Sarawak noodle dishes, bringing flavors from East Malaysia into the busy food district of Petaling Jaya. Handmade noodles, rich Sarawak laksa, kolo mee, the kind of food that leans on springy noodles and aromatic broths rather than showmanship. It felt more neighborhood favorite than trend machine, which suited me fine.

## Thursday, May 7 - Gearbox Soup and Late Arrivals

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a bowl of beef-bone soup built around a massive marrow-filled leg joint called a gearbox. It is slow-simmered for hours with spices and herbs until the broth goes deep and savory and peppery, with tender meat and marrow you can scoop out of the bone. At least, that is the theory, and it is a strong theory.

Ordering did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. Everything was done in Malay, so I naturally understood nothing. After some back and forth we sat down, and when the bowls arrived, the source of the confusion became clear. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but mine came with rice instead. Since I had been hinting lately that I was getting a little tired of rice, this was not quite the outcome we had aimed for. She offered to go get noodles for me instead, but I was hungry and the bowl looked good, so I decided it was fine.

The beef in my soup seemed like it maybe had not been cooked quite long enough. I had expected fully fall-off-the-bone tenderness, and some pieces were indeed like that, soft and excellent. But other parts were fairly tough. If all of it had matched the best bites, it would have been an A+ dinner. As it was, it was still very good. A solid B.

After the meal, I got sent to pay for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a separate place. When I got to the counter, the man said "lapan," which is the Malay word for eight. We stared at each other for a moment while my blank expression communicated very clearly that I was not going to solve this on my own.

Then he switched strategies and started counting on his fingers.

"One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight."

Aha.

I handed him a ten ringgit bill, and he gave me two in change. International commerce, eventually triumphant.

On the way out we also ordered some food to go for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. There was more confusion because we wanted just broth and noodles, no meat. They either did not understand or politely ignored the request and gave us meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my Airbnb, my cousins texted that they were at the airport. Great. Then they texted that they were confused about how Grab worked. Less great. Then another message came through saying they had managed to order one, but had selected pay with cash and did I happen to have any?

Eventually they arrived. We all went back upstairs, and I warmed up the broth and noodles for them, leaving out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. By the time we finally finished, it was getting close to one in the morning. Very late for the two locals. Maybe not so late for the newcomers.
