## April 27 to May 9 - The Lost Week, More or Less

```poem
I stopped my notes for reasons not obscure
A park, some soup, and dates became the cure
My socks at Batu died a swampy death
But wagyu briefly gave my soul fresh breath
```

This title is a little different than the others because it spans many days.

I had stopped journaling during this stretch, but for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started hanging out more and more, going places every night after work, and somewhere in there I found myself with neither the time nor the real desire to write things down. I wanted to spend that time with her instead. So this became a kind of mega-post, stitched together from memory, food, and whatever details survived the week.

**Monday, April 27**

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I am sure there was breakfast, but I don't remember what it was. Maybe I was too tired for breakfast and just got a latte and a Grab to work. That would track.

After work, though, we went to a park nearby. It was a nice park, the sort with wide curved pathways made for walking slowly, talking without rushing, and making up stories because the evening allowed it. Sunset was settling in, and we kept taking turns finding new angles for sunset pictures while also asking each other questions, the basic early architecture of getting to know someone.

At some point we met a cat, and from there we made up a story about two people who got locked in the park after losing track of time.

*The Magic Tree in the Park* stayed with me because it was exactly the kind of nonsense that feels completely reasonable at sunset. In the story, the couple found a tree and crawled under it for shelter as the sun went down. Then they heard a noise and got startled, only to discover it was the same cat from earlier, now talking. "Thank you for the pets earlier! They were magnificent!" the cat said. Then a squirrel arrived and spoke too. The couple spent the whole night talking with the animals: an owl, a tiny ant, a moth that fluttered in, stayed a while, then left again. By morning the couple woke to find the cat and squirrel asleep beside them, the cat softly purring even in his sleep. They all said goodbye, but promised to return, and they did, for many years, eventually even bringing their kids. Cat was old by then, a bit gray, and introduced them to his wife and kittens. The magic tree still stood there, and if you were patient enough, and kind enough, maybe you could get a cat to talk to you too.

Back in reality, we were not locked out of the park. We got into the car and drove home, still taking pictures of the sunset as we went.

**Tuesday, April 28**

That night we headed to a mall because I needed to buy new luggage to replace the wounded one I had now. Before we found the luggage store, though, we found a music store. I had been pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and there was: a Yamaha store selling guitars and pianos. I played a couple of songs on guitar, forgot the words to one of the verses completely, which felt traditional for someone who maybe had not practiced in quite a while, and then sat down at a piano and played the standard chords I always seem to play first. Every musician has those little warm-up reflexes, I think. I am certainly no exception.

Eventually we found a store with a lot of suitcase options, including Malaysian brands, which seemed like the appropriate thing to buy. The staff gathered around me until there were four employees involved, producing suitcase after suitcase and explaining the virtues of each one. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. It was a full committee. I finally settled on a hardcase carry-on that I liked.

There was also bowling. I had not bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how fun it is once I admit to myself that I am not very good at it. We had originally planned on one game, but of course it became two. By then we had each won one, which meant obviously we needed a tiebreaker. I won that one, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was basically a tie.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, though I have to admit the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport still overshadowed it. Some meals are forced to compete unfairly.

**Thursday, April 30th**

At work I was treated to lunch by my new work friends. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered a whole table of food to share, what I think of as homestyle, meaning everything lands in the middle and everyone takes what they want. There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I am still not sure what it was called. Some quick Googling later suggested maybe Peranakan-style curry. Whatever it was, I liked it a lot, and I think I may have earned some points for being able to handle the heat.

Afterward my friend and I met up near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think it was some variation of another chicken-and-rice dish, but then I saw a plate of french fries go by and immediately lost all discipline. I ordered those too.

**Friday, May 1st**

That night we had a double-header planned. First was Batu Caves.

Batu Caves is one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks, a series of limestone caves and cave temples rising from a hillside just outside Kuala Lumpur. Visitors are greeted by the towering golden statue of Lord Murugan before climbing the colorful 272-step staircase to the main cathedral cave. Inside, the cavern opens dramatically toward the sky through natural openings in the rock. It is an active Hindu pilgrimage site, not just a tourist stop, and it carries that feeling.

After we made our way up the first set of steps, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend had been there once before and said she would wait there if I wanted to do the second section. I wasn't sure why.

Then I found out.

The second part required taking your shoes off, even though I saw plenty of people not doing that. But being a rule follower, I took mine off. For some reason I also decided to leave my socks on. It had worked well enough at the temple at the base of the stairs, so why wouldn't it work here, in the cave with water dripping down the walls? Sound reasoning from a man who had apparently learned nothing.

I tried to dodge the puddles on the second staircase, but you can only dodge so much. My socks got wetter and wetter, and still I carried on. I made it to the top, to the inner portion of the temple, but by then my interest had been dissolved by wet socks. Then I accidentally stepped in a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my spiritual commitment for the day. I tried not to think about it.

When I went back down to meet her, I said, "I maybe should not have left my socks on," and showed her the dark wet bottoms of what had once been white socks. She smiled at me in the way people do when they saw the obvious problem ten minutes earlier.

We descended the stairs and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold." The cold ones were only about one ringgit more, making that the easiest financial decision of the day. We drank them and then tried to scoop out some of the coconut meat. We were successful, somewhat.

Back at the car, I took off my shoes and my nasty socks and announced that I thought I would just wear my shoes by themselves for the rest of the day. Thankfully, she had spare socks with her. Maybe she knew I would do this. Maybe everyone who goes into Batu Caves comes out wishing for a fresh pair of socks. Either way, they were clean, new, warm, and soft, and I was absurdly grateful.

Then we got back in the car and headed to Genting Highlands, with my job being to pick the music on the way.

Genting Highlands sits high in the Titiwangsa Range, about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, where cool temperatures and misty forests offer a break from the tropical heat below. It is part mountain resort, part casino town, part giant indoor entertainment complex, which is a strange sentence to write and still accurate.

We were hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. At the time I thought it might be the same place I had gone to in Tokyo, but later research reminded me that the Tokyo place was Ichiran. Close enough in the moment, apparently. In any case, the ramen was very good, warming and practical, exactly what I wanted before going back out into the mountain air.

After that we headed for the cable car and were immediately met by a gigantic line. The kind that snakes back and forth enough times to make you question whether the destination is worth it. A proper Disney line. Actually there were two Disney lines, one for tickets and another for boarding. We also discovered there were VIP tickets online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible. I looked them up. They were not much more expensive than the regular ones. Add to cart, two tickets please.

We showed the QR codes to the attendant. A red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway with windows looking out onto the entire miserable queue. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich.

The cable car took us to the top of the mountain where the attractions and mall were waiting. Inside, the bright neon lights of stores and carnival rides made a sharp contrast with the green misty hills outside. And yes, carnival rides, inside the mall. Ferris wheel, games, all of it. Genting has the energy of a place that was conceived in several incompatible meetings and somehow still works.

We played games inside. There was an archery place, and a Halo game with racecar-style seats, giant screens, and guns that tracked on the display. We also played two rounds of basketball. In the first game we did pretty well, sinking twenty-something balls. In the second, we did worse, partly because we picked a different machine and this one had about half as many balls in it. I remain suspicious. I think previous players had transferred inventory from one machine to the other in a black-market basketball economy.

With the last bit of credit on the plastic game card, we found only one machine cheap enough to play: a Street Fighter style game. It turned out to be the best value of all. I made it through multiple fights on a single play before finally reaching a level where the opponent clobbered me in all three bouts. I never stood a chance, but it was money well spent.

Then we headed to the cable car down side, stopping first at a midpoint landing to check out Chin Swee Caves Temple.

Rising from the mist below Genting's casinos and hotels, Chin Swee felt like a different world entirely. Pagodas, incense, a towering Buddha statue, gardens, and broad views over rainforest-covered mountains. After all the blinking lights and indoor noise, it came as a welcome pause.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down, and while walking back to the car I spotted a candy store and went in. They had chocolates of all varieties, including durian-flavored ones, which I immediately decided would be perfect for my cousins when they arrived. I also bought more normal flavors too: almond, sea salt, mint. A diplomatic assortment.

Leaving Genting felt a bit like leaving a ski resort, with cars everywhere and everyone trying to escape at once. We both admitted we had headaches. Maybe altitude, maybe just a long day, maybe carnival basketball intrigue. Eventually the traffic cleared and we made the drive back to Kuala Lumpur, which took a little more than an hour and a half.

**Sunday, May 3**

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and then a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore I had been supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run, which I missed. I have to admit that the 9 a.m. breakfast meet time sounded considerably more achievable.

We met at Luck Bros Kopi, tucked beside the bustle of Petaling Street. It served a polished version of the Malaysian coffee-shop experience. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes. No point pretending otherwise.

Afterward we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe rented them, I was not entirely sure, and we all went inside without shoes. One of the first things I saw were more of those prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I like so much. I made a mental note to try to find where to buy them. The ceiling had an especially intricate mosaic that held my attention.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. This one did not allow photographs inside, so I do not have any, but it was very cool. Founded in 1873, it is the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur, and its entrance tower is covered with sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings.

After that we headed toward KLCC mall. We stopped in a bookstore where I found, but very much did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even converted to USD, that remained a little too committed for me. Also, where exactly was I supposed to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but threaded with wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children. We took a short train ride to TRX, where there was another and larger mall. Inside, we visited the Apple Store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back in my Airbnb because the battery was dead, but also because Apple Pay did not work for me there anyway, which was really what I used it for most back home.

We got lunch and ordered a lot. A Caesar salad was shared between three of us, and the raw vegetables felt strangely refreshing. There was also steak and some spaghetti. It was one of those mixed tables where no cuisine really wins, but everyone does.

Full and content, we all left and headed home. We had work tomorrow after all.

**Monday, May 4**

I was dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out. Since I had to get up anyway to move the car out of the garage, I figured I might as well go for a run.

In the park I met multiple stray dogs and made sure to give a couple of them ear scratches. One of them did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to stop me from walking, which was manipulative and effective.

There were also large ponds with paved walkways and even an elevated path over some wetlands along the pond's edge. I ran laps until I did not feel like running anymore, then ran along the road back to my Airbnb.

**Tuesday, May 5**

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before work. The new restaurant I had talked to the owner about, the one where I had been given a free coffee, was finally open, so I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as eggs Benedict, maybe more, and I also needed to compare it to the one I had at The Botanist, which had been, yes, eggcellent.

This version came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the one from The Botanist, and the toast was good but slightly underdone and closer to bread in some spots. Still, it was their first day open, and based on my earlier conversations with the owner I suspected they would sort things out. The service was superb, and the latte was too. Overall it was a very good shakshuka, but on that day at least I had to give the nod to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night. We decided to go to a cat cafe, or rather a "Catfe." A cafe where you get to play with cats. Sign me up.

We arrived, got our hands sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz, and were immediately surrounded by cats. Most of them were sleeping or uninterested in us, which honestly only confirmed that these were real cats. We got our drinks, which thankfully came with lids. I like cats very much. Their hair, slightly less.

Eventually we found some cats interested in us, perhaps with a little assistance from treats. We spent a long time there, long after our drinks had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea, because neither of us really wanted to leave the furry staff.

Afterward I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat. There were plenty of restaurants along the street where the Catfe was, but none of them were calling to us. I found a Chinese grocery and went in to see if they had any ginger coconut drink. I even asked one of the clerks, but she just shook her head no.

So we got in the car and I started looking at maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though when we got there they were only serving drinks or something. But next to it was BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food.

TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU felt tucked away and intimate, the sort of Japanese whisky bar that would seem more expected in Tokyo than in Kuala Lumpur. We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to cause a slight operational crisis. The woman taking our order went back and forth between us several times to confirm, then brought out a younger man who I think was a chef and spoke better English. Eventually we all agreed that yes, we wanted the non-alcoholic versions. I think mine was a mojito.

We ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu beef, true to the current hysteria around it, stole the show so completely that I ordered another plate. I took the photo in about two seconds because I was too eager to eat it properly.

**Wednesday, May 6**

Fresh off the success of the Catfe, we decided the next night to try Huskiss, the same concept but with husky dogs. I am a dog person. She is, not so much. In fact, on the way there she informed me that she is afraid of dogs.

When we arrived, I could see she was nervous and not especially happy about several large dogs coming directly over to say hello. Fair enough. But before long the dogs won. Soon she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

In a tropical city where the heat can feel like a wet blanket, Huskiss was a very strange and very funny concept: an hour spent with a pack of fluffy Arctic-looking dogs sprawled across the floor, demanding attention and accepting belly rubs as their due. It was quirky, a little ridiculous, and hard not to enjoy.

But leave we had to, because our hour was up and they were closing, and also because we were hungry. Back to Google Maps I went, and I found a place called Noodle Art. We headed that way and, as often happens in Kuala Lumpur and much of Southeast Asia, discovered that where there is one restaurant there are probably many more. That was true here too. We made a round down the street and even into a parking lot next door where food trucks formed a ring around tables. Still, after looking around, we ended up choosing the original plan: Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specialized in Sarawak noodle dishes, bringing flavors from East Malaysia into the busy food district of Petaling Jaya. Handmade noodles, Sarawak laksa, kolo mee, aromatic broths, the kind of meal built on springy noodles and comfort rather than spectacle.

**Thursday, May 7**

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a bowl of rich beef-bone soup built around a massive marrow-filled leg joint, the so-called gearbox. The broth is deep and peppery, and the whole thing arrives looking gloriously messy and not interested in being elegant.

Ordering did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. It was all in Malay, so I obviously did not understand anything either person said. After some back and forth we sat down and our two bowls arrived, and the source of confusion became clear. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but mine came with rice. I had been hinting lately that I was getting tired of rice, which was the whole reason we ordered noodles. She asked and offered to go get me noodles instead, but I decided it was fine the way it was. Also, I was hungry and this looked pretty good.

I think the beef in my soup maybe had not been cooked quite long enough. I had been expecting fully fall-off-the-bone tenderness, and some of it was, but other parts were fairly tough. The tender pieces were excellent. If the whole bowl had been like that, it would have been an A+ dinner. As it was, still very good. Solid B.

After the meal I was assigned the job of paying for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a different place. When I got there, the man at the counter said "lapan," the Malay word for eight. We stared at each other for a while until he realized I was clearly an idiot who could not even count to ten in the local language.

He started counting on his fingers. "One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight!"

Aha.

I handed him a ten-ringgit bill and he gave me two in change.

On the way out, we also ordered some takeaway for my two cousins who would be arriving that night. There was more confusion as we tried to order just broth and noodles, no meat. They either did not understand or pretended not to, and gave us meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my Airbnb, my two cousins texted that they were at the airport. Then they texted that they were confused about how this whole Grab thing worked. Then another text came saying they had managed to order a Grab, but had selected pay with cash and did I happen to have any?

Eventually they arrived. We went back upstairs and I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, leaving out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. By the time we were done it was getting close to one in the morning. Very late for the two locals, maybe not so much for the newcomers.
