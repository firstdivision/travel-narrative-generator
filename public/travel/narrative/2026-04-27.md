## April 27 to May 9

This title is a little different than the others because it spans many days.

I had stopped journaling during this stretch, and it was for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started hanging out more and more, going somewhere every night after work. I found myself with neither the time nor much desire to write things down. I wanted to spend that time with her instead. So this became a kind of mega-post for that stretch of days.

## Monday, April 27 - Sunset Stories in the Park

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I'm sure there was breakfast, but I don't remember what it was. Maybe I was too tired for breakfast and just got a latte and a Grab to work. That feels plausible.

After work, we went to a nearby park. It was a nice one, with wide curved pathways that seemed built for walking slowly, talking without purpose, and making up stories because the evening left room for them. Sunset had started doing its usual work on everything, and we took turns finding new angles for sunset pictures while also asking each other questions, the kind that are partly casual and partly not.

At some point we met a cat, and from there we made up a story about two people who lost track of time, got locked in the park after the gates closed, and couldn't leave.

The story turned into *The Magic Tree in the Park*. A couple found a tree and crawled under it for shelter as the sun went down. They heard a noise and got startled, only to realize it was the cat they had met earlier. Relief lasted about two seconds because then the cat spoke.

"Thank you for the pets earlier! They were magnificent!"

Before they could recover, a squirrel arrived and spoke too.

"Oh cat! Leave those two alone!" the squirrel said.

"I am not bothering them, I was thanking them!" the cat replied.

After that, the couple spent the whole night talking with animals. An owl. A tiny ant. A moth who fluttered in, stayed a while, contributed whatever a moth contributes to a nighttime conversation, then flew away again. By morning the couple had fallen asleep, and when they woke up the cat and squirrel were asleep beside them, the cat softly purring even in sleep. Everyone said goodbye, but the couple promised to return. And they did, for years, eventually bringing their kids. Cat had grown old by then, his fur slightly gray, and he introduced them to his wife and kittens. The story kept going, as good nonsense tends to do.

For us, there was no dramatic overnight imprisonment in a magical park. We got in the car and drove home, still taking pictures of the sunset on the way out.

## Tuesday, April 28 - Suitcases and a Bowling Tiebreaker

That night we went to a mall because I needed new luggage to replace the wounded one I had been dragging around. Before we found the luggage store, though, we found a music store. I had been pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and I wanted to find it, and we did: a Yamaha store full of guitars and pianos.

I played a couple songs on guitar and completely forgot the words to one of the verses, which felt normal enough for someone who maybe had not practiced in quite a while. I also sat at a piano and played the standard chords I always seem to play first when I sit down in front of one. Every musician has some version of this, I think. I certainly do.

Eventually we found a store with lots of luggage options, including Malaysian brands, which seemed like the appropriate thing to buy while in Malaysia. The employees gathered around me until there were four of them, all producing suitcase after suitcase and explaining the virtues of each one. This one had a front zipper. This one was also very nice. Everyone was extremely helpful and very determined that I should not leave without understanding the full moral character of each suitcase. Eventually I settled on a new hardcase carry-on, which felt like a meaningful improvement over the old battered one.

There was also bowling.

I had not bowled in quite a while, and I had forgotten how much fun it is once I admit to myself that I am not very good at it. We originally planned on one game, but of course one game became two. By then we had each won one, which meant we obviously needed a tiebreaker because we are apparently people who take unserious things seriously once scorekeeping is involved. I won the third game, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was basically a tie.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. It was good, but I have to be honest, the memory of the crispy chicken in the Singapore airport was still hanging over everything like an unfair comparison.

## Thursday, April 30 - Work Lunch, Then Fries Anyway

At work I was treated to lunch by my new work friends. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered a table full of dishes to share, what I called homestyle, meaning everything went in the middle and everyone took what they wanted. It is one of the better ways to eat, especially when you are still learning what shows up on a Malaysian table and in what order.

There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I wasn't sure what it was called, though some quick Googling later made me think it might have been a Peranakan-style curry. Whatever it was, I liked it a lot. It had heat, enough heat that I may even have earned some points for handling it well.

Afterward my friend and I met up near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think it was some variation on another chicken-and-rice situation, but when I saw a plate of french fries go by, I couldn't resist and ordered those too. Sometimes the body wants local cuisine and also fries. These are not contradictory urges.

## Friday, May 1st - Wet Socks, VIP Rope, Mountain Neon

We had a double-header planned that night.

First was Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks. The huge golden statue of Lord Murugan stands out before the climb, and then there are the colorful 272 steps leading up to the main cave. It is a place that is both a major tourist stop and an active Hindu pilgrimage site, and inside the immense limestone cavern opens toward the sky through natural gaps in the rock. Even with crowds, it has scale that makes people look temporary.

After we made our way up the first set of stairs, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend, who had been there before, said she would wait there if I wanted to go do the second part. I wasn't sure why.

Then I found out.

The second part required taking off your shoes, even though I noticed plenty of people not doing it. But I am, unfortunately, a rule follower in these situations, so off came the shoes. For some reason, though, I decided to keep my socks on. This had worked just fine back at the temple near the base of the stairs, so why wouldn't it work here, inside the cave with water dripping down the walls? Excellent question.

As I climbed the second set of stairs, I tried dodging puddles, but you can only dodge so much. My socks got wetter and wetter. I kept going anyway and made it up to the inner part of the temple, but by then I was no longer enjoying this activity. Wet socks have a way of narrowing the soul. Then I stepped in a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my spiritual ascent. I tried not to think too hard about it.

When I got back, I told her, "I maybe should not have left my socks on," and showed her the dark wet bottoms of what had once been white socks. She gave me the sort of knowing smile reserved for people watching someone else make avoidable mistakes in real time.

We went back down the stairs and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold," and the cold ones were only about one ringgit more, which made the decision feel almost insulting in its obviousness. We drank them and then tried to scoop out the coconut meat. We were successful, somewhat.

Back at the car I started taking off my shoes and those now-unholy socks and said I would probably just wear the shoes barefoot for the rest of the day. But she had spare socks with her. Clean ones. New ones. Warm and soft. Maybe she knew I would do something like this. Maybe everyone comes out of Batu Caves wanting a fresh pair of socks. Either way, it was an act of mercy.

From there we drove to Genting Highlands, and my job was to pick music for the drive. Genting sits high in the Titiwangsa Range, about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, all cool temperatures and mist and hill-station atmosphere, except also malls, casinos, theme parks, and enough entertainment infrastructure to suggest that someone asked, "What if a mountain resort also had every possible distraction?" and no one said no.

We were hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. In the moment I thought it was the same place I had gone to in Tokyo, but later research revealed that Tokyo had been Ichiran. Close enough to fool a tired brain, not close enough to be true. Still, the ramen was very good, hot and comforting and exactly what I wanted in the cool mountain air.

After that we headed for the cable car and were immediately confronted with a gigantic line. The kind that zigzags back and forth in a way that makes time look organized. A Disney line. There were actually two of them, one for tickets and one for boarding. Then we discovered there were VIP tickets you could buy online to skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible. Truly. The tickets were not that much more expensive, so I bought two on the spot.

We showed the QR codes to an attendant. A red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway with windows looking out over the full line twisting back and forth. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich.

The cable car carried us up over the forest to the top of the mountain, where the attractions and mall waited. Inside, the bright neon lights of stores and carnival rides felt almost absurd against the green misty hills outside. And yes, actual carnival rides inside the mall. Ferris wheel, games, all of it sitting there on top of a mountain like someone lost a county fair in the clouds.

We played some games. There was an archery place and a Halo game with racecar-style seats, big screens, and mounted guns tracking your aim. We also played two basketball games. In the first one we did pretty well, sinking twenty-something balls. In the second one we did much worse, mostly because we had chosen a different machine and it had maybe half as many balls in it. I remain convinced previous players had transferred balls from one machine to the other in pursuit of arcade dominance.

With the final bit of credit left on the plastic game card, we searched for something cheap enough to play and landed on a Street Fighter-style game. It turned out to be the best value of the lot. I made it through multiple fights on a single play before finally reaching an opponent who absolutely clobbered me in all three bouts. I had no chance at all. Money well spent.

On the way back down we stopped at the midpoint landing to see Chin Swee Caves Temple. Suspended in the mist below Genting's hotels and casinos, it felt like a different world entirely. Pagodas, incense, a towering Buddha statue, prayer halls, gardens, mountain views over the rainforest. After the synthetic noise of the mall, it was a welcome reset.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down, and while walking back to the car I spotted a candy store and ducked inside. They had chocolates of all kinds, including durian-flavored ones, which I could not resist buying for my cousins when they arrived. I also got more normal options like almond, sea salt, and mint, in case I decided not to greet them solely with edible provocation.

Leaving Genting was like leaving a ski resort at closing time. Cars everywhere, everyone departing at once, that collective sluggish escape from fun into traffic. We both admitted we had headaches, maybe from the altitude, maybe from the long day, maybe from the neon plus mountain plus cave plus arcade combination. Eventually traffic cleared and we made the hour-and-a-half drive back to Kuala Lumpur.

## Sunday, May 3 - Breakfast, Temples, and a Book I Was Not Buying

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore I had been supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run, which I missed. This time the 9 a.m. breakfast meet felt much more realistic, a schedule for people who have at least accepted their own limitations.

We met at Luck Bros Kopi, near the bustle of Petaling Street. It felt like a polished version of the Malaysian coffee shop experience, and I ordered an egg and tomato dish and a latte. I do like my lattes. This is not in dispute.

After breakfast we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe rented them, I wasn't quite sure, and we all went inside without shoes. One of the first things I saw was more of those prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I like so much. I made a mental note to figure out where to buy them. The ceiling had especially intricate mosaic work, and I stood there looking up at it longer than I probably needed to.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown, the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur, founded in 1873. Its entrance tower is covered in sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings. Photography inside wasn't allowed, so I don't have pictures, but it was impressive inside in a way that doesn't particularly need my camera to validate it.

After that we headed toward KLCC mall. Along the way we stopped in a bookstore where I found, but definitely did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even after converting it to USD, it remained firmly in the category of absolutely not. Also, where would I have put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but also full of wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children. The contrast was very Kuala Lumpur: polished towers overhead, greenery and humid pathways below.

We took a short train ride to TRX, where there was another, larger mall. Inside, we visited the Apple Store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at the Airbnb because its battery was dead, but also because Apple Pay didn't work there for me anyway, which is mostly what I use it for back home.

We had lunch there too, with several things ordered and shared: a Caesar salad between three of us, which felt like a welcome delivery of raw vegetables into my life, plus steak and some spaghetti.

After that, all of us full and pleasantly worn down, we left and headed home. We did have work tomorrow after all.

## Monday, May 4 - Run, Dogs, and One Very Persuasive Paw

I got dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out. Since I had to get up anyway to move the car out of the garage, I figured I might as well go for a run too.

The park had large ponds with paved walkways and even an elevated path over some kind of wetlands along the pond's edge. I ran laps along those paths until I didn't feel like running anymore, which is usually how I determine the proper end of a run.

I also met multiple stray dogs and made sure to give a couple of them ear scratches. One of them did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to stop me from walking away. It was not an efficient delay tactic, but it was emotionally effective.

Afterward I ran back along the road to my Airbnb.

## Tuesday, May 5 - Shakshuka, Cats, and Wagyu at the Whisky Bar

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before work. The new restaurant I had previously talked to the owner about, the one where I had been given a free coffee, was finally open, so I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as Eggs Benedict, maybe more, and I was immediately comparing it to the one I had at The Botanist, which had been, yes, "eggcellent."

This version came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as The Botanist's version, and the toast was slightly underdone, crossing over into bread territory in places. But it was also their first day open, and based on my earlier conversation with the owner I suspected they would iron things out. The service was superb, and the latte was too. Overall it was a very good shakshuka, but on that day I still had to give the edge to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night, and we decided to go to a "Catfe," a cafe where you get to play with cats. A concept I accepted immediately and without reservation.

We arrived, got our hands sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz, and were promptly surrounded by cats. Most of them were either sleeping or not particularly interested in us, which was reassuring evidence that these were real cats and not some sort of hospitality staff in fur coats.

Our drinks came with lids, thankfully. I like cats very much. I am less enthusiastic about cat hair in beverages. Eventually we found a few cats willing to engage with us, perhaps encouraged by treats, perhaps merely bored enough to permit affection.

We stayed a long time, well after the coffees had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea both sitting there abandoned as we kept petting cats and not leaving. Neither of us seemed eager to go.

Afterward I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat. There were plenty of restaurants along the same street, but none of them called to us. I found a Chinese grocery and went in to see if I could find any ginger coconut drink. I even asked one of the store clerks, but she just shook her head no.

Back in the car, I started studying maps and found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though I don't fully remember because when we got there they were only serving drinks or something close to that. Next to it, though, was TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food.

It felt tucked away and intimate, more like something I would have stumbled into in Tokyo than in Kuala Lumpur. We ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to create a small but noticeable amount of confusion in a Japanese whisky bar. The woman taking our order went back and forth between us a few times to confirm, then eventually brought over a younger man, I think a chef, whose English was better. We finally established that yes, we did indeed want the non-alcoholic versions. I think I had a mojito.

For food we ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu stole the show exactly the way current hysteria around wagyu would suggest it might. It was rich and soft and gone very quickly. I ordered another plate. The attached photo I took of it later did not do it justice because I had snapped it in about two seconds, too eager to eat to bother composing anything properly.

## Wednesday, May 6 - Huskies First, Then Noodles

Fresh off the success of the Catfe, we decided to go to a place called Huskiss, the same basic concept but with husky dogs instead of cats. I am a dog person. She, not so much. In fact, she told me on the way that she is afraid of dogs.

When we got there I could tell she was nervous, and not especially happy about the large dogs coming right up to say hello. But dogs have a way of wearing people down through persistence and fluff, and before long she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

In a tropical city where the afternoon heat can feel like a wet blanket, spending an hour in a room full of huskies was slightly absurd in the best way. They posed for photos, demanded attention, sprawled across the floor, and generally acted like oversized fur rugs with opinions.

But eventually we had to leave, partly because our hour was up and partly because they were closing, but also because we were hungry. Back onto Google Maps I went, and I found a place called Noodle Art. We headed that way, though as is often the case in Kuala Lumpur and in Southeast Asia generally, one restaurant turned out to mean many restaurants clustered in the same area.

We wandered down the street and even into a parking lot next door where food trucks formed a ring around shared tables. After making the rounds and considering our options, we decided on the original plan after all and went to Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specializes in Sarawak noodle dishes, bringing flavors from East Malaysia into a busy Petaling Jaya food district. The setting was bright and casual, and the whole place had the feeling of a neighborhood favorite rather than something curated for effect.

## Thursday, May 7 - Gearbox Soup and Midnight Cousins

Date night took us to Sup Gearbox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a bowl of beef-bone soup built around a giant marrow-filled leg joint called a gearbox. It arrives looking serious. The broth was deep and savory and peppery, and the whole thing had the kind of glorious messiness that suggests eating it with dignity is not really an option.

Ordering, however, did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. The whole exchange was in Malay, so I obviously understood none of it. After some back and forth, we sat down and our bowls arrived, at which point the source of the confusion became clear. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but what came instead was rice. I had been hinting lately that I was getting a little tired of rice, which was exactly why we had ordered noodles. She asked and offered to go get it fixed, but I decided it was fine as it was. I was hungry, and it looked pretty good.

The beef in my soup, I think, maybe had not been cooked quite long enough. I had expected fully fall-off-the-bone tenderness, and some of it was that, soft and excellent, but other parts were fairly tough. If it had all been like the tender pieces, it would have been an A+ dinner. As it was, it was still very good. A solid B.

After the meal I was sent to pay for our drinks, which for some reason had come from and needed to be paid for at a different place. When I got to the counter, the man said "lapan," the Malay word for eight.

We then had a long, silent moment in which we both looked at each other and he realized I was apparently incapable of even counting to ten in the local language.

So he switched tactics and counted on his fingers. "One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight."

Aha.

I handed him a ten-ringgit bill and he gave me two back. International understanding restored.

On the way out we also ordered some takeaway for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. We wanted just broth and noodles, no meat. This either was not understood or was politely ignored, because they gave us meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my Airbnb, my cousins texted that they were at the airport. Great. Then they texted that they were confused about how this whole Grab thing worked. Less great. Then another message came saying they had managed to order one but had selected "pay with cash" and did I happen to have any?

Eventually they arrived, and we all went back upstairs to the Airbnb, where I warmed up the beef and noodles for them and left out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that same night. By the time we finished it was getting close to one in the morning. Very late for the two locals. Maybe not quite so late for the newcomers.
