## April 27 to May 9 - sunsets, soup, and several questionable plans

This title is a little different than the others because it spans many days. I had stopped journaling during this stretch, and for a very good reason. The "new friend" I had alluded to earlier and I started hanging out more and more, going somewhere almost every night after work. I found myself with neither the time nor much desire to write things down. I mostly wanted to spend that time with her instead. So this became a kind of mega-post, stitched together from memory and whatever details still stuck.

**Monday, April 27**

It was Monday, my first day back from Singapore. I worked, of course. I am sure there was breakfast, but I don't remember what it was. Maybe I was too tired to bother and just got a latte and a Grab to work instead. That feels plausible.

After work we went to a park nearby, a good park for slow walking and talking, with wide curved pathways that seemed built for exactly that kind of evening. The sun was dropping while we wandered, and we kept taking turns spotting new sunset angles and new places to stop for photos. We were also asking each other questions, the kind that feel casual at first and then gradually less casual.

At some point we met a cat, and from there we made up a story about two people who lose track of time in the park, the gates close, and they get locked in for the night.

Under what we called the magic tree, the couple in the story crawled in for shelter as the light faded. Then they heard a noise and got scared, only to realize it was the same cat they had met earlier. That should have been reassuring enough, except then the cat spoke.

"Thank you for the pets earlier. They were magnificent."

Before they could recover, a squirrel arrived and spoke too, scolding the cat for bothering them. Then the whole park opened up. An owl. A tiny ant. A moth that fluttered by, stayed to talk a while, then flew off again. The couple spent the whole night talking with the animals, fell asleep under the tree, and woke at sunrise with the cat and squirrel asleep beside them, the cat softly purring. They promised to come back, and in the story they did, for many years, eventually even bringing their children. By then the cat was old, his fur gone slightly gray, and he introduced them to his wife and kittens.

The story kept going because neither of us seemed in any hurry to end it.

For us, though, there was no overnight enchantment and no actual lock-in. We got in the car and drove home, still taking sunset pictures on the way out.

**Tuesday, April 28**

That night we went to a mall because I needed new luggage to replace my wounded old one.

Before we found the luggage store, we found a music store. I had been pretty sure there was one in there somewhere, and there it was: a Yamaha store, all guitars and pianos and the quiet danger of letting me touch instruments in public. I played a couple songs on guitar and, as tradition apparently requires, forgot the words to one of the verses completely. Then I sat at a piano and played the standard chords I always seem to play first. Every musician has those little reflexes, I think. I am certainly no exception.

Eventually we found a luggage store with plenty of options, including Malaysian brands, which felt like the appropriate thing to buy. Soon there were four employees involved, producing suitcase after suitcase and demonstrating the virtues of each one. This one has a front zipper. This one is also very nice. There is something oddly intimate about several strangers earnestly trying to sell you a carry-on. I finally settled on one I liked, a hardcase carry-on that felt like a proper upgrade.

There was also bowling. I had not bowled in quite a while and had forgotten how much fun it is once I admit to myself that I am not good at it. We planned to play one game, then somehow that became two. By that point we had each won one, so obviously the only reasonable thing to do was play a tiebreaker. I won that one, but by such a narrow margin that we agreed it was basically a tie.

Dinner was in the mall food court: chicken rice and broth. Good, definitely, but still living in the shadow of the crispy chicken at the Singapore airport. Not entirely fair, but that is how memory works.

**Thursday, April 30th**

At work I was treated to lunch by my new work friends. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered what I think of as homestyle, meaning plates and bowls all landed in the middle and everyone helped themselves. Shared food always reveals something about how a table functions. There were many dishes, but my favorite was a yellow spicy curry. I am not sure what it was called. My later Googling suggested maybe Peranakan-style curry. Whatever it was, I liked it a lot. I may also have earned a few points by handling the spice without obvious distress.

Afterward my friend and I met up near where I was staying and had dinner at a nearby restaurant. I think it was some variation on another chicken and rice dish, but when I saw a plate of french fries go by, I lost focus immediately and ordered those too. Sometimes the body wants novelty. Sometimes it wants potatoes.

**Friday, May 1st**

This was a double-header kind of night.

First we went to Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks, a series of limestone caves and cave temples just outside Kuala Lumpur. Visitors are greeted by the enormous golden statue of Lord Murugan before climbing the colorful 272-step staircase up to the main cathedral cave. Inside, the cavern opens upward through natural gaps in the rock, and sunlight comes down in dramatic shafts onto shrines and worshippers and, yes, the occasional monkey. It is not just a tourist stop. It is an active Hindu pilgrimage site, and the center of the Thaipusam festival, when huge numbers of devotees gather there.

Then we got back in the car and headed to Genting Highlands, with my assigned job being to choose the music on the drive. A grave responsibility.

Genting Highlands sits high in the Titiwangsa Range, about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, where the air is cooler and the hills are wrapped in mist and forest. It is part hill station, part resort city, with hotels, casinos, shopping, theme parks, and enough entertainment to make you briefly forget you are on a mountain. Many people arrive by cable car, gliding over dense rainforest and up into the clouds.

We were hungry first, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. In the moment I thought it might be the same place I had gone to in Tokyo, but later research informed me that I was thinking of Ichiran. Close enough to confuse a tired person, apparently. In any case, the ramen was very good. It warmed us up and gave us the energy for the next round.

Then we went straight for the cable car and were immediately met with a gigantic line, the kind that folds back on itself over and over until time loses shape. A Disney line. In fact there were two of them, one for tickets and one for boarding. We also discovered there were VIP tickets online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible. This is one of my less noble but more consistent values.

I checked the price, saw they were not much more expensive than the regular ones, and bought two. We showed the QR codes to an attendant, a red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway while looking through windows at the whole packed queue twisting back and forth beside us. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich. Or at least mildly upgraded.

The cable car took us to the top of the mountain, where the attractions and mall sat in the clouds. Inside, bright neon from shops and carnival rides hit with strange force against the green misty hills outside. And yes, carnival rides in the mall. Ferris wheel, games, the whole thing. Genting does not seem interested in choosing one identity when it can have six.

We played games inside. There was an archery place, and a Halo shooter setup with racecar-style seats, giant screens, and mounted guns tracking your aim. We also played two basketball games. In the first we did pretty well, making twenty-something shots. In the second we switched machines and did much worse, which I blame at least partly on the fact that the new machine had about half as many balls in it. I strongly suspect previous players had redistributed the inventory in the interests of competitive advantage. The arcade economy is ruthless.

With the last little bit of credit left on the plastic game card, we could only afford one cheap game, a Street Fighter-style fighting game. It turned out to be the best value of the lot. I made it through multiple fights on a single play before finally reaching an opponent who clobbered me in all three bouts. Total destruction. Money well spent.

After that we went back to the cable car for the ride down, but stopped at a midpoint station to see Chin Swee Caves Temple. Rising out of the mist below Genting's casinos and hotels, it felt like a separate world entirely, all pagodas and incense and giant Buddha statues hanging over a sea of rainforest. It has a towering Buddha, a nine-story pagoda, prayer halls, gardens, and wide views out across the mountains. After the noise and neon, it felt calm in a way that was almost suspicious.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down. On the walk back to the car, I saw a candy store and ducked in. There were chocolates of all kinds, including durian-flavored ones, which I could not resist buying for my cousins when they arrived. I also bought more conventional ones too: almond, sea salt, mint. I am not reckless all the time.

Leaving Genting felt a lot like leaving a ski resort. Cars everywhere, everyone trying to escape at once, traffic compressing into one shared headache. In fact we both admitted we had headaches. Maybe altitude, maybe just a long day. Eventually the traffic loosened and we made the drive back to Kuala Lumpur, which took a little more than an hour and a half.

**Sunday, May 3**

I woke early to meet my coworkers in downtown Kuala Lumpur for breakfast and a walkabout. The weekend I went to Singapore, I had been supposed to meet them for a 7 a.m. run, which I missed. This time the plan was breakfast at 9 a.m., a much more civilized proposal.

We met at Luck Bros Kopi. Tucked near the bustle of Petaling Street, it offered a polished version of the Malaysian coffee shop experience, with strong kopi, fragrant nasi lemak, and a menu moving easily between Chinese, Malay, and Peranakan influences. I ordered an egg and tomato dish and, naturally, a latte. I do like my lattes.

Afterward we walked to a nearby temple. The girls put on loaner robes, or maybe they had to rent them, I am not entirely sure, and we all went inside without shoes. One of the first things I noticed was more of those little prayer saucer things burning oil from a wick, which I continue to like disproportionately. I made a mental note to find out where to buy them. The ceiling had an especially intricate mosaic that kept pulling my eyes upward.

Around the corner was another temple, the famous Sri Maha Mariamman Temple in Chinatown. This one did not allow photographs inside, so I do not have any, but it was very cool. Founded in 1873, it is the oldest Hindu temple in Kuala Lumpur. Its entrance tower rises 22.9 meters and is covered in more than 200 sculpted figures of Hindu gods, goddesses, and mythological beings in the South Indian Dravidian style. During Thaipusam it serves as the starting point for the big pilgrimage procession to Batu Caves.

After that we headed toward KLCC mall. We stopped in a bookstore where I found, but very much did not buy, a book set priced at 3,561 ringgit. Even after converting that into U.S. dollars, it remained a bit ambitious for my blood. Also, where exactly was I going to put it?

There was also a large outdoor park nearby, dominated on one side by the Petronas Towers but threaded with wooded running paths, water features, and even a small water park for children. It felt designed to remind people that a city can try to contain multiple realities at once.

From there we took a short train ride to TRX, where there is another, larger mall. We visited the Apple store and looked at Apple Watches. Mine was back at my Airbnb with a dead battery, and also Apple Pay did not work there for me anyway, which is mostly what I use it for at home. A watch that cannot pay for things starts to feel suspiciously like a watch.

We also had lunch, with a lot of dishes ordered. Three of us shared a Caesar salad, and the raw vegetables felt genuinely welcome at that point. There was also steak and some spaghetti.

With full stomachs, we all headed home. We had work the next day after all.

**Monday, May 4**

I got dropped off at a park near me that someone had pointed out. I had to get up anyway to move the car out of the garage, so I figured I might as well go for a run too.

The park had large ponds with paved walkways and even an elevated path over wetlands along the pond's edge. I ran laps there until I did not feel like running anymore, then ran along the road back to my Airbnb. Along the way I met multiple stray dogs and made sure to stop and give a couple of them ear scratches. One of them did not want me to leave and kept putting his paw on my foot to stop me from walking. It was not an efficient system, but emotionally it was quite effective.

**Tuesday, May 5**

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before work. The new restaurant I had spoken to the owner about, the one where I had once been given a free coffee, was finally open, so I decided to try it.

I saw shakshuka on the menu and could not resist. I may like shakshuka almost as much as Eggs Benedict, maybe more, which is not something I say lightly. I also had to compare it to the one I had at The Botanist, which had been, regrettably, eggcellent.

This version came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the Botanist version, and the toast was good but a bit underdone, closer to plain bread in some spots. Still, it was also their first day open, and based on my conversations with the owner I suspected they would sort things out. The service was superb, and the latte was too. Overall it was a very good shakshuka, though on this particular day I still had to give the nod to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night, this time at a "Catfe," a cafe where you get to hang out with cats. An excellent business model, in my opinion.

When we arrived, our hands were sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz, and then we were immediately surrounded by cats. Most of them were sleeping or completely uninterested in us, which was honestly reassuring. These were clearly real cats. No one had briefed them for customer engagement.

We got our drinks, thankfully with lids. I like cats, but not enough to invite their fur directly into my latte. Eventually we found some cats who were interested in us too, possibly with a little assistance from treats. We stayed there a long time, well after the drinks had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea both abandoned to the higher calling of petting small indifferent mammals. Neither of us seemed to want to leave.

Afterward I was given the responsibility of finding dinner. There were lots of restaurants on the same street as the Catfe, but none of them really called to us. I found a Chinese grocery and went in to see if I could locate any ginger coconut drink. I even tried asking one of the clerks, but she just shook her head no.

So we got back in the car and I started consulting maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori, though when we got there they were only serving drinks or something along those lines. Next to it, though, was TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU, and they were still serving food.

Tucked away near Jalan Ampang, it felt more like a Japanese whisky bar you might stumble into in Tokyo than anything I expected to find that night in Kuala Lumpur. The space was intimate and dim in that deliberate way, offering Japanese whiskies, highballs, cocktails, teppanyaki, yakitori, and small plates. It had that late-night hideaway feeling where conversation naturally slows.

We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to create more confusion than I expected in a Japanese whisky bar. The woman taking our order went back and forth between us a few times to confirm, then eventually brought out a younger man, who I think was a chef and spoke better English. We finally established, yes, we really did want the non-alcoholic versions. I think mine was a mojito.

We ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu absolutely stole the show, enough that I ordered another plate of it. It was so good that the photo I took of it does not do it justice, partly because I took it in about two seconds, too eager to start eating.

**Wednesday, May 6**

Fresh off the momentum of the Catfe, we decided to visit Huskiss, which is the same concept except with husky dogs. I am a dog person. She, not so much. In fact, on the way there she informed me that she is afraid of dogs.

When we arrived I could tell she was nervous, and not at all thrilled by large dogs coming over to say hello. But the dogs won, as dogs often do. Before long she was smiling and petting the black-and-white huskies.

In a tropical city where the heat can feel like a wet blanket, Huskiss was an absurdly specific kind of detour, an hour spent with fluffy Arctic-looking dogs who seemed equally interested in posing for photos, demanding attention, and collapsing on the floor in search of belly rubs. It was quirky and a little ridiculous and hard not to enjoy.

But eventually we had to leave, partly because our hour was up and partly because they were closing, but mostly because we were hungry. Back onto Google Maps I went. I found a place called Noodle Art, and we headed that direction. Like many places in Kuala Lumpur and Southeast Asia generally, one restaurant turned out to mean many restaurants clustered nearby. We made a full round of the street and even checked a parking lot next door where food trucks formed a ring around some tables. In the end, after all that, we went with the original plan and chose Noodle Art SS2.

Noodle Art specializes in Sarawak noodle dishes, bringing East Malaysian flavors into the busy food district of Petaling Jaya. Handmade noodles, rich Sarawak laksa, kolo mee, and other regional dishes are the focus there, simple and satisfying in the best way.

**Thursday, May 7**

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya.

The specialty there is a rich beef-bone soup built around a massive marrow-filled leg joint known as a gearbox. The broth is slow-simmered and deep and savory, slightly peppery, and the meal arrives looking like a project as much as a dinner. Part soup, part treasure hunt.

Ordering did not go as smoothly as either of us had hoped. It was all done in Malay, so I obviously did not understand anything anyone was saying. After some back and forth, we sat down and our bowls arrived, at which point the source of the confusion became obvious. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup, but mine came with rice instead. I had been hinting lately that I was getting tired of rice, which was exactly why we had ordered noodles. She offered to go get the noodles fixed, but I decided it was fine as it was. Mostly because I was hungry and the bowl in front of me already looked pretty good.

The beef in my soup seemed like it maybe had not been cooked quite long enough. I had expected everything to be falling off the bone, but parts of it were fairly tough. A few pieces were very tender and those were excellent. If the whole bowl had been like that, it would have been an A-plus dinner. As it was, still very good. A solid B.

After the meal I got sent to pay for the drinks, which for some reason had come from and needed to be paid for at a different place. At the counter, the man said "lapan," the Malay word for eight. Then we both stared at each other for a while until he realized I was clearly an idiot who could not even count to ten in the local language.

So he switched strategies and started counting on his fingers. "One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight."

Aha. Communication restored.

I gave him a ten-ringgit bill and he handed me two back.

On the way out we also ordered some takeaway for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. We wanted just broth and noodles, no meat. This generated another round of confusion, and whether they did not understand or simply chose not to understand, we ended up getting meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back at my Airbnb, my two cousins texted that they were at the airport. Great. Then they were confused about how Grab worked. Less great. Then another text came saying they had managed to order a Grab and were in it, but had selected cash payment and did I happen to have any cash?

Eventually they arrived, and we all went upstairs to the Airbnb, where I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, leaving out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. By the time we were done it was getting close to one in the morning. Very late for the two locals. Maybe not quite as late for the new arrivals.
