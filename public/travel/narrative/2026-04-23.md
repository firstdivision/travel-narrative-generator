## Thursday, April 23 - Cardamom, Rain, and a 2 A.M. Decision

I went to work and, at lunch, ate with the work crew at a new restaurant called India Portal. I ordered a coffee drink whose name I of course cannot remember, which feels consistent with how travel has been going, but I do remember exactly what mattered about it. Cardamom. Warm, fragrant, almost sweet without being sweet, and immediately enough to win me over. Cardamom is one of the best spices in the world, and I feel qualified to say that because my family makes cardamom bread. If you hand someone a fresh warm loaf of that bread, there is a real chance they will respond with something like, “this is the best [expletive] bread I’ve ever had in my life,” which is in fact a thing that once happened when my mother gave someone a loaf.

So I was already in a good mood before the food even arrived.

I also ordered a braised lamb shank, and for reasons I did not fully examine at the time, I gladly accepted a plate that came with not one but two kinds of rice. White rice and biryani rice, both piled up in quantities that made the single lamb shank look outnumbered and faintly doomed. By this point I had already started to feel a little worn down by how many meals here arrive with rice, beside rice, under rice, or in some other way dominated by rice, and this dish did nothing to challenge that pattern. Still, it was very good. The lamb was tender enough to come apart without much argument, and it sat in a dark, rich sauce that matched the rice perfectly. I couldn’t finish either mound of rice, despite trying, but the lamb shank was worth the effort. And that cardamom coffee, with its other spices I also failed to identify properly, absolutely stole my heart.

Later that night I met up with my new friend for a Kuala Lumpur night bus tour. It was the same bus used for the daytime hop-on hop-off city tours, except this version was a roughly two-hour drive through the city after dark, with two stops where everyone could get off, walk around, and take pictures. It sounded touristy in a way I was willing to forgive because I was tired and curious, which is honestly when I make a lot of my travel decisions.

About twenty minutes before the bus was supposed to arrive, there was a tremendously loud crack across the sky. Not a subtle rumble. A full theatrical warning shot. Thunder and rain were coming, and then, almost immediately, they arrived with real conviction. The rain came down in torrential sheets and turned the road into something that looked less like a street and more like an impatient brown river. Everyone waiting for the bus crowded under an overhang, pressed into that temporary little alliance that forms whenever weather makes fools of everyone equally. And still, one by one, people darted out into the downpour to record quick videos. Not even torrential rain can stop us from posting to the socials. If anything, it seems to improve the material.

When the bus finally arrived, my friend and I took seats on the inside because it was still raining hard. But not for long. Soon the rain eased off, and we migrated upstairs to the open top section, where the seats were still slightly wet. We wiped them down as best we could with our hands, which is not an especially effective drying method, but by then we didn’t care. We sat anyway.

The bus rolled past many of Kuala Lumpur’s landmarks lit up at night, including the KL Tower, the Petronas Towers, and KLCC. We also passed a temple whose name completely escaped me, which was mildly annoying, though I assume the photos will eventually rescue me from my own memory. That has become a recurring arrangement between me and my camera. I forget first, then hope to be corrected later.

After the tour, we spent some time walking around Central KL. The city had that late-night energy where nothing feels finished yet. We came across live music at two different intersections, two separate bands with drums and amplified voices sending sound down the roads and off the buildings. The music drifted through the streets and pulled us toward the crowd almost before we had decided to go. We kept walking, following the noise.

At some point we found a shawarma place and ordered shawarma fries and a soda for takeaway. Yes, takeaway. Not to-go. I’m assimilating.

Later that night, back at my apartment, or more accurately the next day at around 2 a.m., I realized that it was now Friday, I had no plans for the weekend, and this seemed like a poor use of geography. Family and friends back home had been texting me with very strong opinions that I should go to Singapore for the weekend. At two in the morning, this seemed not only reasonable but obvious.

So I booked a flight and a hotel, then went to sleep.

I still had to work later that day, which felt like a problem for future me. But at the end of it, I’d be getting on a plane to another new country.
