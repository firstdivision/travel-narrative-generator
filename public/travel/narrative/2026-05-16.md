## Saturday, May 16 - Coconut Coffee, Hard Lessons, and CLUNK CLUNK CLUNK

```poem
I checked the hard box, a confident fool,
Got folded like laundry by spa-side rule.
Paid dearly for martinis, then slept by a thunk,
As Hanoi rolled onward in a clatter and clunk.
```

We slept in, which felt earned, and had breakfast at the hotel buffet again. There was something reassuring about starting the day in the same way, with the same easy hotel rhythm, before heading back out into Hanoi for one more stretch of wandering.

The plan was practical enough. We went to Hanoi train station to drop off our bags in a locker so we could explore the city a bit more before the overnight train. That done, we went to Kong Coffee for another Vietnamese coconut coffee, because once had clearly not been enough. It was still cold and rich and slightly excessive in exactly the right way, the kind of drink that makes you forgive heat, traffic, and your own bad planning for a little while.

From there we walked around toward the old train station, drifting more than navigating. Hanoi kept doing that thing it does, where one street feels frantic and tangled and then a turn later something older and quieter rises out of it. St. Joseph's Cathedral appeared like that, its tall twin bell towers lifting unexpectedly above the shopfronts and wires of the Old Quarter. It was large and neo-Gothic and very French in origin, built in the late 1800s during the colonial period, and one of the oldest churches in Vietnam. Up close, the details were all there: arched windows, ribbed vaulting, stained glass, stone darkened by age. The whole exterior looked weathered in a way that felt honest rather than decorative, streaked by humidity and rain and pollution and decades of tropical weather. It did not look preserved so much as it looked like it had simply endured.

At some point it started to rain, which made our next decision feel both opportunistic and completely justified. We found a place called Orient Spa and went in for massages because, really, why not. I signed up for the Thai massage and, in a moment of confidence unsupported by either wisdom or experience, checked the "hard" box to indicate how much pulling and stretching I wanted. This turned out to be a poor but educational choice. Quite a lot of it was genuinely painful. Not catastrophic, just the specific pain of realizing too late that your idea of toughness has somehow been entered into a real system and is now being acted upon by a professional.

Afterward we went to Train Street, another one of those Hanoi things that has become a must-do whether anyone likes the phrase or not. We found seats at a small cafe right on the tracks and ate and drank while two trains passed by close enough to touch, or at least close enough to make that seem like a terrible idea. It was one of those absurdly physical travel experiences where everyone suddenly becomes very aware of elbows, chairs, bags, table edges, and the exact dimensions of their own body. Then the train is past, everyone exhales, and food resumes as if this is perfectly normal.

I tried to order more noodles for my pho using the trick I had learned in Tokyo for getting noodle refills with ramen. In my head, this was a clever cross-cultural efficiency. In reality, it was just me making things confusing in a second country.

"Just noodles, please."

The waitress pointed at my bowl. "Another of this one?"

"No, just plain noodles."

She had no idea what we were asking for, which was fair enough. Another waitress came over to help interpret. We went through the same exchange again. She left appearing satisfied that she understood.

What arrived was another entire bowl of pho broth and noodles.

Close enough. I ate all of it, including both bowls of broth, because by then it seemed rude to everyone involved, including myself, not to commit.

Eventually we went back to the actual train station, where we learned that our VIP room did not in fact have the showers we had been planning to use before boarding the overnight train. This was annoying in that very specific travel way where you are not furious, just grimly aware of your own sweat.

Next to the station was a hotel called The Mango. We went there and booked a room for three hours. The front desk clerk understood what we wanted almost immediately, which suggested we were very far from the first people to have this exact station-adjacent hygiene emergency. We showered, reset ourselves, and went back to the station VIP room smelling like soap instead of a long day in Hanoi.

There, I ordered gin martinis that were expensive. Later in the trip I would check my credit card statement and discover that they had charged me $130 US for two martinis and two non-alcoholic drinks. The only explanation we could come up with was that maybe they had accidentally charged me a 300% card fee instead of 3%. Or something equally stupid. Who knows. Travel sometimes presents you with a mystery and then leaves the country.

When it was finally time, we boarded the train and were shown to our cabin, which had four single beds: two on the bottom and two that swung down from above. It was compact but manageable, the kind of setup that asks everyone to cooperate with both gravity and timing.

We had some complimentary drinks and sampled the snacks we had brought, discovering one by one that nearly all of them were gross. The cheddar potato chips, however, were good, and they introduced us to our favorite new Vietnamese phrase: "Vi Pho Mai!" We were pretty sure it meant cheese flavored. Pretty sure was enough. For the rest of the night everything became "Vi Pho Mai," which sent us into the kind of laughter that is only partially about the joke and mostly about being tired on a train with nowhere else to direct your brain.

We played a couple games of Monopoly Deal and then went to sleep, or tried to. I had been hoping the experience would feel like the last time I slept on a train, many years earlier as a child on the sleeper from San Francisco to Seattle. It did not. Under us, somewhere in the mechanical underside of the cabin, there was a piece of hardware that groaned and let out a loud CLUNK CLUNK CLUNK every time the train went around a corner, and there were a lot of corners. Whatever that thing was, it needed grease in a serious way.

So that was the night: clean from our emergency hotel shower, slightly over-martini'd, laughing about cheese flavor, and trying to sleep while the train announced every turn like a threat.
