## Sunday, May 10 - Snorkels, Sand, and Fridge Beer

```poem
A king-size bed made me briefly feel wise
Then I chased fish under theatrical skies
I drank the minibar beer without asking the fee
A noble financial strategy, clearly, by me
```

I woke up genuinely rested, which felt unusual enough to notice. Giant king-size beds will do that. There are some problems in life that cannot be solved by sleep, but a ridiculous amount of mattress certainly improves the mood.

Breakfast was free with our stay, which always gives food an extra layer of satisfaction. It was a Thai-style buffet: rice with an egg on top, fresh-cut fruit, coffee lattes, chicken and peppers. It was the kind of breakfast that made sense immediately once I saw it, solid and practical and a lot more appealing than pretending I wanted something dainty in tropical heat.

After that, we got on the boat to go snorkeling. It was raining on the way out to the snorkel spot, the kind of rain that makes everything look flatter and farther away at the same time. We went so far offshore that the land disappeared behind us, and then another island slowly emerged out of the mist ahead, as if the day had decided to replace one backdrop with another. Then the clouds parted, the sun came out, and we dove into the water with masks, fins, and snorkels.

The snorkeling area was roped off for conservation, meant to keep anchors and the usual human damage from tearing up the environment any more than necessary while still letting people experience it. It was a reasonable compromise, or at least as reasonable as tourism ever gets. Underwater, we saw sea urchins, sea slugs, a moray eel, a spiny lobster, and sea anemones with clown fish, which of course my brain still insists on identifying first as Finding Nemos.

They took us to a second spot after that, and then anchored just off a beach. The boat was a jetboat pontoon, which meant they could get close enough that we could just walk in. The sand was fine. No, not fine. *Super* fine. The sort of sand that makes a beach feel less like ground and more like a surface someone forgot to finish solidifying. There were swings hanging from the trees, their ropes a little threadbare, which somehow only made them more interesting. Slightly unsafe beach infrastructure has a way of advertising itself as charm.

Lunch was on the boat: rice, chicken, deep-fried pork, salad, and a brownie. There were beers too, plural, which felt correct for the stage of the day we had reached. Salt on the skin, damp clothes, a little hunger, a little fatigue, and something cold in hand. Hard to argue with that.

On the ride back to the resort, we asked if it was okay to go up top, and were told yes. They had beanbag-type chairs up there, which seemed like an absurdly comfortable thing to encounter on the return leg of a boat day. So we did what people do in that situation: took photos, slept, half-napped, drifted in and out of consciousness. The usual things you do when riding home on a boat after a long day on the water having fun.

Back at the resort, we spent some time at the pool and made a trip to the bar for more beverages. It had become that kind of day, one activity dissolving into the next in a pleasant, sun-softened blur. Later we went back to the room to swim some more. More coconuts were ordered. Then I discovered there were beers in our fridge. I had one. I had no idea what they were going to charge me for the in-room fridge beer, but at that point I decided I didn't care. Some decisions are financial mistakes and emotional common sense at the same time.

A magnificent sunset carried us toward evening while a light rain made sleep sound extremely tempting. The whole place seemed to exhale into softer colors and dimmer light. But we had dinner to go to, so sleep would have to wait. We showered and changed into nice threads, trying to look like people with energy left rather than people who had spent the day floating, drinking, napping, and making questionable minibar choices.
