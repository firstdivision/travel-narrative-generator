## Sunday, May 10 - Snorkels, Sand, and Fridge Beer

```poem
A king bed made me briefly trust my plans,
Then rain shoved us offshore with damp theatrical hands.
I snorkeled, drank, and stole one minibar beer without grace,
And ended the night asking strangers how dessert came to exist in the first place.
```

I woke up actually rested, which felt notable. Giant king size beds will do that. Travel has a way of making sleep feel either impossible or medicinal, and this one had apparently landed on medicinal.

Breakfast was included with our stay, a Thai-style buffet that felt generous without making a show of it. I had rice with an egg on top, fresh cut fruit, coffee lattes, and chicken with peppers. It was the kind of breakfast that made sense for the day ahead, solid and practical, not decorative. I was happy to eat it.

Then we got on the boat to go snorkeling.

It was raining on the way out to the snorkel spot. We went far enough offshore that the land disappeared behind us, which is always a slightly unsettling development even when everything is fine. Ahead, a new island started to emerge from the mist in front of us, and then the clouds split open and the sun came through as if someone had decided the weather had made its point. We put on masks, fins, and snorkels and got in.

The snorkeling area was roped off for conservation, meant to keep anchors and the rest of human enthusiasm from destroying the environment more than necessary while still letting people experience it. That seemed like a fair compromise, or at least the best version of one. Under the water we saw sea urchins, sea slugs, a moray eel, a spiny lobster, and sea anemones with clown fish, which I still automatically translate in my head as Finding Nemos because apparently some parts of the brain are permanent.

They took us to a second spot, and after that anchored just off a beach. It was a jetboat pontoon, so they could get close enough that we could simply walk in. The sand was fine. No, “super fine sand” is what we found, the sort that seems to erase the fact that your body has been rattled around by saltwater and sun for hours. There were swings hanging from trees, the ropes a little threadbare, which somehow only made them more appealing. Or more questionable. Sometimes those are the same thing.

Lunch was served on the boat: rice, chicken, deep fried pork, salad, and a brownie. There were beers too, plural, which felt appropriate for the setting and probably for the level of sun exposure we had all agreed to endure.

On the ride back to the resort, we asked if it was okay to go up top, and it was. They had beanbag-type chairs set out there, and we claimed them immediately. There were photos, sleep, and naps, the usual things you do on a boat ride home after a long day in the water when your skin is salty and your brain has mostly shut down in self-defense.

Back at the resort, we did what people do when they are trying not to waste a good place. Pool time. A trip to the bar for more beverages. Then back to the room to swim some more, because apparently we had decided to commit fully to the aquatic theme of the day. More coconuts were ordered. At some point I discovered there were beers in our fridge. I had one. I had no idea what they were going to charge me for the in-room fridge beer, and for once I truly did not care.

A magnificent sunset carried us toward evening while a light rain tried to talk us into staying in and falling asleep. It was a persuasive argument. But we had dinner to get to, so we showered and changed into nicer clothes.

Another buggy was ordered, and it delivered us to the resort’s Thai-themed restaurant. Spice N’ Rice was not trying to reinvent Thai food, which was one of the best things about it. It did not need to. The kitchen leaned into the flavors of southern Thailand with total confidence: lemongrass, turmeric, coconut milk, chilies, and seafood from nearby waters, all of it allowed to speak plainly for itself. Set against the jungle-covered hills and the warm evening air of Koh Lanta, the meal felt like a direct introduction to the island. Spicy, fragrant, colorful, and very easy to keep eating.

I think I ordered a chicken curry, though I’m writing this particular part a month after the fact and relying a bit on an unreliable memory. That feels worth admitting. I remember the shape of the evening more clearly than the exact contents of my plate.

What I do remember very clearly was asking about one of the desserts. We wanted to know how it was made. Our waitress wasn’t sure, so she called someone else over, who then called over another person. Together, taking turns, they explained the ingredients and how the dessert was carefully made in the kitchen. It was such a small thing, and somehow not small at all.

Another reminder that we were at a five-star resort, staffed by enthusiastic, intelligent, extremely capable people who took the question seriously instead of brushing it aside. By then I was tired in the satisfying way, sun-worn and well-fed, carrying salt and rain and curry and beer through the end of the night.
