## Friday, April 24 - Window Seat, Wet Streets

After falling asleep sometime around two or three in the morning, I woke up again to work. I don't remember the exact hour, which felt about right for the state I was in. And once I knew I was flying to Singapore later that day, concentrating on anything useful became mostly theoretical.

Eventually the time came to leave work and head back to the apartment. I did my usual last round of packing, hasty but based on a system that somehow still feels logical to me. I am one of those travelers who doesn't quite pack by putting things straight into the bag. First I lay everything out on a table or the floor and make what I call a "snowman." I think the name came from my skiing days, when the analogy probably made more sense. The point is that the snowman has to visibly contain everything he needs for the trip. Even with this technique, I have still come dangerously close to forgetting underwear for the snowman, which does suggest some limits to the method.

Then everything went into my broken roller bag, the one with two bad wheels that go THUMP THUMP THUMP if I make the mistake of trying to roll it the wrong way. From there it was into a Grab to KLIA Terminal 2.

Compared to the 13 hour 45 minute flight from Dallas to Tokyo, this one was barely a flight at all. An hour, maybe. The kind of trip where the plane only just gets itself organized before it has to start coming down again. It felt like flying from Providence to Washington, DC. Efficient, slightly absurd, over before the body fully understands it has traveled.

As usual, I booked a window seat. That part of me has never changed. Somewhere inside, there is still a child who is fascinated by flight and cannot stop staring out the window at whatever is passing underneath.

We began the approach to Singapore at night, with the cabin still in lights-out mode for the whole flight. Below me, the black expanse that tells you you're over water slowly gave way to scattered yellow lights. Not land at first. Ships. Cargo ships of every size and shape, anchored and waiting. Oil, gas, dry cargo, all of them sitting out there in the dark, holding position outside one of the busiest ports in the world. I didn't have long to think about any of it before the first real lights of the city appeared beneath us. Flaps. Gear. Touchdown. I was in Singapore.

As advertised, the airport was clean and efficient. I got through immigration quickly thanks to the digital entry forms, and before I really had time to register that I'd arrived in another country, I was waiting for another Grab.

More lights slid by outside the window as I sat in the back seat, looking out and wondering what this city was going to show me. That same child again, apparently still on duty.

The hotel was The Sultan in Kampong Glam. I arrived sweaty and grimy from the usual combination of airports, airplanes, sitting still too long, and then moving all at once. A travel constant. The air-conditioning in the lobby felt instantly medicinal. Check-in was quick and efficient, and they offered me a few bottles of water from a shelf behind me, which I appreciated more than I probably expressed.

I was pointed toward a pair of double doors cut into the wall of what had clearly been the original building before the hotel conversion. Beyond them was a slightly creaky walkway, almost more of a path between buildings than a hallway. There were bathrooms along the way, and gated entries leading to rooms that were not mine. Eventually I found my gate, tapped in with the magnetic key card, and stepped inside.

My first impression was mustiness. Not unbearable, just noticeable, the kind of smell that feels less like neglect than a running battle against humidity that nobody fully wins in climates like Kuala Lumpur and Singapore. I unpacked and put away the snowman's clothes.

Then I investigated the shower. Like my apartment back in KL, this one had a dedicated wall switch for the water heater. It was already turned on. Almost like they knew I was coming. The shower itself had one of those waterfall heads, and there was plenty of hot water, which was exactly what a grimy traveler needed.

Then hunger took over.

I changed into fresh clothes from the snowman's reserve and headed back out. Even though I was traveling and should have been in photographer mode, I reverted almost immediately to my more introverted instincts. The Nikon stayed tucked in my backpack. I didn't exactly forget to take photos. It was more that I felt self-conscious, especially at night, especially around people.

So I walked.

I found one very happening stretch that seemed to be an alley packed with bars, restaurants, and musicians, with tiny little places hidden behind dense clusters of outdoor seating. To get through it, I had to weave between chairs and bodies and noise. It was loud, chaotic, full of drunk people and the kind of energy that demands you either join in or get out of the way. I was not looking for that at the moment, so I kept going.

Eventually I found an Indian place on a street corner. It had sticky tables, open-air seating, and enthusiastic employees whose English wasn't great but whose intent was very clear: sit down, order something, we'll sort it out. What I was actually hunting for was chicken satay. I had seen it on the photo signboard outside, but once I had the menu in my hands, I couldn't find it anywhere.

So I ordered chicken curry and naan instead. A classic choice, and if I'm being honest, a somewhat safe one for an American traveler who was tired and hungry and not in the mood to negotiate too much with fate.

The curry arrived with a bone-in chicken thigh, extremely tender for the most part, sitting in a rich, dark sauce pooled across the plate. I still had only a spoon and fork to work with. No knife, though I'm sure they could have found one if I'd bothered to ask. Instead I did what tired people do and adapted. I scraped and cut at the chicken with the spoon, working around the bone as best I could, and sometimes used pieces of naan the way I might use a tortilla back in Texas, scooping up not rice and beans this time but soft pieces of chicken and that deep, spiced sauce underneath. Classic curry spices, dark and warm and exactly right. I ate all of it, finished my lemonade, and finally felt settled.

By then it was nearly midnight. I went back to the hotel, photoless but fed. I had a lot of ground to cover the next day, and sleep came fast.
