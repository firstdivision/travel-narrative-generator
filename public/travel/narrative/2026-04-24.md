## Friday, April 24 - Short Flight, Sticky Tables

```poem
I packed for the snowman and hoped he had socks,
Dragged a bag that protested in rhythmic old knocks,
Flew one brief hour to ships in the night,
Then ate curry by midnight and called that a life.
```

After falling asleep at two or three in the morning, I woke up again to work. I honestly don't remember which. Either way, concentration was mostly theoretical once I knew I was flying to Singapore later. There are limits to professionalism, and one of them is trying to care about anything while mentally already in an airport.

When the time finally came, I left work and went back to the apartment for the last round of packing. I have a system, if "system" is the generous word for it. I rarely pack in a neat, complete way from the start. Instead I lay everything out on a table or the floor and make what I call a "snowman," a term left over from ski days where, I assume, this analogy once made more sense. The idea is simple enough: make sure the snowman has everything he needs for his trip. Even with this supposedly foolproof method, I have still come dangerously close to sending the snowman into the world without underwear.

In the end I threw everything into my broken roller bag, the one with the two ruined wheels that go THUMP THUMP THUMP if I try to push it the wrong way, and got into a Grab to KLIA Terminal 2.

Compared to the 13-hour-45-minute flight from Dallas to Tokyo, this was barely a flight at all. About an hour, if that. Like flying from Providence to Washington, DC. The plane hardly had time to settle into the idea of cruising before it was already preparing to descend. Still, I had done what I always do and booked myself a window seat. Somewhere inside me there is still a child completely fascinated by flight, the kind who cannot stop looking out the window no matter how many times he's done this.

We began our approach into Singapore at night. The cabin had been in lights-out mode for the whole flight, so outside the window everything started as black water, a flat dark expanse broken only gradually by yellow lights. At first I thought they were land, but no, they were cargo ships anchored offshore. So many of them. Different shapes, different sizes, all waiting their turn to enter the port and load or unload, oil and gas and dry cargo, the whole floating bureaucracy of global trade. There wasn't much time to sit with the thought. Soon the actual lights of the city appeared, then flaps, gear, touchdown. I was in Singapore.

As advertised, the airport was clean and efficient. Immigration went quickly thanks to the digital entry forms, and before long I was waiting for another Grab, watching more lights slip by through the window from the back seat, again feeling a little like a child and wondering what exactly this city was going to show me.

The hotel was The Sultan in Kampong Glam. I arrived sweaty and grimy in that familiar way produced by a full chain of travel: apartment, car, airport, airplane, another car, all of it in heat and motion. The air-conditioning in the lobby felt like immediate mercy. Check-in was quick and efficient, and they offered me a few water bottles from the shelf behind me, which felt like a smart and decent thing to do for someone who looked as if he had recently been shipped in a crate.

I was pointed toward a pair of double doors leading through the wall of the original building the hotel had been converted from. Beyond them was a slightly creaky walkway, almost more of a passage between buildings than a proper hall, with bathrooms along the way and gated entries leading to rooms that were not mine. I found my gate, opened it with the key card, and stepped into my room.

My first impression was musty. That seemed less like a failure and more like a permanent negotiation with humidity, the same basic problem as back in Kuala Lumpur and probably all over Singapore too. I unpacked and put away the snowman's clothes. Then I investigated the shower.

Like my KL apartment, the room had a dedicated wall switch to turn on the water heater. This one was already on. It was almost like they knew I was coming. The shower had one of those waterfall heads, and there was plenty of hot water, exactly what a grimy traveler needed. I stood there until I felt human again.

Then hunger took over.

I changed into some fresh clothes from the snowman's stockpile and headed out. Even though I was traveling and should have been in photographer mode, I reverted almost immediately to my usual introverted habits and did not take photos, despite the Nikon tucked into my backpack. "Forgot" is not really the right word. It was more that I felt self-conscious and didn't particularly want to be pointing a camera at people at night.

I walked the streets for a while and found one very happening area, basically an alley packed with bars, musicians, and restaurants, tiny places tucked behind crowded outdoor seating that I had to weave through just to pass. It was loud, chaotic, full of drunk people and movement and competing music. It was probably exactly what some version of me was supposed to want in Singapore on a Friday night. It was also not what I wanted at that moment, so I kept going.

Eventually I found an Indian place on a street corner, one of those open-to-the-outside spots with sticky tables and enthusiastic employees asking what I wanted in English that was perfectly functional but not built for nuance. What I was actually hunting for was chicken satay. I had seen it on the photo signboard outside, but then somehow it wasn't on the menu they handed me, which felt like a minor personal betrayal by modern signage.

So I went with chicken curry and naan instead. A classic choice, and if I'm honest, also a somewhat safe one for this American traveler.

The food arrived with a bone-in chicken thigh, extremely tender for the most part, sitting in a rich dark sauce pooled across the plate. I still had only a spoon and fork to work with. No knife, though I assume they could have found me one if I had bothered to ask. I didn't. Instead I used the spoon to scrape and cut my way through the chicken, awkwardly but effectively, sometimes using pieces of naan the way I might use a tortilla back in Texas to scoop up rice and beans. Here it was delicate pieces of chicken, rice, and that deep curry sauce with its familiar spice mix, dark and warm and a little oily in the best way. The naan helped. The spoon did its best. I ate all of it and finished a lemonade, and by the end I was satisfied in the plain, physical way that matters most after a travel day.

It was late by then, nearly midnight. I went back to the hotel photoless but fed. I had a lot of ground to cover tomorrow, and sleep came fast.
