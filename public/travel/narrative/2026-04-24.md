## Friday, April 24 - Night Arrival, Musty Room, Curry Fix

After falling asleep at two or three in the morning, I woke up again to work. I honestly don't remember which. Either way, when you know you're flying to Singapore later, concentration becomes mostly theoretical. I went through the motions, looked at the clock too often, and waited for the day to finally let me leave.

When it did, I headed back to the apartment and did a last round of packing, hastily and with the usual amount of self-inflicted doubt. I am one of those travelers who never seems to pack in a clean, competent way. I don't neatly organize everything into cubes or stacks. Instead I lay all my stuff out on a table or the floor and make what I call a "snowman," which I think comes from my skiing days, back when that analogy made more sense to me. The idea is simple enough: make sure the snowman has everything he needs for the trip. Even with this system, I have still come dangerously close to sending the snowman into the world without underwear.

Eventually I threw everything into my broken roller bag, the one with two damaged wheels that go THUMP THUMP THUMP if I try to roll it the wrong way, and called a Grab to KLIA Terminal 2.

Compared to the 13-hour-45-minute flight from Dallas to Tokyo, this one barely counted as a flight at all. One hour, if that. More like flying from Providence to Washington, just enough time to settle in before the whole thing starts ending. The plane barely reached cruise before we were descending again.

As usual, I had booked myself a window seat. That part of me has never gone away, the child who is still fascinated by flight and can sit there staring out at the world below as if this is all somehow still impossible.

The approach into Singapore happened at night, which felt right somehow. The cabin had been in lights-out mode for the whole flight, and outside the window the black spread below first suggested open water. Then yellow lights started appearing in the darkness, scattered and strange. Not land. Ships. Cargo ships at anchor, all shapes and sizes, waiting their turn for the port. Oil, gas, dry cargo, probably all of it mixed together out there in the dark. I didn't have time to think much about the logistics of global trade because then the real lights of the city began to appear. Flaps. Gear. Touchdown. I was in Singapore.

As advertised, the airport was clean and efficient. Immigration was quick thanks to the digital entry forms, and before I knew it I was waiting for another Grab. Then it was more lights sliding by through the car windows while I sat in the back seat, again feeling a bit like a child, wondering what this city was going to show me.

The hotel was The Sultan, in Kampong Glam. By the time I arrived, I felt sweaty and grimy in that specific travel-day way that seems to combine airports, airplane air, sitting still too long, and hauling bags through humid air. The cold lobby AC was immediate relief. Check-in was quick and efficient, and I was offered a few bottles of water from a shelf behind me, which felt like exactly the kind of practical kindness I could appreciate at that hour.

From there I was pointed toward a pair of double doors that led out through the wall of the original building the hotel had been converted from. The route to the room was unexpectedly odd and memorable: a slightly creaky walkway, almost more of a path between buildings than a hallway, with bathrooms along the way and gated entries leading to rooms that were not mine. Eventually I found my gate, opened it with the key card, and stepped inside.

My first impression of the room was musty. Not dramatically, not in a scandalized way, just that damp, enclosed smell that feels almost inevitable in humid places like Kuala Lumpur and Singapore. I unpacked and put away the snowman's clothes. Then I investigated the shower.

Like my apartment back in KL, the room had a dedicated wall switch to power the water heater. It was already on. Almost like they knew I was coming. The shower had one of those waterfall heads, and there was plenty of hot water, which was exactly what a grimy traveler needed. I stood there longer than I probably needed to and let the day rinse off.

Then hunger took over.

I got dressed again using some of the snowman's reserve stock and headed back out. Even though I had been traveling and should have been in photographer mode, I reverted to my usual introverted self and didn't really take photos, despite the Nikon being tucked into my backpack. It wasn't really forgetting. More that I felt self-conscious, especially about taking photos of people at night, and so the camera stayed where it was.

I walked the surrounding streets and found one area that was very obviously happening, an alley full of bars, musicians, restaurants, and tightly packed seating. Tiny places were tucked behind the crowds, and I had to weave my way through chairs and people just to pass through. It was loud and chaotic, full of drunk energy, and immediately clear that it was not what I was looking for in that moment. So I kept walking.

Eventually I found an Indian place on a street corner, one of those open-to-the-outside restaurants with sticky tables and enthusiastic employees whose English wasn't great but whose intent absolutely was. What I was really searching for was chicken satay. I had seen it on the photo signboard outside, but once I was seated and looking at the menu in my hand, I couldn't find it anywhere.

So I went with chicken curry and naan instead. A classic choice, and if I'm being honest, a somewhat safe one for this American traveler.

It arrived as a bone-in chicken thigh in a rich, dark sauce, with naan alongside. The chicken was extremely tender for the most part, though eating it involved the usual negotiation with spoon and fork because there was no knife. I probably could have asked for one. I did not. Instead I used the spoon to scrape and cut my way through the meat, awkwardly but effectively, and sometimes tore off pieces of naan to scoop up chicken and sauce the way I might use a tortilla back in Texas for rice and beans. The sauce pooled under everything, dark and thick with that familiar curry spice profile, and the naan did exactly what I needed it to do. I finished all of it, along with a lemonade, and finally felt settled.

By then it was nearly midnight.

I went back to the hotel photoless but fed, which was probably the right trade. I had a lot of ground to cover the next day, and sleep came fast.
