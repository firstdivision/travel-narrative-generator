## Introduction

This is meant to be a travel log for Asia. Or at least my attempt at making sense of a small corner of it as I go.

I want to keep a record of the movement, the logistics, the places themselves, and whatever happens in between. "I'm doing it my way," as the song goes. Bullet points that become paragraphs.

That is the plan, anyway.

Asia is too large, too varied, and too contradictory to pretend I am going to understand it in any complete way in so short an amount of time. So this is not that kind of project. It is simply a running account, one day at a time, of where I went, what I noticed, what surprised me, and what stayed with me by the end of the day.

What follows includes overnight flights and broken luggage. Cherry blossoms and tropical thunderstorms. Airports, train stations, temples, markets, office buildings, and apartment towers that briefly became home. There are security guards who seemed to know more than I did, taxi drivers who rescued me from confusion, bowls of noodles consumed with great enthusiasm, and at least one meal that left me questioning whether I had correctly understood what I had ordered.

There are wrong turns, language barriers, software deployments, late-night meetings, overcrowded streets, quiet gardens, and the strange experience of trying to build an ordinary routine on the opposite side of the planet.

Some memories are exactly the sort of things people travel halfway around the world to see. Others are surprisingly small: a conversation at an airport gate, a cup of coffee during a rainstorm, a phone call from home, a street vendor making dumplings at dusk. Those are often the moments that linger the longest.

Mostly, this is a journal about movement. About the gradual transition from unfamiliar places to familiar ones. About discovering that the distance between two points on a map is often the least interesting part of the journey.

Not an attempt to understand Asia. Just an attempt to pay attention.

So this is the beginning.

There are a lot of miles ahead.
