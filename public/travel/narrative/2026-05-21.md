## Thursday, May 21 - Airport Purgatory and a Corn Dog

```poem
Five hours vanished under terminal light
Then thirteen more through dayless night
I bought my way home and paid with a groan
Then ate a corn dog at thirty-seven thousand alone
```

Somewhere: South Korea's Incheon Airport

Somewhen: Unsure.

We were in the airport purgatory of a long-haul layover, the kind that flattens time until it stops behaving like anything measurable. Four hours? Five? It hardly mattered. The only thing with any real shape to it was the knowledge that the *next* flight would be thirteen hours long.

With time to kill, I remembered I still had not bought my final ticket home to Austin from JFK, which was where the next plane was taking me. For a while I had entertained the idea of visiting family for what would be a long holiday weekend back in the USA. It was a reasonable thought. Almost wholesome. But by then I had a more powerful condition, a specific strain of travelitis better described as get-home-itis. So I dropped almost $800 on a nonstop flight from JFK to AUS. Another five hours after we landed there. It was going to be a very long "day," and because we were chasing the clock back across the globe, it would somehow still be the 21st when we landed. Convenient, if you're a calendar. Less impressive if you're a person.

At some point we boarded. It was an A380, the big double-decker, which still has enough novelty to make boarding feel faintly exciting even when your body has given up on enthusiasm. Soon after takeoff, we all fell asleep.

Somewhere over the Pacific, I woke up with my head propped against the plastic wall of the plane. For a moment I had the strange feeling that I was on my way to somewhere new, some next place I had not learned yet, and in the fog of sleep I could not quite remember where. Then another few moments passed and I realized the trip was over, and this plane was going home, eventually to Austin.

It was not a great feeling.

There is something bleak about understanding your own mood at 37,000 feet and 1079 KPH of ground speed. Yes, I looked it up on the in-flight screen in front of me, because apparently if I couldn't control the feeling, I could at least inventory the altitude and velocity of it.

One of the meals they served us on the plane was a "Korean Hot Dog," which is better known to many people as a corn dog. It was oddly comforting. The sweetness of the breading took some of the edge off the bitterness that had settled over me after waking. Airplane food usually asks only to be tolerated, but this one managed to do something useful.

Next to me, in the center seat, Kate was drawing in her travel sketch notebook. I watched for a while as the pencil made shapes and lines. Watching people draw has always relaxed me. After a bit I realized she was sketching the hills of Sapa, Vietnam, the slopes covered in rice paddies. It felt like looking backward while moving forward at impossible speed.

I was pretty sure all three of us were carrying the same conflicted feeling. We wanted to go home. There is always that inexorable pull. But we are travelers too, and we want that as well. That restless opposite pull. It is probably why we had already started planning the next trip before this one was even fully over.

Eventually the plane landed at JFK. They went off to catch a train to a train that would take them home, after discovering that the Uber would be $130 or something absurd enough to make public transit suddenly feel noble. I went the other way, back into the airport for one more flight.

I spent the time in Terminal 4 at a bar, nursing a gin and tonic and noting that it was not very good, but serviceable. Which is more or less all you can ask of an airport gin and tonic. Around me, other travelers recounted how there had been some sort of major problem the day before, and they had all been stranded there and were trying again today. It was the same story that seems to be playing at nearly every airport bar in the world: delayed departures, missed connections, a system quietly failing in familiar ways, and everyone retelling it over expensive drinks as if testimony might improve the odds.

I listened, waited, and stayed in motion without really moving. The trip was over, except for the part where it still wasn't.
