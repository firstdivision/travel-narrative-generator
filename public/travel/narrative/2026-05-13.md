## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, Visas, and Canadian Grandpa

```poem
DayQuil for breakfast felt medically chic
Then Cambodia asked for paperwork technique
I got renamed Grandpa for wearing socks on shore
And drank the wrong blue cocktail like a travel amateur
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, headed for a flight to Siem Reap, Cambodia. I was already feeling a little off, not fully sick but suspicious enough to take a DayQuil and hope for the best. That felt like the kind of decision travel encourages. Maybe practical, maybe delusional.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in, then retreated to the Bangkok Airways lounge to wait it out with coffee and fresh popcorn, the sweet-and-salty kind coming out of one of those carnival-style poppers. It was a strangely cheerful airport detail, and for a few minutes I let myself enjoy it. Then we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were supposed to take tomorrow *night*, had been rescheduled to tomorrow *morning*. Which was, obviously, not going to work if we wanted to spend the day actually in Siem Reap, seeing temples instead of sprinting straight back to an airport. So we booked new tickets on VietAir to get us from Siem Reap to Hanoi.

By the time that was done, we had spent longer on logistics than intended and suddenly needed to get to the gate. We made it, I took more cold medicine, and then boarding involved one of those airport buses that drives you out past the edges of the terminal and keeps going long enough that you start to wonder if your plane is actually parked in another province. Eventually we reached it, sitting off in some remote parking area.

On the plane, we did the *New York Times* crosswords, which had quietly become one of our trip rituals, seeing if we could solve all three: the mini, the midi, and the big one. Somewhere between clues and sniffles, we were served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. When we asked if it was free, the stewardess looked almost offended by the question. “Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!” Fair enough. The curry arrived as a complete surprise and tasted even better for that reason.

Landing in Cambodia brought us straight into the kind of bureaucratic confusion that seems designed to test how awake you really are. We went to the bathrooms, then to immigration, only to learn that our e-visas were not, in fact, the full story. We still needed real ones, which we had to buy on the other side of the room from the immigration lanes.

The process was strange in a very specific airport way. We were shuffled to “lane 1,” where we handed over passports and were abruptly told, “Go to lane 2!” At lane 2 we handed over credit cards and were sent to lane 3. At lane 3 everything was returned to us, along with receipt-like slips of paper that apparently counted as our visas. Then it was back to the real immigration line, where an officer told me I also needed my boarding pass. I couldn’t find it. Maybe I left it on the plane, maybe it was crumpled in some pocket that had become unreachable in the general churn of travel. I told him I didn’t have it. He motioned for me to just hand over the rest. I did. A moment later I had my passport and visa back and was waved through, officially in Cambodia by what felt like a technicality.

We took an airport car to Viroth’s Villa. On the drive, I looked out the window and saw some familiar signs of harder lives sliding by. Stray dogs with giant nipples swaying beneath them from litter after litter of puppies. Emaciated livestock trying to eat grass that didn’t seem to exist anymore. It was the kind of view that resists easy commentary. Just a reminder that, even in the US, most people do not have to endure that level of struggle, and that comfort is distributed with a cruelty so ordinary it can start to look invisible.

At one point we passed a building and sign called “Tourismcity,” which felt almost too on the nose. A literal sign for where we were headed. Maybe more temples where you are asked to remove your shoes out of respect, but also encouraged to stop by the gift shop on your way out. I also saw rows and rows of rental mopeds and bicycles, and for some reason it brought to mind the Michael McCloud lyric: “Just another day in this tourist town bar.”

When we pulled up to Viroth’s Villa, the first thing I noticed was how quickly the noise of Siem Reap seemed to disappear. One minute we were in traffic and tuk-tuks, and the next we were passing through a small doorway into a courtyard with a pool framed by palms and thick greenery. The place had clean lines, warm wood, and a retro Cambodian style that felt effortlessly cool without trying too hard.

Check-in came with a brief financial complication. The shuttle driver needed to be paid $65 USD, and I had been under the impression that the charge would be added to the room and handled at checkout. Instead, there we were, needing to come up with the money on the spot. I had enough USD in my wallet, plus some extra in my pocket that I had meant to use as a tip. Then, in the middle of sorting it out, I asked if I could pay in Cambodian riel, which I had loaded up on at the airport. There was some hesitation, then they converted it and accepted the riel. I paid the driver and only later realized I had completely forgotten the tip I had intended to give. One of those moments where the details get scrambled by fatigue, currency, and mild confusion.

We didn’t stay put long. We headed out almost immediately to the shopping boutique district on Hap Guan Street, and it felt like stepping into a different version of Siem Reap. Just a few blocks away from the noise around Pub Street, the crowds thinned, the pace slowed, and the whole area seemed to exhale. There were leafy sidewalks, boutique shops, small art galleries, coffee houses, and carefully arranged stores selling Cambodian crafts and design goods. Cafés sat under shady trees. Shop windows held locally made textiles and artwork. It felt creative, relaxed, and just a little tucked away, as if I had accidentally wandered into the part of Siem Reap where it keeps its better secrets.

There were cats, too, which naturally improved matters. I stopped to say hello to them. One tolerated my attention until something more important presented itself, and a few others seemed to be working through some kind of territorial dispute. The energy was very clear even without translation. No, this is *my* place where I sleep in front of a store.

We also met a woman named Jodi, who owns a clothing store on the street and seemed to possess an endless supply of stories. At some point she looked at my Perry boat shoes, noticed I was wearing socks with them, and decided to name me “Canadian Grandpa.” Which, honestly, could have gone worse. I told her that I used to sail on boats, and that when we were back on land we would wear socks with our shoes because after spending days not wearing socks, comfort started to outrank style. Her opinion of me seemed to improve after that. Maybe she liked the story. Maybe she appreciated someone willing to laugh at himself instead of getting precious over a light joke. As a shopkeeper, I imagine she has seen enough people taking themselves far too seriously.

I bought some art there: three stiff-paper prints depicting tigers in the jungle, and a postcard. Then I walked to the post office to mail it, passing tuk-tuks along the way, each driver calling out, “Need ride?” The question repeated itself often enough that it became part of the street noise.

Afterward we went to River Cafe for dinner. I ordered something called a “Burn in Hall,” hoping, perhaps irrationally, that it might burn the cold out of me. What arrived was blue and had no detectable taste of alcohol. Kate got her drink, which had a chili in it. Later we figured out she had gotten mine and I had gotten hers, which explained a lot. By then the cold had started to settle in more seriously, turning into a full runny nose.

After dinner we went back to the room. I stayed behind to nurse the cold while the girls went out for spa massages. When they were asked if they wanted the 60-minute or 90-minute massage, I called after them, “Get the 90!” It felt like practical advice from a man full of cold medicine and bad airport timing.

I took a shower, climbed into bed, and fell asleep watching airplane videos while the cold medicine made its rounds through my bloodstream.
