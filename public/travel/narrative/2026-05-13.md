## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, visas, and Canadian Grandpa

```poem
DayQuil for breakfast, always a classy start
Airline math tried to rearrange the heart
I crossed a border with a lost boarding pass somehow
And got called Canadian Grandpa before dinner, so here we are now
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, headed for Siem Reap, Cambodia. I was already feeling that faint, annoying pressure of a possible cold, the kind that makes you suspicious of your own throat before the day has properly begun, so I swallowed a DayQuil and decided that was medicine enough for optimism. Hurt, tired, not fully convinced I was getting sick but not exactly thriving either, I went through the motions.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in, then drifted into the Bangkok Airways lounge to wait it out. I had coffee. There was fresh popcorn too, sweet and salty, coming out of one of those carnival-style poppers that somehow makes even airport snacking feel slightly festive. For a little while, the morning held together.

Then we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were supposed to take in two days, had been rescheduled to tomorrow morning. Not tomorrow in the abstract, but tomorrow morning, which was inconvenient in the very specific way that destroys actual plans. We were supposed to spend the next day visiting Siem Reap and its temples, not racing back to an airport because an airline had developed new ideas about time. So we booked new tickets on VietAir from Siem Reap to Hanoi instead.

This took longer than we expected, naturally. Travel logistics seem to smell urgency and respond by becoming slower and more intricate. By the time we finished, we realized we needed to get to the gate immediately.

At the gate I took more cold medicine, and when boarding started we were loaded onto a bus outside the terminal and driven a surprisingly long distance to the plane, which was parked off in some remote aircraft holding pen. It felt less like boarding and more like being escorted to a parked secret.

On the plane we did the *New York Times* crossword puzzles, which had quietly become one of our trip traditions, seeing if we could finish all three: the mini, the midi, and the big one. There was something grounding about it, that familiar little mental routine while countries changed around us.

Then we were served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. I asked if it was free, still suspicious in the way air travel has trained me to be, and the stewardess said, “Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!” It was such a cheerful answer that I almost believed air travel could still be charming. The curry was warm, comforting, and more substantial than I had mentally budgeted for.

We landed in Cambodia, went to the bathrooms, and then headed to immigration, where we discovered that our eVisas were not, in fact, all we needed. We needed real ones too, which could be bought on the other side of the room from the immigration lanes. So began a strange little bureaucratic shuffle.

We were sent to “lane 1” to hand over passports, then told abruptly, “Go to lane 2!” At lane 2, we gave them our credit cards and were told, just as abruptly, to “Go to lane 3.” In lane 3 we got everything back, along with receipt slips that were apparently now our “visas.” It all felt improvised and rigid at the same time, which is a hard combination to pull off but immigration offices around the world seem committed to the craft.

Back at the actual immigration lane, I was told I also needed my boarding pass. I couldn’t find it. Maybe I had left it on the plane, maybe it was crumpled in some unreachable pocket, maybe it had entered the afterlife reserved for travel documents needed at the exact worst moment. I told the officer I didn’t have it. He motioned for me to hand over the rest anyway. I did. A moment later I had my passport and visa back and was waved through, officially in Cambodia by means I did not entirely understand.

We took an airport car to Viroth’s Villa. On the drive, I looked out the window and saw familiar signs of hard living sliding past. Stray dogs with giant nipples swaying underneath them from litter after litter of puppies. Emaciated livestock trying to eat grass that didn’t seem to exist anymore. Those passing roadside scenes that remind you, uncomfortably and usefully, how much struggle is simply built into ordinary life for a lot of people. Even in the US, for all its noise and mythology, most people do not have to endure that level of daily abrasion.

At one point we passed a sign and building called “Tourismcity,” which felt almost too perfect, a literal sign announcing where we were headed. Maybe more temples where you remove your shoes out of respect and are also gently guided toward the gift shop on the way out. I also noticed rows and rows of rental mopeds and bicycles. It brought to mind the Michael McCloud lyric, “Just another day in this tourist town bar.” That line drifted through my head as the road rolled on.

When we pulled up to Viroth’s Villa, the first thing I noticed was how quickly the noise of Siem Reap seemed to disappear. One minute we were moving through traffic and tuk-tuks, and the next we were stepping through a small doorway into a courtyard with a pool framed by palms and thick greenery. The place had clean lines, warm wood, and a retro Cambodian style that didn’t need to show off. It was not flashy. It was composed. After airports and immigration lanes and shuttle confusion, it felt almost improbably calm.

There was, however, still the matter of paying the shuttle driver $65 USD. I had somehow been under the impression that the charge would be added to the room and settled at checkout, which is the sort of assumption that always seems reasonable until it very much is not. We had to come up with the money right there. I had enough USD in my wallet, and I also had some in my pocket that I had intended to use as a tip. Instead I asked if I could pay in Cambodian riel, which I had stocked up on at the airport. There was some hesitation, some conversion, a few moments where no one seemed especially eager about the arrangement, but eventually it worked. I paid the driver the required riel and, in the shuffle of it all, completely forgot to tip. One of those travel moments where the details slip out of your hands while everyone is trying to be practical.

We checked in and almost immediately headed out to the shopping boutique district on Hap Guan Street. It felt like stumbling into a different version of Siem Reap. Just a few blocks away from the noise around Pub Street, the crowds thinned, the pace dropped, and the whole place shifted into something quieter and more deliberate. There were leafy sidewalks, boutique shops, small art galleries, coffee houses, and neatly curated stores selling Cambodian crafts and design goods.

Cafés sat under shady trees. Shop windows held textiles and artwork. The frantic tourist energy seemed to have been turned down several notches. It felt creative, relaxed, and just a little tucked away, as if I’d wandered into the part of Siem Reap that keeps better company.

There were cats too, which improved the afternoon immediately. I made sure to say hello to them. One accepted petting when it wasn’t too busy with whatever cat business outranked me. A few others seemed to be in the middle of some territorial dispute, with the unmistakable energy of, no, this is *my* sleeping spot in front of this store and I will not be taking questions.

We also met a woman named Jodi, who owned a clothing store on the street and seemed to possess a bottomless supply of stories. At some point she decided to name me “Canadian Grandpa” because I was wearing socks with my Perry boat shoes. Fair enough. I told her how I used to sail on boats, and that when we were back on land we’d sometimes wear socks with our shoes because we had just spent days not wearing socks and wanted, very simply, to be comfortable. Her opinion of me seemed to improve after that. Maybe she liked the story. Maybe she appreciated someone who could absorb a light joke without getting precious about it. As a shopkeeper, I imagine she has seen her share of people who take themselves far too seriously. One of her stories from the past suggested exactly that.

I bought some art there, three stiff-paper prints depicting tigers in the jungle, along with a postcard. Then I headed to the post office to mail it, walking past tuk-tuks the whole way, each driver calling out some variation of “Need ride?” It became part of the street rhythm, the same question tossed lightly into the heat over and over again.

Afterward we went to River Cafe for dinner. I ordered a “Burn in Hall,” intending it as some medicinal strategy to burn out the cold. What showed up was blue and had no taste of alcohol whatsoever, which was not exactly the fiery cure I’d imagined. Kate got her drink, and hers came with a chili in it. Later we figured out that she had gotten my drink and I had gotten hers, which explained a lot and also somehow felt very in character for the day.

By then the cold had developed from vague suspicion into a full runny nose. That was the official progression. Dinner over, we went back to the room, and I stayed behind to nurse my cold while the girls went out for spa massages. When they were asked whether they wanted the 60-minute or 90-minute option, I yelled after them, “Get the 90!”

It seemed important to advocate for excess when someone else was getting the massage.

I took a shower, let the cold medicine keep doing whatever chemical diplomacy it was attempting in my bloodstream, and fell asleep watching airplane videos. That felt like the right ending for a day built out of terminals, paperwork, mistaken drinks, and the odd honor of becoming Canadian Grandpa in Siem Reap.
