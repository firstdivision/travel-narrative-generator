## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, Visas, and Canadian Grandpa

```poem
I chased a cold through airports half-awake,
Booked the wrong tomorrow for tomorrow’s sake,
Got called Canadian Grandpa in sensible shoes,
Then drank the wrong blue cocktail with a runny nose and views.
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, headed for Siem Reap, Cambodia. I was already feeling a little off, not fully sick yet but circling the idea, so I took a DayQuil and figured it could not hurt. That is usually how these things begin, with optimism and over-the-counter medicine.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in, then drifted into the Bangkok Airways lounge to wait. I had coffee. There was fresh popcorn too, the sweet and salty kind, popping in one of those carnival-style machines that somehow makes even airport snacking feel faintly theatrical. It was all pleasant enough until we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were supposed to take in two days, had been rescheduled to "tomorrow." Which was, of course, the exact day we had planned to actually see Siem Reap. Modern air travel has a real gift for using the same calendar as everyone else while still managing to feel abstract.

So we booked new tickets instead, this time on VietAir from Siem Reap to Hanoi. By the time that was done, we had spent much longer fixing the future than intended and suddenly needed to get to the gate.

At the gate I took more cold medicine. Then when boarding began, we were sent onto a bus outside the terminal and driven a surprisingly long way across the airport to a plane parked out in what felt like some remote aircraft holding pen. Bangkok was already beginning to blur into that strange in-between travel state where everything is fluorescent, air-conditioned, and mildly inconvenient.

On the plane we did the *New York Times* crossword puzzles, which had quietly become one of our trip traditions. The goal was always to see if we could finish all three: the mini, the midi, and the big one. Somewhere over the region, between fatigue and clue-solving, we were also served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. When we asked if it was free, the stewardess said, "Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!" It was one of those genuinely pleasant airline moments that felt almost suspicious. The curry was warm, comforting, and exactly the kind of food that seems improbably welcome when you are in a pressurized tube with a headache forming behind your eyes.

We landed in Cambodia, went straight for the bathrooms, and then headed to immigration, where we learned that our e-visas were not, in fact, all we needed. We had to get real visas on the other side of the room first. Buying them was a strange little bureaucratic relay. We were shuffled to "lane 1" to hand over our passports, then told abruptly to go to lane 2, where we handed over our credit cards, then sent to lane 3, where everything came back to us along with receipt-like slips of paper serving as our visas. It all had the efficient opacity of a process that has no interest in being explained.

Back at the actual immigration lane, I was told I also needed my boarding pass. I could not find it. Maybe I had left it on the plane, maybe it was crumpled in some forgotten pocket, maybe it had simply passed into that parallel dimension where important travel documents go when requested at a counter. I told the officer I did not have it. He motioned for me to just hand over the rest of my documents, which I did. A moment later I had my passport and visa back and was waved through. Just like that, I was officially in Cambodia.

The airport car took us to Viroth's Villa. Along the drive I looked out the window and saw familiar signs of places carrying more than their share of struggle. Stray dogs with giant nipples swaying beneath them from litter after litter of puppies. Emaciated livestock trying to eat grass that did not seem to exist anymore. The kind of roadside scenes that are hard to romanticize and should not be. They were reminders, too, that even in the US, most people do not have to endure that level of hardship.

At one point we passed a sign and building called "Tourismcity," which felt almost too on the nose, a literal marker of where we were headed. Maybe more temples where you remove your shoes out of respect and then are gently encouraged to stop by the gift shop on the way out. I also noticed row after row of rental mopeds and bicycles. It brought to mind the Michael McCloud lyric: "Just another day in this tourist town bar."

When we pulled up to Viroth's Villa, the first thing I noticed was how quickly the noise of Siem Reap seemed to disappear. One minute we were weaving through traffic and tuk-tuks, and the next we were stepping through a small doorway into a courtyard with a pool framed by palms and lush greenery. The architecture stood out immediately, not flashy or extravagant, just clean lines, warm wood, and a retro Cambodian style that felt effortlessly cool.

Check-in came with a slight money confusion. The shuttle driver needed to be paid $65 USD, and I had been under the impression that the charge would be added to our room and settled at checkout. Suddenly we needed to produce the cash on the spot. I had enough USD in my wallet, and also some in my pocket that I had intended to use as a tip. Instead, I asked if I could pay in Cambodian riel, which I had loaded up on at the airport. There was some hesitation, but eventually they converted it and accepted the payment in riel. I paid the driver and completely forgot about the tip I had been planning to give. One of those moments where the details get scrambled in transit and everyone just keeps moving.

We dropped our things and immediately headed out to the shopping boutique district on Hap Guan Street. It felt like stumbling into a different version of Siem Reap. Only a few blocks from the noise around Pub Street, the crowds thinned, the pace slowed, and the whole place shifted into something leafy and curated. There were boutique shops, small art galleries, coffee houses, and carefully arranged stores selling Cambodian crafts and design goods. Cafés sat under shady trees. Shop windows held locally made textiles and artwork. The atmosphere was relaxed, creative, and oddly tucked away, as if I had wandered into the neighborhood where Siem Reap kept its more thoughtful side.

There were cats, too, and I made sure to say hello to them. Some tolerated petting with the detached professionalism of animals who have seen many tourists and formed opinions. A few others seemed occupied with a territorial dispute that needed no translation. No, this is *my* place where I sleep in front of a store.

We met a woman named Jodi, who owned a clothing store on the street and appeared to have an endless supply of stories. She took one look at me in my Perry boat shoes with socks and dubbed me "Canadian Grandpa." Fair enough. I told her that I used to sail on boats, and that on land we would wear socks with our shoes because after days of not wearing them, comfort begins to outrank style in a very practical way. Her opinion of me seemed to improve after that. Maybe she liked the explanation. Maybe she just appreciated someone willing to take a light joke without getting precious about it. As a storekeeper, I am sure she has met enough people who take themselves far too seriously.

I bought some art there: three stiff-paper prints depicting tigers in the jungle, and a postcard. After that I went to the post office to mail the postcard, walking past one tuk-tuk after another, each driver calling out some variation of "need ride?" It was persistent but not hostile, just part of the street's background rhythm.

Later we went to River Cafe for dinner. By then my cold had started to bloom into a proper runny nose, and I ordered something called a "Burn in Hall" in the hope that it might somehow burn the sickness out of me. What arrived was blue and had no detectable taste of alcohol. Kate got a drink with a chili in it. Only later did we realize she had been served mine and I had been served hers, which explained both the color and the complete absence of the medicinal effect I had been counting on. Not ideal, but almost funny.

After dinner we went back to the room. I stayed behind to nurse the cold while the girls went out for spa massages. When they were asked whether they wanted the 60- or 90-minute massage, I yelled after them, "Get the 90!" It felt important to advocate for endurance where possible.

I took a shower, let the cold medicine keep doing whatever chemical diplomacy it was trying to do, and fell asleep watching airplane videos. It was not glamorous, but it was the exact right ending for the day.
