## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, Paperwork, and Curry

```poem
I took cold meds and trusted the day,
while airline logic wandered away.
Three visa desks and one missing pass,
somehow we still arrived at last.
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, headed for a flight to Siam Reap, Cambodia. I felt off, not fully sick but not exactly well either, the kind of travel-day uncertainty where you start bargaining with over-the-counter medicine. I swallowed a DayQuil and decided that if I was getting sick, at least I could be getting sick while in motion.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in, then drifted into the Bangkok Airways lounge to wait. I had coffee. There was fresh popcorn too, the sweet-and-salty kind, coming out of one of those carnival-style poppers that make even an airport lounge feel briefly unserious. It was all pretty pleasant until we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were meant to take in two days, had been rescheduled to "tomorrow," meaning the same day we were landing in Cambodia. Airline scheduling has a special talent for making a sentence technically true and completely unusable.

So we booked new tickets on VietAir from Siam Reap to Hanoi.

By the time we finished dealing with that, we realized we had spent more time rebooking than we thought and suddenly needed to get to our gate. We hurried over, I took more cold medicine, and then boarding turned into one of those airport routines that always feels slightly absurd. We got on a bus outside the terminal, and it drove us a surprisingly long way out to where our plane was parked in some remote aircraft holding area, as if the plane had been sent away to think about what it had done.

On the flight we did the *New York Times* crossword puzzles, which had become a tradition on this trip. The mini, the midi, and the big one, all three if possible. It gave the flight some shape. Then we were served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. Unexpected is often the best seasoning. When we asked if it was free, the stewardess said, "Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!" It was such a cheerful line that it almost made me forget we had spent part of the morning repairing our itinerary.

We landed in Cambodia, went to the bathrooms, and then headed to immigration, where we learned that our e-visas were not, in fact, all we needed. We still needed real ones, which we could buy on the other side of the room from the immigration lanes. The whole thing had the energy of a process designed by people who deeply trust momentum and very little else.

Buying the visas was strange. We were shuffled to "lane 1," where we handed over our passports and were abruptly told, "Go to lane two!" At lane two we were told to hand over our credit cards and then sent to lane 3. At lane three we finally got everything back, along with little slips of receipt paper that were apparently our "evisas." It felt less like obtaining entry documents and more like being passed through a series of mildly irritated game-show stations.

Back at the actual immigration line, I was told I also needed my boarding pass. I couldn't find it. Maybe it was on the plane. Maybe it was crumpled in some lost pocket of my bag. Maybe it had simply decided it had done enough for one day. I told the officer I didn't have it. He motioned for me to hand over the rest of my documents anyway. So I did. A moment later he gave me back my passport and my visa slip, and that was that. I was officially in Cambodia.

We took an airport car to Viroth's Villa. On the drive, I looked out the window and saw familiar signs of harder lives sliding by. Stray dogs with giant nipples swaying under them from litter after litter of puppies. Emaciated livestock trying to eat grass that didn't seem to exist anymore. The kind of roadside scenes that flatten whatever lazy ideas you might still be carrying about what struggle really looks like. A reminder, too, that even in the United States, most people do not have to endure that level of daily difficulty.

That was my first real look at Cambodia, through a car window after a day of airports, cold medicine, curry, visa confusion, and one boarding pass that disappeared at exactly the wrong moment.
