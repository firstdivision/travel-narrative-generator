## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, Visas, and Canadian Grandpa

```poem
I chased a cold through three immigration lanes,
Booked backup flights while chewing lounge popcorn grains,
Got called Canadian Grandpa in sensible shoes,
Then lost to a blue cocktail and a runny nose blues.
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, headed for Siem Reap, Cambodia. I was feeling *something* coming on, not dramatic yet, just enough to make me suspicious, so I swallowed a DayQuil and decided that was probably the sort of responsible optimism travel sometimes requires. Or maybe just denial with branding.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in and then retreated to the Bangkok Airways lounge, which turned out to be a pretty good place to be temporarily stranded by logistics. I had coffee. There was fresh sweet-and-salty popcorn coming out of one of those carnival-style poppers, which felt oddly cheerful for an airport lounge. While we were sitting there, we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were supposed to take tomorrow *night*, had been rescheduled to tomorrow *morning*. Which was not going to work at all, since the whole point was to spend the day in Siem Reap actually seeing Siem Reap. Temples, ideally. Not an airport departure board.

So we booked new tickets on VietAir to get from Siem Reap to Hanoi instead. By the time we finished doing that, we realized we had spent longer on the rescue operation than expected and now needed to hurry to our gate.

At the gate I took more cold medicine. Then, when boarding started, we were sent onto a bus outside the terminal and driven an unexpectedly long way out to the plane, which was parked somewhere in what felt like a remote aircraft exile zone. The glamour of air travel remains extremely committed to surprise.

Once on board, we did the New York Times crossword puzzles, which had quietly become a tradition on this trip. The mini, the midi, and the big one, all of them if possible. Somewhere over Southeast Asia, this had become what we do.

We were also served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. When we asked if it was free, the stewardess looked almost amused and said, “Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!” It was one of those airline moments so pleasantly out of sync with expectation that it almost felt staged.

We landed in Cambodia, went straight for the bathrooms, and then headed to immigration, where we discovered that our e-visas were apparently not the final form of visas after all. We still needed the real ones, which had to be bought across the room from the immigration lanes.

The process was strange in a very procedural way, like a bureaucratic game show with less explanation. We were shuffled to Lane 1 to hand over passports, then told abruptly, “Go to lane two!” At Lane 2 we handed over our credit cards and were sent to Lane 3. At Lane 3 everything came back to us, along with little receipt slips that were now, somehow, our “e-visas.”

Then it was back to the actual immigration line, where I was told I also needed my boarding pass. I could not find it. Maybe I left it on the plane. Maybe it was buried in a pocket, folded into some useless little square. I told the officer I didn’t have it. He motioned for me to hand over the rest of my documents anyway, so I did. A moment later he gave me back my passport and visa slip, and that was that. I was officially in Cambodia.

On the drive from the airport to Viroth’s Villa, I watched the roadside slide by through the car window. There were the familiar signs of hardship that show up around tourist economies and stay with you longer than the polished parts do: stray dogs with giant nipples swaying under them from litter after litter of puppies, thin livestock trying to eat grass that looked as if it had long ago given up. It was a reminder, not a new one but a useful one, that even in the US most people do not live under that degree of strain.

At one point we passed a sign and building called **Tourismcity**, which felt almost too on-the-nose to be real, a literal sign of what we were driving toward. I found myself thinking of temples where you are asked to remove your shoes out of respect but are also gently steered toward the gift shop on the way out. We also passed rows and rows of rental mopeds and bicycles. It brought to mind the Michael McCloud lyric, “Just another day in this tourist town bar.”

Then we arrived at **Viroth’s Villa**.

The first thing I noticed was how quickly the noise of Siem Reap dropped away. One minute we were in traffic and tuk-tuks, and the next we were stepping through a small doorway into a courtyard with a pool, palms, and thick greenery softening everything around it. The place wasn’t flashy. It didn’t need to be. Warm wood, clean lines, a retro Cambodian style that felt calm and quietly self-assured. It was effortlessly cool in a way that usually takes a lot of effort.

Check-in came with a small wrinkle. The shuttle driver needed to be paid $65 USD, and I had somehow gotten the impression that this would just be added to the room and paid later at checkout. Instead we needed to produce the money right there. I had enough USD in my wallet, plus some in my pocket that I had meant to use as a tip. For some reason, in the moment, I asked if I could pay in Cambodian riel, which I had plenty of after changing money at the airport. There was some hesitation, then eventually they converted it and accepted the riel. I paid the driver and completely forgot to tip him at all, which was not ideal, but this is the kind of thing that happens when money, fatigue, and cross-language uncertainty all arrive in the same five-minute window.

We headed out almost immediately for the boutique shopping district on Hap Guan Street. It felt like entering a slightly altered version of Siem Reap. Just a few blocks from the noise around Pub Street, there were leafy sidewalks, small art galleries, cafés, boutique shops, and stores carefully arranged with Cambodian crafts and design goods. It was quieter, more curated, but not too polished to hide the reality underneath. There were little unfinished construction bits everywhere too: a pile of dirt, some concrete, a stack of bricks. Things that reminded me of tourist-dependent Caribbean islands I’ve visited, where the postcard version and the in-progress version coexist on the same block. Instagram, naturally, prefers one of those versions. Which is exactly why it is better to go see places for yourself.

There were plenty of good storefronts to look into, textiles and artwork in windows, cafés tucked under shade trees. The street felt creative and relaxed, almost as if I had wandered by accident into the neighborhood where Siem Reap keeps its more interesting ideas.

There were cats too, and I made a point of saying hello to them. Like a lot of stray or formerly stray cats, they were immediately friendly and practical about affection, dropping into the gravel to be petted as if this had been the correct purpose of my arrival all along. Then, as soon as they had enough attention, they returned to what seemed to be a territorial dispute among three of them. I could practically hear it: no, this is *my* place in front of this store where I sleep.

We also met a woman named Jodi, who owns a clothing store on the street and seemed to have an endless inventory of stories. She looked at my Perry boat shoes and the socks I was wearing with them and decided to name me “Canadian Grandpa.” Fair enough. I told her that I used to sail on boats, and that when we were back on land we would wear socks with our shoes because after days without them, comfort won. Her opinion of me seemed to improve after that. Maybe she liked the explanation. Maybe she appreciated that I could take a joke and laugh with it. I suspect shopkeepers meet a lot of people who take themselves far too seriously.

I bought some art there: three stiff-paper prints of tigers in the jungle, plus a postcard. Then I walked to the post office to mail it, passing plenty of tuk-tuks on the way, each with a driver calling out, “Need ride?” It became the rhythm of the street after a while, not aggressive, just constant, folded into the air along with heat and traffic and the practical fact that everyone was trying to make a living.

Afterward we went to River Cafe for dinner. I ordered a drink called a “Burn in Hall,” partly because I was hoping to burn out this developing cold by force of cocktail naming alone. What arrived was blue and tasted of no alcohol at all. Kate got her drink, which came with a chili in it. Only later did we realize she had gotten mine and I had gotten hers, which explained a few things, though not why my mystery blue drink tasted like liquid tourism.

By then the cold had advanced from suspicion to a full runny nose. That was the day’s real souvenir.

After dinner we went back to the room. I stayed behind to nurse the cold while the girls went out for spa massages. When they were asked whether they wanted the 60- or 90-minute massage, I yelled after them, “Get the 90!” It felt like the only morally correct position.

Then I took a shower and fell asleep watching airplane videos, with cold medicine circulating through my bloodstream and Siem Reap somewhere just beyond the walls, waiting for tomorrow.
