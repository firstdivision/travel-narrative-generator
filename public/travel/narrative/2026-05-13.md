## Wednesday, May 13th - Airports, Visas, and a Blue Drink

```poem
I chased a cold through airports before dawn's first light
Then lost a flight to tomorrow in administrative spite
Cambodia stamped me through with paperwork and doubt
By dinner I drank the wrong blue thing and called the day a rout
```

We woke early in Bangkok for the ride to BKK, heading for Siem Reap, Cambodia. I was already feeling a little off, the vague scratchy pre-sickness that makes you suspicious of your own body, so I took a DayQuil and decided that was probably the kind of practical optimism travel required.

At the Bangkok Airways counter we checked in without much drama, then went to the Bangkok Airways lounge to wait. It was a good lounge in the way airport lounges become very impressive when you are tired enough. I had coffee. There was sweet-and-salty popcorn being made fresh in one of those carnival-style poppers, which felt oddly cheerful for an airport morning. Then, while sitting there in that temporary comfort, we discovered that our flight from Cambodia to Vietnam, the one we were supposed to take in two days, had been rescheduled to "tomorrow" which in this case meant the same day we would land in Cambodia. A clever piece of scheduling if the airline's goal was to remove us from physics altogether.

So we sat there and booked new tickets on VietAir from Siem Reap to Hanoi. By the time that was done, we realized we had spent longer on it than we meant to and now needed to get to the gate. Airport calm evaporated immediately.

At the gate I took more cold medicine, and when boarding started we were sent onto a bus outside the terminal. It drove us a surprisingly long way across the airport to where our plane was parked in some remote aircraft storage wilderness. We climbed on board and settled into what has quietly become a trip tradition: doing the New York Times crossword puzzles and seeing whether we could finish all three, the mini, the midi, and the big one.

Somewhere in the middle of that, we were served an unexpected Japanese curry and rice meal. I asked if it was free, because years of airline economics have taught me to be suspicious of any unsolicited kindness at 30,000 feet. The stewardess said, "Of course! You should fly Bangkok Airways more often!" It was a fair pitch. Curry and rice on a short flight was not what I expected, but there it was, warm and oddly comforting.

When we landed in Cambodia, we made straight for the bathrooms and then immigration, where we learned that our e-visas were not, in fact, all we needed. We still had to buy the real ones on the other side of the room from the immigration lanes. The whole process had the energy of a bureaucratic relay race. We were shuffled to lane 1, where we handed over passports and were briskly told, "Go to lane two!" At lane 2 we gave them our credit cards and were sent onward again: "Go to lane 3." At lane 3 we got everything back, along with little receipt slips that were apparently our visas now. There was a system here. I cannot explain it, but it existed.

Then back to the actual immigration line, where an officer told me he also needed my boarding pass. I couldn't find it. Maybe I had left it on the plane. Maybe it was folded into some dead corner of a pocket. Maybe it had simply chosen freedom. I told him I didn't have it. He motioned for me to hand over the rest of my documents anyway, which I did, and after a moment he gave me back my passport and visa. Just like that, I was officially in Cambodia.

We took an airport car to Viroth's Villa. On the drive I looked out the window and saw familiar signs of hard living sliding by: stray dogs with giant nipples swinging under them from litter after litter of puppies, emaciated livestock trying to eat grass that didn't seem to exist anymore, the kind of scenes that make even a passing car window feel uncomfortably honest. They were reminders that even in the US, for all its failures, most people do not have to live at that level of struggle.

At one point we passed a sign and building called "Tourismcity," which felt almost too on the nose, a literal announcement of where we were headed. Maybe more temples where you're asked to remove your shoes out of respect but are also gently funneled toward the gift shop on the way out. I also saw rows and rows of rental mopeds and bicycles. It brought to mind the Michael McCloud lyric, "Just another day in this tourist town bar," and that seemed about right.

We checked in at Viroth's Villa and then immediately headed out to the shopping boutique district. There I met some cats and a woman named Jodi, who seemed to possess an endless reserve of stories and who dubbed me "Canadian Grandpa" because I was wearing socks with my Perry shoes. Fair enough. I bought some art and a postcard, then went to the post office to mail it. Along the way I passed one tuktuk after another, each driver calling out, "Need ride?" with that practiced mix of hope and routine.

Afterward we went to River Cafe for dinner. I ordered something called a "Burn in Hall," hoping it might burn out whatever cold was moving into my head and chest. What arrived was blue and seemed to contain no detectable alcohol at all. Kate got a drink with a chili in it. Later we realized she had gotten my drink and I had gotten hers, which explained a few things, though not the color. By then the cold had progressed from possibility to fact. My nose had crossed fully into runny territory.

After dinner we went back to the room. I stayed behind to deal with the cold while the girls went out for spa massages. When they were asked if they wanted the 60- or 90-minute option, I called after them, "Get the 90!" It felt like both good advice and the only useful contribution I had left in me.

I took a shower, let the cold medicine continue whatever negotiations it was conducting with my bloodstream, and fell asleep watching airplane videos.
