## Saturday, April 25 - Kismet, Cold Air, and Chicken Sticks

```poem
I chased a bus and found a lion fish instead,
Paid for a photo and ignored what my wallet said,
Made a brief new friend between heat and mall café light,
Then ate satay like a person done making choices right.
```

I stumbled out of my room at the Shelton early, backpack on, camera packed, water filled, and set off in search of food. After wandering around a while and not finding much open, I came upon a place called **Ya Kun Kaya Toast**. The seats outside were full, and there was a line leading into the place where you ordered, which in my book is always a good sign, so I joined everyone else. I had also heard that this toast-and-coffee breakfast situation was something I needed to do.

Ordering was a little clumsy at first, for all the obvious reasons: language barriers, my unfamiliarity with the process, and the mildly chaotic nature of a busy breakfast place. After staring at the menu long enough to pretend I understood it, I chose what seemed like the default popular order: toast with an egg and coffee.

One thing surprised me. They did not ask how I wanted my eggs cooked. So in true traveler fashion, and following the example of the people ahead of me, I decided to just wait and see what arrived. Sometimes that is confidence. Sometimes it is fatigue wearing a fake mustache.

Afterward I went back to the hotel to regroup, and when I came back out, a hop-on hop-off bus was just pulling up. Kismet, I thought. I bought a ticket and got on.

I plugged my little red headphones into the jack on the side of the bus and listened to the history of the streets as we rolled by. Looking at the map, I saw an upcoming stop called **Marina Boulevard**. That felt mandatory based on the name alone.

Then came another bit of kismet. While wandering around, I happened upon the **Merlion**, one of the famous sights in Singapore. Checkbox checked.

I went to get my Nikon camera out of my bag and found that it was *hot*. Not warm. Hot, like it had been left in direct sun for an hour. I realized I had left it on in video mode and it had been recording for quite a long time while sealed in my bag with no way to dissipate heat. The battery was nearly dead. I panicked for a second, wondering where my spare was, then remembered that for once I had done something right and brought it with me instead of leaving it at the hotel. Crisis averted. The adventure could continue.

I wandered more along the water’s edge and found a large grassy park by the water, but this was fresh water, and I knew I was close to salt water somewhere. I wanted to find it, so I kept moving.

Along the way I found a bike stand. On one side were the new battery-powered rental bikes. On the other side were old bikes, maybe even forgotten ones, rusty but still functional. I stopped there for a moment and imagined a photograph, or maybe some sort of artwork, about that relationship. Old and new. Singapore kept offering that contrast without needing to explain itself.

Eventually I reached the main **Marina Bay Waterfront Promenade**. I stepped through the doors of a shopping mall and felt that first wave of cold air-conditioning drift over me. It was less a breeze than a moral argument against walking outdoors.

I saw a coffee shop called **Ralph’s** and decided that sounded good. I ordered a coffee and sat in the “outside” seating area so I could people-watch as people moved through the mall.

An older couple sat down next to me. I pretended to write in my journal, but I was really listening.

The woman took a sip of her coffee and said, “Oh! That’s a mighty fine cup of coffee!”

The man agreed. “This is lovely.”

“So much better than walking around in the heat,” she said.

He paused. “Not being rushed.”

I loved that little exchange. In my head I built an entire story for them. I imagined they were on some kind of singles tour, not very familiar with each other, supposed to be seeing the whole city in one heroic day, and instead had quietly defected in favor of coffee and air-conditioning. A far more respectable itinerary.

Then I looked at the clock and realized it was already 2 PM. The day was flying by and I was just sitting there drinking coffee. I was the one who needed to be rushing. But I was in the same place as that older couple. I was loving the air-conditioning. Too bad. Time to go.

I left the mall, went down some stairs, and found myself along a river promenade with strange metal gates set up. I thought maybe they were there to keep bikes away from pedestrians, though I wasn’t sure.

I kept walking and finally found one of the main things I had wanted to see: **Gardens by the Bay**.

I bought a ticket for both the **Flower Dome** and the **Cloud Dome**, which, according to the sign, included **Jurrasic Park**. Inside the Flower Dome, the exhibits were arranged by geography, which is a classic botanical garden move. At one point a girl passed by with her significant other and said softly, “It smells so nice in here.”

She was right.

Hundreds of varieties of flowers were blooming at once, and the smell reminded me of my grandmother’s old greenhouse, that mix of flowers and fertilizer that is somehow both earthy and sweet and unmistakably alive. One especially nice exhibit centered on tulips, red and pink and yellow and multicolored, fragrant enough that I slowed down for them.

After a full loop and a sequence of ramps up and down, the route deposited me neatly at the exit, which was conveniently right in front of the entrance to the next area, the **Cloud Dome**.

Inside the Cloud Dome, a spiral path led upward around a central tower covered in ferns and vines. Along the way were animatronic dinosaurs, including a T-Rex and raptors. It was all very well done. The path was wide enough that I didn’t feel rushed, and just as importantly, wide enough to get around people who were in less of a rush than I was.

When I left the Cloud Dome, I wandered outside again and found myself in the **Supertree Grove**. It looked cool, but I decided not to pay to go up into one of the supertrees. I was getting a little worn out by the vacation economics of having to pay for every possible version of standing somewhere.

In any case, I needed to find my hop-on hop-off bus stop. According to the cartoon map, I was in the right area. According to my actual experience, I was apparently not.

Instead I found myself climbing stairs to some kind of bridge crossing a body of water, river or pond, I couldn’t quite tell, but it was leading me toward **Marina Bay Sands**. Another major Singapore checkbox. It looked exactly like the photos everyone has seen: two towers side by side with what appears to be a futuristic space canoe balanced on top. Trees were visible up there too. Nature, it seemed, mattered to Singapore, which made sense when your whole country is essentially one giant city.

I found an elevator that only went down, so I took it one level and got out. From there I saw the doors into the main hotel lobby. You could tell immediately this was an expensive hotel. The floors were polished so smooth they looked wet, and that impression was reinforced by a waist-high laminar-flow water feature running along the wall near the entrance.

*This is where I should’ve stayed,* I thought.

Then I looked up the room prices.

$1700 a night.

Okay, maybe not. My roughly $200-a-night room with a shower and a bed suddenly felt more than acceptable.

Tickets for the rooftop observatory were obtained via QR code, and an escalator took me down a level into one of those history rooms, walls lined with pictures and paragraphs I read dutifully while knowing full well I would forget them as soon as I turned away. Then we were directed to the elevators waiting to take us 57 floors up.

When the elevator opened on the top floor, I was of course immediately stopped for a professional tourist photo. That is how these places work. But the view was exceptional. No denying it. There were more plaques and more facts and more polished presentation, and then the actual skyline doing most of the work anyway.

It was one of those vacation things where you temporarily pretend money is not real. No, the elevator ride and the view probably were not worth the $27. But I was still glad I did it. The middle section of the upper platform was reserved for hotel guests, and from my $27 peasants’ platform I could see the big infinity pool. On the way out, I was persuaded to buy the photo they had taken of me on the way in. I always forget to take selfies, so I made another vacation splurge.

I am embarrassed to admit I paid $65 for that photo, and that was in US dollars. I think I briefly forgot I was on a budget and replaced it with the deeply flawed principle of *well, I’m here now*.

I left Marina Bay Sands and resumed my search for the hop-on hop-off bus. Somehow this led me back to the water. Was I led there or drawn to it? Hard to say. Either way, I ended up near the **Helix Bridge**, another Singapore tourist checkbox, and there I finally understood those metal grates I had seen earlier along the river.

There was a triathlon about to happen.

There was a water station set up on the bridge, so I stopped and asked when the participants would be passing by. “Forty minutes,” they told me. I decided to stay and watch from the bridge. From where I stood I could see part of the running or biking section above, whichever it was at that point, and below me I could also see the ramp where participants would run into the water for the swim. I had never seen that in person.

While I was waiting, a girl walked by and, I assume noticing my Nikon, asked if I could take a picture of her with her camera. I did my best. People pictures are not exactly my specialty, or even something I especially enjoy, but I noticed at one point that her face was in shadow, so I told her, and she moved a few steps until I said, “Okay, that’s good,” and took a few more.

I expected her to leave after that, but instead she stayed and we started talking. We were both solo travelers. She was from Canada. She had started in Tokyo like me, then come to Singapore, and next she was heading to **Kuala Lumpur**. Eventually she would end up in China, where she had family.

The conversation kept going, both of us standing there in the sun on the bridge. We checked the time. There was still a while before the racers were supposed to arrive.

There was a pause, and then, somewhat out of character for me, I decided, what the hell, what’s the worst that can happen, and asked if she wanted to go get a coffee. Maybe in the mall building with all that excellent air-conditioning.

I was more than a little surprised when she said yes.

We found a café, a different one from the earlier stop, and I ordered my standard iced café latte. She got an iced Americano. I learned that she was researching **ALS**. I told her I didn’t know much about it except that it was a neurological disease, and asked whether research was focused on a cure or on management. She said, unfortunately, still just managing it.

We drank our coffees slowly and talked about travel. Had she noticed the same lack of napkins? She had. Had she noticed that GPS always seemed confused? Yes, absolutely. I kept nursing my coffee, not wanting the easy conversation to end, and when I could no longer reasonably stretch out the drink, I suggested we go through the Flower Dome and Cloud Dome together.

Traveling solo can be liberating, but it can also be lonely and isolating. I did not care that I had already seen the exhibits. I had realized in that short stretch of time that this was one of the very few non-work-related conversations I had had in a long while, and I was glad to revisit tourist attractions if it meant not returning immediately to being alone.

The sun was setting by then, and the parts of the domes exposed to exterior light looked different now, warmer with the light of the setting sun. It changed the whole mood of the place without changing the place itself.

After we exited, it was fully dark and the supertrees were all lit up. We stayed there together for a few final photographs, and then, like some temporary chemical or magnetic bond that had reached its natural limit, we went our separate ways. We were both solo travelers again.

My next stop was **Spectra**, the light and water show, another very touristy display of lasers, fountains, colored lights, and an impressively solid sound system with subwoofers hidden somewhere under the giant patio.

After the show, I spotted a self-service fresh-squeezed orange juice machine and had to try it. I had never seen an automated self-service fresh orange juice machine with ice before, which seemed absurdly specific and wonderful.

And it was.

A little farther down the waterfront promenade I found a duo performing with an acoustic guitar. I had not seen much live music on this trip yet, so I stayed a while and listened. But eventually my wandering heart was not really in it. I wanted a travel buddy, or I wanted to fast-forward to the part of the trip where my two cousins arrived. I felt stuck in travel limbo. Maybe it was just the long day of walking in the heat, but I did not think so.

I was hungry, so I started looking for food based mostly on what was open and close by. I chose a pizza place, not because I expected the world’s greatest pizza, but because I was tired and hungry and trying to simplify my standards.

Then, in a very Singapore sort of way, that plan evaporated. While walking toward the pizza shop, I saw a bright glow in the distance, and as I got closer I noticed a crowd. At that hour, there is usually only one explanation for that many people gathering in one place.

Food.

I was not wrong. It was a giant food court with what felt like endless options, and at the back of it, out along an outdoor street, I found **Satay Street**, an entire stretch lined with tables and satay stands, smoke rising from all of them, flames visible as skewers were turned over charcoal. I picked a line. I was pretty sure it did not matter which one.

Again, I was right.

The ten sticks of chicken satay I ordered, served with a sweet peanut sauce, disappeared much too quickly.

The seating was first come, first served, just sit wherever you can. I stayed a while and listened to the conversations around me. There were pitchers of beer on the tables, and I had no idea where they had come from, but I also did not really want to drink alone. I knew I probably could have made friends quickly in a place like that. But I was tired. It had been a long day, and I thought I just needed sleep.

I ordered a **Grab**, and before long I was back at the Shelton. After a hot shower, I crawled into bed and fell asleep almost immediately.
