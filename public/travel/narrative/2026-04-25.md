## Saturday, April 25 - Toast, Tourists, and Temporary Company

```poem
I chased a bus and nearly cooked my gear,
Paid tourist prices with selective fear,
Found flowers, dinosaurs, and satay smoke,
Then slept so fast I barely had a joke.
```

I stumbled out of my room at The Sultan early, backpack on, water filled, camera packed, and one clear objective in mind: find food. That turned out to be less immediate than I had hoped. I wandered around for a while without much luck, the city still not fully awake, until I came across Ya Kun Kaya Toast. The outside seats were full, and there was a line feeding into the ordering counter, which in my book is usually the only breakfast review system I need. I joined it. I had also heard that this toast-and-coffee breakfast setup was something I needed to try, so there I was, sleepy and compliant.

Ordering was clumsy at first, for the usual reasons. Language barriers, my own unfamiliarity with the process, and the slightly chaotic energy that seems to be standard at a busy breakfast place. After staring at the menu long enough to pretend I understood it fully, I went with what looked like the default popular order: toast, egg, and coffee.

One thing surprised me. No one asked how I wanted the eggs cooked. So, in true traveler fashion, and following the example of the people ahead of me, I decided this was a problem for Future Me. Let whatever arrives, arrive.

After breakfast I went back to the hotel to regroup, and when I came back out a hop-on hop-off bus was just pulling up. Kismet, I thought. Or at least a version of it that runs on a published schedule. I bought a ticket and got on.

I plugged my little red headphone set into the jack beside my seat and listened to recorded history while we rolled through the streets. Looking at the map, I saw an upcoming stop called Marina Boulevard. That felt mandatory on name alone.

Then another round of luck. While wandering around there, I happened upon the Merlion, one of the famous sights to see in Singapore. Checkbox checked.

I went to pull my Nikon out of my bag and it was *hot*. Not warm. Hot like it had been sitting in direct summer sun for an hour. I realized I had left it on in video mode and it had been recording inside my bag for who knows how long with absolutely no way to vent heat. The battery was nearly dead. I had a quick shot of panic while trying to remember where the spare was, and then remembered that, for once, I had behaved like a competent photographer and brought it with me instead of leaving it at the hotel. Crisis averted. The adventure could continue.

I wandered farther along the water’s edge and found a large grassy park by the water, but this was fresh water, and I knew I was close to salt water somewhere. I wanted to find it, or at least convince myself I could tell the difference. So I moved on.

At one point I came across a bike stand. On one side were new battery-powered rental bikes. On the other side were old bikes, maybe forgotten, rusty but still seemingly usable. I paused there longer than I expected, imagining a photograph or some kind of artwork about the relationship between the two. Old and new, both still present, neither fully replacing the other.

Eventually I made my way to the main Marina Bay Waterfront Promenade. I stepped into a shopping mall and felt that first wave of cold air-conditioning drift over me, the kind that makes you forgive a building for being a mall.

I saw a coffee shop called Ralph’s and decided that sounded right. I ordered a coffee and sat in the “outside” seating, which was still inside enough to be comfortable, and watched people move through the mall.

An older couple sat down next to me. I pretended to write in my journal, but really I was listening.

The woman took a sip of her coffee and said, “Oh! That’s a mighty fine cup of coffee!”

The man agreed. “This is lovely.”

“So much better than walking around in the heat,” she said.

He paused for a moment. “Not being rushed.”

I loved that little exchange. I started inventing a whole story for them in my head, that they were on some kind of singles tour, that they barely knew each other, that they had looked at the schedule for seeing the entire city in one day and quietly decided not to bother. Maybe they had bailed together. Maybe they had just met. Whatever the truth was, they seemed happy with coffee, air-conditioning, and nowhere urgent to be.

Then I looked at the clock and realized it was already 2 PM. The day was moving fast, and I was the one sitting there drinking coffee like time had stopped. But I understood exactly what the older couple meant. I was also enjoying not being rushed. Too bad. Time to go.

I left the mall, went down some stairs, and found myself on a river promenade with odd metal gates set up along it. I guessed they might be there to keep bikes away from pedestrians, but I wasn’t sure.

I kept walking and finally found one of the major things I had wanted to see: Gardens by the Bay.

I bought a ticket for both the Flower Dome and the Cloud Dome, which, according to the sign, included “Jurrasic Park.” Spelling aside, that sounded promising.

Inside the Flower Dome, the exhibits were laid out by geography, which is a classic botanical garden strategy but still effective. A girl walked by with her partner and said softly, “It smells so nice in here.”

She was right. It did. Hundreds of different flowers blooming at once had created that greenhouse smell I remembered from my grandmother’s old greenhouse, a mix of flowers and fertilizer, sweet and earthy at the same time. One especially nice display featured tulips in the center, red, pink, yellow, and multicolored, fragrant enough that they felt almost excessive.

After looping through ramps and exhibits, I came out at the exit and, conveniently, the entrance to the Cloud Dome was right there in front of me.

Inside, a spiral path wound up around a central tower covered in ferns and vines, and along the way there were animatronic dinosaurs, including a T-Rex and raptors. It was actually very well done. The path was wide enough that you didn’t feel squeezed along with everyone else, and also wide enough to pass people who were operating at a more leisurely pace than I was.

When I left the Cloud Dome, I wandered outside and ended up in the Supertree Grove. It looked cool, but I decided not to pay extra to go up into one of the supertrees. I was getting a little vacationed out, if that’s a phrase, by having to pay for every single thing I did everywhere I went.

In any case, I needed to find my hop-on hop-off bus stop. According to the cartoon map, there should have been a yellow stop somewhere around here. I looked at the map, looked at the surroundings, looked back at the map, and remained unconvinced by all available information.

Instead of finding the bus, I found myself climbing stairs onto some kind of bridge over a body of water I couldn’t quite identify as river or pond. It was leading me toward Marina Bay Sands, though, and that was certainly another checkbox. It looked exactly like the photos everyone has seen: side-by-side towers topped with what appears to be a futuristic space canoe. Trees were visible on top. Nature, it seemed, was important to Singapore, which makes sense when your entire country is basically one giant city.

I found an elevator that only went down, so I got in, went down one level, and walked out into the hotel. The lobby announced itself immediately as expensive. The floors were polished so smooth they looked wet, and that impression was reinforced by a sheet-like laminated water feature running along the wall near the entrance.

*This is where I should’ve stayed,* I thought.

Then I checked the room price. $1700 a night.

Okay, maybe not. Maybe my roughly $200 room with a bed and a shower was, in fact, perfectly respectable.

Tickets to the rooftop observatory were sold via QR code, naturally, and an escalator led me down one level into a sort of history-of-this-place room, which is to say a room lined with photos and text I dutifully read while knowing full well I would forget most of it as soon as I turned away. Then we were directed to a bank of elevators waiting to take us 57 floors up.

At the top, of course, you were first intercepted for a professional photo. Tourist machinery was working exactly as designed. The view, though, was undeniably worth seeing. There were more plaques, more photos, more facts to absorb and then immediately lose. The middle portion of the upper platform was reserved for hotel guests, including the famous infinity pool, which I viewed from what felt very much like the peasants’ platform my $27 ticket allowed me to access.

Was the elevator ride and the view worth $27? Probably not in any rational accounting sense. But I was still glad I did it. Vacation math is its own branch of economics. On the way out, I was persuaded to buy the photograph they had taken of me on the way in. I always forget to take selfies, so I made another vacation exception. I am slightly embarrassed to admit I paid $65 for it, in US dollars. Apparently I had temporarily suspended my relationship with money.

After leaving Marina Bay Sands, I resumed my search for the hop-on hop-off bus. It drew me back toward the water. Or I drifted there on purpose. I’m not sure there’s much difference.

Steps led up to the Helix Bridge, another Singapore checkbox, and once I got there I finally learned what those metal grates along the river were for. A triathlon was about to happen.

There was a water station set up on the bridge, so I stopped and asked when the participants would come through. Forty minutes, they told me. I decided to stay and watch. From where I was standing I could see part of the running or biking route on the bridge, whichever section it was, and down below I could also see the ramp where participants would run into the water for the swim. I had never seen that in person.

While I was waiting, a girl walked by, noticed my Nikon, and asked if I could take a photo of her with her camera. I did my best, even though photographing people is not my specialty and not something I especially enjoy. At one point I noticed her face was in shadow, so I told her, and she moved. She seemed to appreciate that I had noticed. She took a few steps over, I said that looked good, and I snapped a few more.

I expected that to be the end of it, but instead she stayed and talked. We were both solo travelers. She was from Canada. She had also started in Tokyo, like me, but unlike me she had come straight from there to Singapore. Next she was headed to Kuala Lumpur, and eventually to China, where she had family.

We stood on the bridge talking in the sun. We checked the time. There was still a long wait before the racers arrived.

Then there was a pause in the conversation, and, very out of character for me, I thought, *what the hell, what’s the worst that can happen?* I asked if she wanted to go get a coffee, maybe in the mall, where there was also that wonderful air-conditioning. I was more than a little surprised when she said yes.

We found a café, a different one from the earlier stop, and I ordered my standard iced café latte. She got an iced Americano. I learned that she was researching ALS. I told her I didn’t know much about it beyond the fact that it was a neurological disease, and I asked whether the research was for a cure or for management. Unfortunately, she said, still just management.

We drank our coffees slowly and talked about travel. Had she also noticed the weird lack of napkins? She had. Had she noticed GPS always seemed confused? Yes. Me too. I nursed my drink because I didn’t want the conversation to end. It had been a long time since I’d had such an easy, non-work-related conversation with anyone.

When I finally couldn’t stretch the coffee out any further, I suggested we go to the Flower Dome and Cloud Dome together.

Traveling solo can be liberating, but it can also get lonely in a quiet, persistent way. I didn’t care that I had already seen the exhibits. By then I had realized this was one of the few genuine conversations I’d had in a long time, and I was more than willing to revisit tourist attractions to keep it going.

So we went back through them together. By then the sun was setting, and the parts of the exhibits lit by exterior light had changed. Everything looked warmer under the evening light.

When we came out, it was fully dark, and the supertrees were lit up. We stayed there a while longer taking final photos, and then, like some temporary chemical or magnetic bond losing its charge, we split off and went our separate ways. We were both solo travelers again.

My next stop was the Spectra light and water show, another heavily touristed production of lasers, fountains, colored lights, and a surprisingly solid sound system with subwoofers hidden somewhere under the giant patio. It was big, loud, polished, and exactly what it intended to be.

Afterward I spotted a self-service fresh-squeezed orange juice machine and had to try it. I had never seen an automated fresh orange juice machine with ice before, which seemed almost absurdly efficient. It sounded amazing. It was.

A little farther down the waterfront promenade, I found a duo performing with an acoustic guitar. I hadn’t seen much live music on this trip yet, so I stayed for a while and listened. But after a bit my wandering heart just wasn’t in it. I wanted a travel buddy, or I wanted to fast-forward to when my two cousins arrived. I felt stuck in travel limbo. Maybe it was just a long day of walking in the heat. I don’t think it was only that.

I was hungry, so I started looking for food based on the entirely respectable criteria of it being open and nearby. I had settled on a pizza place, not because I expected greatness but because I was tired and hungry and pizza is at least honest about what it is.

But on the way there, Singapore did that thing it sometimes does where it casually rearranges your plans. I saw a bright concentration of light in the distance, and as I got closer I noticed crowds gathering around it. At that hour, that many people in one place usually meant one thing.

Food.

I was right. It was a giant food court with what felt like endless options, and toward the back, opening onto an outdoor street, I found Satay Street, lined with tables and satay stands, smoke rolling upward from charcoal grills, flames flashing as skewers were turned over the heat. I picked a line. I was pretty sure it didn’t matter which one. It was going to be good.

And again, I was right.

The ten sticks of chicken satay I ordered came with a sweet peanut sauce, and they disappeared embarrassingly fast. The seating was first come, first served, just sit wherever you can. I stayed a while and listened to the people around me talking. There were pitchers of beer on the tables and I wasn’t entirely sure where they had come from, but I also didn’t want to drink alone. I knew I probably could have made friends quickly in a place like that. But I was tired. It had been a long day, and I think what I really needed was sleep.

So I ordered a Grab, and before long I was back at The Sultan. After a hot shower, I crawled into bed and fell asleep almost immediately.
