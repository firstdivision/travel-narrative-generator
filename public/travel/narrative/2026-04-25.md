## Saturday, April 25 - Toast, Towers, and Temporary Company

```poem
I chased my breakfast with a baffled stare,
Paid tourist prices for expensive air,
Made a friend between the heat and bay,
Then fed my tired soul with satay.
```

I stumbled out of my hotel room at The Sultan early, backpack on, full of water and carrying my camera, in search of food. After wandering around for a while and not finding much open, I came upon a place called Ya Kun Kaya Toast. The seats outside were full, and there was a line leading into the place where you ordered. In my book, that is always a good sign, so I joined everybody else in line. I had also heard that this toast-box breakfast was something I needed to partake in.

Ordering was a little clumsy at first, for all the usual reasons: language barriers, my unfamiliarity with the process, and the somewhat expected chaos of a busy breakfast restaurant. After studying the menu, I went with what seemed like the default, most popular order: toast, an egg, and coffee.

One thing surprised me. They did not ask how I wanted my eggs cooked, so in true traveler fashion, and following the example of those before me, I decided I would just see what showed up.

What showed up was not quite what I expected.

The egg appeared to be a barely boiled egg, with very little solid white. It was soupy. I took the small spoon from the tray and slurped up some of this egg slop. Not exactly my cup of tea.

The toast, though, was much more to my liking. It was essentially four triangles of toast, and it came with some sort of brown sauce. Not knowing what it was, I jammed a piece of toast into it, picked up a good blob of the brown stuff, and put the whole thing in my mouth. I was pleasantly surprised that it was sweet and sugary. I had been at least half prepared for it to be fish-flavored.

My eyes drifted back to the eggs. They looked at me, watery and challenging, daring me to eat more. I did. I ate it all, because apparently I wanted to be a *traveler*.

Next week, back in KL, I would tell this story to a coworker. When I told her I had eaten kaya toast, she smiled and asked, "Did you eat the egg?" I proudly told her, "Yes! I ate the whole thing with the little spoon!" She laughed and said, "You are supposed to dip the toast into the egg."

Oh.

Yeah.

That makes way more sense.

I felt like Randy Marsh from *South Park*.

After I finished all my eggs, coffee, and toast, I went back to the hotel to regroup, and when I left again a hop-on hop-off bus was just pulling up. Kismet, I thought. I bought a ticket and got on.

I plugged my little red headphone set into the jack on the side of the bus and listened to the history of these streets as we rolled by. Looking at the map, I saw an upcoming stop called Marina Boulevard. That felt mandatory based on name alone.

Then another small case of kismet: while wandering around, I came upon the Merlion, one of the famous sights to see in Singapore. Checkbox checked.

I went to get my Nikon out of my bag and found it was *hot*, like it had been sitting in direct summer sun for an hour. I realized I had left it on in video mode, and it had been recording for quite a long time while stuffed inside my bag with no way to dissipate heat. The battery was nearly dead. I panicked for a moment, wondering where the spare was, until I remembered that, like a good photographer, I had brought it with me instead of leaving it back at the hotel.

Crisis averted. The adventure could continue.

I wandered around the water's edge some more and found a large grass park by the water, but this was fresh water, and I knew I was close to salt water. I wanted to find it, so I moved on.

Along the way I found a bike stand. On one side were the new battery-powered rental bikes. On the other side were old bikes, maybe even forgotten ones, rusty but still in working order. I paused and imagined a photograph, or some sort of artwork, trying to depict that relationship. Old and new. Singapore seemed to like that contrast.

Further around the waterfront I reached the main Marina Bay Waterfront Promenade. I stepped through the doors of a shopping mall and felt the sweet breath of cold air-conditioning wash over me. It was one of those immediate, physical pleasures you feel in your shoulders.

I saw a coffee shop called Ralph's and decided that sounded good. I ordered a coffee and sat down in the "outside" seating so I could people-watch as everyone moved through the mall.

An older couple sat down next to me. I pretended to write in my journal, but really I was listening.

The woman took a sip of her coffee and exclaimed, "Oh! That's a mighty fine cup of coffee!"

The man agreed. "This is lovely."

"So much better than walking around in the heat," she said.

He paused, then added, "Not being rushed."

I loved that little exchange. I started inventing a story for them in my head. Maybe they were on some kind of singles tour. They did not seem very familiar with each other. Maybe the tour was supposed to cover the entire city in one day, and they had met, looked at each other, and silently agreed that perhaps they did not actually want to be hustled from attraction to attraction like overbooked cattle.

I looked at the clock and realized it was already 2 PM. The day was flying by, and I was sitting there sipping coffee. I was the one who needed to be rushing. But I understood exactly what that older couple meant. I was loving that air-conditioning.

Too bad. Time to go.

I left the mall, went down some stairs, and found myself along a river promenade with funny gates set up. I thought maybe they were there to keep bikes away from people, though I was not sure. I kept walking and finally found one of the things I had been looking for: Gardens by the Bay.

I bought a ticket for both the Flower Dome and the Cloud Dome, which, according to the signage, included "Jurrasic Park."

Inside the Flower Dome, you are led through a series of exhibits separated by geography, a standard botanical-garden tactic. A girl walked by with her significant other and said softly, "It smells so nice in here."

Yes. It did.

Hundreds of different varieties of flowers all blooming at the same time, and the smell reminded me of my grandmother's old greenhouse, that mix of fertilizer and flowers. One especially nice exhibit had tulips on display in center stage: red, pink, yellow, multicolor, and fragrant. After moving around a loop and up and down some ramps, I was deposited at the exit, and conveniently enough, right in front of the entrance to the next area: the Cloud Dome.

Inside the Cloud Dome, there was a spiral path leading up a central tower covered in ferns and vines, and along the way were animatronic dinosaurs, including a T-Rex and raptors. It was really well done. The path was wide enough that you did not feel rushed, which also meant it was wide enough to get past people who were in even less of a rush than you were.

I left the Cloud Dome and wandered outside some more until I found myself in the Supertree garden. It looked cool, but I decided not to pay to go up into one of the super trees. I was getting a little vacationed out, if that is a phrase, or maybe just tired of having to pay for everything I did everywhere I went.

In any case, I needed to find my hop-on hop-off bus stop. It was supposed to be somewhere around there. I kept looking at my map, then at the things around me, and I swore it had to be close. I was in Gardens by the Bay, and that was exactly where the cartoon map showed a yellow stop.

Instead, I found myself climbing a set of stairs to some kind of bridge over a piece of water, pond or river, I could not tell, but it was leading me toward Marina Bay Sands. That was certainly another checkbox. You have probably seen photos of it: the futuristic space canoe balanced on top of the towers, with trees visible up there. Nature, it seemed, was important to the people of Singapore, which made sense when your entire country is essentially one giant city.

I found an elevator that only went down, so I got in, went down one level, and stepped out near the main hotel lobby. You could tell immediately this was a very expensive hotel. It was the kind of place where the floors were polished so smooth they looked wet, and this impression was reinforced by a waist-high laminated-flow water feature lining the wall next to the entry.

"This is where I should've stayed," I thought.

Then I looked up the prices.

$1700 a night.

OK, maybe not. Suddenly my roughly $200-a-night room with a shower and a bed seemed more than acceptable.

Tickets for the rooftop observatory were obtained through a QR code, and an escalator led me down a level to a sort of history of the history of this place, which is to say a room with walls lined with pictures and words that I read despite knowing perfectly well I would forget all of it as soon as I turned away. Then we were directed to a room with a line of elevators waiting to take us 57 floors up.

At the top, of course, I was stopped so a professional photographer could take my picture. This was, after all, a tourist attraction. But the view was exceptional. There was no denying that. More plaques, more facts, more words I dutifully read and promptly forgot. One of those vacation things where you pretend money does not exist. No, the elevator ride and the view probably were not worth the $27. But I was still glad I did it.

The middle part of the upper platform was reserved for hotel guests. There was a big infinity pool, visible from what felt like the peasants' platform my $27 ticket restricted me to.

On my way out, I was convinced to buy the photograph of myself taken by the photographer on the way in. I always forget to take selfies, so I made another "it's vacation" splurge. I am a little embarrassed to admit that I paid $65 for the photo, and that was in US dollars. I think. I do not remember. I was on vacation, which is a dangerous state of mind for arithmetic.

I left Marina Bay Sands and resumed the search for my hop-on hop-off bus. It led me back toward the water. Was I led there or drawn to it? Is there a difference? Steps carried me up to the Helix Bridge, another Singapore tourist checkbox, and there I finally learned the reason for those metal grates lining the river.

There was a triathlon about to happen.

There was a water station on the bridge, so I stopped and asked when the participants would be by. "Forty minutes," they said. I decided to stick around and watch from that spot on the bridge. I could see the running or biking section up on the bridge itself, whichever it was, but below me in the water I could also see the ramp where they would run into the water for the swim. I had never seen that in person.

While I was waiting, a girl walked by and, I assume because she saw my Nikon camera, asked if I would take a photo of her with her camera. I did my best, even though people pictures are not really my specialty, or even something I especially enjoy. At one point I noticed her face was in shadow, so I told her. She moved a few steps, and when I said, "OK, that's good," I snapped a few more.

I expected her to leave, but instead she stayed to chat for a while. We were both solo travelers. She was from Canada, traveling through Asia. Tokyo first, like me, then Singapore, and next she was headed to Kuala Lumpur. Eventually she would end up in China, where she had family.

The conversation continued with both of us standing in the sun on that bridge. We checked the time. There was still a long wait until the racers were supposed to arrive.

There was a pause in the conversation, and out of character for me, I decided, what the hell, what is the worst that can happen? I asked if she wanted to go get a coffee, maybe back in the mall with all that wonderful air-conditioning.

I was more than a little surprised when she said yes.

We found a café, a different one from the one where I had stopped earlier. I ordered my standard iced café latte. She ordered an iced Americano. I learned that she was researching ALS. I told her I did not know much about it except that it was a neurological disease. I asked if she was searching for a cure or just managing it, and she said, unfortunately, still just managing it.

The coffees were drunk slowly while we talked about travel.

Had she noticed the same lack of napkins?

She had.

Had she noticed that GPS always seemed confused?

Yes. Me too.

I nursed my coffee, not wanting this easy, flowing conversation to end, and when I could no longer stretch it out, I suggested we go to the Flower Dome and Cloud Dome together.

Traveling solo can be liberating, but it can also be lonely and isolating. I did not care that I had already seen those exhibits. I had realized, in the time I had spent talking with this person, that this was one of very few non-work-related conversations I had had in a long time, and I would gladly revisit some tourist attractions to keep it going.

So we went back through the exhibits again. The sun was setting now, so the parts exposed to exterior light looked different, warmer in the light of the setting sun.

After we exited, it was truly dark, and the super trees were all lit up. We stayed there together for final photographs, and then, like some temporary chemical or magnetic bond dissolving, we went our separate ways.

We were both, again, solo travelers.

My next stop was the Spectra light and water show, another very touristy display of lasers, water fountains, colored lights, and an impressively solid sound system complete with subwoofers hidden somewhere beneath that giant patio.

After the show, I saw a self-service fresh-squeezed orange juice machine, so I had some. I had never seen an automated self-service fresh-squeezed orange juice machine with ice in it before, which sounded amazing.

And it was.

A few steps farther down the waterfront promenade, I found a duo performing with an acoustic guitar. I had not seen much live music on this trip yet, so I stayed a while and listened to the songs they played. But soon my wandering heart did not seem to be in it anymore. I wanted a travel buddy, or I wanted to fast-forward to the part where my two cousins arrived. I felt stuck in travel limbo. Maybe it was just a long day of walking in the heat.

But I did not think so.

I was hungry, so I started trying to find food for no better reason than that it was open and close by. I chose a pizza place. I was not expecting the world's greatest pizza, but you never know. Maybe there would be something original about it. In reality, I was just tired and hungry.

Then, in a display of Singapore city magic, while walking toward the pizza shop I saw a bright light in the distance. As I got closer, I noticed there were lots of people there too. At that late hour there is usually only one reason so many people are gathered in one place.

Food.

I was not wrong.

It was a giant food court with what seemed like endless options, and at the back of it, out on an outdoor street, I found Satay Street, an entire street lined with tables and nothing but satay stands. Smoke billowed up from them. Flames flashed as skewers turned over charcoal. I picked a line. I was pretty sure it did not matter which one.

And again, I was not wrong.

The ten sticks of chicken satay I ordered, along with a sweet peanut sauce, disappeared much too quickly.

The seating was first come, first served, and you just sort of sat wherever you wanted. I stayed a while and tried to listen to the people around me having their conversations. There were pitchers of beer on the tables and I did not know where they had come from. But I also did not want to drink alone. I knew I probably could have made friends quickly in a place like that, but as mentioned previously, I was tired. It had been a long day, and I thought I needed sleep more than anything else.

I ordered a Grab, and a short while later I was back at my hotel. After a hot shower, I crawled into bed and fell immediately asleep.
