## Saturday, April 25 - Toast, Towers, and Air Conditioning

```poem
I chased breakfast with a camera near defeat,
Found flowers, fake dinosaurs, and refuge from the heat,
The city kept offering detours I could not deny,
And somehow it was 2PM before I knew why.
```

I stumbled out of my room at the Shelton early, backpack on, water filled, camera packed, and the usual vague but urgent mission of finding food. I wandered for a while without much luck, which is always a little deflating when you're hungry enough to start lowering your standards by the minute. Then I came across Ya Kun Kaya Toast.

The seats outside were full, and there was a line leading into the place where you ordered. In my book, that is almost always a good sign, so I joined the line. I had also heard this toast-box breakfast was something I needed to do, so there was no point pretending I was still deciding.

Ordering was clumsy at first, for all the predictable reasons: language barriers, my own unfamiliarity with the process, and the slightly chaotic rhythm of a busy breakfast place moving people through as fast as possible. I studied the menu, tried to act less confused than I felt, and settled on what seemed like the default popular order: toast, an egg, and coffee.

One thing surprised me. Nobody asked how I wanted my eggs cooked.

So, in true traveler fashion, and following the example of the people ahead of me, I decided not to overthink it and simply wait to see what arrived. There is a certain kind of breakfast surrender that travel forces on you.

Afterward I went back to the hotel to regroup. When I left again, a hop-on hop-off bus was just pulling up outside. Kismet, I thought. Or at least the kind of timing that makes you feel briefly endorsed by the universe. I bought a ticket and climbed on.

I plugged my little red headphone set into the jack beside the seat and listened to the recorded history of the streets as we rolled past them. Looking at the map, I saw an upcoming stop called Marina Boulevard. That felt mandatory on name alone.

Then came another bit of kismet. Wandering around after getting off, I happened upon the Merlion, one of the famous sights in Singapore. Checkbox checked. It is always satisfying when you accidentally find the thing you were supposed to deliberately go see.

I reached into my bag for my Nikon and found it alarmingly hot, like it had been left on a car dashboard in direct summer sun. For a second I had that clean flash of panic. Then I realized what I'd done: I had left it on, in video mode, and it had been recording for quite a while inside my bag with no way to dissipate heat. The battery was nearly dead. I started mentally spiraling toward the question of where my spare battery was, then remembered that, for once, I had behaved like a competent photographer and brought it with me instead of leaving it at the hotel. Crisis averted. The adventure could continue.

I kept wandering along the water's edge and found a large grassy park by the water. But this was fresh water, and I knew I was close to salt water somewhere, so I kept moving, still vaguely determined to find it. That was the mood of much of the day: walking toward things I only half understood.

Along the way I found a bike stand that caught my attention. On one side were new battery-powered rental bikes, neat and current and clearly part of the city's future-facing machinery. On the other side were old bikes, maybe even forgotten ones, rusty but still apparently functional. I stopped there for a moment imagining a photograph, or some kind of artwork, built around that contrast. Old and new. It felt almost too on-the-nose, which probably meant it was real.

Further around the waterfront I reached the main Marina Bay Waterfront Promenade. At some point I stepped through the doors of a shopping mall and felt that first breath of cold air conditioning wash over me. It was one of the best moments of the day, maybe not spiritually, but physically, which can matter more.

Inside I saw a coffee shop called Ralph's and decided that sounded right. I ordered a coffee and sat in the "outside" seating area so I could watch people moving through the mall. There is a very specific kind of rest that comes from sitting still in artificial cold while everyone else keeps walking.

An older couple sat down next to me. I pretended to write in my journal, but really I was listening.

The woman took a sip of her coffee and said, "Oh! That's a mighty fine cup of coffee!"

The man agreed. "This is lovely."

"So much better than walking around in the heat," she said.

He paused, then added, "Not being rushed."

I loved that little exchange. I immediately started inventing a backstory for them, which was none of my business but hard to resist. In my mind they were on some sort of singles tour, not very familiar with each other, maybe supposed to be seeing the entire city in a single over-optimized day. Maybe they met, looked at each other, looked at the schedule, and decided absolutely not. So instead they bailed and sat with coffee in the air conditioning like two sensible people reclaiming a day from logistics.

Then I checked the time and realized it was already 2 PM. The day was flying by and I was sitting there sipping coffee while acting as if I had all the time in the world. I was the one who should have been rushing. But I also understood exactly what that man meant. Not being rushed. It felt good. Too good.

Too bad. Time to go.

I left the mall, went down some stairs, and found myself along a river promenade with these odd little gates set up at intervals. I guessed they were there to keep bikes away from pedestrians, but I wasn't sure. Singapore kept presenting things that looked highly intentional, whether or not I understood the intention.

I walked more and finally found one of the places I had been wanting to see: Gardens by the Bay.

I bought a ticket for both the Flower Dome and the Cloud Dome, which, according to the signage, also included "Jurrasic Park." Spelling aside, I was in.

Inside the Flower Dome, the path led through a sequence of exhibits organized by geography, the familiar botanical garden method of making the planet legible one climate zone at a time. The air inside was cool and rich with plant smell. At one point a girl passed by with her significant other and said softly, "It smells so nice in here."

Yes, it did.

Hundreds of varieties of flowers were blooming all at once, and the scent reminded me of my grandmother's old greenhouse, that particular mixture of flowers and fertilizer, sweet and earthy at the same time. Not polished. Real. Memory has a way of arriving through smell before anything else has a chance to prepare for it.

One especially nice display was the tulip exhibit in the center, with red, pink, yellow, and multicolored blooms gathered together in a way that felt almost excessive, which is exactly what tulips deserve. They were fragrant too, not just decorative.

After looping through the dome and going up and down ramps, I was deposited at the exit, and conveniently enough the exit put me right in front of the entrance to the next area: the Cloud Dome.

Inside, a spiral path wound its way up around a central tower thick with ferns and vines. Along the route were animatronic dinosaurs, including a T-Rex and raptors. It was actually very well done. The path was wide enough that I didn't feel rushed, which also meant it was wide enough to get around people who were in even less of a rush than I was. That detail mattered.

The place managed to be both lush and faintly absurd in a way I appreciated. Plants, mist, engineered spectacle, prehistoric monsters, and tourists all moving upward together under controlled climate conditions. Modern travel in a nutshell, maybe.

When I left the Cloud Dome, I wandered outside again and found myself in the Supertree Grove, still moving through the day by a combination of intention, accident, and whatever amount of energy I had left.
