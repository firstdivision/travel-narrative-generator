## Saturday, April 25 - Toast, Towers, and a Long Walk

```poem
I chased a bus and found a lion by the bay
My camera nearly cooked itself halfway
I bought the view, then paid too much for me
Singapore is efficient, except economically
```

I stumbled out of my room at the Shelton early with my backpack, water, and camera, mostly in search of food. After wandering around for a while without finding much open, I came across Ya Kun Kaya Toast. The seats outside were full, and there was a line leading inside to the ordering counter. In my book, that is always a good sign, so I joined the line. I had also heard this toast-and-coffee breakfast situation was something I was supposed to do, so there I was, doing my duty.

Ordering was a little clumsy at first for all the usual reasons: language barriers, my unfamiliarity with the process, and the slightly chaotic energy that seems to hover over any busy breakfast place. After studying the menu, I went with what looked like the default popular order, toast with egg and coffee.

One thing surprised me. Nobody asked how I wanted my eggs cooked. So, in true traveler fashion, and following the example of everyone before me, I decided I would simply wait and see what appeared.

After breakfast I went back to the hotel to regroup, and when I came back out, a hop-on hop-off bus was just pulling up. Kismet, I thought. I bought a ticket and climbed on.

I plugged my little red headphones into the jack on the side of the bus and listened to the recorded history of the streets as we rolled past them. Looking at the map, I saw an upcoming stop called Marina Boulevard. That felt mandatory on name alone, so I got off there.

Then another case of kismet: while wandering around, I came upon the Merlion, one of the famous Singapore sights. Checkbox checked.

When I reached into my bag for my Nikon, it was *hot*. Not warm. Hot, like it had been lying in direct summer sun for an hour. I realized I had left it on in video mode and it had been recording inside my backpack with no way to dissipate the heat. The battery was nearly dead. I had a brief moment of panic about where the spare battery was, then remembered that, for once, I had actually brought it with me instead of leaving it in the hotel. Crisis averted. The adventure could continue.

I wandered farther along the water's edge and found a large grassy park beside the water, but this was fresh water, and I knew I was close to salt water somewhere. For reasons I can't fully explain, that distinction felt important, so I kept moving.

Along the way I found a bike stand. On one side were the new battery-powered rental bikes. On the other side were old bikes, maybe even forgotten ones, rusty but still apparently functional. I paused there for a while, imagining a photograph or some kind of artwork about that relationship. Old and new. Singapore kept handing me that contrast all day.

Further around the waterfront I reached the main Marina Bay Waterfront Promenade. I stepped through the doors of a shopping mall and felt that first wash of air-conditioning drift over me, the sweet artificial breath of civilization. It was hard not to feel grateful.

Inside I saw a coffee shop called Ralph's and decided that sounded right. I ordered a coffee and sat in the "outside" seating area so I could people-watch while still benefiting from the mall's refrigerated atmosphere.

An older couple sat down next to me. I pretended to write in my journal, but really I was listening.

The woman took a sip of her coffee and said, "Oh! That's a mighty fine cup of coffee!"

The man agreed. "This is lovely."

"So much better than walking around in the heat," she said.

He paused, then added, "Not being rushed."

I loved that little exchange. I immediately invented a backstory for them, naturally. In my head they were on some kind of singles tour, meant to be seeing the whole city in one hectic day, but they had met, realized they preferred coffee and air-conditioning to scheduled fun, and quietly defected. It seemed plausible enough.

Then I looked at the clock and realized it was already 2 PM. The day was disappearing while I sat there sipping coffee. I was the one who was supposed to be rushing. But I also understood exactly what the older couple meant. Not being rushed had its own appeal. Still. Too bad. Time to go.

I left the mall, went down some stairs, and found myself along a river promenade with these odd metal gates set up at intervals. I thought maybe they were there to keep bikes away from pedestrians, but I wasn't sure.

I kept walking and finally found one of the things I had been looking for all along: Gardens by the Bay.

I bought a ticket for both the Flower Dome and the Cloud Dome, which, according to the signage, also included "Jurassic Park."

Inside the Flower Dome, the path led through a series of exhibits divided by geography, which is a classic botanical garden move. A girl walked by with her significant other and said softly, "It smells so nice in here."

It did. Hundreds of varieties of flowers all blooming at once created that greenhouse smell I remembered from my grandmother's old greenhouse, part flowers, part fertilizer, warm and earthy and slightly artificial in the way growing things often are when concentrated indoors.

One especially nice exhibit centered on tulips. Red, pink, yellow, multicolored, all arranged in a bright concentrated burst and fragrant enough to stop me for a while.

After looping around and going up and down the ramps, I reached the exit, which very conveniently deposited me almost directly in front of the entrance to the next area, the Cloud Dome.

Inside the Cloud Dome, a spiral path led upward around a central tower of ferns and vines. Along the way were animatronic dinosaurs, including a T-Rex and raptors. It was actually very well done. The path was wide enough that I didn't feel rushed, and more importantly, wide enough to get around people who were in less of a rush than I was.

After that I wandered back outside into the Supertree Grove. It looked cool, but I decided not to pay to go up into one of the supertrees. I was getting a little worn down by the constant transaction of tourism, the way every interesting angle seemed to come with its own separate ticket. Vacation economics have a way of becoming abstract until they suddenly don't.

In any case, I needed to find my hop-on hop-off bus stop. It was supposed to be somewhere around there. I kept checking the map and the surrounding landmarks and muttering to myself that it had to be close. I was in Gardens by the Bay. The cartoon map clearly showed a yellow stop in Gardens by the Bay. This seemed straightforward, which probably should have made me suspicious.

Instead, I found myself climbing a set of stairs onto a bridge over some body of water, river or pond, I couldn't really tell, heading toward Marina Bay Sands. That was certainly another major Singapore checkbox. It looked exactly like the photos: two towers side by side with what looked like a futuristic space canoe balanced on top. Trees were visible up there. Nature, apparently, was important to Singapore, which made sense in a country that was basically one giant city.

I found an elevator that only went down, so I got in, went down a level, and stepped out near the main hotel lobby. You could tell immediately this was an expensive hotel. The floors were polished so smooth they looked wet, and this impression was reinforced by a waist-high laminated water feature running along the wall near the entrance.

"This is where I should've stayed," I thought.

So naturally I looked up the room prices.

$1700 a night.

Okay, maybe not. Maybe my roughly $200-a-night room with a shower and a bed was suddenly looking perfectly respectable.

Tickets for the rooftop observatory were obtained through a QR code, and an escalator led me down one level to what was essentially a history-of-the-history room, walls lined with photos and text that I dutifully read despite knowing full well I would forget most of it almost immediately. Then we were directed to a bank of elevators waiting to take us 57 floors up.

At the top, of course, I was first stopped for a professional photo. This was a tourist site, after all. But the view was exceptional. No point pretending otherwise. There were more plaques and more carefully presented facts, more words I would promptly lose, but the skyline itself did the real work. The middle section of the upper platform was reserved for hotel guests, with the famous infinity pool visible from what I came to think of as the peasants' platform, where my $27 ticket allowed me to stand and admire wealth from an approved distance.

Was the elevator ride and the view worth $27? Probably not, strictly speaking. Was I still glad I did it? Yes. Vacation math is not serious math.

On my way out, I was convinced to buy the photo they had taken of me on the way in. I always forget to take selfies, so I made another "it's vacation" splurge. I am slightly embarrassed to admit I paid $65 for that photo, and yes, that was in US dollars. I think at that point I had fully entered the temporary mental state where money becomes an abstract concept with unfortunate consequences later.

I left Marina Bay Sands and resumed the search for my hop-on hop-off bus. The route led me back toward the water. Was I led there or drawn there? Hard to say. There were steps leading up to the Helix Bridge, another Singapore tourist checkbox, and there I finally learned the purpose of those metal grates lining the river. A triathlon was about to happen.

There was a water station on the bridge, so I stopped and asked when the participants would be coming through. About forty minutes, they said. I decided to stick around and watch from the bridge. From there I could see either the running or biking section on the bridge itself, and below me I could also see the ramp where participants would run into the water for the swim. I had never seen that in person.

While I was waiting, a girl walked by, noticed my Nikon, and asked if I could take a photo of her with her camera. I did my best, even though taking pictures of people isn't really my specialty or, if I'm honest, something I especially enjoy. At one point I noticed her face was in shadow, so I mentioned it. She moved a few steps, I told her when it looked better, and I took a few more.

I expected her to leave after that, but instead she stayed and started chatting. We were both solo travelers. She was from Canada. She had started in Tokyo too, though unlike me she had come to Singapore right after. Next she was headed to Kuala Lumpur, and eventually to China, where she had family.

We stood there talking in the sun on the bridge and checked the time. There was still a long wait until the racers were due.

Then there was a pause in the conversation, and, very much out of character for me, I decided to go with what the hell, what's the worst that can happen, and asked if she wanted to go get a coffee, maybe back in the mall with the generous air-conditioning. I was more than a little surprised when she said yes.

We found a different café from the one I'd been in earlier. I ordered my standard iced café latte. She ordered an iced Americano. I learned that she was researching ALS. I told her I didn't know much about it beyond the fact that it was a neurological disease. I asked whether the work was focused on a cure or management, and she said, unfortunately, still management.

We drank our coffees slowly and talked about travel. Had she noticed the same weird lack of napkins everywhere? She had. Had she noticed that GPS always seemed confused? Yes, that too. I nursed my coffee longer than necessary because I didn't want the easy flow of conversation to end. When I finally couldn't stretch it any further, I suggested we go through the flower and cloud exhibits together.

Traveling solo can be liberating, but it can also be lonely in a very specific way. I didn't care that I had already seen the exhibits. I had realized, while sitting there talking with her, that this was one of the very few non-work-related conversations I had had in quite a long time, and I was more than willing to revisit tourist attractions to keep it going a little longer.

So we went back through them together. By then the sun was setting, and the parts of the domes exposed to exterior light had changed. Everything looked warmer in the late light, softer, less clinical than before.

When we came back out, it was fully dark and the supertrees were lit up. We stayed there together for a while taking final photos, and then, like some temporary chemical or magnetic bond losing its charge, we went our separate ways and became solo travelers again.

I made one attempt to extend the time by suggesting we meet for kaya toast breakfast in the morning, but her flight left early in the afternoon, which meant she needed to pack and get moving much sooner than I did.

After that I went to the Spectra light and water show, another thoroughly touristy production of lasers, fountains, colored lights, and a surprisingly solid sound system with subwoofers hidden somewhere beneath that huge patio.

After the show I found a self-service fresh-squeezed orange juice machine and had to try it. I had never seen an automated machine making fresh orange juice with ice before, which sounded almost absurdly appealing. It was.

A few steps farther down the waterfront promenade I came across a duo performing with an acoustic guitar. I hadn't seen much live music on this trip yet, so I stayed a while and listened. But after a bit, my wandering heart wasn't really in it. I wanted a travel buddy, or I wanted to fast-forward to the part of the trip when my two cousins arrived. I felt stuck in a kind of travel limbo. Maybe it was just the long day of walking in the heat. I don't think it was only that.

I was hungry, so I started looking for food mostly because it was open and nearby. I picked a pizza place, not because I expected greatness but because I was tired and hungry and practical considerations were beginning to outrank curiosity.

Then, in a display of Singapore city magic, I saw a bright patch of light in the distance, and as I got closer I noticed the crowd gathered around it. Late at night, a crowd like that usually means one thing. Food.

I was right.

It was a giant food court with what felt like endless options, and at the back, out on an outdoor street, I found Satay Street, lined with tables and satay stands, smoke rising from all of them, flames visible as skewers turned over charcoal. I picked a line. I was pretty sure it didn't matter which one. It was going to be good.

Again, I was right.

The ten sticks of chicken satay I ordered came with a sweet peanut sauce, and they disappeared much too quickly. The meat had that smoky, charred edge that makes you eat faster than you mean to, and the sauce was thick and sweet and nutty enough to drag each bite through almost automatically.

The seating was first come, first served, just sit wherever you can. I stayed a while and listened to the conversations around me. There were pitchers of beer on the tables and I wasn't sure where people had gotten them, but I also didn't particularly want to drink alone. I knew I probably could have made friends quickly enough in a place like that. But I was tired. It had been a long day, and what I really needed was sleep.

So I ordered a Grab, and before long I was back at the Shelton. After a hot shower, I crawled into bed and fell asleep immediately.
