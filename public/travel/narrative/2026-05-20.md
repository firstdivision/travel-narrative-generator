## Wednesday, May 20 - Clay, Carpets, and a Delayed Departure

```poem
We chased old crafts instead of soup at dawn,
Then shaped sad pottery that looked mildly wrong,
A shower required booking, which nobody knew,
And the plane came back once, just to keep us in view.
```

Breakfast was in the hotel, which had seemed when we booked it like the kind of thing we would never want. Surely we would be out on the street somewhere, hunched over noodles, doing it properly. But in the thick of a trip, a quiet sit-down breakfast with my fellow travelers and a roomful of other hotel guests felt unexpectedly civilized. It bought us a calm hour before the day started in earnest. It also bought us one last reliable bathroom before leaving the hotel, which turned out to matter more than any of us had hoped.

One of us was having a rougher morning than the rest of us. Repeated bathroom trips had moved from inconvenience to full schedule item, and after hearing about a special tea meant for exactly this sort of situation, she went to a pharmacy to get it. The hotel, in a genuinely kind gesture and the sort of polished service places like this trade on, called ahead to explain exactly what she needed so the pharmacy could have it ready. Eventually the tea was drunk, and things seemed, in the technical phrase, to be on the mend. On trips like this, certain bathroom stops become mandatory. A kind of rite of passage, if an especially unglamorous one.

After that we took a Grab fairly far outside the downtown and Old Quarter area, out toward what looked like a real pottery district. The streets were lined with pottery shops and what might have been warehouses full of the stuff, shelves and storefronts packed with ceramic things in every direction, on the way to Bát Tràng Pottery Museum.

Walking into the museum felt less like entering a normal museum and more like stepping inside a giant sculpture. The building rose in layered spirals, drawing on spinning pottery wheels and old kiln shapes, and from some angles it looked like enormous clay vessels colliding with each other. The reddish-brown curves, textured walls, and canyon-like interior spaces made the whole place feel oddly organic, as if it had been shaped by hand instead of built. Inside, contemporary architecture sat alongside centuries of ceramic history from nearby Bát Tràng Pottery Village, one of Vietnam's oldest pottery communities. There were displays of ancient porcelain, glazed jars, ceremonial pieces, tea sets, modern ceramic art, all of it under skylights that poured down bright natural light. Some floors focused on the history of the craft and the families who had kept it going for generations. Other spaces let visitors try making pottery themselves, which meant wet clay, wobbling wheels, and nervous tourists trying to look more competent than they were.

We took the pottery class.

Mine looked, unsurprisingly, like a child had made it. The two girls did much better. Still, it was fun, and I could see the appeal of taking an actual pottery class someday. There was something slow and methodical about it, the same relaxed rhythm as kneading bread. I like relaxing things, especially when I am bad at them and nobody seems shocked.

Afterward we went upstairs for a light painting exhibition. We were directed into a small room that looked almost temple-like. The employees, apart from pointing us toward little sandals to put on after taking off our shoes, mostly ignored us while they walked another couple around and explained everything in what we assumed was Vietnamese.

Then we were seated at a low wooden fish-pond aquarium table with that same South Korean couple, and once again translation apps did the strange modern miracle they had done elsewhere on the trip: they made an actual conversation possible. They told us what they could about the pieces in the room based on what the employee had explained to them. We exchanged names, where we were from, where we had been, where we were going. It was one of those travel interactions that would have been impossible not that long ago and now feels almost casual, though it still retains a bit of science fiction.

After that we sat with them at a table for a group picture, then wrote down our wishes on small bookmark-shaped pieces of wood and tied them to a tree.

Finally we visited another floor with light sculpture art, and this time there was an English-speaking tour guide. The exhibit was part of a relatively new Vietnamese art form called light sculpture art, pioneered by Vietnamese artist Bùi Văn Tự. The concept sounded simple enough in explanation: carved blocks of wood are lit from a specific angle so that their shadows combine into detailed images on a wall. But in person it felt almost supernatural. Rough, chaotic chunks of wood would suddenly produce perfectly recognizable portraits, historical scenes, and landscapes out of shadow and light.

At Bát Tràng Pottery Museum, the exhibit leaned heavily into Vietnamese history and cultural storytelling. Some installations depicted national heroes, historical struggles, or symbolic moments from Vietnam's past. Others felt more philosophical, dealing with memory, time, knowledge, or the hidden meanings inside ordinary objects. The artist's larger idea seemed to be that reality contains invisible images waiting to be revealed, depending on perspective and illumination.

Our guide took us from one exhibit to the next, describing each piece, turning the light on, then off again. Every single time she turned the light off, she said, "When I turn off the light, you will notice that the painting disappears." By the fifth or sixth time it became oddly endearing. Maybe it was some lost-in-translation thing, some phrase with more weight in the original language. Or maybe that was simply her line and she was sticking with it. Either way, each disappearance still worked.

Then we went back to the hotel to repack, because we were now carrying more stuff, most notably our still somewhat soft pottery from class. None of us believed these pieces were making it home in one piece. We did this repacking in the hotel lobby, since the hotel had been holding our bags for us after we checked out that morning. There is a particular kind of travel dignity that vanishes when you are crouched over open luggage in a lobby trying to wrap a lopsided clay object you made yourself.

After that we went to the water theater show at Thang Long Water Puppet Theatre. It is the most famous place in Hanoi to watch traditional Vietnamese water puppetry, and the whole thing is set on an actual pool of water, with puppets gliding across the surface while the puppeteers stand hidden behind a bamboo screen, waist-deep in the water, controlling everything with long underwater rods. The art form goes back nearly a thousand years to flooded rice paddies in northern Vietnam, and the stories revolve around village life, fishing, farming, legends, dragons, spirits, and Vietnamese folklore. Musicians performed live off to the side with traditional instruments, and there were splashing effects, smoke, fireworks, and fire-breathing dragon puppets. It is heavily tourist-oriented now, packed seating, multiple daily performances near Hoàn Kiếm Lake, but it still felt strangely magical. Part folklore, part comedy, part ancient illusion show.

It also smelled musty inside, probably from years of water in the air and water making it onto the carpets. That part felt less magical. One of the instruments I noticed was the same one I had played back in Sapa at the Moana stop, the Dan Tranh. The stories they told were about prosperity and fertility, with figures like swans, phoenixes, and unicorns moving across the water in bright lacquered colors.

Then it was really time to leave. We went back to the hotel, collected our bags, and summoned one final Grab to the airport. There we discovered that you cannot check in or go through security more than three hours before your flight. Our plan had been to arrive early, settle into the airline lounge, shower, eat, drink, and generally pretend to be organized people. Instead we found ourselves stuck outside the useful part of the airport by a rule that I am sure makes sense to someone.

There was an upstairs area we could still access, and we were at least able to get some food. There was also a spa, but we all felt too grody for that if we could not shower first, and when we asked, the girl at the door said no, they did not have showers there. That seemed like a missed business opportunity to me. Surely the crossover between people who feel disgusting in airports and people willing to pay money to feel less disgusting is not small.

Eventually we entered the three-hour window, got through security, and finally made it into the lounge, where there were, blessedly, nice showers. There was a sign saying that showers required a reservation. There was nobody there to make such a reservation with, so we all agreed, silently and efficiently, to pretend we had never seen the sign. When one opened up, we used it. Travel ethics can become flexible when clean hair is involved.

Finally we boarded. The plane left the gate and began taxiing, only to return to the gate again. Information came slowly, dribbling out through announcements from the head purser, each one revealing a little more than the last: luggage, then a passenger's luggage, then a power bank in a passenger's luggage. We were skeptical. Why would the plane taxi halfway to the runway before someone suddenly discovered a power bank in the hold? Still, there was nothing for us to do except sit there and hope we did not start seeing smoke.

We did not.

Eventually the plane taxied out again to the runway and pushed itself into the air.
