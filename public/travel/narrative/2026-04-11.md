## Saturday, April 11 - Trains, Towers, and Noodles

I was up early because I had decided this would be a day for exploration, my first Saturday here in Kuala Lumpur, and I was finally going to figure out the trains and make my way downtown.

Before any of that, my phone rang. I looked at the name and realized it was my cousin, the one I normally have a Friday night call with to recap the week and catch up. He had forgotten that I was in Malaysia, which meant that for me it was already Saturday morning. When I pointed that out, he said, oh right, of course, but we still talked for a while anyway. After that, it really was time to get moving.

In what had by now become my primary means of locomotion, certainly for the past week, I opened the Grab app and summoned a Grab to take me to Cyberjaya Sentral train station. Yes, with an S, not a typo.

It was a short ride, only a little longer than my usual trips to work, and soon I was inside the station. I rode an escalator up and found a long row of ticket machines, all colorful screens and glowing buttons, with no English on them. I assumed this was where I was supposed to get the thing I had been told I needed, a Touch 'n Go card, but I could not decipher the screens well enough to confirm that. So once again I abandoned technology and went looking for a human being.

Once again, involving a human solved the problem almost instantly. A couple of questions, my credit card handed over and returned, and I was given my train pass with 20 Malaysian Ringgit loaded onto it. I was told that should be enough for a few train rides, three, four, maybe five. Good enough. I could always add more later, assuming I ever figured out how.

I touched the card to the turnstile, appropriately enough, the screen turned green, and the doors opened. So far the system and I were getting along.

I took another escalator up to the platform and then immediately realized that while I knew where I wanted to go, I did not know how to get there from here. That is an important distinction. So instead of pretending I had this under control, I went back down to the main office by the turnstiles and asked again. The attendant explained in perfect English that there were two easy ways to get there from this line. Once he showed me on the map, it seemed obvious. This was becoming a theme. I thanked him profusely and went back up to wait for the train.

The trains came roughly every ten minutes, which gave me time to look around. The first thing I noticed was the giant pink painted sections on the concrete where the train doors would line up. In big white letters they informed travelers that those cars were for women only. I was very glad I noticed this in advance instead of blundering onto the wrong carriage like a typical oblivious tourist. Not an ideal start to my first train ride.

When the train arrived, I boarded. This station was the end of the line, so the car was mostly empty and I could sit wherever I wanted. I sat down and immediately appreciated the air-conditioning, which was aggressively cold in the best possible way. In a climate like this, air-conditioning does not feel like a luxury. It feels like proof that civilization, despite all its flaws, has accomplished at least one thing.

It was quite a few stops before I had to change trains, so I spent the ride looking out the window. Up until now I had barely left the narrow corridor between where I lived and where I worked, so even the passing stations felt new. Stops slid by, announced in a flat computer voice in both Malay and English. My ears perked up when I heard TRX.

When the train stopped and the doors opened, I stepped out and realized my next train was directly across from me on the same platform. A rare and beautiful moment of transit convenience. I boarded, and three stops later I got off at Pasar Seni. I had done enough reading beforehand to realize I did not actually need to go all the way to KL Sentral, even though that was what I had first asked the station attendant about. Pasar Seni made more sense for what I wanted to see.

On the way out of the station I passed an ATM, and that reminded me that I still had no Malaysian cash in my wallet, which seemed less than ideal. I walked up, inserted my debit chip card, entered my PIN, and then got hit with one of those questions that sounds simple until it doesn't: *Do you want to pay for a conversion?* Well, not if I do not have to, I suppose. In truth I did not really understand what it was asking. I assumed there would be some kind of conversion fee no matter what. I just wanted local currency. I tried once, said yes, got denied. Tried again, said no, got denied. Tried a third time, same result. At that point I was fairly sure my bank had decided to make this difficult from several thousand miles away, and that solving it would eventually involve a phone call.

I gave up on the ATM and headed to Central Market, which, for some reason, is spelled with a C. I noticed that and filed it away as something to look up later. The entrance was marked by a large ornate overhead sign, and when I walked beneath it I found the market in that in-between state where things were technically open but many vendors were still setting up. One of the stalls was a coffee stand. Have I mentioned Malaysians seem really into coffee? There is coffee everywhere.

I ordered a hot café latte, and while making it the girl running the stand burned her hand trying to clean milk off the steam wand. Steam burns hurt in a very specific, immediate way. In a small act of solidarity, I held up my own thumb, still blistered from one of the last meals I cooked back home, when I stupidly grabbed a scorching cast-iron pan with a dish cloth. The cloth slipped and my thumb landed directly on 500-degree black iron. She smiled and laughed.

Central Market itself felt like two places at once. Outside, there were stalls with food, trinkets, and miscellaneous tourist bait. Inside, there were more textiles, more snacks, a food court, and a small room depicting the history of Kuala Lumpur, spread over two levels. At one point I spotted a public bathroom, which after the coffee seemed like my next very good idea. Unfortunately it cost money to enter, and money was exactly what I still did not have.

So I left Central Market and went in search of a money exchange. I found one soon enough. When it was my turn at the little glass window, I slid through two $20 bills and a $10. The attendant pointed at a sign behind him, $2.50 US per bill. What was I going to do, argue myself into a better financial reality? I nodded. He handed back Malaysian currency, minus the obnoxious fees, along with my passport, which for some reason he also needed to see. I was extremely glad that, for some reason, I had it with me in my backpack.

From there I did something I am very good at, wandering around a city with no real plan beyond looking for interesting things to photograph and, increasingly, something to eat. I had skipped breakfast in anticipation of this adventure. I went down random roads and made decisions at stoplights based on whichever direction looked more interesting or easier in the moment. This is not always efficient, but it is often revealing.

At one point I seemed to be heading toward KL Tower. Surely, I thought, around a major tourist attraction there would be food and activity and generally something useful. Instead the road dead-ended near a waterfall and a sign, and when I checked Google Maps I realized getting to the tower from where I stood would require walking all the way around. By now it was around noon, properly hot and humid, and I was not especially enthusiastic about that idea.

Looking at the map also made it clear that I had drifted quite a bit north of the area I had originally meant to explore, the part with all my little blue pins, the area where I expected to find street food. So I turned back southeast and started correcting course.

Eventually I came across a restaurant that was open with its menu displayed on a pedestal out front, the kind you can stand and flip through. As soon as I did, the host came out and started talking to me. He told me they made their own bread, all sourdough-based. That got my attention immediately. I told him, probably with more enthusiasm than intended, that I made my own bread at home too, and that it was also sourdough. That was enough. I went in. I was hungry, and fresh bread has a way of simplifying decisions.

The downside was that the menu leaned distinctly American, or maybe Eurocentric, and did not have the local dishes I had been hoping to eat downtown. But by then hunger had overtaken principle, so I ordered a chicken katsu sandwich with french fries. It was not a compromise born of inability, just timing. There was a noodle dish on the menu, but when I asked about it I was told it had fish in it, and fish remains one of the things I do not eat.

The sandwich was very good, the chicken crispy and hot. The fries were fresh too, hot and crisp with some kind of spiced seasoning on them. In a little metal ramekin on the side was a dipping sauce that was sweet and chili-based rather than ketchup. I had already heard this was common here. Even my coworkers, when telling me I should go to Burger King just to compare, had said I would probably find this kind of chili sauce instead of ketchup. I was fine with that. Better than fine, actually. It was good.

I left the restaurant feeling restored by the sandwich, the iced peach tea, the water, and a brief stint in very cold air-conditioning. Two streets over I turned a corner and found an entire street covered in yellow umbrellas. Beneath them, various stalls were still in the process of setting up. I walked past one and realized I had finally found exactly what I had been looking for, a charcoal grill being lit, with skewers and kebabs waiting nearby to be placed over the coals. Of course. Naturally, this appeared right after I had eaten a full chicken katsu sandwich. If I had held out just a little longer. I dropped a pin on Google Maps and told myself I would come back tomorrow, maybe.

I kept wandering and eventually came upon Chinatown and Petaling Street, another covered shopping street under overhead awnings. It was loud, crowded, and full of shop owners calling out to convince me I needed a watch. I did not need a watch. I barely used the one I already had.

By the end of this giant loop I had somehow walked, it was 3 p.m. and I had spent a long day out. That seemed like enough. I started heading back toward the train station, cutting back through Central Market, and saw the girl at the coffee stall again, the one who had burned her hand. She did not notice me as I passed. I found myself quietly wondering if anyone else had even seen it happen. I considered stopping to ask whether her hand was better, then decided maybe not.

I took the trains back in reverse order from the morning. Along the way I wrote down notes for the day in my spiral-bound paper notebook. By then I also knew I would need dinner, and I was not really in the mood for a restaurant. So I went to Village Grocer and bought instant noodles. I would have my noodles, even if I had failed to find the version I wanted downtown.

Back at the apartment, I read my book until I got hungry, took a shower, cooked the noodles, and then fell asleep on the couch to some random YouTube show.
