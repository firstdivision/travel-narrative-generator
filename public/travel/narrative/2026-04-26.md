## April 26, 2026 - Rain Delays and Chicken Rice

```poem
I chased dry sidewalks and lost to the sky,
Ordered two lattes and still stayed shy.
The library said maybe, the airport said rice,
And homemade coconut ginger was merely... advice.
```

I woke up hungry for something a little less adventurous. That feeling had been building, I think. After the Box Toast adventure, the idea of a more straightforward breakfast had started to sound less boring and more necessary.

Before going anywhere, I checked out of the hotel and asked if I could leave my bags there for the day. They said of course, happy to. That simple kind of kindness always feels more useful than dramatic hospitality. I set my bags aside, stepped back out into the city, and headed off on foot toward a café that looked promising and, more importantly, walkable.

I wandered a bit on the way, slipping down a few side streets just to see what was there. I found a couple of cool little stores, bought a postcard I liked, and came across a shop selling coconut candy. Still no coconut ginger drink, which by this point had become an ongoing search of mildly unreasonable importance.

At one point I had to backtrack because the road ahead was closed. I was about to head up a set of stairs toward some overpass situation when a friendly older woman came out of a hotel parking lot and stopped me.

“No... there path if you want. You see? It right there in plants.”

I went over to look, and sure enough, there was a narrow little path tucked into the greenery like some unofficial pedestrian workaround. Thanks, random old lady.

The café itself was modern and clean, the kind of place that seemed to believe in smooth surfaces and controlled lighting. I was led through a sliding glass door into an indoor dining area, and once I saw eggs benedict on the menu, that was it. I can never really resist ordering it when it appears. I also got a latte, because they are great, and because apparently I solve many of life's questions with milk and espresso.

It was exactly the kind of breakfast I wanted that morning. Something recognizably Western, something with eggs and structure and not too many surprises. While I ate, it started raining, suddenly and seriously, with those heavy oversized drops that make it sound like the building is being pelted with handfuls of gravel. I considered ordering another coffee and just waiting it out, but eventually the rain tapered off enough for me to pay and leave.

I had seen that the Singapore National Library was nearby, so I started heading that way. Naturally, it began raining again. Fortunately I was right next to another café when it started, smaller and more local-looking, so I ducked inside without much hesitation.

Three young girls were working behind the counter, and one of them took my order for, yes, another café latte. I sat at a table with a slight wobble to it, which was a stark contrast to the perfect tables and polished brightness of the first café. But I liked this one more, I think. It was cozy. The lighting was dim without being gloomy, and there were little groups of two and three people talking animatedly all around me. Since I was close to the library, I decided these must be my people. Creatives, inquisitives, reminisce-ives, all the good "-ives."

So I joined them in spirit and pulled out my journal.

On a trip, I am perpetually at least a couple days behind in my notes, so any chance to catch up has to be taken. My coffee was hot. Somewhere outside, or maybe around the corner and down the hallway this café seemed tucked into, I could still hear the rain. I wrote for a while and had the thought: *It’s not until you put the pen down that you finally think of something to write.* Which was mildly ironic, since I was actively writing when the thought arrived. Still, it felt true. How many ideas have I had and then simply failed to catch in time?

Eventually I reluctantly finished the latte. I thought about ordering another, but I had already consumed two in quick succession, and even I had to admit there are limits. Also, the rain seemed to have stopped.

At the Singapore National Library, I walked in and noticed someone in the main lobby setting up microphones and music stands. I filed that away for later and headed further into the building. I wasn't entirely sure what I wanted from the place. Maybe old books on shelves. Maybe just a quiet place to sit and work on my journal some more.

I rode the escalators up level by level. Drama. Central Arts, with visual art, music, film. Reference library. Historical film. It all sounded promising in that clean institutional way. But each level seemed to have a guard in front, and they all looked like they wanted some kind of ID, student or resident, something more convincing than a Texas driver's license. I scanned a QR code for more information, but it all gave me the impression I probably wasn't getting in.

I considered asking one of the guards, but I didn't. Maybe that was travel fatigue. Maybe I just wasn't fully in the mood to negotiate my way into a library section I hadn't even been sure I needed in the first place. Also, the two lattes had become a pressing matter. The good news was that there was a bathroom near each escalator landing, and I have to note that the bathrooms were clean and very nice. A meaningful detail, honestly.

When I went back down to leave, the musicians in the lobby had started playing. To my surprise, it was a performance of songs from musicals and music-based movies. Disney, *The Sound of Music*, *Wicked*, *The Hunchback of Notre Dame*, that sort of thing. This was extremely up my alley, and outside it was raining in full monsoon style again, so the decision was easy enough. I stayed.

After a few songs, I stepped out into the covered area between the two library buildings and sat on some stone steps with a bunch of other people who seemed like college students. I tried to think of somewhere else to go with the time I had left before needing to head to the airport. Nothing really came to mind, and the rain had narrowed the options anyway, since anything appealing now needed to be indoors. So I admitted the obvious: there was a perfectly good concert happening right here. I went back inside and listened to more.

Eventually it was late enough that I had to stop pretending I had all the time in the world. I ordered a Grab back to my hotel, collected the bags I had left there that morning, then immediately ordered another Grab to the airport.

At the airport I discovered I was in Terminal 4, which felt like slightly bad luck. All the famous Singapore airport things people talk about, the Rain Vortex, Butterfly Garden, Canopy Park, Cactus Garden, all of that, were in the other terminals. I suppose it makes some sense. Maybe Terminal 4 is for shorter-haul flights and less theatrical transit experiences. I don't know.

Still, this terminal was very nice. It also had the best public bathroom I had seen yet, which is not a glamorous category but is a real one. My Tokyo hotel bathroom still reigned supreme, though.

I found a food court, and Crispy Chicken Rice pulled me in almost immediately. It was fantastic. Maybe I was exactly the right amount of hungry, or maybe it was genuinely that good. Possibly both. I nearly ordered two.

The dish was a boneless chicken cutlet coated in a light batter or breadcrumbs and fried until golden and crunchy, then sliced and served over fragrant chicken rice. It came with chili sauce, cucumber slices, and a small bowl of clear soup. It landed somewhere between Hainanese chicken rice and Japanese katsu, but still very much itself. The crunch gave way to hot chicken, the rice carried that savory, slightly oily fragrance that makes you keep eating long past the point of proof, and the chili sauce sharpened everything. It was exactly airport food should not have been, which is to say, very good.

Then it was off to the gate area. My gate changed once, so I moved once, which felt about right. Eventually we boarded and departed. On takeoff, I tried again to capture a cool long-exposure shot of the lights below, to match one I'd taken on arrival, but I failed. Some things are better in memory than in your camera roll, though I would still have preferred both.

From the air, after we crossed into Malaysian territory, I kept seeing fireworks below. I had no idea why. Just little bursts of light going off in the dark, disconnected from me and unexplained.

Soon I was back at Kuala Lumpur airport, standing at the Grab pickup area with the big TV that shows which Grabs are waiting and where. Behind me was a place selling corn. Just corn. I wanted to try it, which says something either good or troubling about my level of curiosity by that hour, but I had already ordered my Grab.

Back at my Airbnb, I made one final effort in the ongoing coconut ginger drink saga. I tried to assemble my own version from the raw ingredients I had bought. It was okay. Drinkable. Respectable, even. But it was not the real thing, and I knew it. Some quests survive another day.
