## Friday, April 5

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there. From 30,000 feet it looked like any other stretch of ocean, flat and distant and slightly unreal, except that I knew it wasn’t. This was Japan ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted to take the train into Tokyo. I’d heard it was a great little ride, and after that many hours in the air, the idea of arriving by rail instead of by another anonymous airport transfer felt important somehow.

I made my way toward the train signs, towing my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on had decided back at the Austin airport that the two front wheels were done participating in this trip. Pulling it the normal way now produced an irritating THUMP THUMP as the ruined wheel treads slapped along behind me. There was something almost offensive about the timing. My trusty carry-on chose the dawn of a two-month trip to leave this world.

At least the signs to the trains were clear, and also in English. What a privilege we Americans entertain, being catered to in so many countries without really expecting how unusual that is.

Even so, I still got lost and had to backtrack to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed to go to the office with the big JR sign over it, Japan Rail, simple enough once an actual human told me. At the JR office, another very nice and very polite woman, I was already starting to see a theme, sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, a nearly direct train to Shinjuku Station, which would be my next stop.

The train arrived on time, of course. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time. Whoever it was, the idea had clearly found fertile ground here. The NRT Express was clean and quiet, exactly as advertised by every stereotype and every person who had ever described Japanese rail to me. I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general travel exhaustion, except this time both were magnified by the many thousands of miles I had just crossed.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station with my broken bag complaining at my side and Google Maps loaded on my phone. Only 1.5 kilometers to the hotel. This would be a snap.

It was not a snap.

The GPS immediately started acting deranged. I would be walking one direction and the little magic blue arrow insisted I was pointed ninety degrees to the right. I felt like a pilot in one of those Air Crash Investigation episodes I watch, staring at bad instruments and trying to decide whether to trust them anyway. Trust your instruments. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong. In the end I gave up on using it as a compass and just watched how my position moved on the map, which felt like a ridiculous workaround, but it was what I had.

Tokyo unfolded in front of me while I tried to sort it out, masses of people moving with purpose, and company men in near-identical black-and-white suits, like a whole city staffed by extras from The Matrix. It was also Friday for them, and about 4 p.m. Time to party, I assumed, or at least to begin the very organized transition toward it.

I had figured out how to keep the suitcase rolling on its two surviving wheels, but every so often I just picked it up by the handle because the noise annoyed me, and I assumed it probably annoyed everyone around me too. This despite the fact that the city itself was loud, and despite the fact that I saw other people doing much the same thing. Still, I had that voice in the back of my head telling me to be a good ambassador. Then again, I do this at home too. I always assume I am somehow the one in the wrong, the one bothering everyone else, when in reality I am probably not on their radar at all.

After staircases, street crossings, a tunnel I think, maybe a climb up an ice wall, my memory was already getting loose around the edges, I finally found Hotel Gracery, the one with the big Godzilla on top. That was me. My home for the next two nights.

I checked in, got my key, and was escorted to the correct elevator by a charming front desk woman who smiled and handed me the key card with both hands. I mangled my first of what I suspected would be many attempts at saying “Arigato Gozaimas,” but she seemed to appreciate the effort. I’m sure she had heard worse, or heard nothing at all, from clueless Americans like me arriving with no local language skills. The comparison that came to mind was a Midwestern lady ordering “kay suh dill uhs” in a Mexican restaurant, and unfortunately that did not make me feel more sophisticated.
