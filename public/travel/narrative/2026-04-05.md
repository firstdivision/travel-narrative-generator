## Friday, April 5 - Broken Wheels, Bright Tokyo

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there, an ocean view from 30,000 feet that could have been anywhere, except I knew it wasn't. This was *Japan* ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted the train ride into Tokyo. I had heard it was a good small introduction, a clean transition instead of being shot straight from airport to city by taxi or bus. First, though, I had to make my way to the train, towing my broken roller bag behind me.

My carry-on had decided to die back in the Austin airport. Both front wheels had more or less self-destructed, which meant pulling it the normal way now produced an irritating *thump thump* as the ruined wheel treads tried, heroically and badly, to continue existing. Impressive timing, really, for a bag that had apparently chosen the dawn of a two-month trip as its preferred moment to exit the world.

I found the signs pointing toward the trains. They were in English too, which always feels like a strange little privilege, how often Americans are quietly accommodated in places that owe us nothing. I still managed to get lost and backtracked to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed the office with the big `JR` sign over it. Japan Rail. Simple enough once someone told me.

At the JR office, another very nice, very polite woman sold me a ticket on the next NRT Express, nearly direct to Shinjuku Station, my next stop. I was beginning to notice a pattern in these encounters. Everyone was calm. Everyone knew what they were doing. I was the only variable.

The train arrived on time, as expected. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time. Whoever it was, they would have enjoyed this. The train was clean and quiet, almost suspiciously so, and I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general travel exhaustion, made heavier by the many thousands of miles I had just crossed.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, with my broken roller bag complaining at my side and Google Maps loaded and ready. Only 1.5 kilometers to the hotel. This would be easy.

Naturally, it was not easy.

The GPS was being weird. I was walking one direction, and the magic blue arrow insisted I was going ninety degrees to the right. I felt like a pilot in one of those *Air Crash Investigation* episodes I watch. Trust your instruments, they always say. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong. In the end I decided to sort that out later and just watch how my movement affected the map itself. It was an unfortunate complication for someone like me trying to navigate a brand new city on very little sleep.

Tokyo unfolded in front of me anyway. There were masses of people and company men in nearly identical black-and-white suits, like extras from *The Matrix*. It was also Friday for them, around 4 p.m., which seemed like an excellent time to be released into the city. Even after I figured out how to balance the roller bag onto its two surviving wheels, I kept lifting it by the handle now and then because the noise annoyed *me*, and I assumed it must be annoying everyone else too. Never mind that the city was loud and I had already seen other people dragging luggage around without apology. There is always that little voice in the back of my head saying, be a good ambassador. More honestly, it is the same voice I have at home, the one that assumes I am probably the one doing something wrong when in reality I am most likely not on anyone's radar at all.

After staircases, street crossings, a tunnel I think, and what may as well have been a climb up an ice wall by the way it felt in my memory, I finally found the Hotel Gracery, the one with the giant Godzilla on top. That was me. Home for the next two nights.

Check-in was smooth. A charming front desk woman smiled, handed me my key card with both hands, and personally escorted me to the correct elevator. I mangled my first of what would be many attempts at saying *Arigato Gozaimas*, and she seemed to appreciate the effort anyway. I am sure she had heard worse, or no attempt at all, from clueless Americans arriving with exactly the amount of local language skill that I had, which was close to none. It reminded me of a Midwestern lady ordering "kay suh dill uhs" in a Mexican restaurant, full confidence, no brakes.

The room was tiny, but not surprisingly so. Hotel rooms in Japan are small. Land is expensive, Tokyo is dense, and places like this are built for efficiency, not lingering. Short business stays, a bed, a shower, enough room to exist but not to spread out. That actually suited me just fine, Mr. "I'm here for two nights and then on a plane again."

My first goal was a shower, and the shower setup was a mystery at first glance. There was a tub and a shower wand on a hose, but the hose was over by the sink. On the floor sat a bowl and a small stool with a suspiciously ass-shaped curve. I was too tired to decode the system properly, so I just stood in the tub holding the shower wand and let the hot water work on me. It washed off airport, airplane, train, and the vague grime that attaches itself to long-distance travel and seems to settle into the joints.

My brain was begging for sleep. The poor thing thought it was 4 a.m. when it was really something like 5 p.m. here. My notes from this part of the day were about as clear as my head was. Still, I denied the brain what it wanted. It was used to disappointing *me* by staying awake when I wanted to sleep, so this felt fair. Revenge, at last.

I stepped back out into a dark sky and the neon brightness of Shinjuku at night. I wandered for a while like a drunken sailor whose only spirit consumed was lack of sleep. There was a place on my list, though, and by this point I had finally figured out how to fix the blue arrow on Google Maps. I was beginning to feel like some kind of expert. The place was Ichiran Ramen, and it was right down the road.

The line was long. Not Disney long, but long enough to vanish around some corner in the stairwell that led down, presumably, to the entrance. About thirty of us moved slowly into what looked a bit like a mine shaft, united by the promise of hot ramen.

Once inside, the ordering system was half machine, half paperwork. At an electronic kiosk I picked the basics, ramen and a beer. Then I got a sheet of paper to refine the order: noodles soft, broth medium, spice level white, that kind of thing. They even had suggested settings marked with a faint oval dotted line, a sort of polite little *circle me and let's move this along*.

I was seated in one of those individual ramen cubicles, a row of narrow personal stalls, like a library carrel designed by someone who wanted less studying and more broth. Soon the ramen arrived. I had never had real ramen before. My previous experience was instant packets, or the version I make at home with plain noodles and whatever happens to be around. So I did not have much to compare it to, but it was very good. The noodles surprised me most. They had more flavor than I expected, and more bite. Maybe it was the flour, maybe they were fresh-made, maybe both. Whatever it was, I understood the appeal immediately.

I also followed the advice of many YouTube videos I had watched while preparing for this trip and ordered a noodle refill. Another slip of paper, more circles around noodle settings, two 200-yen coins. The little door opened, the paper and coins disappeared, two smaller coins came back as change, and before long I was adding fresh noodles to the broth I had left. I could get used to this very easily. I did wish I had ordered the refill noodles soft instead of medium. Soft is how I make them at home, and I should have trusted myself. But by then I was a little flustered and only partially operating on reason.

Eventually, even this had to end. I left. The staff said goodbye and thank you, I think. I butchered the thank-you again. He did not flinch. Like the hotel receptionist, he had almost certainly seen it all before.

Back at the hotel, I finally took advantage of the ass-shaped stool to wash my feet off. At last, the design revealed itself. I could see the logic now. Tomorrow I would try using the whole setup the way it was meant to be used, sitting there properly to wash. But not tonight.

Tonight, sleep was stronger than everything else.

With clean feet and a full stomach, I crawled into the small bed and closed my eyes. Sleep arrived instantly.
