## Friday, April 5

The light came up, the shades went up, and there was water down below. It looked like any other ocean view from 30,000 feet, but I knew it wasn't. This was JAPAN ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted the train ride into Tokyo. I had heard it was a great little arrival, a proper easing into the city instead of just being dropped into it by airport bus or taxi.

First, though, I had to make my way to the train while towing my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on suitcase had decided back at the Austin airport that this was the ideal moment for its two front wheels to self-destruct. Pulling it the normal way now produced an irritating THUMP THUMP as the ruined wheel treads flailed along. There was something almost offensive about the timing. My trusty carry-on, on the dawn of a two-month trip, chose to die.

I found the signs pointing to the trains. They were also in English, which felt like one of those quiet privileges Americans drift through without fully noticing, being catered to in so many countries as a matter of course.

Naturally, I still got lost.

So I backtracked to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed the office with the big JR sign over it, Japan Rail. From there I spoke to another very nice, very polite woman, and by now I was beginning to notice a theme. She sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, a nearly direct train to Shinjuku Station, my next stop.

The train arrived on time, as expected. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time. Whoever it was, the line has survived because it contains at least enough truth to be useful. The train was clean, quiet, and so orderly it almost seemed to lower my blood pressure by force. I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general travel fatigue, magnified by the many thousands of miles I had just come.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, broken roller bag complaining beside me, Google Maps open, and only 1.5 kilometers to the hotel. This, I told myself, would be a snap.

It was not a snap.

The GPS immediately started behaving like it had suffered some sort of spiritual event. I would be walking one way, but the magic blue arrow pointed ninety degrees off to the right. I felt like one of the pilots from those Air Crash Investigation videos I watch. Trust your instruments. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong. Eventually I decided to solve that problem later and just watch how I moved on the map to infer which way I was actually going. Not ideal, especially for someone already trying to navigate a new city while half-conscious.

Tokyo began unfolding around me anyway. Masses of people. Company men in nearly identical black-and-white suits, like extras from The Matrix. It was also Friday for them, and around 4 p.m., which gave the whole place a faint charge, as if everyone had collectively reached the point in the week where restraint could start loosening.

I had figured out how to make my roller bag run on its two surviving wheels, but every so often I carried it by the handle anyway because the noise annoyed me, and I assumed it annoyed the Japanese people around me too. Never mind that the city itself was loud, and never mind that I saw other people dragging noisy bags without apology. There is always that voice in the back of my head telling me to be a good ambassador. Then again, I do this at home too. I tend to assume I am the one bothering everyone, when in reality I am probably not even on their radar.

After staircases, street crossings, a tunnel I think, maybe a climb up an ice wall, my memory gets a little unreliable here, I finally found Hotel Gracery, the one with the big Godzilla on top. That was me. My home for the next two nights.

I checked in, got my key, and was personally escorted to the correct elevator by a charming front desk woman who smiled and handed me the key card with both hands. I mangled my first of what would become many attempts to say "Arigato Gozaimas," and she seemed to appreciate the effort. I am sure she had heard worse, or just as often no attempt at all, from clueless Americans arriving with zero local language skills. The comparison that came to mind was a Midwestern lady confidently ordering "kay suh dill uhs" in a Mexican restaurant.

The room was tiny, but not in any surprising way. Hotel rooms in Japan are small. Land is expensive, Tokyo is dense, and hotels maximize how many rooms they can fit. They are built around short business stays where travelers need a bed and a shower, not a sitting room for introspection. That suited me perfectly, Mr. I'm here for two nights and then on a plane again. Japanese design also seems to favor efficiency and compact living as a principle, not a compromise.

My first goal was a shower, and the shower was a mystery at first glance. There was a tub and a shower head wand on a hose, but the hose was over by the sink. On the floor sat a bowl and a small stool with a very deliberate ass-shaped curve to it. I was too tired to decode any of this properly, so I stood in the tub holding the wand in my hand and let the hot water wash away airport, airplane, train, and the general grime of motion.

My brain was begging for sleep. The poor thing thought it was 4 a.m. when it was really 5 p.m., or something like that. My notes from this point are about as clear as my head was. Still, I denied my brain what it wanted, partly because I needed to stay awake and partly out of spite. It has, after all, denied me sleep plenty of times when I actually wanted it. Revenge is mine.

I stepped back outside into a dark sky and the neon brightness of Shinjuku at night.

I wandered for a while like a drunken sailor whose only spirit consumed was lack of sleep.

There was a place on my list, and now that I had figured out how to fix the blue arrow on Google Maps, I was beginning to feel suspiciously competent. The place was Ichiran Ramen, and it was right down the road.

The line was long. Not Disney long, but long enough to disappear around a corner in a stairwell leading down, presumably, to the entrance below. About thirty of us shuffled slowly into what looked a bit like a mine shaft, all of us united by the promise of hot ramen.

Inside, the ordering system was half efficient machine, half ritual. At an electronic kiosk I chose the basics, ramen and a beer. Then I got a piece of paper to refine the order: noodles soft, broth medium, spice level white, that kind of thing. The form even had suggested settings marked by a dotted oval, a quiet little instruction to circle me and get on with it.

I was seated in one of those individual booths, a row of ramen cubicles, like a driving range but for solitary broth consumption. Soon my ramen arrived.

I had never had real ramen before, at least not in any meaningful sense. My experience was limited to instant packets, or the version I make at home from plain noodles and whatever else happens to be around. So I did not have much basis for comparison, but it was very good. The noodles were the most interesting part. They had more flavor than I expected, and more bite. Maybe it was the flour, maybe the freshness, maybe both. Whatever the reason, I followed the advice of the many YouTube videos I had watched in research and anticipation for this trip and ordered a noodle refill.

That part was almost comically smooth. Another slip of paper. More circles around noodle settings. Two 200-yen coins. The little door opened, paper and coins vanished, two smaller coins came back as change, and soon more fresh noodles arrived for the remaining broth. I could get used to this. My only regret was that I had ordered the refill at medium instead of soft. At home I prefer them softer, and I wished I had thought to do that here. I was, in my defense, a little flustered.

Eventually the meal ended, as meals insist on doing. I left, and the staff called out goodbye and thank you, I think. I butchered the thank you again. He did not flinch. Like the hotel receptionist, he had clearly seen this movie before.

Back at the hotel, I finally made use of the ass-shaped seat stool to wash my feet off. I could see the logic of it now. Tomorrow I would try using the whole setup the way it was meant to be used, sitting there properly to wash.

But not tonight.

With clean feet and a full stomach, I crawled into the small bed and closed my eyes. Sleep arrived instantly.
