## Friday, April 5

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there, flat and distant and looking, from 30,000 feet, like any other stretch of ocean. But I knew it wasn’t. This was JAPAN ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted to take the train into Tokyo. I’d heard it was a great little ride, and after that long flight I liked the idea of arriving by rail instead of being poured directly into a taxi or bus.

Getting there was less graceful. I made my way toward the train, towing my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on had decided, back in the Austin airport, that this was the ideal moment in its life cycle for both front wheels to self-destruct. Pulling it the normal way now produced an annoying THUMP THUMP as the remains of the wheel treads slapped along the floor. The audacity of a supposedly trusty suitcase choosing the dawn of a two-month trip to leave this world.

At least the signs were clear. I found the ones pointing to the trains, and they were in English too. What a privilege we Americans entertain, to be catered to in so many countries.

Even so, I still managed to get lost. I backtracked to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed to go to the office with the big JR sign over it, Japan Rail. So I did. There, another very nice and very polite woman, I was starting to notice a theme, sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, a nearly direct train to Shinjuku Station, which was my next stop.

The train arrived on time, exactly as expected. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time, and here, at least in this moment, it seemed true. The train itself was clean and quiet in a way that made me instantly aware of how travel-worn I was. I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general exhaustion, the kind amplified by the many thousands of miles I had just crossed.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, broken roller bag complaining behind me, Google Maps loaded, and a simple plan in mind. Only 1.5 kilometers to the hotel. This will be a snap.

It was not a snap.

The GPS was being weird. I would be walking one direction, and the magic blue arrow would point ninety degrees to the right. I felt like a pilot in one of those Air Crash Investigation episodes I watch. Trust your instruments. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong. In the end I decided I would sort that out later and just watch how my position moved on the map to work out which way I was actually going.

It was an unfortunate complication for someone like me, trying to navigate a new city on very little sleep.
