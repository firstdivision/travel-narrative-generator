## Friday, April 5

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there. From 30,000 feet it looked like any other ocean view, flat and distant and slightly unreal, but I knew it wasn't. This was Japan ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted the train ride into Tokyo. I'd heard it was a great small entry into the city, a way to arrive gradually instead of being dumped straight into it.

I made my way toward the trains towing my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on had decided, back in the Austin airport of all places, that its two front wheels were done with this life. Pulling it the normal way now produced an irritating THUMP THUMP as the ruined wheel treads slapped along in protest. There was something especially offensive about a suitcase choosing the dawn of a two-month trip as its moment to self-destruct.

At least the signs to the trains were easy to find. They were in English too, which felt like one of those quiet privileges Americans get used to without really earning. Even so, I still managed to get lost and had to backtrack to the trusty information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed to go to the office with the big JR sign over it.

So I did. There, another very nice and polite woman, I was already beginning to see a pattern, sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, a nearly direct train to Shinjuku Station, which was my next stop.

The train arrived on time, exactly as expected. Someone once said something about keeping the trains running on time. Whoever it was, the line has survived because it keeps being true. The train itself was clean and quiet, almost improbably so after the long blur of airports and aircraft. I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general travel exhaustion, both of them amplified by the many thousands of miles I had just crossed.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, standing there with my complaining roller bag and Google Maps open, feeling briefly optimistic. Only 1.5 kilometers to the hotel. This would be easy.

It was not, at least not immediately. The GPS was being weird. I was walking one way, but the magic blue arrow insisted I was headed ninety degrees to the right. I felt like one of those pilots on the Air Crash Investigation shows I watch. Trust your instruments. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong.

In the end I decided I would sort that part out later. For now I just watched how my position moved on the map and used that to work out which direction I was actually going. It was not ideal. In a new city, tired and off balance and dragging a wounded suitcase behind me, it felt like an unfortunate complication.
