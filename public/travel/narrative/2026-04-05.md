## Friday, April 5

```poem
My suitcase died with a thump and a wobbling plea
Tokyo stayed polite while making a fool of me
I chased ramen through neon and jet-lagged fright
Then slept like a stone in Godzilla's sight
```

The light came up and the shades went up with it. There was water down there. From 30,000 feet it looked like any other stretch of ocean, flat and distant and anonymous, but I knew it wasn't. This was Japan ocean.

I had chosen to fly into Narita on purpose, mostly because I wanted the train ride into Tokyo. I'd heard it was a good small introduction, a way to arrive gradually instead of being spat straight into the city. So I made my way toward the trains, towing my broken roller bag behind me. My carry-on had decided, back at the Austin airport, that both front wheels were done participating in this relationship. Pulling it the normal way now produced a steady THUMP THUMP as the ruined wheel treads dragged along in protest. The timing felt almost theatrical, the audacity of this supposedly trusty suitcase choosing the dawn of a two-month trip to leave the mortal plane.

The signs to the trains were easy enough to find, and in English too, which felt like one of those conveniences Americans accept too casually, this expectation that the world will explain itself to us in our own language. Even so, I still managed to get lost and had to backtrack to the trusted information booth, where a Japanese man explained in perfect English that I needed the office with the big JR sign over it. Japan Rail. Hard to miss, once someone has already told you exactly where it is.

At the JR office, another very nice and very polite woman sold me a ticket for the next NRT Express, a nearly direct train to Shinjuku Station, which was my next stop. I was starting to notice a theme with people here. The train arrived on time, of course. Somebody once made a whole thing out of trains running on time, and here it was, exactly as advertised. It was also clean and quiet in a way that made me instinctively lower my own internal volume. I spent the ride looking out the window through a haze of jet lag and general travel exhaustion, the kind that gets amplified by the many thousands of miles you have just crossed.

Then I was deposited into Shinjuku Station, broken roller bag still complaining, Google Maps open and ready. The hotel was only 1.5 kilometers away. This would be a snap.

It was not a snap.

My GPS had apparently developed philosophical doubts. I would be walking one way while the magic blue arrow insisted I was moving ninety degrees to the right. I felt like a pilot in one of those Air Crash Investigation episodes I watch, staring at the instruments and trying to decide whether to trust them. Trust your instruments, they always say. I was trying to, but something was clearly wrong. Eventually I gave up on the arrow and just watched how the dot moved on the map so I could infer what direction I was actually going. It was an unfortunate complication for someone trying to navigate a new city while sleep-deprived and dragging a dying suitcase.

Tokyo unfolded in front of me anyway. There were masses of people, and company men everywhere, black suits, white shirts, the whole uniform, like a citywide casting call for The Matrix. It was Friday for them, around 4 p.m., and the mood on the street had that end-of-week release to it. Even after I figured out how to balance my roller bag on its remaining good wheels, I still found myself carrying it by the handle sometimes because I hated the noise it made and assumed everyone around me hated it too. The city itself was loud enough, and I saw other people doing the same thing with their bags, but that familiar voice in the back of my head kept saying, Be a good ambassador. Then again, I do this at home too, always suspecting that I am somehow the one annoying everyone else, when in reality I am probably not on anyone's radar at all.

After staircases, street crossings, a tunnel I think, and possibly a climb up an ice wall, memory gets a little loose in that section, I finally found Hotel Gracery, the one with the big Godzilla on top. That was me. Home for the next two nights.

Check-in was smooth. A charming front desk woman smiled, handed me my key card with both hands, and escorted me to the correct elevator. I mangled my first of many attempts at "Arigato Gozaimas," and she seemed to appreciate the effort, or at least she was kind enough to pretend to. I am sure she had heard worse, and certainly more nonexistent attempts, from clueless Americans like me arriving with zero local language skills. It brought to mind the Midwestern lady ordering "kay suh dill uhs" in a Mexican restaurant.

The room was tiny, which was not a surprise. Hotel rooms in Japan are small. Land is expensive, Tokyo is dense, and hotels are built for efficiency, especially for short business stays where people need a bed and a shower more than they need space to pace around dramatically. That logic suited me just fine. I was here for two nights and then on a plane again.

My first goal was a shower, and the shower was a mystery. There was a tub and a shower wand on a hose, but the hose lived over by the sink. On the floor sat a bowl and a small stool with a curve so specifically ass-shaped that it seemed impossible to misunderstand its intended relationship to the human body. I was too tired to decode the whole setup properly, so I just stood in the tub holding the wand and let the hot water wash off airport, airplane, train, and the general grime of being in transit for too long.

My brain wanted sleep with the desperate certainty of a thing that believed it was 4 a.m. when it was really around 5 p.m., or something like that. My notes from this point were about as clear as my head was. But I denied my brain what it wanted. It has denied me sleep often enough when I wanted it, deciding instead that now was a great time to be awake. Revenge was mine.

So I stepped back out into the dark sky and the neon brightness of Shinjuku at night.

I wandered around for a while like a drunken sailor whose only spirit consumed was lack of sleep. At some point I fixed the blue arrow problem on Google Maps, which made me feel absurdly competent. There was a place on my list, and now I could actually find it. Ichiran Ramen was just down the road.

The line was long. Not Disney long, but long enough to disappear around a corner in the stairwell leading down, presumably, to the entrance. About thirty of us slowly worked our way into the mine-shaft-looking stairwell, all of us united by the promise of hot ramen.

Inside, the system was precise and oddly soothing. First you ordered the basics at an electronic kiosk, ramen and a beer in my case. Then you got a piece of paper to refine the order, noodle firmness, broth richness, spice level, all of it. The sheet even had suggested settings marked with a dotted oval, basically saying circle me and let's move this along.

I was seated in one of those individual booths, a whole row of personal ramen cubicles, like a driving range if the sport were solitary eating. Soon my bowl arrived. I had never really had real ramen before. My experience topped out at instant packets or whatever improvised version I made at home with plain noodles and whatever happened to be in the kitchen. So I did not have a refined basis for comparison, but it was very good. The noodles were the part that surprised me most. They had more flavor than I expected, and more bite. Maybe it was the flour, maybe the freshness, maybe both. Whatever the reason, I understood quickly why people make a point of this.

I also took the advice of the many YouTube videos I had watched in preparation for the trip and ordered a noodle refill. Another slip of paper, more circles around noodle settings, and two 200 yen coins. The little door opened, the paper and coins disappeared, two smaller coins came back as change, and soon I was dropping fresh noodles into the remaining broth. I could get used to this. I did wish I had ordered soft noodles for the refill instead of medium, since that is how I make them at home, but by then I was a little flustered and had not thought it through in time.

Eventually, even that had to end. I left, and the staff called goodbye and thank you after me, I think. I butchered my thank you again. He did not flinch. Like the hotel receptionist, he had clearly seen it all before.

Back at the hotel, I finally made some use of the ass-shaped stool and washed my feet off. I could see the logic of the setup now. Tomorrow I would try using it the way it was meant to be used, sitting there and washing properly. But for the moment sleep was calling louder than anything else.

Clean feet, full stomach. I crawled into the small bed, closed my eyes, and sleep arrived instantly.
