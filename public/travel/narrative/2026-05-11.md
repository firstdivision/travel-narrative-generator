## Monday, May 11 - Elephants, Airports, and Neon

```poem
I followed giants while negotiating my gut,
A noble jungle walk with a cautious internal strut,
Then Bangkok arrived in beer, pink shops, and light,
And sleep took me down without much of a fight.
```

I woke to a 7 a.m. alarm because we had booked an elephant tour for the morning, at an ethical elephant sanctuary. Before the day properly started, I discovered that the toilet in my bathroom had a window overlooking the trees and the bay below, which felt like an absurdly scenic setting for such a routine piece of plumbing.

At the resort breakfast buffet, I made the rare and reluctant decision to go easy on the spice. Not because I wanted to, but because my insides had lodged a complaint first thing that morning. More of a naval warning shot over the bow than a full mutiny, but enough to get my attention.

After a quick breakfast, we took a resort buggy to the welcome center building, where another truck with an open back was waiting to take us to the elephant sanctuary. The logistics of luxury travel are often just a long chain of increasingly specific vehicles, and this day was committed to the concept.

We arrived at Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary, near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand. It was exactly the kind of place I had hoped it would be: no riding, no tricks, no bathing, no fake intimacy dressed up as animal love. The whole idea was to observe elephants naturally rather than interact with them like props in someone else’s vacation photo. We walked jungle trails and followed them at a respectful distance while they moved on their own schedule through forest, mud, ponds, and bamboo. Many of the elephants had come from former riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work, and the guides explained their histories, their behavior, and their personalities with a calm seriousness that made the whole thing feel reflective rather than performative.

The atmosphere helped. Dense tropical jungle, thick humidity, cicadas drilling away in the trees, mud underfoot, giant leaves, and that heavy rainforest smell that feels almost chewable. It felt more like hiking with wildlife than visiting a zoo. There was something surreal about watching an animal the size of a truck move with that much gentleness, and then disappear almost silently into thick vegetation.

Unfortunately, my intestinal situation reasserted itself a little way into the trip. For the first thirty minutes, while walking the dirt trails, I was listening to my body far more closely than I was listening to the guide list off elephant facts. My mind was occupied with much grander questions: how long would it take to get back to the main entrance where there were toilets, were there toilets anywhere along the route, and exactly how long was this tour supposed to last anyway? It was not the elevated wildlife mindset I had been aiming for.

Thankfully, eventually, my intestines went back to sleep. Once that crisis downgraded itself, I was finally able to enjoy the tour properly and take photographs of the elephants. The best moment came with a baby elephant that had just been born. He was still a little unsure of how to use his legs, all awkward uncertainty and oversized curiosity, and he came up to the edge of the maternity area where he and his mother were staying to say hello. He reached out with his trunk and investigated me, eventually finding my camera, which I assumed was a new experience for him because he spent a while there. We had been told not to touch the elephants back, so as much as I wanted to return the greeting, I just let him do his thing until he got bored and moved on.

At the end of the tour we were served a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink. I did pause for a second and wonder whether this was one of those moments where, strictly speaking, maybe I should not drink it. This was not the Pimalai resort anymore, and I still had a lot of traveling left to do. Still, the fruit was cold, the drink looked good, and exhaustion has a way of making risk assessments feel flexible.

Back at the resort, after a couple more truck and buggy rides, we took one last swim in our pool. Then we ordered room service: club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey and a fried egg, which was unusual and a very welcome improvement on the standard version. We also ordered coconut drinks because they were too good to pass up, and because there are moments when restraint has already lost the day.

Then we had to leave the resort. That had a slight sting to it, but the trip was really just beginning. All points of the travel compass had to be investigated, and tonight we would, hopefully, be in Bangkok. So we set off east, heading to the airport by means of what felt like every transportation mode available: a buggy ride, a van ride, a boat ride, another van ride, and finally the airport itself.

Security was easy, just as the host at the resort had told us it would be since it was a domestic flight. We settled into the familiar rhythm of airport travel, waiting for our boarding announcement to be mumbled over speakers that were somehow both loud and unintelligible at the same time. A real technical achievement.

Eventually our boarding group was called and we got on the plane. It was a short flight to Bangkok, and a shuttle we had reserved delivered us to our hotel, ibis Styles Bangkok Silom.

The hotel sat in Bangkok’s busy Silom district, surrounded by office towers, street food stalls, shopping arcades, cafés, neighborhood markets, and a great many massage shops. At that point in the trip, we were not yet accustomed to the density of them. Later, in Hanoi, we would see the same abundance of massage and spa places and think much less about it. Here, though, we were still trying to figure out what kind of establishment each one was. Looking back from home, I am sure nearly all of them were perfectly normal, upstanding places. Well, except maybe some of the ones near the night market. I still have my doubts about those.

The neighborhood had that full Bangkok feeling people talk about, but in a way that was more physical than abstract. The smell of grilled meat and noodle soup drifted from food carts. After sunset, the area seemed to tense and brighten at the same time as rooftop bars, night markets, and entertainment venues came alive. Modern skyscrapers rose over constant activity at street level. Everything felt busy, lit, and slightly overheated.

We were, as usual, hungry, so after getting our bags up to our rooms, we left the hotel to explore on foot. Into the night of Bangkok we boldly went.

Just down the street, Patpong Night Market unfolded under neon and the steady hum of the city after dark. There were souvenir stalls, food vendors, bargain hunters, bar touts, and flashing signs from the many very, shall we say, racy clubs that made the district famous. The whole place felt equal parts marketplace and carnival. Chaotic, aggressively touristy, and impossible to mistake for anywhere else. Curiosity and caution really did walk side by side there.

We walked up and down the street, then back up again. I found a stall selling chicken and rice, which may have been my body and mind trying to return to Kuala Lumpur, where that had become one of my staple meals. There was also a beer tent, and I was forced, forced I tell you, to purchase a tall-boy Chang beer in order to gain access to their seating area. A hardship, obviously.

After that we wandered a bit more down the main street. The girls were searching for some kind of cosmetics, so we went into a pharmacy and then another store dedicated entirely to cosmetics. I do not remember much about that second store except that everything was pink, it had two floors, and we explored essentially every piece of both of those floors.

By then it was late, and it had been a shockingly long day. We headed back to the hotel to sleep, and as it does on days like this, sleep came fast.
