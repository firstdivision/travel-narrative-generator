## Monday, May 11 - Elephants, Airports, and a Tall Can of Chang

```poem
I started with elephants and a cautious gut,
By night I was in Bangkok, slightly fried but not shut.
A beer bought me a chair, which felt strangely grand,
Travel is mostly moving, then eating where you land.
```

I woke to a 7 a.m. alarm knowing we had booked an elephant tour for the day, at an ethical sanctuary. Before I was fully functional, I discovered that the toilet in my bathroom had a window overlooking the trees and the bay below, which is a fairly absurd place to find a scenic view, but I appreciated the effort.

At the resort breakfast buffet, I made the unusual and reluctant decision to go easy on the spice. Not because I didn't want it, but because my insides had already lodged a mild formal complaint that morning, more of a naval warning shot over the bow than a full mutiny. I took the hint.

Just after breakfast, we caught a resort buggy to the welcome center, where another truck with an open back was waiting to take us to the sanctuary. The transport chain had already begun for the day, and it would not be letting up.

We arrived at Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand, an ethical place built around the idea of leaving elephants alone as much as possible. No riding, no tricks, no bathing them for tourist amusement. The whole experience was set up less like a zoo visit and more like hiking through the jungle with a chance of elephants. The animals roamed freely through forest, mud, ponds, and bamboo on their own schedule, and many of them had come from riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work before ending up here. Animal welfare groups supported the sanctuary, and the tone was quiet and reflective rather than flashy.

The jungle itself did a lot of the work. Dense tropical air, heavy with rainforest smells. Cicadas going full volume. Mud underfoot. Giant leaves pressing in from all sides. The elephants moved through it with that strange contradiction they have, somehow gentle and overwhelming at the same time, all softness and immense force. More than once, it felt surreal to watch something the size of a truck disappear almost silently into thick vegetation.

For the first thirty minutes, though, I was not as spiritually available to this experience as I might have hoped. My intestinal situation reasserted itself a little way into the walk, and while the guide was explaining elephant behavior and personalities and rescue histories, I was listening much more carefully to my own body. I was trying to calculate the distance back to the entrance toilets, whether there might be toilets somewhere along the route, and how long this tour was actually going to take. It was not my most elevated wildlife moment.

Thankfully, eventually, my intestines decided to go back to sleep. Once that crisis drifted off, I could finally pay attention to what was right in front of me and start taking photographs.

There was even a baby elephant that had just been born. He was still a little unsure about his legs, which made him seem both enormous and fragile in a way I wasn't really prepared for. He came up to the edge of the maternity area where he and his mother were staying and reached his trunk out toward me in what I can only describe as a polite investigation. He found my camera, which I assume was new to him, because he spent some time exploring it. We had been told not to touch the elephants back, so as much as I wanted to say hello in a more normal human way, I just let him inspect me until he got bored and moved on.

At the end of the tour, they served us a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink. I did pause for a second and wonder if this was one of those moments where common sense should perhaps step in and say, maybe don't drink the mystery ice beverage. This wasn't the Pimalai resort anymore, and I still had a lot of traveling left to do. Still, there it was, cold and pink and waiting.

Back at the resort, after a couple more truck and buggy rides, we took one last swim in our pool. Then we ordered room service: club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey and a fried egg, which was unusual and a very welcome improvement over the standard format. We also got coconut drinks, because they were too good to pass up and this was, after all, our last stretch before leaving.

We had to leave this resort, but the trip was really just beginning. All points of the travel compass had to be investigated. Tonight, hopefully, we would be in Bangkok, so east it was. To get there required, naturally, a buggy ride, a van ride, a boat ride, and another van ride, which finally delivered us to the airport. Modern travel is mostly a relay race run by strangers with vehicles.

Security was easy, just as the host back at the resort had said it would be for a domestic flight. We settled into the familiar rhythm of airport waiting, that half-conscious state where you stare at gates and screens and wait for your boarding announcement to be mumbled over speakers that are somehow both loud and unintelligible at the same time.

Eventually our group was called and we got on the plane. It was a short flight to Bangkok, and a shuttle we had reserved delivered us to our hotel.

ibis Styles Bangkok Silom sits in Bangkok's busy Silom district, surrounded by office towers, street food stalls, shopping arcades, cafés, neighborhood markets, and what felt like an endless number of massage shops. At that point in the trip, we were not yet accustomed to that concentration of massage and spa places. Later, in Hanoi, we would see the same thing and think much less about it. Here, though, we were still trying to work out what kind of establishment each one actually was. Looking back on it from home, I am sure nearly all of them were perfectly normal, upstanding places. Well, except maybe some of the ones near the night market. I still have questions about those.

The area buzzed in that steady Bangkok way, with office workers, tourists, and locals weaving around one another near the BTS Skytrain stations and along sidewalks lined with vendors and restaurants spilling into the street. Despite all the concrete and traffic and vertical ambition, Lumpini Park was only a short walk away, a surprising patch of broad lawns and shade in the middle of the city. Silom felt like one of those neighborhoods that makes no effort to simplify itself for you. It just keeps moving.

After getting our bags up to the room, we did what we usually did when dropped into a new city at night. We went looking for food.

Just down the street, Patpong Night Market was already unfolding beneath neon lights and the steady hum of Bangkok after dark. Souvenir stalls, food vendors, bargain hunters, bar touts, and flashing signs from the many very, shall we say, racy clubs that made the district famous all collided into one strange atmosphere that was part marketplace, part carnival, and entirely too much if you stood still for too long. It was chaotic and touristy and required a certain amount of caution, but it was also undeniably Bangkok.

We walked up and down the street, then back up again. Eventually I found a stall selling chicken and rice, which I think my body and mind wanted on instinct, as if they were trying to get back to Kuala Lumpur where this had become one of my staple meals. There was also a beer tent, and I was forced, forced I tell you, to purchase a tall-boy Chang in order to gain access to their seating area. A terrible burden, but I bore it.

After that we kept walking a bit farther along the main street. The girls were searching for some kind of cosmetics, so we went into a pharmacy and then into another store dedicated entirely to cosmetics. I don't remember much about that second place except that everything was pink, it had two floors, and we explored essentially every inch of both of them.

By then it was late, and it had been a shockingly long day. Elephants, jungle, fruit, pool, sandwiches, buggy, van, boat, van, airport, plane, shuttle, neon, chicken and rice, cosmetics. A whole day stitched together from different climates and different versions of myself. We headed back to the hotel to sleep, and as it does on days like this, sleep came fast.
