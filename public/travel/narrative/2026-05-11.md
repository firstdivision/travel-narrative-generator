## Monday, May 11 - Elephants, Caution, and Club Sandwiches

```poem
I chased some elephants while my stomach filed a complaint
The jungle was holy, my confidence extremely faint
A baby found my camera more interesting than my face
Then bacon and coconut drinks restored some dignity and grace
```

I woke up to a 7 a.m. alarm knowing we had booked an elephant tour today, at an ethical elephant sanctuary. It felt like the kind of morning that should have been clean and noble and full of purpose, but first there was the resort breakfast buffet, and first there was my stomach.

I went light on the spice. Not because I wanted to, but because my insides had lodged a mild protest earlier that morning, the sort of naval warning shot over the bow that suggests further escalation is available if needed. So I kept breakfast quick and cautious, which is not really how I prefer to approach a breakfast buffet, but travel has a way of forcing negotiations with your own body.

Just after breakfast, we took a resort buggy down to the welcome center building, where another truck with an open back was waiting to take us to the sanctuary. It was one of those small chains of vacation logistics that starts to feel normal after a while: buggy to building, truck to jungle, body still not entirely convinced by the plan.

We arrived at Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand. It is an ethical place, explicitly no riding, no tricks, no bathing, which immediately changed the tone from tourist spectacle to something quieter and more believable. The whole point was to observe elephants naturally instead of treating them like performers. Many of them had come from former riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work, and the sanctuary was supported by animal welfare organizations trying to promote something more humane. The experience was meant to feel more like hiking with wildlife than visiting a zoo, and that turned out to be exactly right.

The jungle helped. Dense tropical air, the sound of cicadas, mud underfoot, giant leaves, a heavy rainforest smell that felt almost chewable. Everything was green, damp, and slightly overgrown in a way that made the elephants seem even stranger when they appeared. They moved through forest, mud, ponds, and bamboo on their own schedule, and the guides kept us at a respectful distance while explaining behavior, history, personalities, rescue stories. It was quiet, reflective, and not especially interested in entertaining me, which I appreciated.

For the first thirty minutes, unfortunately, I was less tuned in to elephant facts than to my own intestinal situation, which had decided to reassert itself a little ways into the walk. As we moved along the dirt trails, I found myself doing practical calculations instead of spiritual wildlife observation. How long would it take to get back to the main entrance where there were toilets? Were there toilets anywhere along the tour? How long was this tour, exactly? It is hard to feel elevated by nature when part of your brain is occupied with emergency route planning.

Thankfully, eventually, my intestines went back to sleep. Once that private drama settled down, I was actually able to enjoy the place and take photographs. And it was worth the mental detour. The elephants really did have that unnerving mix of gentleness and overwhelming size. A truck-sized animal could disappear almost silently into thick vegetation, which never stopped feeling surreal. One second there was a massive grey body in front of us, and the next it had been absorbed by bamboo and leaves as if the jungle had simply folded over it.

There was even a baby elephant that had just been born. He was still a little unsure of how to use his legs, which made his size somehow both ridiculous and impressive at the same time. He came up to the edge of the maternity area where he and his mother were staying and said hello to me in the way baby elephants apparently do, by reaching out his trunk to investigate. He found my camera, which I assume was a new experience for him, because he spent some time with it. We had been told not to touch the elephants back, so as much as I wanted to return the greeting, I just stood there and let him do his thing until he got bored and moved on.

By the end of the tour we were served a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink. The fruit was cold in that deeply satisfying tropical way, the kind that feels almost medicinal after heat and humidity. I did pause for a second over the drink, wondering if this was one of those moments where maybe you should not drink the thing. This was not the Pimalai resort anymore, and I still had a lot of traveling left to do. Caution, at this point, had become part of the day’s personality.

After a couple more truck and buggy rides, we got back to the resort and took one last swim in our pool. That swim had the feeling of an ending even before I admitted it. The water was cool, the room was waiting, and departure was no longer theoretical. Afterward we ordered room service: club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey and crispy bacon, plus coconut drinks because they were too good to pass up. The sandwich hit with the kind of blunt comfort only a club sandwich can manage. Toast, meat, bacon, cold structure, no surprises. Exactly what I wanted. The coconut drinks were sweet and cold and easy, which also felt wise.

We had to leave this resort. But the trip was really just beginning. All points of the travel compass had to be investigated. And tonight we would, hopefully, be in Bangkok.
