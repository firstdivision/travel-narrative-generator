## Monday, May 11 - Elephants and Warning Shots

```poem
My stomach staged a quiet coup at dawn
While jungle heat settled heavily on
An elephant sniffed my camera like a pro
I survived on fruit, pool water, and club sandwich glow
```

I woke to a 7 a.m. alarm with the knowledge that we had booked an elephant tour for the day, at an ethical elephant sanctuary. That sounded noble and serene in theory. In practice, I was awake early and negotiating with my own digestive system.

At the resort breakfast buffet, I made the rare and deeply unromantic choice to go light on the spice. Not because I didn’t want it. I did. But my insides had lodged a formal complaint that morning, the digestive equivalent of a naval warning shot over the bow. So I kept things restrained and tried not to take it personally.

Just after breakfast, we took a resort buggy to the welcome center building, where another truck with an open back was waiting to take us to the sanctuary. Travel, even at a nice resort, has a way of turning into a chain of increasingly specific vehicles.

We arrived at Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary, near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand. It is one of those places built around the idea that elephants should be allowed to be elephants, which felt refreshingly obvious and also apparently rare enough to need saying. No riding, no tricks, no bathing them for tourist photos. The whole point was to observe them naturally, at a respectful distance, as they moved through the forest on their own schedule. Many of the elephants had come from former riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work, and the sanctuary was supported by animal welfare groups pushing for something more humane than the usual circus of convenience.

The setting helped. It didn’t feel anything like a zoo. It felt like hiking through dense tropical jungle while occasionally remembering that somewhere ahead of me there was an animal the size of a truck moving almost silently through the vegetation. The air was thick with humidity and the smell of wet earth and leaves. Cicadas buzzed constantly. The trails were dirt and mud, lined with giant leaves and heavy green growth. Everything looked damp, alive, and slightly intent on taking over the path again as soon as we passed through.

For the first thirty minutes, though, I was not especially available for reflection. My intestinal situation reasserted itself a little way into the walk, and I found myself listening less to the guide explain elephant behavior and personalities and rescue histories than to the highly specific commentary of my own body. I was busy calculating how far back the main entrance might be, whether there were toilets somewhere along the route, and exactly how long this tour was supposed to last. It was not my finest moment as a student of wildlife conservation.

Thankfully, eventually, my intestines went back to sleep.

Once that crisis passed, I could finally pay attention to what was in front of me. The experience was quiet and strangely reflective. The guides talked about the elephants without turning them into performers, and the animals moved through mud, ponds, bamboo, and forest with a mix of gentleness and immense physical force that was hard to process. Their size was overwhelming, but their presence was calm. Every now and then one would disappear into thick jungle so completely and so quietly that it felt unreal, as if several tons of animal had simply been absorbed by the leaves.

I was able to take some photographs once I stopped mentally drafting emergency bathroom plans. There was even a baby elephant that had just been born. He was still a little unsure about how his legs worked, which made him seem both ridiculous and improbably endearing. He came up to the edge of the maternity area, where he and his mother were staying, and said hello in the way baby elephants apparently do, by reaching out his trunk to investigate me. He found my camera, which I assume was a new experience for him, because he spent a while examining it with real concentration. We had been told not to touch the elephants back, so as much as I wanted to return the greeting, I just stood there and let him conduct his inspection until he got bored and moved on.

At the end of the tour, we were served a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink. I did pause for a second and wonder if this was one of those situations where maybe I should not drink the drink. This wasn’t the Pimalai resort anymore, and I still had a lot of traveling left to do. But the fruit was cold, the air was heavy, and the watermelon drink looked exactly like the kind of thing a tired, overheated person makes a questionable peace with.

After a couple more truck and buggy rides, we got back to the resort and took one last swim in our pool. That felt important somehow, even if I couldn’t have explained why. Maybe because leaving always starts before you actually leave. Maybe because the water was cool and the day had been humid and muddy and full of watchful self-management. After that, we ordered room service: club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey and crispy bacon, plus coconut drinks, which were too good to pass up. It was a very satisfying way to end a day that had started with both moral purpose and digestive uncertainty.
