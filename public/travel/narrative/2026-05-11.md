## Monday, May 11 - Elephants, Airports, and a Tall-Can Dinner

```poem
I followed giants while my stomach plotted mutiny,
A baby trunk inspected me with serious scrutiny,
Then boats and vans delivered me to Bangkok neon cheer,
Where rice, chicken, and one forced Chang closed out the day quite clear.
```

I woke to a 7 a.m. alarm knowing we had booked an elephant tour, an ethical sanctuary visit, which felt like exactly the kind of thing I wanted to be awake for even if my body was still negotiating the terms. Before breakfast I discovered that the toilet in my bathroom had a window overlooking the trees and the bay below, which is an absurdly scenic thing to encounter in a bathroom and somehow very on-brand for a resort that had already made daily life feel slightly staged.

At the breakfast buffet I made the rare strategic decision to go light on the spice. Not because I wanted to, but because my insides had lodged a protest earlier that morning. More of a warning shot over the bow than a full mutiny, but still enough to make me approach breakfast with caution instead of optimism.

Just after that quick meal, we took a resort buggy to the welcome center building, where another truck with an open back was waiting to carry us to the sanctuary. The layers of transport had already begun. It would become a theme.

We arrived at Following Giants Elephant Sanctuary near Ko Lanta in Krabi, Thailand. It is an ethical elephant sanctuary with a strict no riding, no tricks, no bathing approach, which immediately changed the tone of the visit. This was not built like a tourist performance. The whole experience was centered on observing elephants naturally and following them through jungle trails at a respectful distance while they moved on their own schedule through forest, mud, ponds, and bamboo. Many of the elephants had come from former riding camps, logging operations, or entertainment work, and the sanctuary is supported by animal welfare organizations promoting humane elephant tourism.

It felt much more like hiking with wildlife than visiting a zoo. The jungle was dense and heavy with itself: cicadas whining in the trees, humidity sitting on the skin, mud underfoot, giant leaves crowding the trail, and that thick rainforest smell of wet earth and green things slowly rotting back into the ground. The guides explained elephant behavior, personalities, rescue histories, and the strange practical details of these massive animals, but for the first thirty minutes I was not the attentive student I might have hoped to be.

My intestinal situation reasserted itself a little ways into the trip, and while everyone else was listening to elephant facts, I was listening to my own body with a concentration usually reserved for bomb disposal. I found myself calculating how far it might be back to the main entrance, whether there were toilets somewhere along the route, and how long this tour was actually going to last. It is difficult to achieve quiet reflection in the jungle when part of your mind is drafting emergency evacuation plans.

Thankfully, eventually, my intestines went back to sleep. Once that crisis passed, I could finally look up and actually be there.

And it was worth looking up.

The elephants moved with that strange combination of gentleness and overwhelming size that never quite makes sense even when you are watching it happen. They were immense, truck-sized really, yet somehow able to disappear almost silently into thick vegetation. One moment an elephant was in front of us, broad-backed and unmistakable, and then it was gone behind bamboo and leaves as if the jungle had simply taken it back.

I took photographs once I was capable of thinking about anything besides logistics. The best moment came with a baby elephant that had just been born. He was still a little unsure of how to use his legs and came up to the edge of the maternity area where he and his mother were staying, apparently interested in saying hello. He reached out with his trunk to investigate me and found my camera, which I assumed was a new experience for him because he spent some time with it. We had been told not to touch the elephants, so as much as I wanted to say hello back, I just stood there and let him do his thing until he got bored and moved on. It was a small, surreal moment, quiet and oddly formal, like I had been inspected and found acceptable.

At the end of the tour we were served a small snack of cold watermelon and pineapple slices, along with a blended-ice watermelon drink. I did pause for a second, wondering whether this was one of those moments when common sense should overrule hospitality. This was not the Pimalai resort anymore, and I still had a lot of traveling left to do. Still, there it was: fruit, ice, heat, thirst, and my usual willingness to make decisions first and evaluate them later.

Back at the resort, after a couple more truck and buggy rides, we took one last swim in our pool. That last swim had the slight sadness of something you know is ending while you're still in it. Afterward we ordered room service: club sandwiches with thick-cut turkey and a fried egg, which was unusual and a very welcome upgrade to the standard format. We also ordered coconut drinks because they were too good to pass up, and because leaving a place is always easier if you can delay it with food.

But we did have to leave. The resort part was over, though the trip itself was really just beginning. All points of the travel compass had to be investigated, and tonight, hopefully, we would be in Bangkok. So we turned east and headed to the airport in the most appropriately fragmented way possible: buggy ride, van ride, boat ride, another van ride, and finally the airport. Travel has a way of making even simple movement feel like a relay race organized by people who hate continuity.

Security was easy, just as the host back at the resort had told us it would be since it was a domestic flight. We settled into the familiar rhythm of airport waiting, that bland suspended state where time moves strangely and every boarding announcement sounds like it is being delivered through a pillow. Loud and unintelligible at the same time, which is a real technical achievement.

Eventually our boarding group was called and we got on the plane. It was a short flight to Bangkok, and a shuttle we had reserved delivered us to our hotel, ibis Styles Bangkok Silom.

The hotel sits in Bangkok's busy Silom district, surrounded by office towers, street food stalls, shopping arcades, massage shops, cafés, and neighborhood markets. The area seemed to buzz at every level at once, with office workers, tourists, and locals weaving between BTS Skytrain stations and sidewalk vendors and all the restaurants spilling out onto the surrounding streets. Even in the middle of that concrete and traffic, Lumpini Park was only a short walk away, a surprising pocket of broad lawns and shady paths in the middle of the city.

Silom really did feel like one of those neighborhoods that compresses a certain version of Bangkok into a few blocks. Morning commuters, grilled meat smoke, noodle soup drifting from carts, elevated train platforms, then after sunset the shift into rooftop bars, night markets, and entertainment venues. Constant activity, modern skyscrapers, and the feeling that if you stood still too long the city would just flow around you without noticing.

We were, as usual, hungry, so after getting our bags up to the room we went back out on foot.

Just down the street, Patpong Night Market was already running under neon lights and the steady mechanical hum of Bangkok after dark. Souvenir stalls, food vendors, bargain hunters, bar touts, and flashing signs from the many, let us say, racy clubs that made the district famous all pressed together into something that was equal parts marketplace and carnival. It was chaotic, very touristy, and one of those places where curiosity and caution naturally end up walking side by side.

We walked up and down the street, then back up again. At some point I found a stall selling chicken and rice, perhaps because some part of my body and mind wanted to be back in Kuala Lumpur, where that had become one of my staple meals. There was also a beer tent, and I was forced, forced I tell you, to purchase a tall-boy Chang beer in order to gain access to their seating area. A cruel system, obviously, but I endured it.

Afterward we kept walking down the main street. The girls were searching for some sort of cosmetics, so we went into a pharmacy and then another store dedicated entirely to cosmetics. I do not remember much about that store except that everything was pink, it had two floors, and we explored every piece of both of those floors. There are forms of travel fatigue no one warns you about, and one of them is the very specific exhaustion of being in a two-story pink cosmetics store late at night after elephants, boats, airports, and beer.

It was late, and it had been a shockingly long day, so we headed back to the hotel to sleep. As it does on days like this, sleep came fast.
