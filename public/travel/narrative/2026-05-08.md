## April 8th, 2026 - First Day, Full Stomach

```poem
We started late and called it fate,
Got soaked in rain but still ate great,
Apple Pay performed a tragic play,
And dumplings carried us through the day.
```

The first day of the Trio Trip had finally arrived. My two cousins and I were about to start a long-awaited Southeast Asia trip: Kuala Lumpur first, then Krabi and Bangkok in Thailand, Siem Reap in Cambodia, Hanoi and Sapa in Vietnam, and then back to Hanoi. It sounded ambitious when listed out loud, which was probably part of the appeal.

But that was the larger shape of the trip. Today was Kuala Lumpur, or at least as much of Kuala Lumpur as we could reasonably force into a single day.

Before any of that, I had to say goodbye to my friend. She was working that day and had to leave early, at least in theory. We lingered anyway, both of us delaying the inevitable. What was supposed to be a 6:30 a.m. departure became 8:30. One final time we rode together through the parking garage in her car, circling the six levels of ramps until we were parked at the main entrance of the apartment complex. Then there was the final goodbye, the kind where you know there is nothing left to say and still don’t want to get out of the car. Eventually I had to. I gave one last wave and turned away to walk back into the building as she drove off. I turned once to look back, like something out of a movie, which was embarrassing and true.

Back in the apartment, one of my cousins, who had slept on the couch, was awake. We sat and talked for a while. I think we made tea. We were waiting for the second cousin to wake up, assuming she was dead to the world from travel fatigue and jet lag, but it turned out she’d been awake the whole time and thought it was much earlier, so she was just quietly relaxing. Eventually everyone was fully operational, showers were taken, and we got ourselves together enough to begin the day.

Our first stop was Tamarind Square. Part of my job, unofficially assigned by me, was not just to show them Kuala Lumpur but to show them what I’d been doing with my month there, and Tamarind Square had become a big part of that life. So naturally I took them to The Botanist, which had become one of my favorite places in the area.

We sat down at a table set for four, which always gives a slightly unnecessary sense of occasion when there are only three of you. I ordered what else but shakshuka. One of my cousins followed my lead and ordered the same, while the other got something mushroom-based with a sunny-side-up egg on top. It was an excellent way to start the day, and both of them agreed, which felt validating in the very specific way food recommendations do. Nothing dramatic, just relief.

After breakfast we went to Village Grocer, partly because I wanted to show them one more piece of how I had actually been living there, and partly because we needed practical things like drinks for the day. We wandered the aisles and bought waters and a few other small things. While we were there, we ran into Alan, the owner of the new restaurant in Tamarind Square where I’d recently had breakfast, also involving shakshuka, apparently a recurring theme in my life. He was in a hurry because the restaurant had some sort of delivery problem and he was there to buy milk or something equally urgent and unglamorous.

Then the three of us got a Grab to the train station, where I gave them my newly acquired mentorship on buying a Touch ’n Go card and getting through the train gates without looking lost. Soon we were on the train into downtown Kuala Lumpur, a system I had only recently learned well enough to pretend I understood. We had tickets for the Petronas Towers, and somewhere along the way we realized we needed to adjust the plan. After checking the map, we figured out we could stay on past TRX, where I had originally planned to get off mostly because that was what I had always done, and continue instead to Persiaran KLCC.

When we got off the train, it was raining. Of course it was raining. Our phones assured us the walk to the Petronas Towers was short, and in fairness they were right, though they neglected to mention that being technically correct is not always comforting when the sky is trying to drown the city. Thankfully much of the route was covered. We moved through the walkway, ducked into the mall entrance, followed signs, went down a level, and ended up in line for the towers with five minutes to spare before our scheduled time, which felt almost suspiciously competent.

Like the Sands hotel in Singapore, the whole thing began with a waiting area and a history video, which is apparently the tax one pays before being allowed a view. Then we shot upward at high speed to the Skybridge connecting the two towers more than 170 meters above the city. From that suspended walkway, Kuala Lumpur spread out in every direction: skyscrapers, mosques, parks, highways, and mountains in the distance, all packed together into a city that looked much larger from above than it ever feels while you are navigating one train line at a time.

On that level we filmed some shots for a skit we were planning. This had become a bit of a tradition on these vacations, trying to come up with some comedy bit to record somewhere inappropriate enough to make it funny and public enough to make it mildly uncomfortable.

The observation deck near the top of Tower 2 was the high point, literally and otherwise. Through the floor-to-ceiling windows, the city flattened into something almost toy-like, neighborhoods and traffic and landmarks arranged below like a scale model that happened to contain millions of people. The towers themselves became more interesting up close too, not just as icons but as objects: the Islamic geometric patterns in the design, the engineering behind them, the ambition built into their shape. It was part architecture, part national statement, part reminder that Kuala Lumpur has not exactly been shy about announcing itself.

On the way out, we had what can only be called the Apple Pay Event. One of my cousins tried to pay with Apple Pay, possibly for the first time. It didn’t work. She tried again. It still didn’t work. Then came another attempt, another error, rising exasperation, and by that point everyone was laughing, including the employees at the Petronas Towers gift shop. That day we gave *them* a gift: laughter, and probably a story that started later with, “Oh, you should have been here Friday.”

Back outside, it was still raining. Hard. There was immediate confusion about where exactly to go next and which station would get us to Central Market. I only knew for certain that we needed Pasar Seni eventually. We decided not to walk back to Persiaran and headed toward another station that appeared to be closer. By then the rain had escalated into the kind of downpour that leaves you looking less like travelers and more like recently rescued animals. I was failing slightly in my role as tour guide, mostly because I was now in a part of the city I didn’t know at all, and confidence turns out to be very weather-dependent.

Luckily the alternative station existed where the map claimed it would, which already put it ahead of many urban navigation experiences. A short ride later we were walking into Central Market, and I felt the immediate relief of being back on familiar ground.

We did some shopping there. I bought a shirt. I think the girls bought dresses and a few other things. It had the pleasant drift of market shopping, not especially efficient, but not meant to be.

After that I took them to Petaling Street, Chinatown. The rain had stopped for the moment, and none of us wanted to comment on that too confidently in case the sky heard us. I was happy to find the entrance to the market without much trouble. We stepped in and were swallowed almost immediately by the noise and movement of it all.

What we actually wanted, though, was food. I was pretty sure I knew which way to go. Thankfully, this time, I was right. We emerged onto the street lined with food vendors, and after a few back-and-forths I mentioned a dumpling place I had really liked before. My cousins, very reasonably, said, “Well, let’s go there then.”

So we did. We sat down, and the older husband and wife prepared pork and chive dumplings for us. They were just as good as I remembered, hot and soft with that satisfying chew in the wrapper and the rich, familiar filling inside. Exactly the sort of food that makes you feel more certain about your decisions, even if only for ten minutes.

We left and kept walking until we came to a large outdoor seating area that was packed. The usual rule applied. We were still hungry, the place was busy, so clearly this was where we should be. We went in.

We were seated right away in the upstairs dining room. There was a slight snafu with the QR code menu, because modern dining likes to make ordering noodles feel like an IT support exercise, but a waiter helped me connect to the Wi-Fi and we managed. We ordered more dumplings, beef ramen, and other noodle soups. It was all very good. We were in it now. This was the version of the trip we had all been hoping for, and I was glad I could get us started on the right foot by sharing my modest, pieced-together knowledge of the city with my two cousins.

After dinner we went back out onto the street and kept walking in the same direction, following the curve I knew would take us back toward Pasar Seni station. Then we found ourselves standing in front of a street vendor selling some kind of fried dessert. Loud rhythmic music was playing, people were laughing and joking, and the whole thing felt too alive to pass by. So we decided to try some too. That was the spirit of the trip, really.

Two of us each ordered a banana and chocolate dessert, not realizing the other had done the same. Small details get lost easily in the medium chaos of street food ordering. When the food was ready, one of my cousins went to pay for hers, and the vendor handed her three, saying the others were free.

“Free? Really?” she asked.

He nodded yes.

We never fully understood why, but we accepted this mystery with gratitude. The desserts were still hot from the griddle, the banana and chocolate soft and molten inside a crisp outer shell. It felt like an unnecessary bonus from the universe, which I am never against.

Then we went to the station and got on the train home, talking about the night and the thirteen days still ahead of us.

What would those days contain? What new adventures were waiting for us?
