## Monday, April 20 - Bendy Spoons and Boiling Soup

```poem
Monday served chicken with utensils built to fail
Then sent up beef in a stone bowl raising steam like mail
Rice kept appearing, persistent, polite, and hard to evade
By night I was fed, slightly wary, and adequately swayed
```

Monday, the beginning of the work week, had a suitably workmanlike shape to it. At lunch I joined one of my coworkers and went to the spot next door, the one with a line and a cluster of what seemed to be mostly permanent food truck or food stall setups. It had that practical lunchtime energy, people queuing because this was clearly the established move, not because anyone was feeling especially romantic about it.

We both ordered the same thing, some sort of bone-in chicken with rice, the name of which I immediately failed to retain and probably did not pronounce correctly when I ordered it anyway. It came with plastic spoons and knives, which continues to be one of those local realities I have not fully made peace with. There is a particular challenge in trying to separate meat from a chicken thigh using small, bendy, fragile utensils that seem engineered to test character. In reality, of course, the answer is obvious. You're supposed to use your hands. This is something I still have not entirely persuaded myself to do without hesitation. Although I ended up using the handwash station afterward anyway, which rather undercut the whole effort. I probably should have just committed.

Back at Tamarind Square, I had dinner there and decided this would be the day to try the Korean place.

I ordered yukkejang because beef sounded good, and because the picture on the menu looked like an actual stew and not another situation involving an enormous mound of rice taking over the plate. That alone gave it a certain appeal.

It arrived in a heavy black stone bowl, still boiling, and I mean actually boiling, not just theatrically steaming for effect. The surface kept bubbling aggressively for a while, so the first few minutes required some caution. It smelled strongly of beef, garlic, chili oil, spice, all of it immediate and direct. Nothing subtle about it. Looking into the bowl, I found beef, mushrooms, bean sprouts, and greens in a dark, rich broth stained red-brown with chili and whatever else they had built into it.

There were also pieces of something that, at first glance, looked like chicken. On closer inspection, it was clearly not chicken. It had the color of chicken, but none of the fibers, none of the structure that would make the matter reassuring. I wasn't sure what it was. Possibly I did not want to know, which felt like a perfectly reasonable position to take over dinner.

The texture was a big part of the whole thing. Soft shredded meat, slightly chewy vegetables, slippery mushrooms, all of it coming together in the same spoonful. The broth was rich and spicy, but not so heavy that it turned into a chore. It had depth without becoming oppressive, which I appreciated.

It came with the standard sides, kimchi that was sharp and sour enough to cut through the richness, and a simple cabbage salad with a light, slightly sweet dressing. Those helped reset everything between bites. There was still rice, because apparently one cannot completely avoid rice, but this time it had been demoted to the role of side dish instead of dominating the meal by sheer volume.

That felt like a small victory.
