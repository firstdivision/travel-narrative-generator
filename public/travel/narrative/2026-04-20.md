## Monday, April 20 - Bones, Broth, Monday

```poem
Monday served chicken with utensils built to lose
By night a stone bowl arrived, still muttering clues
I dodged the rice mountain and followed the beefy glow
Then burned my tongue just enough to let the evening know
```

Monday had the usual beginning-of-the-workweek feel, slightly blunt and already in motion before I was ready for it. At lunch I joined one of my coworkers and went to the spot next door, a place with a line of mostly permanent food truck or food stall setups. It had that practical, no-nonsense lunch energy, people queueing because this was clearly what one did.

We both ordered the same thing, some sort of bone-in chicken with rice, the name of which I immediately forgot and probably had not pronounced correctly when I ordered it anyway. It came with the now-familiar plastic spoon and knife situation, which is its own kind of small test. There is a skill to getting meat off a chicken bone with a bendy, fragile utensil that seems designed for softer ambitions. Really, I know the correct answer is to use my hands. I still have not fully crossed that threshold. Although I ended up using the handwash station anyway, which did make me think I should have just committed and gone all in from the start.

By dinner I was back in Tamarind Square and in the mood to try the Korean place. It felt like the right kind of meal for the end of a Monday, something hot and substantial, something that might reset the day a bit.

I ordered yukkejang because beef sounded good, and the menu photo looked like a stew. More importantly, it did not appear beside a giant pile of rice. That mattered.

When it arrived, it came in a heavy black stone bowl that was still properly boiling, not decorative steam, not residual heat, but actual active bubbling across the surface. It kept going for a while too, so the first few bites required some caution and patience, neither of which I am especially known for when food smells this good.

The broth was deep red-brown, dark with chili oil and flakes and whatever else they had worked into it, and it smelled strongly of beef, garlic, and spice. Not subtle at all, which I appreciated. Looking into it, I could make out beef, mushrooms, bean sprouts, and greens. There were also pieces of something that looked like chicken at first glance, though I was not sure what they actually were. Maybe I did not need that mystery solved.

What made it especially good was the texture. One spoonful had soft shredded meat, slightly chewy vegetables, slippery mushrooms, and that rich broth coating everything. It was spicy and full-bodied without tipping into heaviness, which felt like a small mercy after the day.

It came with the standard sides, kimchi, sharp and sour enough to cut straight through the richness, and often a simple cabbage salad with a light, slightly sweet dressing. Between bites of the soup, those little side dishes did exactly what they were supposed to do, keeping the whole thing balanced.

So that was Monday, awkward combat with bone-in chicken at lunch, then a deeply satisfying bowl of boiling beef soup at dinner. Not a dramatic day, just one that was defined more than usual by texture, heat, and the quiet dignity of getting through it one meal at a time.
