## Friday, May 1 - Wet Socks and Velvet Ropes

```poem
I climbed for culture and came back with swampy feet
Paid extra to skip a line, which felt suspiciously sweet
A mall on a mountaintop somehow seemed perfectly fine
By night we had headaches, chocolates, and not much dignity left to mine
```

Today was a holiday in Malaysia, Labor Day, so naturally we planned a full day and then filled it even further.

First up was Batu Caves, one of Malaysia's most famous religious and cultural landmarks, just outside Kuala Lumpur. The limestone caves and cave temples rise out of the hillside with absolutely no interest in being subtle about it. At the entrance stands the towering golden statue of Lord Murugan, and beside it the famous staircase, all color and vertical commitment, 272 steps leading to the main cathedral cave. Inside, the cavern opened up toward the sky through natural breaks in the rock, and sunlight dropped through in bright shafts onto shrines, worshippers, and the occasional monkey who seemed to regard the whole thing as his personal kingdom. For all the tourists moving through it, it still felt clearly like an active Hindu pilgrimage site rather than a scenic backdrop with stairs attached.

After we made our way up the first set of steps, there was a second section inside the cave. My friend had been here once before, and when we reached it she said she would wait there if I wanted to do the second part. I wasn't sure why.

Then I found out.

The second section required shoes off, even though I saw plenty of people who had apparently decided this rule was for other people. But I am, unfortunately for myself, a rule follower, so off came the shoes. For some reason I also decided to leave my socks on. This had worked well enough at the temple at the base of the stairs, so my brain apparently concluded that all barefoot-adjacent temple situations were identical. A flawed theory. This was a cave. A wet cave. A cave with water dripping steadily down the walls.

As I started up the second set of stairs, I tried weaving around the puddles, but there is only so much weaving available when the floor is damp in every meaningful direction. My socks got wetter and darker with each step, and still I carried on, because at some point pride takes over where judgment has already left. I made it to the top, to the inner part of the temple, but by then I was no longer interested in the spiritual or architectural significance of anything. I was thinking only about my socks. Then I accidentally stepped into a puddle surrounded by bird poop, and that was the end of my devotion to the experience. I tried not to think about it, which of course meant I thought about it constantly.

I went back down to meet my friend. "I maybe should not have left my socks on," I said, showing her the dark, soaked bottoms of what had once been white socks. She gave me a knowing smile while I put my shoes back on, the smile of someone who had wisely chosen not to interfere with a bad decision that needed to complete its natural arc.

We descended the stairs and found a coconut stand. You could buy them "normal" or "cold," and the cold ones were only about one ringgit more, which made this one of the easiest financial decisions of the day. We drank them standing there in the heat, then tried to scoop out some of the coconut meat. We were successful, somewhat. It was one of those messy little efforts that feels more satisfying than efficient.

Back at the car, I took off my shoes and my awful socks and announced that I thought I would just wear the shoes by themselves for the rest of the day. This was not a good plan, but before I could really commit to it, my friend produced a spare pair of socks. Clean, new, warm, soft. Maybe she knew I would do something like this. Maybe everyone who goes into Batu Caves comes out wanting a full sock reset. Either way, I was grateful in a way that probably exceeded the actual monetary value of the socks.

Then we got back in the car and headed to Genting Highlands, and my job along the way was to pick new music.

Genting Highlands sits high in the Titiwangsa Range, about an hour from Kuala Lumpur, and the appeal is obvious even before you get there. Cool temperatures, misty forests, and relief from the tropical heat are powerful marketing tools, especially after a humid morning involving cave stairs and sock regret. It is a mountain resort destination packed with hotels, casinos, shopping, entertainment venues, and theme parks, the kind of place that somehow manages to feel like both a hill station and a self-contained resort city. A lot of visitors arrive by cable car, gliding over dense rainforest and up into the clouds.

We arrived hungry, so our first stop was Ichiban Ramen. In the moment, I thought it was the same place I had gone to in Tokyo. Later research proved that no, the Tokyo one had been Ichiran, not Ichiban. Close enough for exhausted memory, not close enough for accuracy. Still, the ramen was very good, warming and substantial, exactly what I wanted in the cooler mountain air. It settled me and made the next part of the day feel possible.

After that we headed directly for the cable car and were immediately confronted with a gigantic line. The back-and-forth kind. The kind built not for movement but for acceptance. What I call a Disney Line. Actually there were two of them, one for tickets and one for boarding, which felt ambitious. We also discovered there were VIP tickets available online that let you skip the line.

I love skipping lines whenever possible. Deeply. Sincerely. Almost morally.

I looked up the tickets on my phone. They were not much more expensive than the regular ones. Add two to cart, please.

We showed the QR codes to an attendant, a red velvet rope was lifted, and suddenly we were walking down an empty hallway with windows looking out onto the full line snaking back and forth. This, I thought, must be what it feels like to be rich. Or at least what it feels like to spend a little extra money at exactly the right moment.

The cable car carried us up over the forest to the top of the mountain, where the attractions and mall sit like a brightly lit argument against nature. We went inside, and the contrast was immediate. Outside there had been green hills, cloud, and mist. Inside there were neon lights, stores, and carnival rides. Yes, carnival rides. Ferris wheel, games, the whole thing, all inside a mountaintop mall. It was strange in a way I found completely enjoyable.

We played games for a while. There was an archery place, and there were video games, including Halo in one of those setups with racecar-style seats, giant screens in front of you, and guns that track on the screen. It had the particular energy of an arcade machine designed to make you feel more skilled than you are for about three minutes.

We also played two basketball games. The first one went reasonably well. We sank twenty-something balls and felt competent enough. The second one was noticeably worse, and not just because of our own decline. We had chosen a different machine, and this one had about half as many balls in it. I remain convinced previous players had transferred balls from this machine to the other one to improve their odds. A tiny arcade conspiracy. Entirely plausible.

With our last bit of money left on the plastic game card, we searched for something cheap enough to play one final time and found a street-fighter-style game. It turned out to be the best value of all. On a single play I managed to get through multiple fights, and for a brief stretch I felt unexpectedly capable. Then I reached a level where the opponent absolutely clobbered me in all three bouts. I did not stand a chance. Still, it was money well spent.

After that we headed back to the cable car on the "go down" side, but stopped at a midpoint landing to visit Chin Swee Caves Temple.

Rising from the mist below Genting's casinos and hotels, it felt like a different world entirely, a mountainside sanctuary of pagodas, incense, gardens, and towering Buddha statues suspended above a sea of rainforest. After the constant buzz of Genting, the place offered a real pause. The complex includes a towering Buddha statue, a nine-story pagoda, prayer halls, and sweeping views across the surrounding mountains. Everything there felt calmer, more spacious, and less aggressively lit.

We took the cable car the rest of the way down to the main area. Walking back to the car, I spotted a candy store and immediately decided to go in. It was full of chocolates of all varieties, including durian-flavored ones, which I could not resist buying for my cousins when they arrive. I also bought more traditional flavors too, almond, sea salt, mint, just in case I decided not to make durian the only available option.

Leaving felt a bit like leaving a ski resort, with cars everywhere and everyone trying to go home at once. The traffic thickened and stalled as the day emptied itself downhill. At some point we both admitted we had headaches. Maybe it was the altitude, or maybe just the accumulated weight of the day. Wet caves, long lines, mountain malls, arcade basketball, cable cars, candy decisions. Eventually the traffic cleared, and we made the drive back to Kuala Lumpur, which took a little more than an hour and a half.
