## Tuesday, May 5 - Shakshuka, Cats, and Accidental Whisky Bar

```poem
I started with eggs and professional restraint,
Then paid rent in cat hair, or something adjacent.
By night I was sober in a whisky bar glow,
Ordering more wagyu before common sense could say no.
```

I had breakfast in Tamarind Square before going to work, and found that the new restaurant I'd talked to the owner about was finally open. This was the same place where I'd been given a free coffee before, so of course I went in to try it.

Then I saw shakshuka on the menu and that was it. No resistance at all. I may like shakshuka almost as much as Eggs Benedict, maybe more, which feels like a serious admission. I was also still comparing everything, unfairly perhaps, to the one I'd had at The Botanist, which had been, yes, "eggcellent."

This one came with rye toast and a side of lamb sausage. It was a little clunky, not quite as refined as the Botanist version, and the toast was good but slightly underdone, closer to bread in a few spots. Still, it was their first day open, and based on my earlier conversations with the owner, I had a feeling they'd straighten things out. The service was superb. The latte was superb too. Overall it was a very good shakshuka, but for today at least, I had to give the nod to The Botanist.

After work it was time for another date night. We decided to go to a "Catfe," which is exactly what it sounds like, a cafe where you get to play with cats. An easy sell.

We arrived, got our hands sanitized with a quick spray-bottle spritz, and immediately found ourselves surrounded by cats. Most of them were sleeping or completely uninterested in us, which honestly only made the whole thing more convincing. These were real cats. No fake hospitality, no customer service instincts, just furry indifference.

We got our drinks, thankfully with lids. I do like cats, but not so much their hair, which seems capable of traveling independently through the air and settling on anything. Eventually we found a few cats willing to acknowledge us, possibly helped along by treats. Bribery remains one of the more reliable cross-species languages.

We stayed a long time, long after our coffees had gone cold, my latte and her matcha tea both abandoned in favor of sitting there with the cats. Neither of us really wanted to leave our furry friends.

Once we did leave, I was given the responsibility of finding somewhere to eat, which is always flattering right up until it becomes stressful. There were plenty of restaurants along the same street as the Catfe, but none of them were calling to us. I found a Chinese grocery and went in to see if I could find any ginger coconut drink. I even tried asking one of the clerks, but she just shook her head no.

So we got back in the car and I started scrolling through maps. I found a place I think was called Mori Kori. I don't fully remember, because when we got there they were only serving drinks or something along those lines. But next to it was a place called **BAR MOKU**, and they were still serving food.

Tucked away behind an unassuming entrance near Jalan Ampang, **TEPPANYAKI KONOMU × BAR MOKU** felt like the kind of Japanese whisky bar I'd expect to stumble into in Tokyo rather than Kuala Lumpur. The space was intimate and dim without trying too hard about it, with Japanese whiskies, highballs, cocktails, teppanyaki, yakitori, and small plates all sharing the same room. It managed to feel refined and slightly hidden at the same time, like a place where the conversation naturally slows down and the city outside stops mattering for a while.

We both ordered non-alcoholic drinks, which seemed to be a slightly unusual move in a Japanese whisky bar. The woman who took our order went back and forth between us a couple of times to confirm, and eventually brought out a younger man, maybe a chef, who seemed to speak better English. We finally established that yes, we really did want the non-alcoholic versions. I think mine was a mojito.

Then we ordered yakitori skewers: chicken and onion, chicken hearts, and wagyu beef. They were all good, but the wagyu, true to the current hysteria surrounding it, absolutely stole the show. It was rich and quick and almost unfairly satisfying, the kind of food that interrupts conversation because your brain has other priorities. I ended up ordering another plate of the wagyu. I was so eager to eat it that the photo I took barely did it justice. It was the visual equivalent of inhaling first and documenting later.
