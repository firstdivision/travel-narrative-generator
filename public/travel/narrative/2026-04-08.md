# Monday, April 8 - Doors, Coffee, and Concrete Mazes

```poem
Jetlag kept watch for a tiger that never came near
I fought with smart doors, which made the rules very clear
Coffee cost more than a ride, which felt slightly obscene
Then butter, rice, and a pink knife made the day almost serene
```

I woke up early, partly from lingering jet lag, partly from the standard problem of being in a new place and not quite trusting it yet. I never sleep well the first couple of days anywhere unfamiliar. Some old reptile-brain part of me seems determined to stay alert and scan for danger, as if a sabertooth tiger might still be a plausible nighttime concern. Only once that primitive little security guard is satisfied that nothing is going to eat me in my sleep do I ever really settle in.

I decided to walk over to Tamarind Square, the shopping center across the street. First I had to learn how to leave my building, which turned out to be less intuitive than it should have been. On the lobby floor by the elevator there were double doors leading outside, and a sign on the glass said “push.” So I pushed. Nothing happened. I pushed harder. Still nothing.

There was a man from the cleaning staff in the lobby, so I turned and asked him. He didn’t speak English, but he said something and pointed back toward the door. Fair enough. I pushed even harder. At that point both he and one of the guards in the outside lobby saw what I was doing and started yelling and pointing. I stopped. Off to the side of the door, tucked away with no sign acknowledging its existence, was a button. Apparently that was what you pushed to unlock the door from the inside.

I found myself wondering what happens if the power goes out, or the switch breaks. Hopefully the door fails safe and unlocks itself. Or maybe if you push really, really hard, it opens. Or shatters into ten thousand diamond-shaped pieces. Either way, lesson learned. I now knew how to open one of the doors.

Then I started walking out through what looked like the road entrance and got yelled at again. Another pointed finger redirected me. There was another exit, reached by a path with stones and another gated door. This one required my RFID key to open. A lot of the morning, it seemed, was going to be about discovering that a thing which looked like an exit was not necessarily an exit.

I made it across the four-lane street, the kind with no crosswalk where one might reasonably want one, and reached Tamarind Square itself. It had a futuristic brutalist look, but softened by trees growing from balconies and the roof. It looked like it belonged in some post-apocalyptic movie, war-ravaged concrete where civilization had returned just after nature had made a serious attempt to reclaim it.

Then I failed to find my way inside.

There were stores and restaurants along the outer road level, but most were still closed, except for the daycares, which were busy with morning drop-offs. Through one doorway I spotted an escalator. It wasn’t moving and appeared to lead to a dark hallway. It looked like it *should* have been the way in, but also very much like it *could not* be the way in. I kept going. I tried a stairwell instead and found myself in a strangely Disney-behind-the-scenes service area where restaurant employees seemed to be preparing for the day. A woman bustled past and gave me a look that clearly translated to, *What exactly are you doing here?* I retreated back down to the road.

Eventually I found an entrance to a parking garage. Inside was an elevator with signs on it, which felt almost luxurious by this point. I selected level three, got out, and finally found myself properly inside the place. There were more roadways with cars parked, more storefronts, more restaurants. I wandered, which is apparently one of the few things I am reliably good at.

Village Grocer was still closed until 10:30, but Celsius Coffee right next to it was open, and I do like coffee, so in I went. One thing I would quickly learn about Kuala Lumpur was that there are a lot of coffee places, and coffee is relatively expensive. My Grab rides to work would eventually average around $2, while a caffe latte would run me about $3. On my first day in KL, my sense of money was already collapsing.

After coffee, the grocery store still wasn’t open, so I kept walking and found a little restaurant tucked into a labyrinth of stairs and hanging greenery. Birds were chirping. The tables felt both inside and outside at the same time. It was perfect in a way I had not expected to find in the middle of all that concrete. I sat down, scanned the QR code menu, and ordered from my phone.

I had a drink made from carrot, pineapple, and orange juice, and it was excellent. With it came a simple plate of toasted bread with jam and butter, the good kind of butter, full-fat and salted. It made me think of breakfasts in Paris, those quiet mornings when you can find a small café and order coffee and a baguette with butter and jam, and for a little while everything seems to sit in the right place. The same feeling found me here, unexpectedly, in this tucked-away café in the middle of a concrete maze that still somehow made room for greenery and birdsong. I was starting to settle in.

By then Village Grocer was open, so I went in to accomplish my real mission, cleaning supplies. The Airbnb was fine, but it needed work. I bought sprays, sponges, and microfiber cloths, all intended to replace their weary predecessors back in the apartment.

Then I returned and cleaned. And cleaned some more. I washed dishes and gave them a special place in the cabinet reserved for things that I had washed and therefore knew were actually clean. I did the same with part of the utensils tray. I cleaned the butter knife. Yes, *the* butter knife, singular, the only one. It was plastic and pink, like it had escaped from an adult-sized Barbie Dream Kitchen.

In the evening I went back to Tamarind Square for dinner. It was much busier now, fully awake, and I wandered again, slowly learning my way around this maze of concrete, escalators, and stairs that seemed to lead nowhere until they suddenly did.

I paused at one restaurant to look at the menu. A lot of the menus I had seen already looked broadly similar, with rice as the unifying factor, the starchy glue knitting together nearly every meal photograph. A hostess came out to greet me. Her hijab framed her face as she smiled and asked if I would like to come in and eat. It was an effective tactic. It would have felt rude to say no. So I went in and took a seat.

Another QR code, another order placed by phone. I got garlic fried rice with ginger chicken, plus a passionfruit mint longan drink. Both were very good, and by the end I was comfortably full. As I left, all three women in the restaurant waved and thanked me with smiles. I waved back.

Back at the apartment I learned how to stream YouTube from my phone to the TV, which felt like one more small victory in a day built out of oddly specific ones. I was more comfortable now. I had a full belly, I had explored, and for once I did not fall asleep on the couch. I moved to the bedroom.

Tomorrow would be my first day working in the Malaysia office, and I needed the rest.
