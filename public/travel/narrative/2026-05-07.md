## Thursday, May 7 - Gearboxes, Cash, and Late Arrivals

```poem
I ordered rice while doubting rice with zeal,
Then paid in gestures for my mystery meal.
My cousins landed late with Grab and cash in sight,
And eVisas carried us politely into night.
```

Date night took us to Sup GearBox Utara Putrajaya, which felt like exactly the kind of place I wanted to end up in: somewhere with a specialty so specific it practically came with its own anatomy lesson. The thing to get there was the gearbox, a bowl of rich beef-bone soup built around a massive marrow-filled leg joint, slow-simmered for hours with spices, herbs, and aromatics until the broth turned deep, savory, and slightly peppery.

She asked if I wanted rice or noodles, and I said rice. She handled the ordering in a quick burst of Malay, and I obviously understood none of it, either from her or from the person taking the order. There was some back and forth, fast and efficient in that way that makes you feel both useless and hungry, and then we sat down and the bowls arrived.

I had been thinking lately that I was getting a little tired of rice, so naturally I ordered rice. An excellent system. Maybe my brain just defaulted to something familiar before I had really thought about it. Or maybe I still didn't fully understand what I was ordering. Hard to say. Travel often reveals that a lot of my decision-making is just muscle memory wearing a confident face.

When I mentioned it, she offered to go get me noodles instead, but it was fine the way it was, because it was. I was hungry, and the bowl in front of me looked good enough to quiet whatever abstract anti-rice position I had been developing.

The soup had weight to it. The broth was dark and full, with that long-cooked depth that settles in immediately, and a peppery edge that kept it from feeling heavy in a dull way. The beef, though, was a little uneven. Some of it was exactly what I had hoped for, tender enough to almost collapse, the kind of meat that makes you forgive a lot. Other parts felt like they had not quite been cooked long enough, and what I had expected to be fully fall-off-the-bone turned out, in places, fairly tough. If every piece had been like the good ones, this would have been an easy A+ dinner. As it was, it was still very good. Solid B. No drama. I kept eating.

Afterward I was assigned the task of paying for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a different place. Naturally. At the counter, the man told me "lapan," the Malay word for eight. We both stared at each other for a moment while I failed to become a person who could count to ten in the local language through sheer force of need.

Then he realized, correctly, that I was not going to meet him halfway on this, and he started counting on his fingers.

"One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight."

Aha.

I handed him a ten Ringgit bill, and he gave me two back. Between us, through patience and basic arithmetic, we found a way.

On the way out we ordered some to go for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. We wanted just broth and noodles. No meat. This turned out to be harder to communicate than expected. They either did not understand us or pretended not to understand us, and we were given meat anyway. Oh well. There are worse problems than accidentally receiving beef soup with beef in it.

Later that night, back in my AirBnb, my two cousins texted to say they were at the airport. Yay. Then almost immediately came the next part: confusion about how this whole Grab thing worked. Oh no. A little after that they sent another text saying they had managed to order a Grab and were already in it, but they had selected pay with cash, and did I perhaps have any?

They finally arrived, and we all went back up to the AirBnb. I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, though I left out the beef. It was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night. So we sat there doing travel admin at an hour when no one should be making important form-based decisions.

It was getting toward one in the morning by the time we finally finished. Very late for the two locals, maybe not so much for the newcomers. The day ended not with some dramatic flourish, just paperwork, reheated soup, tired people, and that specific late-night feeling when everything has taken longer than expected but somehow still got done.
