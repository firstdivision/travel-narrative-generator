## Thursday, May 7 - Gearboxes and Late Arrivals

```poem
I ordered noodles and got rice with a sigh
The cashier said lapan and watched me blink dry
My cousins arrived with cash problems near one
Travel, as usual, was needlessly fun
```

Date night took us to **Sup Gearbox Utara Putrajaya**, which is a place built around one specific kind of commitment: a bowl of rich beef-bone soup centered on a giant marrow-filled leg joint, the "gearbox." The broth had been simmered for hours with spices, herbs, and aromatics until it turned deep, savory, and slightly peppery, and the whole thing arrived looking like part soup, part excavation site. Tender meat clung to the bone. Soft marrow waited inside to be scooped out like some absurdly luxurious kind of beef butter. There were lime wedges, chilies, fresh herbs, and the general sense that this was going to be gloriously messy if it went well.

Ordering, unfortunately, did not go as smoothly as either of us had probably imagined. Everything was done in Malay, so I understood absolutely nothing anyone said, which always gives me the strong tactical advantage of standing there with a polite face while events happen around me. After some back and forth, we sat down and our two bowls arrived, and the source of the confusion became obvious almost immediately. I had asked for noodles with my beef soup. What I got was rice.

This was especially irritating because I had been hinting lately, not very subtly, that I was getting tired of rice, which was the whole reason we ordered noodles in the first place. She asked about it and offered to go get me noodles instead, but by then I was hungry and the bowl in front of me looked too good to reject on principle. So I left it alone.

The soup itself was still very good, just not quite the triumph I had been primed for. I had expected the beef to be fully in that advertised fall-off-the-bone state, but some of what I got seemed like it had not been cooked quite long enough. Parts of it were fairly tough. A few pieces, though, were exactly right, very tender, and those bites made it clear what the whole bowl could have been. If every piece had landed at that level, this would have been an A+ dinner. As it was, I gave it a solid B, which is still a respectable thing to be while sitting over a bowl of peppery beef broth in Putrajaya.

After we ate, I was assigned the task of paying for our drinks, which for some reason came from and had to be paid for at a different place. Naturally. At the counter, the man told me, "lapan," the Malay word for eight. We then stared at each other for a few seconds while it became increasingly clear to him that I was a grown man who could not count to ten in the local language. He adjusted with the weary practicality of someone who has seen this before and started counting on his fingers.

"One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight!"

Aha. Progress.

I handed him a ten Ringgit bill, and he gave me two back. We had done it. International commerce survived another day.

On the way out, we ordered some takeaway for my two cousins, who were arriving that night. We asked for just broth and noodles, no meat. This turned into another small round of confusion. They either did not understand us or pretended not to understand us, which I can respect on some level, and we ended up with meat anyway. Oh well.

Later that night, back in my Airbnb, my two cousins texted that they were at the airport. Yay. Then came the next text explaining that they were confused about how this whole Grab thing worked. Oh no. Then another message arrived saying they had managed to order a Grab and were inside it, but they had selected pay with cash and did I perhaps have any?

They finally arrived, and we all went back up to the Airbnb. I warmed up the beef and noodles for them, though I left out the beef. By then it was already late, but we decided to push through and finish all of our eVisas for the rest of the trip that night anyway. It was getting close to one in the morning by the time we were finally done. Very late for the two locals. Maybe not so late for the newcomers. Either way, it had become that familiar travel hour when everyone is tired, paperwork starts to feel faintly ridiculous, and the day somehow keeps going long after it should have ended.
