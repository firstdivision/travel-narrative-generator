## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Brake Rotors, and Tiny Spa Underwear

```poem
We chased our breakfast straight into the rain,
Then boarded a boat with a truck-engine brain.
By night I wore spa underwear far too small,
And still somehow managed to eat through it all.
```

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00am alarm for breakfast, which sounded reasonable until the morning started behaving like a morning in transit. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be quick: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, this took longer than expected. The clock kept moving, our boat trip time got uncomfortably close, and when the order finally arrived I basically pounded the apple pastry on the spot while everything else came with us.

Kate led the way through the rain to the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. There is a specific kind of travel stress that comes from being damp, caffeinated, and not fully sure you are in the right place. We finally found it.

After a brief safety meeting and some forms to sign, we boarded our long-tail boat. You probably know what a long-tail boat is even if you don’t realize you do: a narrow wooden boat powered by what looks like a car or truck engine mounted on a long exposed driveshaft sticking out behind it like a tail. Hence the name, which at least has the decency to be literal. They’re often brightly painted, with ribbons, flowers, and bits of fabric tied to the bow for good luck.

Ours took us up the river, the narrow bow cutting through brown water, which is not the most romantic description but was true. At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry mechanical mouth, then motored slowly inside. The gate slid closed behind us, and shortly after, the one in front opened. I got the impression it was more about separating one body of water from another than changing elevation, though I wasn’t entirely sure. I tried to ask our guide, but I don’t think she understood the question. She probably doesn’t get many lock-system inquiries on the average boat tour.

After racing through smaller canal-like waterways, we arrived at the temple.

Rising above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi, Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen really was impossible to miss. Its colossal golden Buddha gazed serenely across western Bangkok, towering over homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature beneath it. Approaching by long-tail boat made the whole thing more dramatic. The statue emerged bit by bit above the skyline, then grew larger with every bend in the canal until it dominated everything.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself had a quieter pull. Shoes off to go in, and this time I remembered to remove my *socks* too, not wanting a repeat of the wet-sock episode from Batu Caves. I am, apparently, capable of growth.

Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of the most striking modern Buddhist interiors I’d seen, all of it balancing ancient tradition with contemporary design in a way that didn’t feel forced. In the glass pagoda, an emerald-colored stupa glowed beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky, and the atmosphere was almost otherworldly without tipping into theme-park nonsense. Our guide came with us, explaining the history of the place as we went, and the whole complex felt both deeply spiritual and unmistakably modern, where Bangkok’s past and present met in the shadow of that giant gold figure.

We reboarded the long-tail boat and retraced our path back. After a short stop to refuel at a fuel dock, we were dropped off at the terminal again, and our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass, river reflections, designer perfume, boat traffic, durian snacks, and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it’s probably ICONSIAM.

Sitting directly on the banks of the Chao Phraya, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody had tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as this massive mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment. But the strange charm of the place was that beneath the marble floors and Louis Vuitton storefronts there was also SOOKSIAM, a fake indoor floating market that I mean affectionately. Downstairs there were smoky grilled seafood stalls, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to carry a civilization through collapse. Touristy, yes. Also legitimately fun.

Hidden inside the mall was one of Bangkok’s stranger residents: a colossal dachshund named Sunshine. It stretched across the room like a sleeping giant, almost alive, chest slowly rising and falling while shoppers stopped to stare, laugh, and pull out their phones. It was part art installation, part photo opportunity, part fever dream. Bangkok has a talent for that.

When we left the mall, we needed to get back across the river. At the ticket booth in the big patio area out front, we asked about tickets and learned the next ferry we needed was leaving in just a few minutes. We had to pay in cash. I was suddenly digging through my wallet trying to count foreign currency during a minor travel emergency, which is always a dignified look. Finally the woman behind the glass just pointed at the exact bill I needed to hand over. She tossed back some change and three tickets and instructed us to RUN.

We made it to the boat slightly out of wind but also feeling very alive. Out of breath, yes, but brimming with energy.

The ride to our stop was short. We got off and started walking, though I can’t remember if we had a specific destination in mind. I think it was more a general area, one with lots of pins on the shared Google Map we’d been building while planning the trip. We drifted into smaller streets, the kind where maybe a car can fit in one direction but the place is really meant for people and motorcycles.

The girls went into a 7-Eleven for drinks, I think, and I finally checked off a major Bangkok box by finding a real street food vendor: a little old lady with a wok, a pile of ingredients, and a compact setup loaded with vegetables in Tupperware containers. I stood there watching her cook an order for a man who had been there when I arrived. I was just about to attempt ordering when another equally adorable old lady came over and started helping me. She must have seen the uncertainty on my face and correctly guessed I was about to make a complete mess of the process. With her help I managed to order pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container.

It was one of those Bangkok moments that feels like it belongs in someone else’s travel documentary or YouTube video, except this time it was *me*. I ate the whole thing while we walked.

Somewhere along the way we passed what seemed to be an entire parts district. One whole store of used brake rotors, another of just water pumps, then around the corner more general parts, screws and bushings of various sizes, all of it looking as if it had been harvested from cars and machinery and given a second life on shelves. Bangkok is very good at abruptly changing subject.

In a somewhat bougie area, while the girls were doing some clothes shopping, I found a place selling pistachio milk. I had no idea what that actually was, which of course meant I had to try it.

Pistachio milk in Bangkok felt like one of those drinks invented by someone who got tired of ordinary coffee shop menus and decided the world needed something softer, greener, and slightly luxurious. The first thing I noticed was the color. Not bright minty cartoon green, but a muted pistachio shade, pale jade, almost dusty. It came cold, in a tall plastic cup sweating immediately in the heat. The flavor landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted ice cream, less sugary than I expected, with that naturally buttery, roasted pistachio richness underneath. Smooth, cold, slightly sweet. It had a toasted note to it, like warm nuts fresh from a pan, except turned into something built for Bangkok weather.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. At first we were planning on facials, mostly because it was something I’d never done before, and when I travel I like checking off the “never done that before” category whenever possible. But while looking at the spa menu and hearing the host describe the whole facial process, the stages, the chemicals, the equipment, all of it began to sound less like indulgence and more like a science fair happening on my face. I decided maybe I didn’t want that after all and switched to an aroma massage instead.

The host brought out a tray of boxed scents for me to choose from. I honestly don’t remember what mine was called. It was whatever one she suggested, and I remember it smelling very nice, which felt like enough expertise on my part.

I was led to a room and instructed to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Fine. Straightforward. Then I opened the package and discovered an extremely small piece of knitted underwear that looked alarmingly optimistic about my dimensions. I spent a minute or two in complete indecisive paralysis, just standing there wondering if there had been some sort of mistake.

Finally I opened the door, where the masseuse was waiting patiently, probably wondering what exactly was taking me so long.

“Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?”

“Yes,” she said.

“Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking.”

I closed the door.

Welp. Here goes.

I stripped down, put on the tiny knitted situation, gave the door a knock, and then basically made a diving leap to the table. But very soon I forgot about all of that. The massage was one of the most relaxing things I have probably ever done. The aroma came from the traditional scented oil, and it was exactly what a weary traveler needed. Also, after conversion, it was only about $20 US, which felt almost suspiciously generous.

Afterward we were all tired of walking and decided to take a Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned down a street and immediately we started seeing signs like “XXX Erotic Massage,” which was not entirely the culinary lead-up I had pictured.

At the end of the road it was dark, and we couldn’t see our restaurant anywhere. The driver opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us. It read: “Bury everything and burn it.”

Obviously something had gone slightly wrong.

He tried again. This time the app produced: “My day is over.”

That seemed like our cue to get out.

So we got out onto this dark road and started walking back toward the XXX lounges because there didn’t seem to be much else to do. Then, down a side road, we spotted a blue sign with some kind of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, something like the place I’d gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31, it felt like one of those places you could walk past three times before realizing half the city’s food nerds were hiding inside. The second the door opened, it smelled like smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, and hot rice. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes, and after the day we’d had, this all landed with the kind of deep, physical relief that good late food can bring.

After that, back to the hotel to sleep.

As I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was apparently asking about our flight tomorrow, wanting to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we’d given them when we checked in, and eventually we all drifted off to sleep.
