## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Brake Rotors, and Very Small Underwear


We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00 a.m. alarm for breakfast, which sounded civilized in theory and felt slightly aggressive in practice. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be quick: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, it took longer than expected. The time for our boat trip was getting uncomfortably close, and by the time everything finally arrived I had to pound down the pastry and carry the rest with us.

Kate led the way through the rain to the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. We found it eventually, which felt like enough of a victory without making a whole thing of it. After a brief safety meeting and some forms to sign, we boarded our long-tail boat.

You probably know what a long-tail boat is even if you do not realize you know. It is one of those narrow wooden boats powered by what looks like a car or truck engine mounted on a long exposed driveshaft trailing behind like a tail, which is exactly what gives it the name. They often have colorful ribbons, flowers, and fabric tied to the bow for good luck. Ours pushed up the river with the narrow front slicing through brown water, not a romantic description exactly, but true.

At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry metal machine, then motored slowly inside. The gate closed behind us, and after a bit the one ahead opened. I got the impression it was less about elevation and more about separating one body of water from another, though I tried asking our guide and do not think she understood what I meant. She probably does not get many questions about the lock system. Fair enough.

After racing through smaller canal-like waterways, we arrived at the temple. Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen rose above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi in a way that made it impossible to miss. The colossal golden Buddha gazed out over western Bangkok, towering above homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature. Coming in by long-tail boat made the whole approach more dramatic. The statue kept appearing in fragments above the skyline, then growing larger with every bend until it dominated everything.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself had a quieter pull. I took my shoes off to go inside and, this time, remembered to remove my socks too. I was not interested in repeating the wet sock episode from Batu Caves. Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of the most striking modern Buddhist interiors I have seen, all of it balancing old tradition with contemporary design. In the glass pagoda, an emerald-colored stupa glowed beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky. The whole room felt almost otherworldly in a way that was somehow still calm. Our guide came with us and explained the history as we walked.

We reboarded the boat, retraced the route back, stopped briefly at a fuel dock to refuel, and were dropped at the terminal again. Our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass and river reflections and designer perfume and boat traffic and durian snacks and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it is probably ICONSIAM. Sitting directly on the Chao Phraya, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody had tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a huge mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, promenades, and entertainment. Which is one thing. But the weird magic of it was downstairs in SOOKSIAM, the fake indoor floating market that I mean affectionately. Beneath the marble floors and Louis Vuitton storefronts were smoky grilled seafood, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to keep civilization going after the collapse. Touristy, yes. Also genuinely fun.

Then there was Sunshine, perhaps Bangkok's most unexpected resident, a colossal dachshund stretched across a room like a sleeping giant. Its chest slowly rose and fell as if it were alive while people stopped to stare, laugh, and lift their phones. It was part art installation, part photo opportunity, part fever dream. Bangkok does this kind of thing very well.

Leaving the mall turned into a minor sprint. We went to the ticket booth in the big patio area out front to ask about crossing back over the river, and the next ferry we needed was leaving in a few minutes. Payment had to be in cash, of course, so I was suddenly digging through my wallet and trying to count foreign currency during a travel emergency, one of the least elegant activities available to a person. Finally the woman behind the glass pointed directly at the bill I needed to hand over. She tossed back some change and three tickets and told us to run.

So we ran.

We made it to the boat slightly out of wind but also weirdly exhilarated. A little breathless, very awake. It was only a short ride to our stop, and from there we got off and started walking. I cannot remember whether we had a specific destination in mind at that point. I think it was more that we were headed toward a general area with lots of pins on the shared Google Map we had been building during trip planning.

We ended up in smaller streets, the kind that can maybe fit a car in one direction but are really for people and motorcycles. The girls went into a 7-Eleven for drinks, I think, and I finally got to check off a major item on my mental list: a real street food vendor. A little old lady stood over a wok beside a cart stacked with vegetables in Tupperware containers and other ingredients. I watched her make an order for a man who had been standing there when I arrived. I was about to try ordering when another similarly adorable old lady appeared and started helping me. She had clearly taken one look at me and decided I was about to make a complete mess of this. She clarified things, translated enough, and with her help I managed to order pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container.

It was one of those Bangkok moments that feels like it belongs in a travel documentary or a YouTube video, except this time it was me standing there holding the container. I ate the whole thing while we walked. Hot rice, pork, the richness of the egg, all of it somehow exactly right in that moment.

The neighborhood seemed to have an entire parts district folded into it. One whole store of used brake rotors. Another of just water pumps. Around the corner were more bins of screws, bushings, and various mechanical pieces that looked harvested from old cars and machines. It was one of those oddly specific urban landscapes that says more about a city than the polished places ever do.

In a somewhat bougie area while the girls were shopping for clothes, I saw a place selling pistachio milk and decided I had to try it. The drink arrived in that muted pistachio shade, pale jade, almost dusty, not bright cartoon green. In the Bangkok heat the plastic cup started sweating instantly. The flavor landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted ice cream, but less sugary than I expected. It was cold, silky, slightly sweet, with that naturally buttery pistachio richness and a toasted note underneath. It felt like one of those drinks invented because somebody got bored with ordinary café menus and wanted the world to be a little softer and stranger.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. The original plan was facials, which appealed to me mostly because I had never had one and I like using travel to check off things I have never done before. But while we were looking at the spa menu and the host was describing the facial process in detail, all the stages and chemicals and equipment involved, I started backing away from the concept mentally. I switched to an aroma massage instead. The host brought out a tray of boxed scents for me to choose from. I do not remember what mine was called. It was whatever she suggested, and it smelled very nice, which was enough for me.

I was led to a room and told to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Fine. Straightforward. Then I opened the package and found a very small piece of knitted material that I could only assume was the underwear. I spent a minute or two in complete indecisive paralysis.

Finally I opened the door, where the masseuse was patiently waiting and probably wondering what on earth was taking me so long.

"Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?"

"Yes," she said.

"Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking."

I closed the door.

Well then. I stripped down, put on the tiny garment that did in fact stretch quite a bit, and understood that the knitted construction was doing a lot of heavy lifting for the phrase "one size fits all." I gave the door a quick knock and then made a face-down diving leap onto the table.

Soon I forgot that entire episode because the massage was one of the most relaxing things I have probably ever done. The scent came from the traditional oil rubbed into the skin, and it was exactly what a weary traveler needed. Also, after conversion, it was about twenty U.S. dollars, which felt almost suspiciously reasonable for that level of relief.

Afterward we were tired of walking and decided to take a Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned down a street and immediately we started seeing signs like "XXX Erotic Massage," which was not especially reassuring. There was a brief collective moment of wondering what exactly we had been sent to.

When the driver reached the end of the road, it was dark and we could not see our restaurant anywhere. He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us. It read: "Bury everything and burn it." Which, while memorable, was not helpful. He tried again. This time it translated as: "My day is over." That seemed to be our cue to get out.

So we stepped onto a black, dark road with no obvious restaurant and started walking back toward the XXX lounges because that at least seemed to be the direction of visible civilization. The alternative was pure darkness. Then, down a side road that looked more like a driveway, we noticed a blue sign with some kind of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, something like the place I had gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out because at that point it was either that or continue the mystery tour on foot.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31, it was the kind of place you could walk past three times before realizing what it was. Inside, it really did feel like the Kuala Lumpur spot: a bar area with a bunch of men drinking whiskey and some tables lining one wall. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes, with tea to go with it. Once again the travel gods took something that had been teetering toward inconvenience and turned it into a comfortable meal.

After that it was back to the hotel to sleep. Just as I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in the other room. The hotel was asking about our flight tomorrow to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we had given them at check-in, and eventually we all drifted off.
