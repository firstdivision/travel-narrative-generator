## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Malls, and Tiny Underwear

```poem
We chased a boat with coffee sloshing near,
Then bought a mall-side ferry fueled by fear,
I paid for calm in knitted underwear,
And ended up with dumplings in the air.
```

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00 a.m. alarm for breakfast, which sounded reasonable when it existed as a plan and much less reasonable once it became real life. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be quick: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, this took longer than expected, because food and coffee only become “fast” when you have nowhere important to be. Our boat trip time was getting uncomfortably close by the minute. When everything finally arrived, I basically pounded the apple pastry on the spot while the drinks and fruit came with us.

Kate led the way through the rain to the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. We finally found it, which felt less inevitable than it sounds. After a brief safety meeting and signing some forms, we boarded our long-tail boat.

A long-tail boat is one of those things most people have seen without knowing the name. Narrow wooden boats, powered by what looks like a car or truck engine bolted onto the back, with a long exposed driveshaft extending behind it like a tail. Hence the name, in a rare triumph of straightforward labeling. They are often brightly painted, with colorful ribbons, flowers, and fabric tied to the bow for good luck.

Ours took us up the river, the narrow bow slicing through brown water, which is not a romantic description but was the truth of the matter. At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry metal contraption, then slowly motored inside. The gate slid closed behind us, and shortly after that the one in front opened. I had the impression this was less about changing elevation and more about separating one body of water from another, though I was not entirely sure. I tried to ask our guide, but I do not think she understood what I was getting at. She probably does not get a lot of tourists asking follow-up questions about the lock system.

After racing through smaller canal-like waterways, we arrived at the temple.

Rising above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi, Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen was impossible to miss. Its colossal golden Buddha gazed serenely across western Bangkok, towering over homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature beneath it. Coming by long-tail boat made the approach more dramatic. The statue gradually emerged above the skyline, growing larger with every bend in the canal until it dominated the horizon.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself offered a quieter kind of wonder. Shoes off to go in, and this time I remembered to remove my socks too, not wanting a repeat of the wet sock episode from Batu Caves.

Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of Thailand’s most striking modern Buddhist interiors, all of it balancing ancient tradition with contemporary design. In the temple’s glass pagoda, a glowing emerald-colored stupa sat beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky, giving the whole space an almost otherworldly feeling. Our guide came with us and explained the history as we went. Together, the monumental Buddha and the tranquil temple grounds made Wat Paknam feel both deeply spiritual and unmistakably modern, a place where Bangkok’s past and present met in the shadow of a golden giant.

We re-boarded the long-tail boat, retraced our path back, and after a short stop to refuel at a fuel dock, got dropped off at the terminal again. Our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass, river reflections, designer perfume, boat traffic, durian snacks, and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it is probably ICONSIAM.

Sitting directly on the banks of the Chao Phraya River, ICONSIAM felt less like a mall and more like somebody tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a gigantic mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment venues. The strange part was that beneath the Louis Vuitton storefronts and the marble floors, there was also a fake indoor floating market called SOOKSIAM. I mean that affectionately. Downstairs there were smoky grilled seafood stalls, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to sustain civilization after the collapse. Touristy, yes. Also legitimately fun, and probably one of the easiest ways in Bangkok to sample regional Thai food without crossing half the city.

Hidden inside ICONSIAM was perhaps Bangkok’s most unexpected resident: a colossal dachshund named Sunshine. It stretched across the room like a sleeping giant, almost alive, its chest slowly rising and falling while shoppers stopped to stare, laugh, and pull out their phones. Equal parts art installation, photo opportunity, and fever dream. Bangkok has a talent for that kind of thing.

When we left the mall, we needed to get back across the river. We went to the ticket booth in the large patio area in front of the mall and asked about tickets, only to learn that the next ferry we needed was leaving in a few minutes. We had to pay in cash. I was suddenly frantically digging through my wallet trying to count foreign currency during a travel emergency, which is one of those situations that makes you feel both very adult and completely helpless. Finally, the woman behind the glass pointed at the exact bill I needed to hand over. She threw some change and three tickets our way and told us to RUN.

We made it to the boat slightly out of wind but also feeling very alive. Sometimes the most memorable parts of trips are exactly that kind of pointless logistical sprint.

It was a short ride to our ferry stop. We disembarked and started walking. I cannot remember if we had a specific destination in mind at that point. I think it was more just a general area with lots of pins on the shared Google Map we had been building while planning the trip.

We ended up in smaller streets, the kind where maybe one car can fit in one direction, but really they seem meant for people and motorcycles. The girls went into a 7-Eleven for drinks, I think, and I finally checked off a major personal box by finding a real-deal street food vendor. A little old lady with a wok and a pile of ingredients. I watched her make an order for a man who was standing there when I arrived. She had a small cart loaded with vegetables in Tupperware containers and a wok going over heat. I was about to order when another similarly adorable old lady came up and started helping me. She must have seen the hesitation on my face and realized I was moments away from making a complete mess of this. She clarified things for me, and with her help I managed to order pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container.

It was one of those Bangkok moments that felt like something out of a travel documentary or a YouTube video, except it was me standing there this time, not watching someone else do it. I ate the whole thing while we walked down the street.

There seemed to be an entire parts district nearby. One whole store of used brake rotors, another of just water pumps. Around the corner there were more general parts, screws, bushings of various sizes, all of it looking as if it had been harvested from old cars and bits of machinery. It had that specific urban feeling of extreme specialization, where every storefront appears to be dedicated to one oddly narrow category of object and somehow survives.

In one somewhat bougie area, while the girls were shopping for clothes, I saw a place selling pistachio milk. I had no real idea what that was, which made me want it immediately.

Pistachio milk in Bangkok felt like one of those drinks invented by somebody who got bored with ordinary coffee shop menus and decided the world needed something softer, greener, and vaguely luxurious. The first thing I noticed was the color. Not bright cartoon green like mint ice cream, but a muted pistachio shade, pale jade, almost dusty. It came over ice in a tall plastic cup sweating instantly in the Bangkok heat. Flavor-wise, it landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted marzipan, but less sugary than I expected. Pistachio has that naturally buttery, almost roasted richness, and this version was cold, silky, slightly salty, slightly sweet, with a toasted note underneath it all, like warm nuts fresh from a pan. It was an odd thing to be drinking while wandering around Bangkok, which was part of the appeal.

Later we went to a spa in another mall, initially planning to get facials. That was appealing mostly because I had never had one before, and when I travel I do enjoy checking off categories of “never done that before.” But while looking at the spa menu and listening to the host describe the process, all the stages and chemicals and equipment involved, I decided maybe I did not want to experiment with my face after all. Instead I opted for an aroma massage. The host brought over a tray of boxed scents. I do not honestly remember what I picked. It was whatever she suggested, and I remember it smelled very nice. Good enough.

I was led to a room and instructed to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Fine. Simple. Easy. Then I opened the package and discovered what appeared to be an extremely small piece of knitted material. I stood there for a minute or two in complete indecisive paralysis, trying to determine whether there had been some mistake or whether this was simply my life now.

Eventually I opened the door, where the masseuse was patiently waiting, probably wondering what on earth was taking so long.

“Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?”

“Yes,” she said.

“Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking.”

I closed the door.

Welp. Here goes.

I stripped down, put on the tiny knitted underwear, knocked on the door, and then made a sort of diving leap onto the table. Soon enough I forgot the whole awkward event, because the massage itself was one of the most relaxing things I had probably ever done. The aroma came from the traditional scented oil, and it was exactly what a weary traveler needed. Also, after conversion, it was only about $20 US, which felt almost suspiciously reasonable for that level of relief.

Afterward we were all tired of walking and decided to Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The Grab turned down a street and immediately we saw signs for things like “XXX Erotic Massage.” Well. Maybe I should have gone there for a massage instead.

When the driver got to the end of the road, it was dark, and we could not see our restaurant anywhere. He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us. It read, “Bury everything and burn it.” Obviously something had gone slightly wrong there. He tried again, and this time it came out as, “My day is over.” That seemed to be our cue to get out.

So we got out onto a black, dark road and, with no better option, started walking back toward the XXX lounges. Then, down a side road, we spotted a blue sign with some sort of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, similar to the one I had gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31, it felt like one of those places you could walk past three times before realizing half the city’s food nerds were hiding inside. The smell hit as soon as the door opened: smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, hot rice. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes.

Then it was back to the hotel to sleep. As I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was apparently asking about our flight the next day, just to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we had given them when we checked in, and eventually we all drifted off to sleep.
