## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Brake Rotors, and Bad Translation

```poem
We chased a boat with coffee running late,
Bought mall air cold enough to refrigerate.
A translation app advised arson with flair,
Then dinner saved the day with smoke in the air.
```

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00 a.m. alarm and the kind of optimistic plan that depends on everything moving efficiently. So of course the first thing we did was take a Grab to a coffee shop and order what we thought would be quick. Takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and for me an apple pastry from the case. It all took much longer than expected, which made the whole thing feel faintly ridiculous, since the point of takeaway is usually to take it away before your next appointment starts without you.

By the time the order finally arrived, our boat trip was getting uncomfortably close. I basically pounded the apple pastry on the spot and carried everything else with us. Kate led the way through the rain toward the riverside terminals, where our boat tour was supposedly waiting. Bangkok in the rain has a way of making every surface feel either slick or vaguely temporary, and I was mostly focused on not dropping a drink while trying to keep up.

We found the boat eventually. That felt earned.

The ride took us up the river and through locks to a Buddha temple, which gave the morning a sudden shift in tone. After all the rushing and coffee logistics, there we were taking off our shoes before stepping inside. This time I remembered to ditch my socks too, which sounds minor but felt like progress. Dry socks in a wet city can become a strangely meaningful victory if you let them.

After the tour dropped us back at the terminal, we had ferry tickets to cross the river over to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass and river reflections and designer perfume and boat traffic and durian snacks and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it is probably ICONSIAM. Sitting right on the Chao Phraya, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody had tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship and then polished the floors until they reflected your fatigue back at you.

It opened in 2018 as this huge mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment venues, and somehow it still manages to contain a fake indoor floating market downstairs called SOOKSIAM. I mean that affectionately. Under the marble floors and Vuitton storefronts, there were smoky grilled seafood stalls, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to keep a civilization going after the collapse. Touristy, yes. Also fun, yes. Sometimes those things are allowed to coexist.

We did more shopping after that. Clothing stores, fabric stores, the usual slow drift from one bright retail space to another.

Somewhere in there I had pistachio milk, which in Bangkok feels like the kind of drink invented by someone who got bored with normal café menus and decided the world needed something softer, greener, and faintly expensive. The color was a muted pistachio, pale jade, not cartoonish, and the drink had that cold silky texture that seems to sweat through the cup the second it hits the heat outside. It tasted nutty and buttery, almost like melted marzipan but less sweet than expected, with a slightly salty toasted note underneath. A little ridiculous, maybe. But good.

At some point I finally checked off something I had really wanted: a real street food vendor. Not curated market ambiance, not mall food theater. An actual little old lady with a wok and a pile of ingredients. I was just about to order when another similarly old lady appeared and more or less took over the operation on my behalf. "You want tomato?" she asked. "This pork, not chicken." With her help, I managed to order a pork fried rice topped with a fried egg, packed into a takeaway container. I ate the whole thing while we walked down the street, which felt exactly right. Hot rice, oil, pork, the fried edges of the egg, all of it balanced in my hands while traffic and heat moved around us.

Then there was what I can only describe as a parts district. Whole stores dedicated to categories of mechanical remains. One place seemed to sell nothing but used brake rotors. Another was just water pumps. Around the corner were more general parts, screws and bushings of various sizes, all of them looking as if they had been harvested from cars or other machinery and sorted into a second life. It was one of those hyper-specific city zones that makes no sense until you are standing in it, and then suddenly it feels obvious that of course this exists.

More shopping followed, because apparently we were committed to exhausting every possible retail format in Bangkok.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. At first we were planning to get facials, mostly because I like using travel to check off never-done-that-before boxes. But then we started looking at the spa menu, and the host described the process in enough detail that I realized I maybe did not want that particular new experience after all. I pivoted to an aroma massage instead.

Good decision. It was better than the other massage, more traditional, with oil, genuinely relaxing and exactly what a weary traveler needs after a day of rain, boats, malls, and accidental industrial archaeology. Also it came out to something like twenty U.S. dollars, which felt almost suspiciously reasonable for how much better I felt afterward.

By then we were tired of walking, so we decided to take a Grab to a restaurant GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned down a street and immediately we started seeing signs like "XXX Erotic Massage," which did at least raise the possibility that I had chosen the wrong massage venue earlier.

Then the driver reached the end of the road, and it was dark, and the restaurant was nowhere in sight.

He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us. It read: "Bury everything and burn it."

Which, while vivid, did not feel logistically helpful.

He tried again. This time the app produced: "My day is over."

That seemed like our cue to get out.

So we stepped onto this black, dark road with no restaurant visible and started walking back toward the XXX lounges, because there was not much else to do. On the way, down a side road, we saw a blue sign with some sort of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, a bit like the one I had gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone else agreed we should check it out.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31 like one of those places you would walk past three times before realizing half the city's food obsessives were hiding inside, it smelled like smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, and hot rice the second the door opened. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes. After the strange little dead-end detour, the place felt almost improbably right, like the day had finally stopped messing with us and decided to feed us properly.

After that we went back to the hotel to sleep. Just as I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was asking about our flight tomorrow and wanted to re-double-triple-confirm the times with us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we had given them when we checked in, and eventually everyone drifted off. Bangkok had one more logistical twitch left in it before bed, apparently.
