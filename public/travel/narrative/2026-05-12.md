## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, malls, and very small underwear

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00am alarm for breakfast, which felt optimistic but necessary. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be the quick version of breakfast: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, this took longer than expected, because travel has a way of punishing any sentence that begins with "this should be fast." By the time everything finally arrived, our boat trip was approaching at an uncomfortable speed. I pounded the apple pastry on the spot and carried the rest with us.

Kate led the way through the rain to the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. We finally found it, which already felt like an achievement. After a brief safety meeting and signing some forms, we boarded our long-tail boat.

You probably know what a long-tail boat is even if you don't think you do. It's one of those narrow wooden boats powered by what looks like a car or truck engine stuck onto a long exposed driveshaft trailing behind it like a tail. Hence the name, not exactly subtle. They're often bright with ribbons, flowers, and bits of fabric tied to the bow for luck, and ours looked exactly like the kind of boat that belongs in your mental file of Southeast Asia.

It took us up the river, the narrow bow slicing through brown water. Not the most romantic description, but true. At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry metal contraption, then slowly motored inside. The gate slid shut behind us, and not long after that the one in front opened. I got the sense it was less about changing elevation and more about separating one body of water from another, but I wasn't fully sure. I tried to ask our guide, though I don't think she understood what I was asking. She probably doesn't get many tourists showing up eager to discuss lock mechanics.

After racing through smaller canal-like waterways, we arrived at the temple.

Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen rose above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi in a way that made subtlety impossible. The colossal golden Buddha gazed out over western Bangkok, towering above homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature beneath it. Coming in by long-tail boat made the whole thing more dramatic. The statue kept appearing and reappearing around bends in the canal, growing larger each time until it dominated everything.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself had a quieter pull. Shoes off to go in, and this time I remembered to remove my socks too, not wanting a repeat of the wet sock episode from Batu Caves. That felt like growth.

Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of the most striking Buddhist interiors I've seen, modern in a way that didn't cancel out the oldness of the place. In the glass pagoda, a glowing emerald-colored stupa sat beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky, and the whole room had an almost otherworldly atmosphere. Our guide came with us and explained the history as we went. The place felt deeply spiritual and unmistakably modern at the same time, with the giant golden Buddha outside casting its own strange scale over everything.

We reboarded the long-tail boat and retraced our route back. After a short stop to refuel at a fuel dock, we were dropped at the terminal again, where our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass, river reflections, designer perfume, boat traffic, durian snacks, and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it's probably ICONSIAM. Sitting right on the banks of the Chao Phraya River, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a massive mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment venues, and somehow it still manages to feel even bigger when you're standing in it.

The weird magic of ICONSIAM is that beneath the Louis Vuitton storefronts and marble floors, there's also a fake indoor floating market called SOOKSIAM. I mean that affectionately. Downstairs there was smoky grilled seafood, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to keep civilization going after the collapse. Touristy, yes. Also legitimately fun.

Hidden inside the mall was maybe the strangest resident of all: a colossal dachshund named Sunshine. It stretched across the room like a sleeping giant, its chest slowly rising and falling while shoppers stopped, stared, laughed, and pulled out their phones. Equal parts art installation, photo opportunity, and fever dream. Bangkok does seem to enjoy being slightly absurd in exactly the right way.

When we left the mall, we needed to get back across the river. We went to the ticket booth in the large patio area in front and asked about tickets, only to learn that the next ferry we needed was leaving in a few minutes. It was cash only, apparently, and I found myself frantically digging through my wallet trying to count foreign currency during a travel emergency. A woman behind the glass finally pointed at the exact bill I needed to hand over. She shoved some change and three tickets our way and told us to run.

So we ran.

We made it onto the boat slightly out of wind but feeling very alive. Maybe a little out of breath, but full of that jittery travel energy that only shows up after a minor logistical panic. The ride to our stop was short. We got off and started walking, and I don't remember if we had a specific destination in mind at that point. I think it was more a general area, one with lots of pins on the shared Google Map we'd been building while planning the trip.

We ended up in smaller streets, the kind where maybe a car can fit in one direction, but really the road belongs to people and motorcycles. The girls went into a 7-Eleven for drinks, I think, and I finally checked off a major Bangkok box by finding a real street food vendor: a little old lady with a wok and a pile of ingredients. I watched her make an order for a man who was standing there when I arrived. She had vegetables in Tupperware containers, a wok, and the kind of setup that makes you trust somebody immediately.

I was about to order when another similarly adorable old lady appeared and started helping me. She must have seen the unease on my face and recognized that I was one sentence away from absolutely butchering the process. She clarified things, guided me through it, and with her help I got pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container.

It was one of those Bangkok moments that feels like something lifted from a travel documentary or a YouTube video, except this time it was me standing there, getting handed the container. I ate all of it while we walked down the street.

There was also what seemed to be an entire parts district. One whole store of used brake rotors. Another of water pumps. Around the corner were more general parts, screws and bushings of various sizes, all of them looking as if they had been harvested from old cars and machinery. It had that wonderfully specific urban energy where an entire micro-economy lives on a few blocks and everybody around seems to understand it except you.

In a somewhat bougie area, while the girls were shopping for clothes, I found a place selling pistachio milk and decided I had to try it. The drink had that soft, muted pistachio color, pale jade and dusty rather than cartoon green, and it came cold in the Bangkok heat. The flavor landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted marzipan, less sugary than I expected, with a buttery roasted note underneath it all. Smooth, slightly salty, slightly sweet. It felt like one of those drinks invented by someone who got bored with ordinary coffee shop menus and decided to make life a bit greener.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. The original plan was facials, mostly because I'd never had one, and travel does make me want to collect a few "never done that before" experiences. But while we were looking at the spa menu and having the host explain the process, all the stages, chemicals, and equipment involved, I realized maybe I did not actually want strangers doing all that to my face. So I switched to an aroma massage instead. The host brought out a tray of scented boxes and I chose whatever she suggested. I don't remember what it was called, only that it smelled very nice, and at that point that seemed like enough criteria.

I was led to a room and instructed to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Fine. Easy. Except when I unwrapped the package, I was fairly certain there had been some kind of mistake. The underwear was very small and made of knitted material. I spent a solid minute or two in complete indecisive paralysis, just staring at it and trying to figure out whether this was normal, survivable, or some kind of test.

Eventually I opened the door, where the masseuse was patiently waiting.

"Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?"

"Yes," she said.

"Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking."

I closed the door. Welp. Here goes. I stripped down, put on the underwear, knocked on the door, and then made what felt like a diving leap onto the table.

Soon enough I forgot that whole episode completely, because the massage was probably one of the most relaxing things I've ever done. The aroma came from the traditional scented oil, and it was exactly what a weary traveler needed. Also, after conversion, it was only about $20 US, which remains an almost offensively good deal.

Afterward we were all tired of walking, so we took a Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned down a street and immediately we started seeing signs for things like "XXX Erotic Massage." At that point I briefly considered whether I had chosen the wrong massage venue earlier.

When the driver reached the end of the road, it was dark, and we couldn't see our restaurant anywhere. He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us. It read: "Bury everything and burn it." Clearly something had gone a little wrong there. He tried again, and this time the translation came out as: "My day is over."

That seemed like our cue to get out.

So we stepped onto the dark road and started walking back toward the XXX lounges because there wasn't much else to do. Then, down a side road, we spotted a blue sign with some kind of logo. I guessed it might be a Japanese bar and grill, something like the one I'd gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out.

It was exactly that.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31 like the kind of place you'd walk past three times before realizing half the city's food nerds were hiding inside. The smell hit as soon as the door opened: smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, hot rice. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes. At that point the day had already covered rain, boats, temples, ferries, luxury retail, street food, pistachio milk, spa confusion, and accidental arrival in a massage district, so settling into a room that smelled like grilling meat and soy felt oddly clean and sensible.

After that, it was back to the hotel to sleep. As I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was apparently asking about our flight tomorrow to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we'd already given them at check-in, and eventually all drifted off to sleep.
