## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Malls, and Questionable Underwear

```poem
We chased a boat through rain and latte delay,
Then floated past a Buddha the size of a weekday.
I bought tiny spa underwear and kept my pride on low,
Bangkok, as usual, put on quite a show.
```

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00am alarm for breakfast, which felt ambitious even at the time. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be the quick, efficient version of breakfast: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, this took much longer than expected. The time for our boat trip was getting uncomfortably close, so when everything finally arrived, I pounded the apple pastry on the spot and carried the rest with me like a person pretending this was all under control.

Kate led the way through the rain to the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. Eventually, after the usual amount of vague uncertainty that seems required before any transit experience becomes real, we found it. There was a brief safety meeting, some forms to sign, and then we boarded our long-tail boat.

A long-tail boat is one of those things most people have seen without knowing the name. Narrow wooden hull, brightly painted details, ribbons and flowers tied at the bow for luck, and behind it all a car or truck engine bolted to a long exposed driveshaft trailing behind the boat like a tail. Functional, noisy, slightly improvised-looking. Perfect, really.

Our boat took us up the river, the narrow bow slicing through the brown water, which is not a romantic description but it is the honest one. At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry metal contraption, then slowly motored inside. The gate slid shut behind us, and before long the one ahead opened. It did not seem like much of an elevation change, more like separating one body of water from another, but I was not entirely sure. I tried to ask our guide and do not think she really understood what I meant. She probably does not get many questions about the lock system from people on a morning canal tour.

We sped through smaller canal-like waterways and eventually arrived at the temple.

Rising above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi, Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen was impossible to miss. Its colossal golden Buddha gazed serenely across western Bangkok, towering over homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature beneath it. Coming in by long-tail boat made the whole thing more dramatic. The statue kept appearing and reappearing above the skyline, larger with every bend in the canal, until it dominated everything.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself had a quieter kind of pull. Shoes off to go in, and this time I remembered to remove my socks too, not wanting to repeat the wet sock episode from Batu Caves. Progress.

Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of the most striking modern Buddhist interiors I have seen, all of it balancing ancient tradition with a very contemporary kind of design. In the glass pagoda, an emerald-colored stupa glowed beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky, and the whole room felt almost unreal, not in a theatrical way, just in that rare way a place can alter the air around you. Our guide came with us and explained the history as we walked, which helped ground the place a little, because otherwise it might have floated off into pure atmosphere.

We got back on the boat, retraced our route, stopped briefly at a fuel dock to refuel, and were dropped at the terminal again. Our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to ICONSIAM.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass and river reflections and designer perfume and boat traffic and durian snacks and air conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it is probably ICONSIAM.

Sitting directly on the banks of the Chao Phraya, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody had tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a giant mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, promenades, entertainment, the whole polished futuristic package. And yet the strange charm of it is that beneath the marble floors and Louis Vuitton storefronts there is also SOOKSIAM, a fake indoor floating market that I mean affectionately. Downstairs were smoky grilled seafood stalls, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to carry civilization through a mild collapse. Touristy, yes. Also genuinely fun.

Then there was perhaps the least expected thing in the building: a colossal dachshund named Sunshine. It stretched across a room like a sleeping giant, chest slowly rising and falling as if it were actually alive. People stopped, laughed, stared, pulled out phones. It was equal parts art installation, photo opportunity, and fever dream, which felt about right for Bangkok.

At some point I finally checked an item off an internal list and found what felt like a real-deal street food vendor: a little old lady with a wok and a pile of ingredients. I was about to order when another similarly old lady appeared and started helping me through it.

"You want tomato?"
"This pork, not chicken."

Thanks to her intervention, I managed to order pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container. One of those Bangkok moments that feels like it belongs in a travel documentary or a YouTube video, except this time it was me, standing there, getting help from a stranger and trying to keep up. I ate the entire thing while we walked along the street, hot rice and pork and egg disappearing fast because apparently walking is no obstacle when you are hungry enough.

Later we wandered through what seemed to be an entire parts district. One whole store appeared to sell used brake rotors. Another seemed to specialize in water pumps. Around the corner were more general parts, screws and bushings in various sizes, all of it looking as if it had been harvested from cars and machinery and sorted into a second life. Bangkok can turn a single category of object into an entire neighborhood.

In a somewhat bougie area, while the girls did some clothes shopping, I spotted a place selling pistachio milk. I had no idea what that meant, which made me want it immediately.

It arrived the color of pale jade, muted and slightly dusty rather than bright green, in a plastic cup sweating instantly in the heat. The flavor landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted marzipan, but less sweet than I expected. It was cold, silky, slightly salty, slightly sweet, with a toasted richness underneath that made it feel both soft and oddly luxurious. The kind of drink that seems invented by someone who got bored with ordinary coffee shop menus and decided the world needed a greener, calmer beverage.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. The original plan was facials, partly because I had never had one and I do enjoy checking off the "never done that before" category when I travel. But while we stood there looking at the spa menu and listening to the host describe the process in detail, all the stages, chemicals, and equipment involved, I realized I did not actually want that at all. So I switched to an aroma massage.

The host brought over a tray of scent boxes for me to choose from. I honestly do not remember what I picked. It was whatever she suggested, and I remember it smelling very nice, which seemed sufficient as a decision-making process.

I was led to a room and instructed to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Simple enough in theory. Then I opened the package.

The underwear was very small and made of some kind of knitted material. I stood there for a full minute or two in total indecisive paralysis, convinced there had been some mistake. Finally I cracked open the door where the masseuse was patiently waiting, probably wondering what on earth was taking so long.

"Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?"

"Yes," she said.

"Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking."

I closed the door.

Well. All right then.

I stripped down, put on the tiny knitted mystery garment, knocked on the door, and made what felt like a diving leap onto the table. But I forgot about the whole absurd episode almost immediately, because the massage itself was one of the most relaxing things I have probably ever done. The scented oil, the cool quiet room, the feeling of finally stopping after a day of movement and noise, it was exactly what a weary traveler needed. And after conversion it was only about $20 US, which frankly felt almost suspiciously reasonable for how good it was.

Afterward we were all tired of walking, so we decided to Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned down a street lined with signs reading things like "XXX Erotic Massage," which did make me think, briefly, that perhaps I had chosen the wrong massage venue earlier.

When the driver reached the end of the road, it was dark and we could not see our restaurant anywhere. He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed it to us.

It read: "Bury everything and burn it."

Clearly something had gone slightly wrong in transmission. He tried again. This time it came out as: "My day is over."

That seemed like our cue to get out.

So we stepped onto the black road with not much to go on and started walking back toward the XXX lounges because there was, at minimum, lighting in that direction. Then down a side road we spotted a blue sign with some sort of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, the same general idea as the one I had gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31, it was the kind of place you could walk past three times before realizing half the city's food nerds were probably inside. The second the door opened I got smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, and hot rice. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes, and after the strange detour through darkness and translation chaos, it felt like we had accidentally landed somewhere solid.

After that, back to the hotel to sleep.

As I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was asking again about our flight tomorrow, apparently needing to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we had already given them at check-in, and eventually we all drifted off. Bangkok had managed, in a single day, to give us rain, riverboats, a giant Buddha, a luxury mall, fried rice from a wok, pistachio milk, tiny spa underwear, erotic massage signage, a cursed translation app, and a late-night Japanese grill.

Honestly, that felt about right.
