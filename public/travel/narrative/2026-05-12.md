## Tuesday, May 12, 2026 - Boats, Brake Rotors, and Tiny Spa Underwear

```poem
We chased our coffee straight into the rain,
Then boarded a boat with a truck-engine brain.
By night I wore mesh underwear with grace,
And still found dumplings in a questionable place.
```

We woke up in Bangkok to a 7:00 a.m. alarm for breakfast, which had seemed sensible when it was theoretical. In practice it meant moving quickly before I felt fully assembled. We took a Grab to a coffee shop and ordered what we thought would be a fast, efficient breakfast: takeaway lattes and teas, fruit cups, and an apple pastry from the case for me. Naturally, the “quick” option took longer than expected, because travel has a way of punishing optimism in very ordinary ways. Our boat trip time was getting uncomfortably close by the minute. When the order finally arrived, I basically inhaled the apple pastry on the spot and carried everything else with us.

Kate led the way through the rain toward the riverside terminals where our boat tour was supposed to be waiting. We finally found it, which felt like enough of an accomplishment to deserve applause, though nobody offered any. After a brief safety meeting and some forms to sign, we boarded our long-tail boat.

You probably know what a long-tail boat is even if you do not immediately recognize the name. It is one of those narrow wooden boats powered by what looks like a car or truck engine mounted on a long exposed driveshaft trailing behind the boat like a tail. Hence the name, helpfully literal. They are often painted brightly, with colorful ribbons, flowers, and bits of fabric tied to the bow for luck, and ours looked exactly like the kind of boat that makes you feel you have properly arrived somewhere.

It took us up the river, the narrow bow slicing through brown water, which is not a romantic description but was the honest one. At one point we went through a lock. We paused while one giant steel gate opened in front of us like some hungry metal contraption, then slowly motored inside. The gate slid closed behind us, and shortly after that the one in front opened. I had the impression this was less about elevation and more about separating one body of water from another, though I was not completely sure. I tried to ask our guide, but I do not think she understood what I meant. She probably does not get many tourists showing up with questions about lock mechanics.

After racing through smaller canal-like waterways, we arrived at the temple.

Rising above the low rooftops and winding canals of Thonburi, Wat Paknam Phasi Charoen was impossible to miss. Its colossal golden Buddha gazed serenely across western Bangkok, towering over homes, markets, and waterways that suddenly looked miniature beneath it. Approaching by long-tail boat really did add to the effect. The statue emerged gradually above the skyline, then grew larger with every bend in the canal until it dominated the horizon.

Beyond the giant Buddha, the temple itself offered a quieter kind of wonder. Shoes off to go in, and this time I remembered to remove my socks too, not wanting to repeat the wet-sock episode from Batu Caves. I was learning, slowly.

Inside were intricate halls, peaceful courtyards, and one of the most striking modern Buddhist interiors I had seen, all of it balancing ancient tradition with contemporary design. In the glass pagoda, a glowing emerald-colored stupa sat beneath a ceiling painted like a cosmic night sky, and the whole space felt almost otherworldly. The monumental Buddha outside and the tranquil temple grounds inside made Wat Paknam feel both deeply spiritual and unmistakably modern, one of those places where the city’s past and present seem to stand together without arguing about it. Our guide walked with us and explained the history as we went.

We reboarded the long-tail boat, retraced our route back, and made a short stop at a fuel dock before being dropped at the terminal again. From there our guide helped us buy ferry tickets to cross the river to Iconsiam.

If Bangkok has a monument to modern excess, all glass, river reflections, designer perfume, boat traffic, durian snacks, and air-conditioning cold enough to preserve mammoths, it is probably ICONSIAM. Sitting directly on the banks of the Chao Phraya River, it felt less like a mall and more like somebody had tried to compress all of Thailand into a luxury spaceship. It opened in 2018 as a giant mixed-use development with luxury retail, restaurants, residences, art spaces, river promenades, and entertainment venues.

But the strange charm of ICONSIAM is that beneath the marble floors and Louis Vuitton storefronts, there is also a fake indoor floating market called SOOKSIAM, and I mean fake with affection. Downstairs there was smoky grilled seafood, aunties pounding curry paste, boat noodles, sticky rice desserts, neon fruit juices, and enough fried things on sticks to carry civilization through a mild collapse. Touristy, yes. Also fun, and one of the easiest ways to sample regional Thai food without crossing the city.

Hidden inside the mall was maybe the most unexpected thing of all: a colossal dachshund named Sunshine. It stretched across the room like a sleeping giant, almost convincing in its stillness except that its chest slowly rose and fell. Shoppers stopped to stare, laugh, and pull out their phones. Equal parts art installation, photo opportunity, and fever dream. Bangkok does seem to specialize in that exact category.

When we left the mall, we needed to get back across the river. At the ticket booth in the big patio area out front, we asked about tickets and learned that the ferry we needed was leaving in just a few minutes. Naturally it required cash, so I found myself frantically digging through my wallet and trying to count foreign currency during a minor travel emergency. Finally the woman behind the glass simply pointed at the bill I needed to hand over. Some change and three tickets were thrown our way with the instruction to run.

So we ran.

We made it to the boat slightly out of wind but also oddly energized, maybe because near-misses are irritating and exhilarating in almost equal measure. It was a short ride to our stop. We got off and started walking, and I cannot remember if we had a specific destination at that point or just a general area with a suspicious number of pins on our shared Google Map from trip planning.

We ended up in smaller streets, the kind where a car might fit in one direction if everyone else politely vanished. Really they were for people and motorcycles. The girls went into a 7-Eleven for drinks, I think, and I finally checked off one of the boxes I had quietly been hoping for: a real street food vendor. A little old lady with a wok and a pile of ingredients was making food for a man who had been standing there when I arrived. She had vegetables in plastic containers on a small cart, a wok, and the kind of setup that suggests she had done this more times than I had crossed streets in Bangkok.

I was about to order when another similarly adorable old lady appeared and started helping me. She probably saw the uncertainty on my face and realized I was seconds away from making a complete mess of the interaction. She clarified things, guided me through it, and with her help I managed to order pork fried rice topped with a fried egg in a takeaway container.

This was one of those Bangkok moments that can feel like something from a travel documentary or a YouTube video, except this time it was me standing there holding the container. I ate the whole thing while we walked. The rice was hot and oily in the best possible way, the pork salty and satisfying, the fried egg soft enough to drag into everything else as I went, all of it balanced on that thin edge between comfort food and street-corner improvisation.

As we kept walking, we passed what felt like an entire parts district. One whole store of used brake rotors, another full of water pumps, and around the corner more general parts, screws and bushings of various sizes, all of it looking harvested from cars and machinery and given a second life on shelves. There was something almost beautiful about the specificity of it, whole businesses devoted to one narrow mechanical category.

In a somewhat bougie area while the girls were shopping for clothes, I saw a place selling pistachio milk and decided I had to try it. I did not know what it was, which was exactly the point.

Pistachio milk in Bangkok felt like a drink invented by someone who got bored with ordinary café menus and decided the world needed something greener, softer, and slightly more indulgent. The first thing I noticed was the color. Not bright mint green, not cartoonish, but a muted pistachio shade, pale jade and a little dusty-looking. In the Bangkok heat the plastic cup started sweating immediately. The flavor landed somewhere between nutty gelato and melted ice cream, but less sugary than I expected. It was cold, silky, slightly sweet, with a buttery roasted note underneath that made it feel richer than it looked.

Later we went to a spa in another mall. The original plan was to get facials, mostly because I like using travel to check off things I have never done before. But while looking over the spa menu and listening to the host describe the process in increasing detail, all the stages, chemicals, and equipment involved, I decided maybe I did not want that particular adventure after all. Instead I opted for an aroma massage. The host brought over a tray of boxed scents and I picked whatever she suggested. I do not remember what it was called. I just remember it smelled very good, which seemed like enough of a qualification.

Then I was led to a room and instructed to disrobe and put on the underwear in the package on the table. Fine. Simple. Except when I opened the package, the underwear inside was very small and made of knitted material, and I became briefly paralyzed by indecision. I stood there for a minute or two trying to determine whether there had been some sort of mistake, or whether this was simply one of those travel moments where the correct move is to stop asking the universe for explanations.

Eventually I opened the door, where the masseuse was patiently waiting for me to change, probably wondering what on earth was taking so long.

“Um... hi. Uhhh, so I am supposed to put those on and nothing else?”

“Yes,” she said.

“Oh, ok. Thank you. Just checking.”

I closed the door. Welp. Here goes. I stripped down, put on the tiny knitted underwear, knocked on the door, and then made a sort of diving leap for the table.

Soon enough I forgot the whole awkward prelude. The massage was one of the most relaxing things I have ever done. The aroma came from the scented oil, and after all the walking and humidity and constant sensory input, it felt exactly like what a weary traveler needs. Also it worked out to about twenty U.S. dollars after conversion, which is the kind of value that makes you reconsider your entire home country.

Afterward we were all tired of walking, so we decided to take a Grab to a restaurant that GPT had told us was featured on the OTR YouTube channel. The car turned onto a street and immediately we saw signs for “XXX Erotic Massage.” Well. Maybe I should have gone there for a massage instead.

When the driver reached the end of the road, it was dark and our restaurant was nowhere in sight. He opened his translation app, typed something, and showed us the screen. It read, “Bury everything and burn it.” Clearly a small detail had gone astray in translation. He tried again. This time it said, “My day is over.” That seemed like our cue to get out.

So we got out onto a black, dark road and started walking back toward the XXX lounges, because apparently that was the available strategy. Then down a side road we spotted a blue sign with some kind of logo. I thought it might be a Japanese bar and grill, something like the one I had gone to in Kuala Lumpur, and everyone agreed we should check it out.

That was exactly what it was.

Tucked into Sukhumvit 31 like one of those places you could walk past three times before realizing half the city’s food obsessives were hiding inside, it smelled like smoke, soy sauce, caramelized sugar, and hot rice the second the door opened. We ordered chicken dumplings, tuna sashimi, boneless chicken wings, and green scallion pancakes. After the strange detour and translation-app apocalypse, this felt like a very satisfying correction.

After that we went back to the hotel to sleep. As I was drifting off, I got a WhatsApp from my travel companions in another room. The hotel was asking about our flight the next day, apparently needing to re-double-triple-confirm the times for us. We gave them the latest info, which was the same info we had already given them at check-in, and eventually we all drifted off to sleep.
