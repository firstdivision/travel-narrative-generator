## Saturday, April 6

My first full day began before sunrise, annoyingly enough. I woke too early, made myself go back to sleep, then surfaced again a couple of hours later when the room had gone noticeably lighter. I lifted my head, opened the curtains, and found a pink sunrise waiting there. I took a photo, then lay back down for a little longer, which felt like the correct decision.

As promised to myself, I took a bath in the traditional style, seated on the special little stool. It was actually quite enjoyable. Relaxing, practical, easier to reach your feet and toes. There was something about doing it the way the room seemed to be asking me to do it that made me feel slightly less like I was just passing through.

I made it to hotel breakfast right after they opened, 6:30, I think. It was buffet-style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple that was very fresh and properly ripe, and I went back for seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down. I had not been entirely sure what it was, but I had agreed anyway, and I was glad I did.

Over breakfast I started trying to plan the day. Rain was supposed to arrive later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the Go app for taxis, one of those modern travel irritations that is also somehow unavoidable. Every country now has its own ecosystem of required apps and cards and systems. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and Touch 'n Go. Travel can still feel romantic right up until you are staring at your phone trying to create yet another account.

Then coffee did what coffee does. I went back to the room for a bathroom break and, in another first, used the fancy Japanese bidet. It had a control panel for type, strength, and other variables I had not previously considered part of this category of life. It was unusual at first, but I understood the logic quickly. The internet argument suddenly seemed less theoretical. If you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel. Annoyingly, they have a point.

Fresh and clean, I headed outside and ordered my Go taxi. While I was waiting, I noticed a man leaning against the hotel’s stone facade with what was clearly a level 100 hangover. Head in hands, posture collapsed, he looked like a cautionary tale in human form. Tokyo can be very polished, but it still allows for the occasional glimpse of someone thoroughly defeated by the previous night.

My ride arrived, and through our translation apps we confirmed where I wanted to go. I got the sense the driver was slightly puzzled that I was heading somewhere not especially famous, certainly not the kind of standard tourist destination one might expect from somebody leaving the Godzilla-themed hotel in the middle of tourist central. The apps did their job. I asked him about the cherry blossoms, and he replied that they were all in bloom everywhere.

That turned out not to be an exaggeration.

When we arrived, he opened the door for me, then held up one finger while typing something into his phone. He finished, turned the screen around, and it read: “Enjoy the cherry blossoms!” I smiled, and he saw that I had. Then we got into a brief Arigato Gozaimas loop, each saying it back to the other a few times until I finally gave a last wave and walked off.

I climbed up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were already out with their dogs, photographing the dogs with the trees, or holding them up for selfies together. Tradition, maybe. I had no idea. It was wholesome enough that I did not feel the need to analyze it too hard. I took my own photos, minus a dog, then walked down one of the promenades along the river and took more.

I was just about to head to a museum nearby when my intestines made their position known.

Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.

No. Oh no.

I tried to ignore it at first. I walked to an intersection, took a few more photos, and hoped it had been a one-off. It was not. The grumbling came back, followed by a hot feeling in my belly, as if I had swallowed a baked potato whole. It felt like I needed to fart, but with the kind of certainty that tells you this is not, in fact, a fart situation.

So I gave up on culture and ordered a cab back to the hotel.

It was all I could think to do. Most places were not open yet. I did not know the word for bathroom, and even if I had, I was not exactly eager to test the social etiquette of emergency restroom requests in a country where I was already trying not to blunder around too obviously. The cab ride was about fifteen minutes, which was longer than I wanted and shorter than I needed it to be.

I made it.

It was not a bluff. The bidet got a second shift.

After that, I waited on the bed for an hour or so to make sure there was not going to be a round two. When it finally seemed safe to trust my body again, I decided the next move would be Meiji Jingu, temple, shrine, park, the whole complex. It was about a 30-minute, 2-kilometer walk, which under normal circumstances is nothing, but under recently abnormal circumstances felt like a meaningful commitment. It had started raining, so I took the hotel-issued umbrella and went out into streets full of people moving around each other with practiced efficiency.

I started learning how to dodge umbrellas by watching everyone else. Lift yours, angle it slightly, adjust at the last second, keep moving. As usual, the fastest way to blend in was to imitate the locals until my body learned the pattern.

Meiji Jingu was busy at the main shrine, but still had space to breathe. I walked the paths, toured the temple and grounds, visited the garden and the well. The garden required an extra payment and, perhaps because of that, was much quieter. I liked it more. That was not a surprise. I am very much an introvert, and I am usually most at peace in places like that, somewhere quiet and self-contained, or else out on the water. The main temple had a crowded energy to it. The garden felt like it had exhaled.

Afterward, I doubled back and headed toward the hotel. The rain kept changing its mind. Sometimes it came down hard, sometimes it was just a mist, what we would call spitting back home.

At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned earlier as somewhere to check out. It was a giant mall with many floors, and given the weather, it seemed like an excellent time to go inside. I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed, but I hesitated. I was not sure whether the hotel would let me get rid of my old one there, and I did not want to buy a better suitcase only to end up dragging two around. So I left without it, though I suspected the one I liked was far better quality than the battered thing I already had. Maybe I could find something similar in Malaysia, where I would have more time to deal with practical problems like buying luggage.

The mall also had a basement section that I had read was full of food places. Somehow I forgot this entirely and walked back out into the rain toward the hotel instead.

I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but by then I had stepped in a few too many puddles and my socks and shoes were wet. That tipped the scales. I decided to regroup, with myself, back at the hotel.

I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, where everyone was still asleep and would read them later in their morning, and checked the radar. A clear patch was coming. It looked like enough time to go back out and check the chicken katsu box after all. So I put on fresh socks, listened to my stomach grumble its approval, and headed back out.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the destination, another of the many blue pins on my Google map. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately. Ordering was effortless too, another touchscreen doing most of the work. The only hiccup was the waitress having to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. All right. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side sat what looked like an ocean of brown beef gravy. I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, because apparently I am an expert now. Or at least willing to act like one.

Back outside, I was not quite sure what to do next. I had a little spell of analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., but was actually only 3 p.m. Jet lag had dissolved any real authority time normally has. So I decided to just wander and see what was around.

I heard what sounded like music and found an outdoor J-pop concert underway. I tried to take a video, but a security guard shut that idea down quickly. Fair enough.

Some time later, maybe 5:30, though time still had very little meaning, I went back to the hotel again. At the front desk I asked about getting a taxi for tomorrow. They made a reservation for me on the spot. Well, that was easy.

Back in the room, I tried not to fall asleep. I failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up again at 9. Since I was leaving the next day, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure.

Then more texts. Then my phone rang.

It was one of my friends from back home. The sun had come up on that side of the planet, and someone was awake and curious what I was doing over here. So I took my first phone call from this side of the world back to home, which felt oddly intimate and disorienting at the same time, like stitching two completely different days together.

I filled out this journal from 10:30 to 11:30. By the time I was fading toward sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in my room were swaying back and forth. At first I just watched them, then realized it was the wind. I could hear it now. It had been predicted, and it had arrived. The sound was steady and soothing enough that my eyes gave up before I did.
