## Saturday, April 6 - Blossoms, Bidets, and Rain

```poem
Pink dawn, wet socks, and intestines with opinions to air
Cherry trees looked perfect, my timing less debonair
I chased a shrine through drizzle with an umbrella held just so
Then ate gravy on katsu and let the loud night wind blow
```

It was my first full day, and I woke before sunrise, then forced myself back to sleep. A couple of hours later the room had gone lighter, enough that I finally lifted my head and opened the curtains. Outside was a pink sunrise. I took a photo, then lay back down again, not exactly noble, but honest.

As promised to myself, I tried the traditional-style bath setup in the room, seated on the little *special stool*. It was actually enjoyable. Relaxing, practical, and much easier to reach my feet and toes than the usual awkward hotel routine.

I made it to breakfast right after they opened, 06:30 I think. It was buffet-style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple that tasted properly fresh and ripe, and I got seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down, and I had agreed without really knowing what I was signing up for. This turned out to be one of the better decisions of the morning.

Over breakfast I started trying to plan the day. Rain was supposed to come later, so cherry blossoms first felt like the obvious move. I downloaded the `Go` app for taxis, one of those modern travel arrangements I both appreciate and resent. Every country seems to require its own little digital ecosystem, its own transport app, its own payment cards, its own tiny bureaucracies. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and a Touch 'n Go card. The world has become very convenient in a strangely fragmented way.

Before leaving, coffee did what coffee does, and I went back upstairs for a bathroom break. There I had another first, the fancy Japanese bidet, complete with a control panel for type and strength and whatever other refinements civilization has apparently achieved while I was elsewhere. It was unusual at first, then immediately sensible. Internet comment sections, for once, may have had a point. If you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel and call it a triumph.

Fresh and clean, I headed outside and started ordering my `Go` taxi. Right by the hotel wall was a man in what I could only describe as a level-100 hangover. He was leaning against the stone facade with his head in his hands, looking as if every decision of the previous night had filed a complaint.

My ride arrived, and through the combined miracle and absurdity of translation apps we confirmed where I wanted to go. I had the impression the driver found my destination a little odd, not exactly a standard tourist stop for someone emerging from the Godzilla-themed hotel in the middle of tourist central. I asked him about the cherry blossoms, and he told me they were all in bloom everywhere.

When we arrived, he opened the door for me, then held up a finger while typing on his phone. He turned the screen around. It said, “Enjoy the cherry blossoms!” I smiled, he noticed, and we ended up in a brief *arigato gozaimasu* competition, repeating it back and forth until I finally gave up, waved, and walked away still smiling.

I went up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were out with their dogs, taking photos of the dogs and the trees, or lifting the dogs up for selfies together. Tradition, maybe. Or maybe everyone had simply recognized that dogs improve almost any scene. Either way, it was fun and oddly wholesome.

I took my own photos, minus a dog, and walked down one of the promenades along the river. The blossoms were fully out, soft and pale against the gray morning. I kept stopping to photograph branches hanging over the water, and the whole place had that temporary, crowded calm that forms around something everyone knows will not last. I was about to head to a nearby museum when my intestines entered the story.

“Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.”

No. Oh no.

I walked to the intersection and took a few more photos, hoping it had been a one-off. It was not. The grumbling came back, followed by a hot feeling in my belly, like I had swallowed a baked potato whole. I felt like I needed to fart, but I was also extremely sure this was not a gas situation.

So, reluctantly, I ordered a ride back to the hotel. It was all I could think to do. Most places were not open yet, and I did not know the word for bathroom, much less the etiquette of begging for one as a non-customer in a growing digestive emergency. Those thoughts were looping in my head for the entire fifteen-minute cab ride.

I made it back.

It was not a bluff.

The bidet saw active duty for the second time that day.

Afterward I lay on the bed for an hour or so to make sure there would not be a second round. When it finally seemed safe enough, I decided to walk to Meiji Jingu, shrine, temple grounds, park, all of it. About a 30-minute, 2K walk. Under normal circumstances, nothing. Under less stable conditions, it had the psychological dimensions of an expedition.

By then it had started raining, so I took the hotel-issued umbrella and joined the flow of people out on the streets. I had to learn quickly how to dodge other umbrellas, lifting mine, tipping it sideways, adjusting by instinct. As usual, the easiest way to blend in was to copy the locals until the body learned what the brain had not yet caught up with.

Meiji Jingu was busy in parts and quiet in others. I walked the paths, saw the temple and the garden and “the well.” The main shrine area had a steady flow of people and a low hum of activity. The garden, which required an extra payment, was much less crowded. I liked it more immediately. Quiet places tend to reset me. I am, after all, very much an introvert, and I am usually most at peace somewhere still, or out on the water, or both.

After that I doubled back and started making my way toward the hotel. The rain kept changing its mind. Sometimes heavy, sometimes just a mist, what we would call *spitting* back home.

At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned earlier as a place to visit. A giant mall, many floors, and under the circumstances it seemed almost rude not to go inside. Rain has a way of simplifying decisions.

I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed, but I was not sure whether the hotel would let me get rid of my old suitcase there, or help me throw it away somehow. So I did not buy the one I liked, though I strongly suspected it was much better quality than the one I was dragging through this trip. I told myself maybe I would find something similar in Malaysia, where I would have more time to deal with practical things like replacing luggage.

The mall also had a basement food section, which I had specifically read about. Then somehow I forgot this entirely and walked back out into the rain toward the hotel.

I had planned to stop for chicken katsu on the way back, but by then I had stepped in too many puddles and my shoes and socks were wet. That was enough to lower my ambition. I decided to regroup, with myself, back at the hotel.

Back in the room I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, where everyone was asleep and would read them in their morning, and checked the radar. A clear patch was coming. It looked like enough time to go back out and finally check the “Chicken Katsu” box. So I put on fresh socks, listened to my stomach making its case, and headed out again.

The destination was CoCo Chicken Katsu, another of the blue pins on my Google Map. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately. Ordering was effortless too, another touchscreen. The only small hitch was the waitress having to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. Fine. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crisp, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side an ocean of brown beef gravy, because I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, clearly the move of a seasoned professional. Or at least someone pretending to be one.

Back outside, I stood on the street and had no idea what to do next. A little analysis paralysis had set in. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., and was actually only 3 p.m. Time had become decorative.

So I wandered. That seemed easiest. Eventually I heard music and found an outdoor JPOP concert underway. I tried to take a video, and a security guard shut that idea down almost instantly. Fair enough. A clean, efficient correction.

Sometime later, maybe 5:30, though time still meant very little, I went back to the hotel again. At the front desk I asked about getting a taxi for tomorrow, and they simply made the reservation for me on the spot. Well. That was easy.

Back in the room I tried not to fall asleep and failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up at 9. Since I was leaving the next day, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure.

Then more texts. My phone rang, and it was one of my friends from back home. The sun had come up there, it was their morning, and suddenly I was taking my first phone call from this side of the world back to that one. Someone, apparently, was awake and curious about what exactly I was doing over here.

From 10:30 to 11:30 I filled out this journal. By the time I was fading toward sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in the room swaying back and forth. At first I just watched them. Then I understood it was the wind. I could hear it now too. It had been predicted, and it had arrived. The sound was steady and oddly soothing, and my eyes, at that point, made the final decision for me.
