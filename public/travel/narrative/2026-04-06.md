## Monday, April 6 - Doors, Coffee, and Concrete

```poem
Jetlag kept watch for a tiger unseen
I lost to some doors and a shopping machine
Coffee cost more than a cross-town car ride
By night I had settled, if only with pride
```

I woke up early, partly from lingering jetlag, partly from the standard problem of being in a new place and not quite trusting it yet. I never sleep especially well the first couple of days anywhere new. Some old reptile part of my brain seems determined to stay awake and scan for danger, as if a sabertooth tiger might still be lurking just beyond the curtains. Only once that primitive little security system is satisfied that nothing is going to eat me in the middle of the night does it finally allow real sleep.

In the morning I decided to walk over to Tamarind Square, the shopping center across the street. First, though, I had to solve the immediate problem of escaping my own building.

On the lobby floor by the elevator there were double doors leading outside, and a sign on the glass said “push.” So I pushed. Nothing happened. I pushed harder. Still nothing. There was a man from the cleaning staff in the lobby, so I turned and asked him. He didn’t speak English, but he said something and pointed back toward the door. Fair enough. I pushed even harder, and this time both he and one of the guards in the outside lobby saw what I was doing and started yelling and pointing. I stopped.

What they were pointing at, tucked off to the side with no sign whatsoever acknowledging its existence, was a button. Apparently you push *that* to unlock the door from the inside. This naturally raised several reassuring questions about what happens if the power goes out, or the switch breaks. Maybe it fails safe and unlocks itself. Maybe if you push really, really hard the door eventually opens. Or maybe it shatters into ten thousand diamond-shaped pieces. In any case, lesson learned. I now knew how to open at least one category of door.

I started walking out through the road entrance and got yelled at again. Another pointed finger redirected me. There was a different way out, a path with stones and another gated door that required my RFID key. A lot of this morning seemed to involve being informed that I was opening things incorrectly.

Eventually I made it across the four-lane street, the kind with no crosswalk where one ought to have a more formal plan than simply walking and hoping, and reached the actual Tamarind Square lot. The place had a vaguely futuristic brutalist look to it, all concrete mass and hard angles, but softened by trees growing out of balconies and from the roof. It looked like the set of some apocalyptic film, war-ravaged architecture after civilization had returned, with nature having had a decent run at reclaiming the place in the meantime.

Then I failed to get into it.

There were stores and restaurants along the outer road level, but most were still closed except for the daycares, which were very much open and busy with morning drop-offs. It was still early. Through one doorway I saw an escalator. It wasn’t moving and seemed to lead into a dark hallway, which felt like it *should* be the entrance and also absolutely could not be. I moved on. I tried a stairwell instead and ended up in a distinctly behind-the-scenes area, something almost Disney-esque if Disney specialized in restaurant prep corridors. Employees were bustling around getting ready for the day. One woman gave me a look that clearly asked, *What are you doing here?* It was a fair question. I retreated back down to the road.

Eventually I found the entrance to a parking garage. Inside it there was an elevator with signs on it, which was the first genuinely encouraging development of the morning. I selected level three, and when the doors opened I finally found myself on the inside of the place. Even there it still felt half like infrastructure and half like a shopping center, with roadways, parked cars, and rows of restaurant and shop fronts. I wandered, which at this point was less a tactic than a personality trait.

Village Grocer was still closed until 10:30, but Celsius Coffee right next to it was open, and I like coffee, so in I went. Something I would quickly learn about KL was that there were a lot of coffee places, and that coffee was relatively expensive. My Grab rides to work would eventually average around $2, while a caffe latte would run me about $3. On my first day in KL, my internal sense of money was already malfunctioning.

After coffee, the grocery still wasn’t open, so I wandered again and found a little restaurant tucked away inside a labyrinth of stairs and hanging greenery. Birds were chirping. The tables felt both inside and outside somehow. It was perfect. I sat down, scanned the QR code menu, and ordered from my phone. I had a drink made from carrot, pineapple, and orange juice, which was excellent, and a simple plate of toasted bread with jam and butter, the good kind of butter, full-fat and salted.

It reminded me of similar breakfasts in Paris, those quiet morning cafés where all you need is coffee and a baguette with butter and jam for the world to briefly make sense. The same feeling found me here, in this small café hidden in the middle of a concrete maze that had somehow made room for plants and birdsong. I could feel myself starting to settle in.

Once Village Grocer finally opened, I went in to accomplish the day’s main mission, cleaning supplies. The Airbnb was fine, technically, but it needed cleaning. I bought sprays, sponges, and microfiber cloths, all of them destined to replace their worn-out counterparts back in the apartment.

Then I returned and cleaned. And cleaned some more. I washed dishes and put them in a special place in the cabinet for things that I had washed and therefore knew were actually clean. I did the same with one section of the utensil tray. I cleaned the butter knife. Yes, *the* butter knife, singular. The only one. Plastic, pink, and looking like it had escaped from an adult-sized Barbie Dream Kitchen.

In the evening I went back to Tamarind Square for dinner. By then it was much busier, fully alive in a way it hadn’t been that morning. I wandered again, this time slowly learning the logic, or at least the recurring madness, of its concrete passageways, escalators, and stairways that seemed to lead nowhere useful.

I stopped to look at the menu of one restaurant. A lot of the menus I had seen already looked broadly similar, with rice as the unifying principle, the starchy glue binding together nearly every meal photo in sight. A hostess came out to greet me. Her hijab framed her face as she smiled and asked if I’d like to come in and eat. It felt like one of those moments where saying no would require more confidence than I had, so I went in and sat down.

Another QR code, another order placed. I had garlic fried rice with ginger chicken, along with a passionfruit mint longan drink. Both were very good, and both did exactly what I needed them to do. As I left, all three women in the restaurant waved and thanked me. I waved back.

Back at the apartment, I figured out how to stream YouTube from my phone to the TV, which felt like a small but meaningful domestic victory. I was more comfortable now. I had a full belly, I had done some exploring, and for once I did not fall asleep on the couch. I moved to the bedroom.

Tomorrow would be my first day working in the Malaysia office, and I needed the rest.
