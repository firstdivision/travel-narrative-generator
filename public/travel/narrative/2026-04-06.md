# Monday, April 6 - Doors, Coffee, Butter Knife

```poem
Jetlag kept watch for a tiger that never came near
I battled smart doors with confidence, then mild fear
Coffee cost more than the rides, which felt slightly absurd
By night I had rice, one pink knife, and slept like a civilized nerd
```

I woke up early, partly from lingering jetlag and partly from that standard first-few-nights problem of being in a new place and not quite trusting it yet. I never sleep well at the beginning of a trip. Some old reptile-brain part of me seems determined to stay awake and scan for danger, as if a sabertooth tiger might still be a practical concern. Only once that primitive surveillance system is satisfied that nothing with teeth is coming for me in the night does it let me rest properly.

I decided to walk over to Tamarind Square, the shopping center across the street. First I had to learn how to escape my building.

On the lobby floor there were double doors leading outside, and a sign on the glass said "push." So I pushed. Nothing. I pushed harder. Still nothing. There was a man from the cleaning staff nearby, so I turned and asked him. He did not speak English, but he said something and pointed back toward the door. Fair enough. I pushed even harder, and this time both he and one of the guards in the outside lobby started yelling and pointing. I stopped. What they were trying to tell me, with increasing urgency, was that there was a button off to the side of the door, tucked away without any sign acknowledging its existence. Apparently that was what unlocked the door from the inside.

I did wonder what happens if the power goes out, or the switch breaks. Maybe it fails safe and unlocks itself. Maybe if you push *really* hard the door opens anyway. Or maybe it shatters into ten thousand diamond-shaped pieces. In any case, lesson learned. I knew how to open doors now.

Then I started walking out through the road entrance and got yelled at again. Another pointed finger redirected me. There was a different way out, a path with stones and another gated door. That one required my RFID key to open. It was a morning with a lot of door-related education.

I made it across the four-lane street, the kind with no crosswalk where one might reasonably want a crosswalk, and reached Tamarind Square. It had a somewhat futuristic, somewhat brutalist look, but softened by trees growing from balconies and the roof. It looked like it belonged in an apocalyptic film, war-ravaged concrete after the collapse, except civilization had returned just in time to interrupt nature's attempt to reclaim it.

Then came another small failure. I could not figure out how to get inside.

There were stores and restaurants along the outer road level, but most were closed, except for the daycares, which were very much open and busy with morning drop-offs. It was still early. Through one doorway I saw an escalator. It was not moving and appeared to lead up into a dark hallway. It looked like it *should* be the entrance, while also very much looking like it *could not possibly* be the entrance. I kept going.

I tried a stairwell instead and ended up in a Disney-esque behind-the-scenes area where restaurant employees seemed to be getting ready for the day. One woman hurried past and gave me a look that very clearly said, *What are you doing here?* I had no good answer, so I retreated back down to the road.

Eventually I found the entrance to a parking garage. Inside was an elevator with signs on it. I selected level three, and at last emerged into the inside of the place. Even there it still felt only partly inside. There were more roadways, cars parked in odd places, storefronts opening onto unexpected passages. I wandered, which I seem to be fairly good at.

Village Grocer was closed until 10:30, but Celsius Coffee next to it was open, so I went in. I like coffee. Something I would quickly learn about Kuala Lumpur was that there were a lot of coffee places, and that coffee was relatively expensive. My Grab rides to work would eventually average around $2, while a caffe latte would run me about $3. On my first day in KL, my sense of money was already broken.

After coffee, the grocery still was not open, so I walked again and found a little restaurant tucked away in a labyrinth of stairs and hanging greenery. Birds were chirping. The tables felt somehow both indoors and outdoors at once. It was perfect. I sat down, scanned the QR code menu, and ordered from my phone. I had a drink made from carrot, pineapple, and orange juice, which was excellent, and a simple plate of toasted bread with jam and butter, the good kind of butter, full-fat and salted.

It reminded me of similar breakfasts in Paris. In the quiet of the morning, if a place gets that part right, all it really takes is coffee, bread, butter, jam, and a little stillness, and suddenly everything seems arranged properly. I got that same feeling here, in this small café hidden in the middle of a concrete maze that somehow made room for plants and birds and calm. I was starting to settle in.

When Village Grocer finally opened, I went in to accomplish my main mission: cleaning supplies. The Airbnb was fine, but it needed work. I bought sprays, sponges, and microfiber cloths, all of them replacements for their tired predecessors back at the apartment.

Then I returned and cleaned. And cleaned some more. I washed dishes and set them aside in a special place in the cabinet, the section for things that *I* had washed and therefore knew were actually clean. I did the same with part of the utensils tray. I cleaned the butter knife. Yes, the butter knife. The only one. It was plastic and pink, like it had escaped from an adult-sized Barbie Dream Kitchen playset.

In the evening I went back to Tamarind Square for dinner. It was much busier now, fully awake, and had come alive in a way it had not that morning. I wandered again, slowly learning the logic, or maybe anti-logic, of this place with its concrete passages, escalators, and stairs that seemed to lead nowhere useful until suddenly they did.

I stopped to look at the menu at one restaurant. A lot of the menus I had seen already looked broadly similar. Rice was the unifying factor, the starchy glue holding together nearly every meal photo. A hostess came out to greet me. Her hijab framed her face as she smiled and asked if I would like to come in to eat. It was an effective tactic. It felt rude to say no, so I went in and sat down.

Another QR code, another order placed by phone. I got garlic fried rice with ginger chicken, plus a passionfruit mint longan drink. Both were very good, exactly the kind of meal that leaves you calm and satisfied rather than impressed with itself. When I left, all three women in the restaurant smiled, waved, and thanked me. I waved back.

Back at the apartment, I figured out how to stream YouTube from my phone to the TV. A small victory, but still a victory.

By then I was more comfortable. I had a full belly, a better sense of the maze outside, and the apartment felt a little more like mine, or at least temporarily under my management. So I did not fall asleep on the couch. I moved to the bedroom instead.

Tomorrow was my first day working in the Malaysia office, and I needed the rest.
