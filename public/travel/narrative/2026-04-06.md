## Saturday, April 6

My first full day started before sunrise, or at least my body thought it should. I woke up too early, forced myself back to sleep, then surfaced again a couple of hours later when the room had gone noticeably lighter. I pushed the curtains open just enough to see a pink sunrise hanging there over the city, snapped a photo, and then got back in bed. It felt like the right amount of effort.

As promised to myself, I took a traditional-style bath. Seated on the little “special stool,” which had seemed faintly ridiculous in theory and turned out to be quite good in practice. Relaxing, practical, and a lot easier for reaching feet and toes than the usual standing shower routine.

I made it to breakfast right after opening, 6:30, I think. It was buffet style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple that was especially fresh and ripe, and I immediately got seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked when I sat down if I wanted it, and I had said yes without really knowing what I was agreeing to. A rare and welcome success for impulsive decision-making.

While eating, I started trying to sketch out the day. Rain was supposed to arrive later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the Go app for taxis, one of those modern travel conveniences that I both appreciate and resent. Everything now comes with its own app, its own payment system, its own little administrative burden. Here it was Suica and Go. Later in Malaysia it would be Grab and Touch ’n Go. The world keeps getting easier and more annoying in exactly the same ways.

Back in the room, coffee did what coffee does, so I returned for a #2 bathroom break and, in another first, used the fancy Japanese bidet. There was a whole control panel for type, strength, and whatever else a toilet can apparently specialize in. It was unusual at first. Then, annoyingly, it made perfect sense. The internet cliché came to mind, the one people always trot out when arguing about bidets, usually at Americans. If you got shit on your hand, you wouldn’t just wipe it off with a paper towel. Fair enough. I had to admit they had a point.

Fresh and clean, I headed outside and started ordering my Go taxi. Right by the hotel entrance, leaning against the stone facade with his head in his hands, was a man with what I can only describe as a level 100 hangover. He looked utterly defeated, like the previous night had issued terms and he had accepted all of them.

My taxi arrived, and through a combination of translation apps and mutual patience we confirmed where I wanted to go. I got the impression he found my destination a little odd, not exactly the standard tourist route for someone departing from the Godzilla-themed hotel in tourist central. I asked him about the cherry blossoms and he replied that they were all in bloom everywhere. An excellent answer, broad enough to be reassuring and impossible to dispute.

When we arrived, he opened the door for me, then held up a finger and started typing into his phone. He turned the screen around when he was done. “Enjoy the chrry blossoms!” it said. I smiled, he noticed, and we got into a brief Arigato Gozaimas loop, each of us saying it back to the other a few times until I finally gave a wave and walked off.

I crossed up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were already out with their dogs, photographing the dogs with the trees, lifting them up for selfies, arranging tiny moments of seasonal dignity for them. Tradition maybe, or maybe just irresistible pet owner behavior. Either way, it was wholesome and hard not to enjoy.

I took my own photos, minus a dog, and walked along one of the promenades by the river. More photos, more blossoms, that soft brightness everything gets under a morning sky when the day still feels manageable. I was about to head toward a nearby museum when my intestines intervened.

“Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.”

No. Oh no.

I walked to the intersection and kept taking pictures, hoping it was a one-off, some internal misunderstanding. It was not. The grumbling came again, and now there was a hot, heavy feeling in my belly, like I had swallowed a baked potato whole. I felt like I needed to fart, but I was also very sure that was not, in fact, the situation.

So I reluctantly ordered a cab back to the hotel. It was all I could think to do. Most places weren’t open yet, and I didn’t know the word for bathroom, much less whether it was socially acceptable to ask for one if I wasn’t a customer. All of that ran through my head while I stood there trying to look like a person simply waiting for a ride and not a man in active negotiation with his own digestive system.

It was a long 15-minute taxi ride, but I made it. And no, it had not been a bluff. The bidet was called into service again.

Afterward I lay on the bed for an hour or so, waiting to see if there would be a round two. When it seemed there would not, I decided my next move would be Meiji Jingu, temple, shrine, park, the whole complex. It was about a 30-minute, 2-kilometer walk, nothing under normal circumstances, but potentially very far if my earlier crisis renewed itself midway.

By then it had started raining, so I took the hotel-issued umbrella and went out into streets crowded with people moving under umbrellas of their own. I gradually got used to the local choreography, lifting mine slightly, angling it aside, making tiny adjustments to avoid collisions. As usual, the fastest way to learn was to imitate the people who already knew what they were doing, so that’s what I did.

At Meiji Jingu I walked the paths, visited the temple and the garden, and saw the well. The temple area was busy, but the garden, which required an extra payment, was not. I liked the garden more. I am, after all, very much an introvert, and at my most peaceful in quiet places like that, or out on the water. The rain suited it. Everything felt softened, muted, less interested in performing itself.

Afterward I doubled back and started making my way toward the hotel. The rain kept changing its mind, sometimes heavy, sometimes only a mist, what we would call spitting back home.

At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned as a place to visit anyway. A giant mall, many floors, and at that moment a perfectly reasonable shelter from the rain. So in I went.

I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed. My old one was not exactly inspiring confidence. But I wasn’t sure whether the hotel would let me throw the old one away there, so I hesitated and left without buying the one I liked. It was probably much better quality than what I had, but uncertainty won. I told myself maybe I’d find it again in Malaysia, where I would have more time for practical errands like buying luggage.

The mall also had a basement food section, which I had specifically read about beforehand. Somehow I completely forgot this and walked back out into the rain toward the hotel instead. Not my sharpest moment.

I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but after stepping in one puddle too many my shoes and socks were wet, and that took the edge off my enthusiasm. I decided to regroup, with myself, back at the hotel.

There I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, where it was now morning, and checked the radar. A clear patch was coming. It looked like enough time to go back out and finally check the “Chicken Katsu” box. So I put on fresh socks, noted that my stomach was grumbling again, this time in a useful way, and headed back out.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the destination, another of the many blue pins on my Google map. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately.

Ordering was easy, another touchscreen. The only hiccup came when the waitress had to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. Fine. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side an ocean of brown beef gravy. I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, because apparently I was now operating as some kind of seasoned professional in the field.

Back outside, I found myself unsure what to do next. I had a mild case of analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., but was actually only 3 p.m. Time had become slippery in that jet-lagged way where the day seems both endless and oddly used up.

So I wandered.

I heard music somewhere ahead and followed it until I found an outdoor J-pop concert underway. I tried to take a video, and a security guard shut that down almost immediately. Fair enough. I put the phone away and kept moving.

Sometime later, maybe 5:30, though time still had very little meaning, I went back to the hotel again. At the front desk I asked about getting a taxi for tomorrow, and they simply made a reservation for me on the spot. Well. That was easy.

Back in the room, I tried not to fall asleep. I failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up at 9.

Since I was leaving tomorrow, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure. I sent more texts, made my first phone call from this side of the world back home, and then filled out this journal from 10:30 to 11:30.

As I was fading off to sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in the room swaying back and forth. Then I heard it, the wind. It had been predicted, and now it had arrived. Once I noticed the sound, my eyes didn’t have much of a chance. The wind kept moving outside, steady and soothing, and I fell asleep to that.
