## Monday, April 6 - Doors, Coffee, and Concrete

```poem
Jetlag kept watch for a tiger all night
While doors mocked me politely by refusing my might
I paid more for coffee than taxis would claim
Then dined in a maze and learned KL's game
```

I woke early, partly from lingering jetlag and partly from that standard first-few-days-in-a-new-place insomnia that always gets me when I travel. I never sleep well at the beginning. I blame some old reptile-brain function that insists on staying alert to scan for danger. Only once it has confirmed that no sabertooth tiger is going to eat us in the night does it finally let me sleep like a normal person.

In the morning I decided to walk over to Tamarind Square, the shopping center across the street. First I had to figure out how to get out of my building, which turned out to be less straightforward than it should have been. On the lobby floor by the elevator there were double doors leading outside, and a sign on the glass said "push." So I pushed. Nothing. I pushed harder. Still nothing. There was a man from the cleaning staff in the lobby, so I turned and asked him. He didn't speak English, but he said something and pointed back toward the door. Fair enough. I pushed even harder, and this time both he and one of the guards in the outside lobby saw what I was doing and started yelling and pointing.

I stopped.

They pointed to the side of the door, where, tucked away with absolutely no sign acknowledging its existence, was a button. Apparently you push *that* to unlock the door from the inside. I found myself wondering what happens if the power goes out, or the switch breaks. Hopefully the door fails safe and unlocks itself. Or maybe if you push really, really hard the door opens after all. Or maybe it shatters into ten thousand diamond-shaped pieces. In any case, lesson learned. I knew how to open doors now.

Then I started to walk out the road entrance and got yelled at again. Another pointed finger redirected me. There was another way out, a path with stones and another gated door. This one required my RFID key. A lot of this first morning seemed to consist of learning which objects that looked like doors were in fact doors, and how each one had decided it wanted to be operated.

I made it across the four-lane street, which offered no crosswalk where I happened to need one, and reached the actual Tamarind Square lot. It had a vaguely futuristic, brutalist look, but softened, or complicated, by trees growing from balconies and the roof. It looked like it belonged in some apocalyptic film, war-ravaged concrete after civilization had returned, but only after nature had already started taking the place back.

In another small failure, I couldn't find my way into it.

There were stores and restaurants along the outer road level, but most were closed, except for the daycares, which were very much open and busy with morning drop-offs. It was still early. In one doorway I spotted an escalator. It wasn't moving and seemed to rise into a dark hallway. It looked like it *should* be the way in, while also somehow clearly *could not* be the way in. I moved on. I tried a stairwell instead and ended up in a Disney-esque backstage area where restaurant employees seemed to be preparing for the day. At least, I thought, they will eventually be open. A woman bustled by and gave me a look that very plainly asked, *What are you doing here?* I had no answer to that, so I retreated down the stairs and back to the road.

Eventually I found the entrance to a parking garage, and inside it, finally, an elevator with signs. I selected level three. Exiting there, I found myself at last on the inside of the place. There were more roadways, cars parked in odd pockets, more storefronts and restaurants. I wandered, which by now seemed to be the skill I was most reliably bringing to the day.

Village Grocer was closed until 10:30, but Celsius Coffee right next to it was open, so I went in. I like coffee. One thing I would quickly learn about KL was that there were a lot of coffee places, and they were relatively expensive. My Grab rides to work would eventually average around $2, but a caffe latte would run me about $3. On my first day in KL, my sense of money was already broken.

After coffee the grocery was still not open, so I took another walk. This time I found a little restaurant tucked away in a labyrinth of stairs and hanging greenery. Birds were chirping, and the tables somehow felt both indoors and outdoors at once. It was perfect. I sat down, scanned the QR code menu, and ordered from my phone. I had a drink made from carrot, pineapple, and orange juice, which was excellent, and a simple plate of toasted bread with jam and butter, the good kind of butter, full-fat and salted.

It made me think of similar breakfasts in Paris, those quiet mornings when you can find a small café and order coffee and a baguette with butter and jam. The same feeling found me here, relaxation mixed with that brief conviction that everything is right with the world. It was a small café in the middle of a concrete maze that somehow also embraced nature, and for the first time that day I felt myself starting to settle in.

When Village Grocer finally opened, I went in to accomplish my main mission, cleaning supplies. The Airbnb was fine, technically, but it needed cleaning. I bought sprays, sponges, microfiber cloths, all intended to replace their weary counterparts back in the apartment.

Then I returned and cleaned, and cleaned some more. I washed dishes and put them in a special place in the cabinet for things that I had washed and therefore knew were clean. I did the same with part of the utensil tray. I cleaned the butter knife. Yes, *the* butter knife, singular. The only one. It was plastic and pink, like it had escaped from an adult-sized Barbie Dream Kitchen playset.

In the evening I went back to Tamarind Square for dinner. It was much busier now. The place had come alive. I wandered again, slowly learning my way around this odd maze of concrete, escalators, and stairways that didn't always seem to lead where they ought to.

I stopped to look at the menu of one restaurant. Many of the restaurant menus I had already seen looked broadly similar, rice as the unifying factor, the starchy glue holding together nearly every meal photograph I passed. A hostess came out to greet me. Her hijab framed her face as she smiled and asked whether I would like to come in to eat. It felt like one of those invitations that would be rude to decline, so in I went.

I took a seat, scanned yet another QR code, and placed my order. Garlic fried rice with ginger chicken, and a passionfruit mint longan drink to go with it. Both were very good and left me properly satisfied. When I left, all three of the women in the restaurant waved and thanked me with smiles. I waved back.

Back at the apartment I learned how to stream YouTube from my phone to the TV, which felt like a modest but useful victory. I was more comfortable now. I had a full belly, had done some exploring, and the place no longer felt entirely foreign. For the first time, I didn't fall asleep on the couch. I moved to the bedroom.

Tomorrow would be my first day working in the Malaysia office, and I needed the rest.
