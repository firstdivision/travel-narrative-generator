## Saturday, April 6

```poem
I chased pink blossoms and trusted my gut, which was bold
My gut returned the favor, in a way best not retold
Rain, gravy, umbrellas, and time that refused to behave
By night even the hangers were rocking me into the grave
```

It was my first full day, and I woke before sunrise, then forced myself back to sleep. A couple of hours later I noticed the room had gone lighter. I lifted my head, opened the curtains, and found a pink sunrise waiting outside. I took a photo, then lay back down again. It felt like a reasonable compromise with the morning.

As promised to myself, I took a traditional-style bath, seated on the special little stool. It was actually enjoyable, more relaxing than I expected, and a lot easier to reach my feet and toes. There was something very practical about it, which I appreciated.

I made it to breakfast right after they opened, 06:30, I think. It was buffet style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple so fresh and ripe it tasted almost suspiciously good. I went back for seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down. I had not been entirely sure what I was agreeing to, but I had signed up anyway, and I was glad I did.

After breakfast I started trying to plan the day. It was supposed to rain later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the Go app for taxis, one of those modern love-hate parts of travel. Every country now seems to come with its own app, its own transit card, its own little digital ritual. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and the Touch 'n Go card. Travel still worked, but increasingly only after a software installation.

Then I went back to the room for a number two bathroom break, thanks to the coffee, and had another first: the fancy Japanese bidet. It had a control panel for type, strength, the whole operation. It was unusual at first, but I understood the appeal quickly. Honestly, it made more sense than I wanted to admit. The internet argument came to mind, the one that appears every time bidets come up, usually with Americans pretending this is all somehow unnecessary: if you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel. Annoyingly, that is a very strong point.

Fresh and clean, I headed outside. While ordering my Go taxi, I noticed a man leaning against the hotel’s stone facade with what was unmistakably a level 100 hangover. Head in hands, folded into himself, visibly regretting whatever combination of drinks or decisions had brought him there. It was still early and already the day had range.

My taxi arrived, and through a combination of translation apps and patience we confirmed where I wanted to go. I had the sense the driver was a little concerned because my destination was not a standard tourist spot, certainly not the sort of place expected from someone departing the Godzilla-themed hotel in tourist central. Still, the app did its job. I asked him about the cherry blossoms, and he told me they were all in bloom everywhere.

When we arrived, he got out and opened my door for me, then held up one finger while typing into his app. He turned his phone around. “Enjoy the cherry blossoms!” it said. I smiled, and he noticed. We ended up in a little Arigato Gozaimas loop, saying it back and forth to each other several times until I finally turned away with a final wave and smile.

I walked up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were already out with their dogs, photographing the dogs and the trees, or holding the dogs up for selfies together. Maybe tradition, maybe not, I had no idea, but it was wholesome and funny in a very sincere way. I took my own photos, minus the dog, and then walked down one of the promenades along the river.

I took a few more pictures and was about to head to a nearby museum when my intestines announced themselves.

Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.

No. Oh no.

I walked to the intersection and kept taking photos, hoping it was a one-off. It was not. The grumbling came back, and with it a hot feeling in my belly, like I had swallowed a whole baked potato. I felt like I needed to fart, but I was very certain this was not a gas that wanted to leave my body. That kind of certainty arrives with unusual clarity.

Reluctantly, I ordered a cab back to the hotel. It was all I could think to do. Most places were not open yet, and I did not even know the word for bathroom, much less whether it was socially acceptable to ask for one if I was not a customer. My mind was cycling through all of that while my body was focused on a much more immediate deadline.

It was a long fifteen-minute ride, but I made it. And it had not been a bluff. The bidet got a second shift.

After that I lay on the bed for an hour or so, waiting to make sure there would not be a round two. When it seemed safe enough, I decided the next move would be Meiji Jingu, shrine, temple, park, the whole area. It was about a thirty-minute walk, around 2K, nothing under normal circumstances. Under slightly uncertain gastrointestinal circumstances, it felt potentially much farther away. By then it had started raining, so I brought the hotel-issued umbrella and headed out.

The streets were full of people, and I started learning how to dodge umbrellas properly, lifting mine, angling it aside, adjusting in little quick motions. As usual, the easiest way to learn was to imitate the locals until I got the rhythm right, so that is what I did.

At Meiji Jingu I walked the paths, saw the temple, the garden, and the well. The temple was busy. The garden, which required an extra payment, was not. I liked the garden more. I am, after all, very much an introvert, and I am most at peace in quiet places like that, or out on the water. The difference was immediate. The shrine had the weight of a major destination. The garden felt like stepping half a pace sideways out of the city.

Afterward I doubled back and started making my way toward the hotel. Sometimes the rain came down hard, sometimes it was just a mist, what we would call spitting back home. On the way I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had already pinned as somewhere to go. A giant mall with many floors. It seemed like as good a place as any to be while it was raining, so I went in.

Inside I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed. I liked one of the bags, and it was probably much better quality than my old one, but I was not sure if the hotel would let me ask them to throw away my current suitcase there and then, so I did not buy it. Maybe, I thought, I would find it again in Malaysia, where I would have more time for practical errands like buying luggage.

The mall also had a basement section with lots of food places, or at least I had read that it did. Somehow I forgot this completely and headed back out into the rain toward the hotel instead. I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but I had stepped in one puddle too many, and my socks and shoes were wet. That was enough to send me back to the room for another regrouping session with myself.

I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, messages for people who were fast asleep and would read them in the morning. Then I checked the radar and saw a clear patch coming through. Good enough. I could still go back out and check the chicken katsu box. In fresh socks, with a grumbling stomach, I headed out again.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the destination, another of the many blue pins on my Google map labeled Places to See. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately. Ordering was easy, another touchscreen. The only hiccup came when the waitress had to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. Fine. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side was what can only be described as an ocean of brown beef gravy. I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, because apparently I was now enough of an expert to do such things with confidence.

Back outside on the street, I was not quite sure what to do next. I had drifted into analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 PM, felt like 8 PM, but was actually only 3 PM. Time had detached from reality. So I decided to wander and see what was around.

At some point I heard music and followed it to an outdoor J-pop concert. I tried to take a video, and a security guard shut that idea down almost immediately. Fair enough. I kept moving.

Sometime later, maybe 5:30, though time still had no real meaning, I headed back to the hotel again. At the front desk I asked about getting a taxi for tomorrow. They made a reservation for me on the spot. That, at least, was easy.

Back in the room I tried not to fall asleep. I failed. Around 7:30 I took a nap and woke up at 9. Since I was leaving tomorrow, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure.

Then came more texts. At some point my phone rang, and one of my friends from back home appeared on the screen. The sun had come up there, it was morning on that side of the planet, so I answered my first phone call home from this side of the world. Someone, apparently, was awake and wondering what exactly I was up to over here.

From 10:30 to 11:30 I filled out this journal. By then the day had settled into that late-night hotel-room quiet, where everything is done except sleep. As I was fading off, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in the room swaying back and forth. Then I heard it, the wind. It had been predicted, and now it had arrived. Once I noticed it, the sound was strangely soothing. My eyes gave up on resisting it and closed on their own.
