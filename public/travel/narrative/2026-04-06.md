Rained on blossoms, then my stomach staged a coup.  
I learned the stool, the bidet, and umbrella traffic too.  
A taxi driver wished me well, a mall forgot to feed me.  
By night the hangers rocked in wind, and Tokyo finally eased me.

## Saturday, April 6

My first full day began before sunrise, which felt a little too ambitious. I woke early, forced myself back to sleep, then a couple of hours later noticed the room had gone lighter. I lifted my head, opened the curtains, and found a pink sunrise waiting there as if it had been arranged for me. I took a photo, then lay back down.

As promised to myself, I tried bathing in the traditional style, seated on the special little stool. It was actually quite enjoyable. Relaxing, practical, easier to reach my feet and toes. One of those things that sounds faintly ridiculous until you do it and realize it makes perfect sense.

I made it to hotel breakfast right after they opened, 6:30, I think. It was buffet style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple that was fresh and properly ripe, the kind that reminds you how disappointing supermarket pineapple usually is. I got seconds of everything. Then cheese toast arrived from the kitchen. The hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down. I was not entirely sure what I was agreeing to, but I said yes anyway, and I was glad I did.

After breakfast I started trying to plan the day. Rain was supposed to come later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the Go app for taxis, one of those modern travel arrangements I both appreciate and resent. Everything now requires its own app, its own payment card, its own little ecosystem. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and Touch 'n Go. The world has become extremely efficient in a way that still manages to feel annoying.

Then coffee did what coffee does, so I went back to the room for a bathroom break and had another first, the fancy Japanese bidet. It had a control panel for type, strength, all of it. Unusual at first, certainly. But I understood the logic almost immediately. The internet argument has a point. If you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel and call it a day. Mostly Americans seem to resist this idea, but I had to admit, the bidet lobby had made a convincing case.

Fresh and clean, I headed outside. As I was ordering my Go taxi, I noticed a man standing against the hotel’s stone facade with what was clearly a level 100 hangover. Head in hands, leaning into the wall, regretting every decision that had delivered him to that exact patch of pavement. He looked like a warning from the previous night.

My taxi arrived, and through a combination of translation apps and patience we confirmed where I wanted to go. I got the sense the driver was slightly surprised by my destination. It was not a standard tourist spot, certainly not the sort of place he might expect from someone being picked up at the Godzilla-themed hotel in tourist central. The apps did their job. I asked him about the cherry blossoms, and he told me they were all in bloom everywhere.

When we arrived, he opened my door for me, then held up one finger while typing something into his phone. He turned the screen around. It said, “Enjoy the cherry blossoms!” I smiled, he noticed, and we ended up in a brief Arigato Gozaimas duel, each of us saying it back and forth a few times until I finally surrendered with a wave and walked away.

I went up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were already out with their dogs, photographing the dogs and the trees, or lifting the dogs up for selfies with the blossoms behind them. Maybe a tradition, maybe just irresistible. Either way it was wholesome enough to lower my skepticism for a while.

I took my own photos, minus a dog, then walked down one of the promenades along the river and took more. I was about to head to a nearby museum when my intestines made their opinion known.

Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.

No. Oh no.

I walked to the intersection and took a few more photos, hoping it had been a one-off. It was not. The grumbling came back. A hot feeling spread through my belly, like I had swallowed a baked potato whole. It felt like I needed to fart, but I was extremely certain that this was not a gas-only situation.

So I did the only thing I could think of and reluctantly ordered a cab back to the hotel. Most places were not open yet, and even if they had been, I did not know the word for bathroom, much less whether it was socially acceptable to ask for one without being a customer. All of that ran through my head while I waited.

The fifteen-minute ride back felt very long. I made it, though. And it had not been a bluff. The bidet was used again.

Afterward I sat on the bed for an hour or so, waiting to see whether there would be a round two. When it finally seemed there would not, I decided on Meiji Jingu temple, shrine, and park next. It was about a thirty-minute walk, two kilometers, nothing under normal circumstances. Under the current circumstances, it felt potentially much farther. Rain had started by then, so I took the hotel-issued umbrella and ventured out into streets full of other umbrellas. I began learning how to dodge them by lifting mine up or angling it sideways. The usual travel rule applied. If I did not know what I was doing, I could at least imitate the locals until I looked like I did.

At Meiji Jingu I wandered the paths, the temple, the garden, and the well. The temple was busy. The garden, which required an extra payment, was not. I liked the garden more. I am, after all, very much an introvert, and I am most at peace in quiet places like that, or out on the water. The rain seemed to suit it. The whole place felt softened at the edges.

Afterward I doubled back toward the hotel. Sometimes the rain came down hard, sometimes it was only a mist, what we would call spitting back home. At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned as a place to visit. A giant mall, many floors. In the rain, that seemed like a perfectly respectable decision, so I went in.

I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed, but I was not sure whether my hotel would let me ask them to throw away my old suitcase there, so I did not buy the one I liked. I suspected it was much better quality than the battered one I already had. Maybe, I told myself, I would find it again in Malaysia, where I would have more time to do practical things like buy luggage.

The mall also had a basement food section, which I had read about. Somehow I completely forgot this and instead walked back out into the rain toward the hotel.

I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but by then I had stepped in a few too many puddles and my shoes and socks were wet. That was enough to kill my momentum. I decided to regroup, with myself, back at the hotel.

I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, where everyone was fast asleep and would read them in their morning, then checked the radar. A clear patch was coming. It looked like enough time to go back out and finally check the chicken katsu box. So I put on fresh socks, listened to my stomach grumble in a more cooperative way this time, and headed back out.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the target, another blue pin on my Google map. Navigation worked flawlessly, I arrived, and I was seated immediately. Ordering was effortless too, another touchscreen. The only hiccup was a waitress having to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. All right. Fine. Why not.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice, and on the side an ocean of brown beef gravy. I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, because apparently I am now an expert. Or at least willing to cosplay as one.

Back outside, I was not quite sure what to do next. I had a case of analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., but was actually only 3 p.m. Jet lag had taken apart the normal relationship between clock time and body time, and I was wandering around inside the wreckage.

So I just walked the streets to see what was around. At one point I heard music and found an outdoor J-pop concert underway. I tried to take a video, and a security guard shut that idea down almost immediately. Fair enough.

Sometime later, maybe 5:30, though time still had very little meaning, I headed back to the hotel again. I stopped at the front desk to ask about getting a taxi for tomorrow. They made a reservation for me on the spot. Well, that was easy.

Back in the room I tried not to fall asleep. I failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up at 9. Since I was leaving tomorrow, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure.

More texts. Then my phone rang, and it was one of my friends back home. The sun had come up on that side of the planet, it was their morning, so I took my first phone call from this side of the world back home. Someone, apparently, was awake and wondering what I was doing over here.

I filled out this journal from 10:30 to 11:30.

As I faded toward sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in my room swaying gently back and forth. Wind, I realized. Then I could hear it too. It had been predicted, and now it had arrived. The sound was strangely soothing. My eyes gave up before I did.
