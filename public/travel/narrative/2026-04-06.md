## Saturday, April 6

```poem
I chased pink blossoms with a stomach full of doubt
A bidet made its case, and I slowly figured it out
Rain pushed me through shrines, malls, and curry with grace
Then jet lag pinned me flat, while the wind rocked the place
```

This was my first full day, and it began before sunrise, with my body still not convinced what country it was in. I woke early, then forced myself back to sleep. A couple of hours later I noticed the room had gone lighter. I lifted my head, opened the curtains, and found a pink sunrise waiting outside. I took a photo, then lay back down again. It felt like the right amount of effort for that hour.

As promised to myself, I tried the traditional-style bath in the room, seated on the special little stool. It was actually enjoyable, more relaxing than I expected, and a lot easier to reach my feet and toes. Not exactly a life-changing revelation, but clearly there was some logic to it.

I made it to hotel breakfast right after they opened, 6:30, I think. It was buffet style, eggs, potatoes, oranges, pineapple so fresh and ripe it barely seemed related to the stuff back home. I got seconds of everything. A cheese toast arrived from the kitchen after the hostess had asked if I wanted it when I sat down. I had not been entirely sure what I was agreeing to, but I signed up anyway, and I was glad I did.

After breakfast I tried to plan the day. Rain was supposed to come later, so cherry blossoms first seemed like the obvious move. I downloaded the Go app for taxis, one of those modern travel annoyances that I both appreciate and resent. Every country now seemed to come with its own digital ecosystem, its own app, its own payment card, its own little administrative religion. Here it was Suica and Go. Later, in Malaysia, it would be Grab and the Touch 'n Go card. Travel was increasingly a matter of collecting temporary memberships.

Before leaving, coffee did what coffee does, which led to another first, the fancy Japanese bidet. It had a full control panel for type, strength, and other settings I had never considered should apply to this part of life. It was unusual at first. Then, annoyingly, it made sense. The old internet argument drifted into my head, the one people always quote when the bidet debate starts up, usually with Americans digging in their heels. If you got shit on your hand, you would not just wipe it off with a paper towel. I had to admit they had a point.

I headed outside feeling notably cleaner than usual and started ordering my Go taxi. That was when I saw a man leaning against the hotel’s stone facade with what was unmistakably a level 100 hangover. Head in hands, body slumped, he looked like a public service announcement about nightlife. I left him to his private reckoning.

My taxi arrived, and through a mix of translation apps and mutual patience we confirmed where I wanted to go. I had the sense he found the destination slightly unusual, not exactly the standard choice for someone setting off from the Godzilla-themed hotel in the middle of tourist central. I asked him about the cherry blossoms, and he told me they were all in bloom everywhere.

When we arrived, he opened the door for me, then held up one finger while typing into his phone. He turned the screen around. It said, “Enjoy the cherry blossoms!” I smiled. He noticed. We ended up in a brief Arigato Gozaimas contest, both repeating it back and forth a few times with increasing cheerfulness, until I finally turned away with one last wave and smile.

I walked up to a bridge over a river lined with blooming cherry trees. People were out with their dogs, taking photos of the dogs and the blossoms, or holding the dogs up for selfies with the trees behind them. Maybe tradition, maybe just good instincts. Either way, it was wholesome and oddly funny in the best way. I took my own photos, minus the dog, then walked down one of the promenades along the river and kept taking more.

I was about to head to a nearby museum when my intestines made their position known.

Errrrrrruuuuuuuummmmmmm.

No. Oh no.

I kept walking to the intersection and took a few more photos, trying to pretend it had been a one-off, a temporary disturbance, a false alarm. It was not. The grumbling came again, and a hot feeling spread through my belly, like I had swallowed a baked potato whole. I felt like I needed to fart, but I was also very sure this was not a gas looking for a harmless exit.

So I did the only thing I could think to do. I ordered a cab back to the hotel. Most places were not open yet, and I did not know the word for bathroom, much less whether it was socially acceptable to ask for one if I was not a customer. All of that ran through my head while I waited.

It was a long fifteen-minute ride back, but I made it. And it had not been a bluff. The bidet got a second shift.

After that I lay on the bed for an hour or so, waiting to see if there would be a second round. When none arrived, I decided to try again with a walk to Meiji Jingu, temple, shrine, park, all of it. It was about a thirty-minute, two-kilometer walk, which under normal circumstances would have been nothing. Under recently revised gastrointestinal circumstances, it felt like a meaningful commitment.

By then it had started raining, so I brought the hotel-issued umbrella and stepped out into streets full of other umbrellas. I started learning how to dodge them by watching everyone else, lifting mine slightly, angling it to one side, adjusting without stopping. As usual, the easiest way to blend in was to imitate the locals until my body figured it out.

Meiji Jingu was busy, especially around the main shrine area, but I made my way through the paths, the temple grounds, the garden, and the well. The garden required an extra payment and was much quieter. I liked it more. That felt predictable. I am an introvert, and places like that, quiet, damp, a little removed from the main flow of people, are usually where I feel most at peace. Either there or out on the water.

Afterward I doubled back toward the hotel. The rain kept changing its mind. Sometimes it came down properly, sometimes it was just a mist, or what we would call back home spitting. At some point I looked up and realized I was at Isetan Shinjuku, which I had pinned as a place to go anyway. It was a giant mall with many floors, and since I was already there and already wet, it seemed like a sensible place to hide from the weather.

Inside, I found a store selling luggage, which I obviously needed. Mine was old and not improving with age. I saw one I liked, and it was probably much better quality than what I had, but I was unsure whether the hotel would let me throw away the old suitcase there, or help me dispose of it somehow, so I did not buy it. I told myself maybe I would find something similar in Malaysia, where I would have more time for practical errands like replacing luggage.

The mall also had a basement food section, which I had read about beforehand. Somehow I completely forgot this while I was inside and instead wandered back out into the rain toward the hotel like a man with no memory and damp socks.

I had planned to stop at a chicken katsu place on the walk back, but by then I had stepped in a few too many puddles and my socks and shoes were wet. That was enough to kill my momentum. I went back to the hotel instead to regroup, mostly with myself.

Back in the room I dried off, sent some texts to the other side of the world, knowing everyone there was asleep and would read them later, then checked the weather radar. A clear patch was moving in. It looked like enough time to go back out and finally check off the chicken katsu pin. So I put on fresh socks, listened to my stomach grumble in a more ordinary and welcome way, and headed out again.

CoCo Chicken Katsu was the destination, another blue pin on my Google map of things to see and eat. Navigation worked flawlessly. I arrived and was seated immediately. Ordering was easy, another touchscreen, because modern life insists on consistency if nothing else. The only hiccup was the waitress needing to show me where the spoons were, in a box on the counter. Fair enough.

The chicken katsu arrived crispy, salty, and hot. Underneath was a bed of rice. On the side was what can only be described as an ocean of brown beef gravy, because I had ordered the beef gravy upgrade, like a man who had apparently decided he was now operating at an expert level. It was excellent.

Back outside, I stood on the street not quite sure what to do next. I had a little analysis paralysis. It looked like 5 p.m., felt like 8 p.m., but was actually only 3 p.m. Time had become unreliable. Jet lag had turned the day into an optical illusion.

So I wandered.

At some point I heard music and followed it to an outdoor J-pop concert underway nearby. I tried to take a video, and a security guard shut that down almost immediately. Fair enough again. I put the phone away and moved on.

Later, maybe 5:30, though time still had very little meaning, I headed back to the hotel. I stopped at the front desk to ask about getting a taxi tomorrow. They made a reservation for me on the spot. Well. That was easy.

Back in the room I tried not to fall asleep and failed. Around 7:30 I drifted into a nap and woke up at 9. Since I was leaving tomorrow, I packed, took another sitting bath, and organized everything for the morning departure. More texts followed. Then my phone rang, and I saw the name of a friend from back home. The sun had come up over there. Someone was awake and wondering what I was up to on this side of the planet. I took my first phone call from this side of the world back home, which felt both ordinary and strangely impossible.

From 10:30 to 11:30 I filled out this journal. By then I was fading. As I lay there ready to sleep, I noticed the clothes hangers on the open rail in my room swaying back and forth. For a second I just watched them. Then I realized it was the wind. I could hear it now. It had been predicted, and it had arrived. The sound was steady and soothing enough that my eyes gave up on staying open and closed on their own.
