## Sunday May 17 - Heights, Haze, and a Basement Pool

```poem
The mountain was lovely, the cable looked old,
I acted quite calm while internally cold.
We hunted for rooftops, then fled from the heat,
And ended the day by refusing to swim in a basement retreat.
```

The train pulled into Lao Cai station at 6:30, and we learned this not through any gentle transition into morning but through loud knocks on our cabin door. Waiting for us outside was our driver, Thuan, who took us on the roughly hour-long drive to our hotel in Sa Pa, The Azalea.

We were gratefully given an early check-in, which felt less like hospitality and more like a rescue. We took full advantage of it and slept for a while before dragging ourselves up to the top floor, the seventh, for breakfast. It was not as extensive as the Sheraton breakfast buffet, but it was perfectly fine, which was about all I needed at that point.

After that we bought tickets for Fansipan, which involved the cable car, a monorail, and then a final cable-pulled train thing that looked a lot like a cog rail train. The whole system felt very engineered for maximum upward movement with minimal consent from my nerves.

The cable car launched out of the lower station like a detachable quad at a ski resort, and then the ground dropped away so quickly it was hard to process. Soon we were more than a thousand feet in the air, floating over farms that moved slowly below us with the quiet confidence of things that were not dangling from a wire. I realized almost immediately that maybe I am a little afraid of heights. It is very hard not to start thinking practical, unhelpful thoughts like *how old is the cable?* when you are suspended at what feels like airplane height, staring down at the earth.

At the top there was a small village set up to display a bit of classic culture, though it also felt unmistakably touristy. There were dancers, and we stopped to watch a Vietnamese bamboo stick dance, the kind where long bamboo poles are clapped rhythmically against the ground while dancers step and hop and weave between them, trying not to get their ankles caught. At the end they invited people up from the crowd to try. I stayed where I was, committed to the role of interested observer, while my two travel companions went up without hesitation. They are not scaredy-cats like me.

Eventually we reached the top, or at least the place officially designated as such, marked with the familiar sign: *Top of Indochina. 3,143 meters high*. It had the usual feeling of mountaintop infrastructure, part triumph, part queue management.

On the walk down we found something called pancake cheese treats, and somehow none of us thought to say “Vi Pho Mai,” which was a real missed opportunity. A few landings farther down the steps there was a large Buddha, appearing with the kind of scale that makes you stop talking for a minute. Then it was more steps, more descending, and eventually back onto the cable car for the trip down in the other direction, which I endured with the same forced calm as before.

Lunch was at Sapa Nature View Hotel, another reminder that online photos are sometimes engaged in pure fiction. We started in a lower restaurant where the menu looked a little different from the one we had seen online, which is always a promising sign. After talking with the waitress for a bit, she explained that yes, there was another seating area up on the roof. This had to be the one from the photos, we thought. We followed her into an elevator, then up some steps, then out onto a patio that looked less like a restaurant and more like a place for hotel guests to sunbathe, which was exactly what two people were doing.

The view was undeniably nice, but we overheated almost immediately in the direct sun and retreated downstairs again, where it was shady and therefore vastly superior.

Later we walked around and considered getting a drink at Skyline Bar, but when we got there it was completely empty, which somehow made it less appealing than it should have. So we kept going to the park instead. It was still hot, but there was a breeze off the water that made the heat tolerable instead of oppressive, which was enough.

After that we passed Le Gecko Cafe, a French bistro sort of place with outdoor seating, and decided to sit for a while. We had drinks, wine, french fries, and banana desserts. It could have been one of those pleasantly aimless travel pauses, the kind where nobody is in a hurry and everything finally goes a bit soft around the edges, except we were constantly being harassed by peddlers. One old lady tried once, then came back five minutes later to try again. There was also a little boy carrying a smaller girl on his back. It changed the mood of the place for us, and eventually it ruined it enough that we left earlier than we would have liked.

Kate then had the excellent idea to simply google “the best hotel in town” and go to that bar. It was a solid plan. We found Absynth Bar at the top of Hotel de la Coupole, and for once the plan worked exactly as intended. It was a quiet rooftop patio bar, calm and civilized in a way that felt almost suspicious after the rest of the day. I ordered a French Blonde, and we sat there taking pictures of the clouds as they kept shifting around the surrounding mountains, revealing one ridge and then swallowing another. The weather seemed unable to commit to a single version of the view.

Back at our hotel, we went to inspect the pool, which turned out to be in the basement. It was not what we had imagined. It looked grimy, cold, and generally committed to the opposite of relaxation. So we abandoned that idea immediately and went back to the room to sleep instead. We were all very tired.
