## Thursday, April 2 - Coffee Before Contrails

At 2:30 AM, Joe picked me up and took me to the airport.

It was painfully early, but that hardly seemed like the moment to get casual about timing. There had been plenty of news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the latest "Unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how these unprecedented things keep happening so regularly.

It was too early for Taco Deli to be open, which meant I was denied one last taste of Tex-Mex before leaving. A small tragedy, if not exactly a surprising one. I did manage to find a much-needed, much-desired coffee, and since there was nothing decent to eat yet, I sat down and watched people instead.

That kind of hour makes everything feel slightly unreal. I found myself thinking about the situation I had arranged for myself, the scale of it finally landing with a little force. It was April 4, and I would not be back home until the end of May. A shot of adrenaline ran through me when I thought about that, and about my house, asleep without me. The water was turned off. Blankets were spread over the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I had thrown out anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants had been handed off to my cousin down the road. They were on a travel adventure of their own, in a manner of speaking.

Then the airport speaker crackled to life and yanked me out of all that. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes. Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful and short, just an hour and twenty minutes. The layover was four hours, but somehow it passed quickly. I finally found some "real food," an airport egg sandwich that cost $14 and did not exactly justify its own existence. I was in the international terminal, so I kept watching people and wondering which of them might be on my next flight, and which were headed somewhere else entirely. Were they going home, or leaving it? Were they thinking ahead the way I was, trying to picture the shape of the days waiting for them? My thoughts kept circling back to food. I wondered what I would end up eating, what I would find in these foreign lands, what the future might taste like.

One guy near the gate looked especially lost when the agents began announcing that passports had to be signed. He had the expression of a baby deer trying to understand bureaucracy. He kept looking down at his passport, at a very empty signature line, then looking around.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we started talking. It turned out this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him. It felt like the sort of thing airports are full of, these brief crossings of other people's lives, entire plots compressed into a few sentences at the gate.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I told him.

Then the flight to Narita boarded.

It was a 777, but an old one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone that slightly yellowed, ivory color that says an aircraft has been doing this for a long time. Not filthy, exactly, just seasoned. This old lady had clearly been flying for years. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to help the passengers wandering around with their bags, trying to find overhead space. It was mild chaos, the kind that feels more annoying when you know there are almost 14 hours still ahead of you.

As soon as I finally found somewhere to stash my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I could switch places with her because the people to my right were her group. Both seats were aisles, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, almost immediately after I sat down in the new seat, that woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was seated on the left side of the plane.

I looked over and saw him looking back at me with a hopeful expression. I suspect he also caught the look in my eyes, which was saying, as clearly as possible, *please don't make me do this*. He said something to his wife in a language I didn't recognize, and she turned back to me.

"He says it is fine."

Maybe he truly was fine sitting apart from his wife and children for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes. Maybe he had instantly understood that I was at the end of my seat-swapping generosity.

After takeoff, I managed to sleep through a good chunk of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep helped. The plane was freezing, aggressively so, and I eventually figured out a system. I pulled the hoodie strings tight so only my mouth and nose were exposed. The black fabric acted as a sort of improvised sleep mask over my eyes. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It made a little heat igloo, trapping some warmth and even a bit of humidity in that dry airplane air.

There was a meal service somewhere in there, but it was so unremarkable that I remember almost nothing about it.

Then I slept again.

At some point, somewhere over the Pacific, we crossed the international date line and it became Friday.
