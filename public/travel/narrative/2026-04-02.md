## Thursday, April 4 - Airports, Adrenaline, and Blanket Caves

At 2:30 AM, Joe picked me up and took me to the airport. It was painfully early, but these days that did not seem like the kind of thing to argue with. There had been plenty of news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back thanks to the latest "unprecedented" government shutdown, which felt less unprecedented every time it happened.

It was too early for Taco Deli to be open, which meant I was denied one last taste of Tex-Mex before leaving. A minor tragedy, though not one without treatment. I did at least manage to find a much-needed, much-desired coffee. So instead of eating, I sat and watched people.

That was when the scale of what I was doing really settled in. It was April 4, and I would not be back home until the end of May. A little shot of adrenaline ran through me when I thought about that, and about my house, sitting there asleep. The water was turned off. Blankets were spread over the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I had thrown out anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, inside and out, including the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants had been handed off to my cousin down the road. They were on a travel adventure of their own, just with less jet lag.

Then the airport speaker cracked to life and snapped me out of it. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes.

Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was short, just 1 hour and 20 minutes, and uneventful in the way you want domestic flights to be. I had a four-hour layover, but it passed quickly enough. I eventually found some "real food," an airport egg sandwich that cost $14 and still only barely qualified. Since I was in the international terminal, I went back to people-watching. I kept wondering which of the people around me might be on my flight and which were headed somewhere else entirely. Were they going home? Starting something? Dreading something? I found myself thinking mostly about food, which felt predictable enough. What would I find in these foreign lands? What would I be eating in the coming days?

One guy near the gate looked particularly lost when the gate agents started announcing that passports had to be signed. He had that baby-deer expression, all uncertainty and blinking. He kept looking down at his passport, at the very empty signature line, then back up at the room like the answer might be posted on a wall somewhere.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He took it gratefully, and we started talking. This was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him. It was one of those airport conversations that would sound invented if it were not so plainly true.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I told him.

Adventures all around, people crossing paths for a few minutes under fluorescent lights before being scattered across the world.

Then the flight to Narita boarded.

It was a 777, though an older one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone a little ivory-colored with age and use, and nothing about it looked particularly fresh. This old lady had been flying a while. Boarding had a low-grade chaos to it. Passengers were wandering the aisles trying to find places for their bags while the flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to get very involved. Mild chaos, really, but somehow more irritating when you know you are about to spend nearly 14 hours in the same tube.

Once I finally found somewhere to cram my bag and sat down, the woman on my left immediately asked if I could switch with her because the people to my right were her group. Both seats were aisle seats, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, almost as soon as I sat in the new seat, that new woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was across the aisle on the left side of the plane.

I looked over and saw him looking back at me expectantly.

I think he must have caught whatever expression crossed my face, something along the lines of please do not make me keep migrating around this aircraft before we have even pushed back. He said something to his wife in a language I did not understand, and I do not think it was Japanese. She turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually completely fine sitting away from his wife and children for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes. I could not blame him if so.

After takeoff, I managed to sleep for a good part of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep helped. The cabin was freezing, the kind of cold that seeps into your knees and makes the whole plane feel faintly punitive. I pulled my hoodie strings tight until only my mouth and nose were showing, and let the black fabric cover my eyes like a sleep mask. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It made a little heat igloo, trapping warmth and a bit of humidity inside. Not dignified, but effective.

There was a meal service at some point, unremarkable enough that I have no memory of what they gave us. Then I slept again.

Somewhere in all of that, we crossed the international date line and it became Friday.
