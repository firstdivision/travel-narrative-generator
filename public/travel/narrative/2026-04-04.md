# The Great Asia Adventure

## Introduction

This trip began in the dark, with the kind of hour that makes everything feel slightly questionable. At 2:30 in the morning, with my house shut down behind me and the long stretch from April to the end of May opening up ahead, the whole thing felt both carefully planned and faintly absurd. That seemed about right. Travel often starts this way, with logistics, caffeine, and a sudden awareness of what you have set in motion.

What stayed with me from the first day was how much waiting there was, and how much of it turned inward. Airport coffee instead of one last taco deli breakfast. News-cycle anxiety about TSA lines during yet another "unprecedented" government shutdown. Long stretches of people-watching in Dallas, wondering where everyone else was headed and why. Even before leaving the country, the trip already felt like a collection of parallel stories briefly sharing the same fluorescent space.

By the time I boarded the long flight to Narita, the mood had shifted from anticipation to mild chaos to simple endurance. There were seat-switch negotiations, overstuffed bins, a very old 777 that looked like it had seen some things, and the practical problem of staying warm enough to sleep. Somewhere over the Pacific, wrapped in airline blanket and hoodie like a person attempting hibernation in economy class, Thursday quietly turned into Friday.

## Thursday, April 4

At 2:30 am, Joe picked me up for the airport.

It was too early, but you never know these days. There had been a lot of news stories about TSA lines to the moon and back thanks to the ongoing "Unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how they seem to keep happening.

It was also earlier than Taco Deli opens, which meant I would be denied one last taste of texmex before leaving. I did at least find a much-required and much-desired coffee. So instead of eating, I sat down and people-watched. In that strange airport half-light, with everyone either too awake or not awake at all, I started thinking about the somewhat craziness of the situation I had put myself in. It was April 4. I would not be back home until the end of May.

That thought sent a little shot of adrenaline through me.

I thought about my house, sleeping while I sat in the airport with a paper cup of coffee. The water was turned off. Blankets were spread over the couches to keep dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I had thrown away anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Maybe most importantly, my plants were being babysat by my cousin down the road. They were on a travel adventure all their own.

Then the airport speaker came alive and snapped me out of it. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes. Time to get moving.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful, a short hour and twenty minutes. The layover was four hours, but it went by quickly enough. I finally found some "real food," an airport egg sandwich that somehow cost $14, which felt like both a meal and a minor financial insult. I was in the international terminal, so I kept people-watching, wondering who might be getting on my flight and who was going somewhere else entirely. Were they going home? Or were they, like me, leaning forward mentally into whatever came next? The future of food was what kept circling in my mind. I wondered what kinds of things I would find in these foreign lands.

One guy I noticed looked a little lost when the gate agents started announcing that passports had to be signed. He had that baby deer look, slightly stunned, trying to act like he understood what was happening. He kept looking down at his passport, at a very empty signature line, then back up at the room.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we started talking. Apparently this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport even.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

It turned out he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I said.

Adventures all around, really, threads being woven through airports and contrails.

When the flight to Narita boarded, I found myself on a 777, but an old one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone a little ivory-colored with age, not exactly sparkling clean. This old lady had been flying for a while, it seemed. The stewards seemed either unable or unwilling to help passengers wandering up and down the aisle trying to find somewhere to put their bags. Mild chaos, and we still had almost 14 hours to go before landing in Japan.

As soon as I finally found a place for my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I could switch places with her since the people to my right were her group. They were both aisle seats, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, as soon as I sat down in the new seat, this new woman asked if I could switch places with her husband, who was on the left-hand side of the plane. I looked over and saw a guy looking back at me, expectantly. But I think he noticed the look in my eyes that said, please don't make me do this. He said something to his wife in a language I didn't understand, I do not think it was Japanese, and she turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually totally fine sitting by himself in a different aisle from his wife and children for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes.

After takeoff, I managed to sleep through a lot of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep helped. The plane was freezing cold, and I soon figured out a system. I pulled my hoodie strings tight enough to reveal only my mouth and nose, and let the black cloth cover my eyes like a night mask. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the entire airline blanket over my head too. It created a little heat igloo and trapped some humidity as well.

There was a food service at some point, unremarkable enough that I have no recollection of what it was.

Then I fell asleep again.

Somewhere in there we crossed the international date line, and it was Friday.
