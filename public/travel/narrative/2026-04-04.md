# The Great Asia Adventure

## Introduction

The trip began in the dark, at the kind of hour that makes even ordinary decisions feel slightly questionable. By 2:30 in the morning I was already being driven to the airport, headed out on a journey that would keep me away from home until the end of May. There was adrenaline in that, but also a strange domestic stillness left behind, a house shut down for absence, water off, blankets over the couches, perishables thrown out, plants temporarily relocated like small evacuees.

The day itself unfolded in the usual airport rhythm of waiting, watching, and mild absurdity. Shutdown panic had people talking about TSA lines "to the moon and back," though in the end the travel itself moved smoothly enough. There was no last Taco Deli breakfast because it was simply too early for that, which felt like an unnecessarily harsh beginning, but there was coffee, and that counted for something. From Austin to Dallas, then a long layover in the international terminal, I had too much time to think about where I was going and what I might eat once I got there.

By the time I boarded the long flight to Narita, the day had taken on that suspended quality air travel creates, where strangers briefly intersect and everyone seems to be carrying some private future with them. I helped a first-time flyer sign his passport, got caught in a small cascade of seat-switch negotiations on an aging 777, and then surrendered to exhaustion. Wrapped in a hoodie and airline blanket against the freezing cabin air, I slept in fragments while the plane crossed the Pacific. Somewhere in all that dim recycled air, Thursday quietly became Friday.

## Thursday, April 4

At 2:30 AM, Joe picked me up to take me to the airport.

It was too early, but these days you never know. There had been all those news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the ongoing "unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how things keep being unprecedented in such a repetitive way.

It was also too early for Taco Deli to be open, so I was denied one last taste of tex-mex before leaving. That felt a little mean. I did at least find coffee, which was both required and deeply wanted, and so instead of eating I sat down and watched people. At that hour everyone looks a little displaced, either under-caffeinated or overcommitted.

Sitting there, I started thinking about the slightly crazy situation I had put myself in. It was April 4, and I would not be back home until the end of May. A quick shot of adrenaline went through me when I thought about that, and then about the house itself, sleeping in my absence. The water was turned off. Blankets were draped over the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I had thrown away anything that might rot over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants were being babysat by my cousin down the road. They were on a travel adventure of their own.

Then the airport speaker snapped me out of it. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes. Time to get moving.

The flight to Dallas was short, just an hour and twenty minutes, and uneventful. My layover there was four hours, which sounds long until you are actually in an airport and somehow lose half of it accomplishing very little. I eventually found some "real food," an airport egg sandwich that cost $14 and still only barely qualified. In the international terminal I went back to people-watching, which is one of the few airport activities that never really runs out.

I found myself wondering which of the people around me might be on my flight and which were headed somewhere else entirely. Were they going home, or leaving it? Were they thinking ahead the way I was, trying to picture the days waiting on the other side of the ocean? Food dominated my thoughts more than anything else. I kept wondering what I would find in these foreign lands, what exactly the future of eating was about to look like.

One guy near the gate looked especially lost when the agents started announcing that passports had to be signed. He had the expression of a baby deer trying to understand bureaucracy. He kept looking down at his passport, at the very empty signature line, then up at the room, then back down again.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we got to talking. It turned out this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I said.

That was one of those brief airport conversations that feels oddly complete. A few minutes earlier he was just another stranger under fluorescent lights, and then suddenly he had a whole life and a reason for crossing the world. Airports are full of that, little threads being woven together for a moment before disappearing again into different gates and skies.

Then the flight to Narita boarded.

It was a 777, but an older one, the kind where the plastic around the overhead bins had gone faintly ivory-colored with age and use. Not filthy, exactly, just no longer pretending to be new. This old lady had clearly been flying for a while. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to help the passengers wandering the aisles trying to figure out where to stuff their bags, and for a few minutes it was mild chaos, which is not ideal when you still have almost 14 hours to go before landing in Japan.

As soon as I finally found room for my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I could switch seats with her since the people to my right were part of her group. Both seats were aisles, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, almost immediately after I sat in the new seat, that woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was over on the left-hand side of the plane.

I looked over and saw him looking back at me expectantly.

But I think he caught the look in my eyes, the one that said please do not make me keep doing this. He said something to his wife in a language I did not understand, and I do not think it was Japanese. She turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually totally fine sitting by himself in a different aisle from his wife and children for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes.

After takeoff I managed to sleep for a good part of the flight, which was not shocking given that I had only had about three hours of sleep. The plane was freezing. Eventually I figured out a system, pulling the strings on my hoodie tight enough to reveal only my mouth and nose, with the black cloth covering my eyes like a makeshift sleep mask. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It created a small heat igloo, trapping a little warmth and even a little humidity in the otherwise cold dry cabin.

There was a meal service somewhere in there, but it was so unremarkable that I honestly cannot remember what they served. Then I went back to sleep.

Somewhere over the Pacific we crossed the international date line, and just like that, it was Friday.
