## Thursday, April 4

At 2:30 AM, Joe picked me up and drove me to the airport.

It was absurdly early, but these days you never really know. There had been plenty of news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the latest "unprecedented" government shutdown, funny how those keep turning out to be precedented after all.

It was too early for Taco Deli to be open, which meant I was leaving Texas without one last taste of tex-mex. That felt a little wrong. I did at least manage to find a much-needed, much-desired coffee, so instead of eating I sat down and watched people. There is something about an airport before dawn that makes everyone look provisional, as if none of us fully belong to ourselves yet.

Sitting there, I thought about the mild craziness of what I had set in motion. It was April 4. I would not be home again until the end of May. The thought sent a quick shot of adrenaline through me, along with an image of the house as I had left it, "sleeping." The water was turned off. Blankets were draped over the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I threw out anything that might go bad over two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants were being babysat by my cousin down the road. They were off on a travel adventure of their own.

Then the airport speaker crackled to life and yanked me out of it. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes.

Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful, just an hour and twenty minutes. I had a four-hour layover, but it went by faster than I expected. I finally found some real food, meaning an airport egg sandwich that somehow cost $14. Since I was in the international terminal, I went back to people-watching. I kept wondering which of the people around me were heading where I was heading, and which were bound for somewhere else entirely. Were they going home? Were they, like me, staring ahead at a stretch of unknown days? I found myself thinking mostly about food, which felt predictable enough. What was I going to find in these foreign lands? What would I be eating?

One guy near the gate looked especially lost when the gate agents started announcing that passports had to be signed. He had that baby-deer expression, a little stunned, a little alarmed. He kept looking down at his passport, at the very empty signature line, then back up at the room.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we started talking. This was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I said.

Airports do that, I guess. Everyone crossing through with their own separate plotline for a while, threads weaving together under fluorescent lights and departure screens.

Then the flight to Narita started boarding. The plane was a 777, but an old one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone a little ivory-colored with age, not exactly dirty, but not exactly sparkling either. This old lady had clearly been flying a long time. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to do much for the passengers wandering up and down the aisles trying to find space for their bags. It was mild chaos, the kind that feels more ridiculous when you know the flight still has almost fourteen hours to go.

Once I finally found a place for my bag and sat down, the woman on my left immediately asked if I would switch seats with her because the people on my right were her group. They were both aisle seats, and the woman to her left looked much smaller than the large guy sitting to my right, so I agreed.

Then, almost as soon as I had settled into the new seat, this new woman asked if I could switch places with her husband, who was over on the left-hand side of the plane. I looked over and saw him watching me expectantly. I think he also noticed the look in my eyes, the silent please don't make me do this. He said something to his wife in a language I did not recognize, and she turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually perfectly happy to spend the next 13 hours and 45 minutes sitting by himself in a different aisle from his wife and children.

After takeoff, I managed to sleep through a good chunk of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep certainly helped. The cabin was freezing, and I eventually figured out a system: hoodie strings pulled tight so only my mouth and nose were exposed, the black fabric over my eyes doubling as a sleep mask. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It made a little heat igloo, trapping warmth and a bit of humidity.

There was a meal service at some point, unremarkable enough that I cannot remember what it was. Then I went back to sleep.

Somewhere in all that, we crossed the international date line, and it became Friday.
