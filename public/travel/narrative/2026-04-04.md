## Thursday, April 4

```poem
At half past two I started this trick,
With coffee for courage and timing too strict.
I traded my seat, then hid like a mole,
While Thursday slipped east and Friday ate whole.
```

Joe picked me up at 2:30 AM to take me to the airport. It was painfully early, but these days that barely counted as overkill. There had been plenty of news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the latest "unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how unprecedented things keep happening over and over again.

It was too early for Taco Deli to be open, which meant I was denied one last taste of Tex-Mex before leaving. I did at least manage to find a much-needed, much-desired coffee. So instead of eating, I sat down and watched people. At that hour everyone looked slightly unfinished, as if they had been pulled out of bed and set in motion before their minds had fully agreed to it.

Sitting there, I thought about the basic craziness of what I had set in motion. It was April 4, and I would not be home again until the end of May. That thought sent a quick shot of adrenaline through me. I pictured my house asleep in the dark, water turned off, blankets draped over the couches to keep dust off, the fridge nearly empty after I had thrown out anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most important, my plants were being babysat by my cousin down the road, off on a travel adventure of their own, in the most sedentary sense possible.

Then the airport speaker cracked to life and pulled me out of it. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would be boarding in 30 minutes. Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful and short, just 1 hour and 20 minutes. The layover was four hours, which sounded long enough to become a problem, but it went by quickly. I finally found some real food, if an airport egg sandwich that somehow cost $14 can be called real. In the international terminal I went back to people-watching, trying to guess who might be on my flight and who was headed somewhere entirely different. Were they going home, or doing what I was doing, sitting in that strange suspended state before a long trip properly begins? I found myself thinking mostly about food, which felt predictable but honest. What was I going to find in these foreign lands? What would I end up eating first that I had never had before?

One guy near the gate looked genuinely lost when the agents started announcing that passports had to be signed. He had that baby-deer expression, alert but not usefully so. He kept glancing down at his passport, where the signature line was still completely blank, and then looking around at everyone else.

“Need a pen?” I asked.

He accepted it gratefully, and we started talking. It turned out this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

“Wow, go big for the first time,” I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him. It was one of those airport conversations that appears out of nowhere and then vanishes just as quickly, but for a moment it made the place feel less anonymous. Everyone was heading somewhere, each with some private reason that made perfect sense to them.

“Well, you’re going to have a great time, I’m sure,” I told him.

Then the flight to Narita boarded.

It was a 777, but an older one, the kind where the plastic around the overhead bins had gone a little ivory-colored and nothing looked exactly clean, only worn smooth by years of handling. This old lady had clearly been flying for a while. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to help the passengers wandering the aisles trying to find places for their bags, and the result was a mild kind of chaos, not dramatic, just annoying, especially on a flight with almost 14 hours still to go.

Once I finally found a place for my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I could switch with her because the people to my right were her group. Both seats were aisle seats, and the woman to her left looked a lot smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. Then, almost immediately after I sat down in the new seat, that woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was on the left side of the plane.

I looked over and saw him looking back at me with a certain expectation. But I think he also caught the expression in my eyes, which was saying, as clearly as possible, please do not make me do this. He said something to his wife in a language I did not recognize, not Japanese as far as I could tell, and she turned back to me and said, “He says it is fine.”

Maybe he was truly fine sitting away from his wife and children in another aisle for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes. Stranger things have happened, and probably for worse reasons.

After takeoff I managed to sleep for a good chunk of the flight, helped along by the fact that I was running on three hours of sleep to begin with. The plane was freezing. Before long I had tightened the hoodie strings until only my mouth and nose were visible, using the black fabric like a built-in eye mask. Then, borrowing a technique from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head too. It made a small heat igloo and trapped just enough humidity to feel human again.

There was a meal service somewhere in there, so unremarkable that I have no memory of what it was. Then I fell asleep again.

At some point in the dark over the Pacific, without any real ceremony, we crossed the international date line and it was Friday.
