## Thursday, April 30 - Curry Confidence and Too Many Fries

```poem
At lunch I played it cool with curry and heat,
By dinner I was ordering far more than I could eat,
Tamarind Square hid dinner like a concrete game,
And the fries arrived to quietly expose my shame.
```

At work, my new work friends treated me to lunch. We went to a restaurant near the work building and ordered one of my favorite kinds of meals, the kind I think of as family style, with plates and bowls set in the middle and everyone helping themselves to whatever looked good. It is one of the best ways to eat, especially at a new restaurant, and especially when the table keeps filling up with food you have never had before. You get to try a little bit of everything without committing your whole fate to one dish.

This small group was quickly becoming something like a foster family for me, which made the whole thing feel even better. There is a particular comfort in being folded into a table like that, passing dishes around, figuring things out by watching what everyone else reaches for first.

There were a lot of dishes, but the standout for me was a yellow spicy curry. I am still not completely sure what it was called. Some quick Googling later made me think it might have been a Peranakan-style curry, which sounds right, or at least close enough to let me sleep at night. What I had was a golden bowl of coconut-rich gravy, fragrant with lemongrass and turmeric, with spices that made themselves known before the first bite even landed properly. It had a steady heat to it, not reckless, not cruel, just persistent, softened by coconut milk and sharpened by a bright citrus edge. The chicken was tender enough that it nearly came off the bone on its own, and the sauce somehow managed to be comforting and aggressive at the same time. In any case, I liked it a lot.

I may also have earned a few bonus points for being able to handle the spice. This happened more than once on the trip. People seemed mildly surprised that I did not immediately lunge for water and start chugging like a man trying to extinguish an internal fire. Usually I just kept eating and, if anything, wanted more.

We had ordered too much, which is the sort of problem that only exists because everyone was making very good decisions in the moment. So we called in reinforcement. Another friend from work arrived not long after and helped us finish most of it. It felt appropriately communal, the meal expanding to accommodate one more person the way these things often do.

Later that night, my friend and I met up near where I was staying and went out for dinner at a nearby restaurant. I had been there before, but it still took me a while to find it in the concrete maze of Tamarind Square. That place had a way of making simple errands feel more architectural than necessary.

I think we ordered some kind of garlic rice and chicken dish. I do not remember it clearly enough to defend that answer under pressure, but I know I liked the restaurant. It was quiet, which is sometimes harder to find than good food. Quiet has its own appeal when you are tired. The room gave us space to sit and talk without feeling like we had to compete with a wall of noise.

I told her this was close to the kind of sit-down restaurant I had in mind when I had mentioned that earlier. Not exactly what I had imagined, if I am being honest. What I had wanted was to take her somewhere really nice, the kind of place that feels a little polished and intentional. But those seemed much easier to find in downtown Kuala Lumpur than out here in Tamarind Square, where everything felt a little more practical, a little more tucked into the edges of apartment blocks and walkways.

And then there were the fries.

While we were ordering at the counter, I saw a plate of french fries being delivered to another table on our way back.

“I think I’m going to order fries too,” I said.

“Will you be able to eat it all?” she asked.

“I’m not sure. But I think I want them.”

There was a brief pause, the kind that exists solely to give a person one last chance to make a reasonable decision. I ignored it and went back to the counter to order the fries.

As it turned out, I was *not* able to eat everything. Even as an adult, I was still apparently vulnerable to the old and very basic problem of my eyes being bigger than my stomach.
