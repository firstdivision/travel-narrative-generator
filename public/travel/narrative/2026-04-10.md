## Friday, April 10 - Friday, Still Friday

```poem
Nine thousand miles and Outlook still knew where to find me
I chased down comfort food with corporate static behind me
A fishy sauce tried sneaking past my thoroughly suspicious nose
Then a 10 PM meeting bloomed, because of course that's how Friday goes
```

Even roughly 9,600 miles away from home, Friday was still Friday. The last day of work still carried the same dull promise of escape, the coming weekend, the imagined little click of closing Teams and Outlook and VS Code and being done with all of it. In practice, I still skipped lunch and stayed late working on things that were getting in the way of what I would actually have preferred to be doing. Corporate busy work had apparently traveled just as well as I had.

Eventually I took a Grab back to Tamarind Square, this time with a specific mission. I was looking for what I had been told was the national dish, something called Nasi Lemak. Google Maps insisted there were multiple places in Tamarind Square serving it, which seemed reasonable enough if it really was the national dish, and it had also been described to me as a kind of comfort food, coconut rice and fried chicken, which sounded straightforward and promising. I was curious to see what would finally arrive on my plate.

I picked the first restaurant I saw that I knew served it, a place called Kocha Lala. Even though it was technically inside Tamarind Square, Tamarind Square continued to be a little strange in its layout, so the restaurant had a small gated outdoor patio along with an indoor seating area. I went inside and asked the first person I saw for a seat. She gestured in the universal manner that means sit anywhere you want, so I chose one of the outdoor tables, mostly because I wanted to people-watch while everyone moved through the square.

Like a lot of the restaurants I'd been in, ordering meant scanning a QR code and using an online system. I assumed this was partly for convenience and partly for people exactly like me, people who could not necessarily, or really at all, speak the language. It probably also saved the waitresses from having to explain menus to the wandering foreign idiot class.

Even though I already knew what I wanted, I scanned the menu anyway. I had also been handed a paper copy, which gave me an excuse to try Google Translate's camera feature. It quickly informed me that many of the dishes involved fish, and fish is definitely not on my menu, for no grand reason other than that I have never liked it very much. When traveling in foreign countries, avoiding accidentally ordering fish becomes a small ongoing administrative task. Google Translate has made that easier, or at least less mysterious.

Then the online ordering system produced its own little trap. It required a phone number, not optionally, and there was no way to enter a United States country code. So I went back inside and asked the hostess what I should do. She said, simply, "You can pay now if you want." I showed her my phone with the order loaded on the screen. She keyed it into the point-of-sale system, I paid with Apple Pay, and that was that. One more tiny logistical problem resolved by abandoning the app and speaking to an actual person.

The food arrived attractively plated, a perfectly domed pile of rice surrounded by fried chicken, a small salad, and some sort of sauce I couldn't identify. It looked neat and deliberate, the kind of plate that suggests someone cared what it looked like before it got to me. I tasted the sauce with my fork and immediately caught something fishy in it. Not aggressively, but enough. Still, I figured that once it was mixed with the rice and chicken I probably wouldn't mind it, and maybe it would even be good. The next week at work I would ask what that sauce had been and be told it was probably various fish pastes and fish sauce mixed with other things, so apparently my taste buds still worked fine. I can identify fish flavors pretty quickly, even when I would prefer not to. In this case I managed to ignore it. Sometimes eating in foreign countries means deciding, all right, whatever, I'm just going to eat this.

With a full belly, I headed back to my apartment. I had a late-night call to join at 10 PM. Actually, I had been supposed to join that same call every day since Tuesday, but I kept falling asleep and then waking up long after the meeting had ended. One of the less glamorous complications of global business conducted across time zones 13 hours apart is that someone is always on the call who would really rather not be there. Unfortunately, it usually seemed to be the people in the Malaysia time zone getting the worst of that arrangement.

Also, I had had a headache almost every day and still hadn't figured out why.
