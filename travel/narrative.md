Joe picked me up at 2:30 in the morning to take me to the airport, one of those departures that feels unreasonable even when you know there is no safer option. It was too early, but that is the logic of airport timing. Better to be exhausted than stranded.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful, which at that hour was about the best thing it could have been.

Boarding the flight to Narita was a mess. People were wandering up and down the aisle with bags, turning sideways, opening bins that were already full, looking around for help that never really arrived. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to do much about it. "American" Airlines. Sad.

By the time I finally found a place for my bag and sat down, I was ready to disappear into the seat and not negotiate with anyone for the next thirteen hours and forty-five minutes. Instead, the woman on my left immediately asked if I could switch with her so she could sit with the people to my right, who were her group. Both seats were aisles, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed. It seemed like a reasonable trade.

Then, almost as soon as I sat in the new seat, this new woman asked if I could switch with her husband, who was across the plane on the left side of the Boeing 777. I looked over and saw him looking back at me expectantly. I must have had something on my face, some clear expression of, please don't make me do this, because he said something to his wife in a language I didn't recognize, not Japanese as far as I could tell, and she turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually totally fine sitting by himself in a different aisle from his wife and children for the next thirteen hours and forty-five minutes. It was hard to blame him.
