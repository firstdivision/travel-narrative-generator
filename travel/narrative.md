# The Great Asia Adventure

## Introduction

The trip began in that peculiar hour when everything feels both overplanned and faintly absurd. At 2:30 in the morning, with the house shut down behind me and the airport still half asleep, it hit me that I would not be back until the end of May. That kind of realization does not arrive gracefully. It comes as a small shot of adrenaline while you sit with bad coffee, no breakfast, and too much time to think.

What followed was a long day of terminals, waiting, and watching other people move toward their own versions of elsewhere. There was the usual modern travel mood, low-grade uncertainty, overpriced food, bureaucratic announcements, and the strange intimacy of international gates where everyone is either leaving home or heading toward something they cannot quite picture yet. In Dallas, I had time to wonder what I was doing, and what exactly I expected to find on the other side of the Pacific, beyond a general fixation on food and whatever future meals might turn up.

By the time I boarded for Narita, the glamour had worn off and the practical reality of a fourteen-hour flight had set in. The plane was old, the overhead bins were a mess, people were already negotiating seats like a small domestic crisis, and everyone seemed resigned to it. Still, somewhere above the Pacific, wrapped in a hoodie and an airline blanket like a person trying to survive a minor Arctic event, I started to settle into the trip. At some point while I was asleep, the date line slipped by unnoticed, and Thursday gave up.

## Thursday, April 4

At 2:30 am, Joe picked me up and took me to the airport.

It was too early, but these days you never know. There had been all those news stories about TSA lines stretching to the moon and back because of the ongoing "Unprecedented" government shutdown. Funny how those seem to keep happening.

It was also earlier than Taco Deli opens, which meant I was denied one last taste of Tex-Mex before leaving. I did at least manage to find a badly needed and deeply wanted coffee. So instead of eating, I sat down and people-watched.

That kind of waiting leaves room for thoughts you might otherwise outrun.

I kept thinking about the slight craziness of what I had set in motion. It was April 4, and I would not be home again until the end of May. A small surge of adrenaline ran through me when I thought about that, and then about the house itself, sleeping in my absence. The water was turned off. Blankets covered the couches to keep the dust off. The fridge was nearly empty after I threw out anything that might go bad over the course of two months. The garbage cans were empty, both inside and out in the municipal wheeled bins. Most importantly, my plants had been relocated to my cousin down the road, where they were on a travel adventure of their own.

Then the airport speaker cracked to life and pulled me back into the present. Flight AA1658 to Dallas would begin boarding in 30 minutes.

Time to move.

The flight to Dallas was uneventful, a short hour and twenty minutes. The layover was four hours, but it passed faster than expected. I finally found some real food, if an airport egg sandwich that somehow costs $14 can be called real food. I was in the international terminal, which is good territory for people-watching, and I kept wondering which of the faces around me might end up on my flight and which were heading somewhere else entirely. Were they going home? Starting something? Were they, like me, trying to imagine the days ahead without being able to see them clearly yet?

Mostly, I kept thinking about food. I wondered what I would find in these foreign lands, what I would end up eating, what would become familiar and what would stay strange.

One man near the gate looked distinctly lost when the agents began announcing that passports had to be signed. He had that baby-deer expression, a little stunned, a little stranded. He kept looking down at his passport, with its very empty signature line, then up at the room around him as if someone might materialize with instructions.

"Need a pen?" I asked.

He accepted it with visible relief, and we started talking. It turned out this was his first time flying, his first time in an airport at all.

"Wow, go big for the first time," I said.

He told me he had a friend in Japan who builds custom cars, and he was going there for six months to work with him.

"Well, you're going to have a great time, I'm sure," I said.

Airports are full of that sort of thing, little intersecting lines of other people's lives, briefly visible before everyone disappears into different gates and different skies.

Eventually the flight to Narita boarded. It was a 777, but an old one. The plastic around the overhead bins had gone slightly ivory with age and use, not exactly sparkling clean. This old lady had been flying a while. The flight attendants seemed either unable or unwilling to help the passengers wandering the aisles looking for somewhere to put their bags. It was mild chaos, which felt ambitious for a flight with nearly fourteen hours still ahead of it.

As soon as I finally found a place for my bag and sat down, the woman on my left asked if I would switch seats with her so she could sit with the people to my right, who were part of her group. They were both aisle seats, and the woman on her left looked much smaller than the large guy on my right, so I agreed.

Then, almost immediately after I sat down in the new seat, this new woman asked if I could switch places with her husband, who was on the left side of the plane. I looked over and saw him watching me expectantly. But I think he caught whatever expression was on my face, something close to please don't make me do this. He said something to his wife in a language I did not understand, and I do not think it was Japanese. She turned back to me and said, "He says it is fine."

Maybe he was actually completely fine sitting apart from his wife and children in a different aisle for the next 13 hours and 45 minutes.

After takeoff, I slept through a good portion of the flight. Running on three hours of sleep helped. The plane was freezing, properly freezing, and I soon figured out a system. I tightened my hoodie strings until only my mouth and nose were exposed, and the black fabric worked as a kind of eye mask. Then, taking a cue from my seat neighbor, I pulled the airline blanket over my head as well. It made a little heat igloo, trapping warmth and even a bit of humidity.

There was a meal service somewhere in there, but it was unremarkable enough that I have no memory of what they served.

Then I slept again.

At some point, without ceremony, we crossed the international date line.

And it was Friday.
